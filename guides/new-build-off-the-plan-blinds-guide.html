Blinds for New Builds & Off-the-Plan Properties in Melbourne

You've spent months choosing tiles and tapware. Window furnishings are often an afterthought — until settlement day arrives and you're hanging towels over windows. Here's what to plan, when, and what it will actually cost.

1. When to Order — the Timing Problem

New builds create a specific challenge: windows don't have final measurements until the build is complete. Measure too early and plaster shrinkage, last-minute window changes, or delayed fit-out can mean your blinds don't fit. Wait until moving day and you'll spend weeks without privacy.

1

Frame stage — start planning

Research products and get preliminary quotes. Select a supplier and agree on a measure date for when the build is closer to complete.

2

Lock-up — confirm motorisation

If you want motorised blinds with hardwired power, your electrician must rough-in power points above window frames before plastering. This is the only affordable window. A 240V point above each window costs $60–$120 at frame stage vs $400+ after plaster.

3

Practical completion — book your measure

Once architraves, window sills, and skirting are installed (but before you move in), book your professional measure. Frames are now locked in. This is the right moment.

4

3–5 weeks later — installation

Custom blinds take 3–5 weeks to manufacture. Schedule installation for the week of or after settlement. Move in to a furnished, private home.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Asking the builder to include blinds in the contract. Builder allowances ($2,000–$3,000) rarely reflect actual market cost and are tied to a single supplier. You usually get better value and quality by opting out and sourcing independently — confirm this is possible in your contract.

2. Which Products Suit New Builds

New builds in Melbourne tend to have larger windows, open-plan living areas, and higher ceilings than older homes. This shapes which products perform best.

Most Popular

Dual Roller Blinds

  • Sunscreen + blockout in one bracket
  • Works on large windows up to 2.4m wide
  • Clean, modern aesthetic suits new builds
  • Motorises easily — one motor, two fabrics
  • Mid-range cost: $280–$480 per window
  • Fabric choice matters — cheap fabrics degrade fast
  • Requires quality brackets for longevity
Premium

Plantation Shutters

  • Adds structural value — buyers notice them
  • No fabric to fade or replace
  • Excellent light control with louvre tilt
  • Works beautifully with high ceilings
  • Lifetime product with minimal maintenance
  • Higher cost: $450–$850+ per window
  • Not ideal for large sliders (use bi-fold track)
Budget-Smart

Motorised Roller Blinds

  • Single fabric, clean look
  • Battery motors avoid wiring if missed at frame stage
  • Smart home integration (Google, Apple HomeKit)
  • Best value motorisation option: $320–$550
  • No privacy + light control in one unit
  • Battery motors need annual recharging
Bedroom Favourite

Blockout Honeycomb Blinds

  • Best insulation of any blind (R-0.8–R-1.2)
  • Excellent blockout in bedrooms
  • Softens the clinical feel of new builds
  • Very quiet operation
  • More expensive than roller blinds
  • Fabric sagging over time on wide spans

Large Windows & Sliders — Special Considerations

Open-plan new builds frequently include bi-fold or stacker doors 3–6m wide, floor-to-ceiling windows, or both. Standard single blinds don't span these openings cleanly. Your options:

  • Panel glide blinds — designed for wide openings. 3–4 fabric panels stack neatly to one side. Works to 6m+ without motorisation, though motors add convenience.
  • Multiple linked rollers — individual rollers on a continuous fascia track. Centre brackets hidden behind fascia. Most popular for windows 2.4–4.5m wide.
  • Motorised with centre split — for very wide sliders, two motors splitting in the centre handles the weight and looks intentional.
  • Outdoor blinds — for alfresco areas, wind-rated roller systems or zip-track systems that keep bugs and weather out while allowing airflow.
💡 Pro Tip

For floor-to-ceiling windows, choose a fascia (pelmet) system rather than standard brackets. It conceals the roller mechanism, looks architecturally intentional, and is essential if your builder installed square, ceiling-flush windows with no recess.

💰 Where Money is Best Spent

Invest in quality for the windows you use most — living room, master bedroom. You can save on lesser-used rooms (spare bedrooms, garage) without compromising the home's visual impression. A premium dual roller in the open-plan living area and standard rollers elsewhere is a sensible split.

4. Room-by-Room Recommendations

Open-Plan Living & Kitchen

The showpiece of the modern new build. Priority here is solar control during the day and privacy at night without blocking the view. The dual roller system (sunscreen + blockout) ticks both boxes. For bi-fold doors to alfresco, link the indoor roller system visually with outdoor blinds on the pergola.

Master Bedroom

Blockout is non-negotiable — new builds often have east-facing bedrooms that get 5am summer light. Choose either blockout roller blinds or blockout honeycomb. If you want softness, consider Roman blinds in a blockout lining. Motorise these if you can — rolling out of bed to open blinds at 6am is underrated convenience.

Children's Bedrooms

Child safety is a regulatory requirement in Australia. All cordless or motor-driven blinds comply with AS 2999:2016. Avoid looped chains in any room where children under 10 will sleep. Blockout honeycomb or cordless roller blinds are ideal. See our child safety guide for the full compliance requirements.

Bathrooms

New build bathrooms often have obscured glass already — but not always. If you need privacy coverage, aluminium venetian blinds resist moisture better than fabric alternatives. For bathrooms with a window above the shower, a PVC vertical blind or waterproof roller in moisture-resistant fabric (POSH ranges, Serene ranges) outperforms timber alternatives in longevity.

Home Office

Glare management is the priority. A sunscreen fabric (1–5% openness factor) reduces screen glare without blocking the view. Position the blind so it intercepts direct sun without darkening the whole room. For east-facing home offices, consider a side-opening blind track to target morning sun precisely.

5. Motorisation — Build It In Now

Motorised blinds in a new build are significantly cheaper than retrofitting later. Here's why the new build window matters:

  • Pre-wiring: An electrician can install a GPO above each window frame before plasterers close the ceiling. Cost: $60–$120 per point. After plaster, this involves ceiling cuts and patch repair — multiply the cost 3–5×.
  • Smart home integration: If your builder is installing a smart home system (C-Bus, Control4, Home Assistant, Google/Apple), your blinds can be integrated into schedules, scenes, and voice control. Plan this with your AV integrator at frame stage, not after handover.
  • Retrofit battery motors: If you missed the pre-wire window, modern rechargeable tube motors (Somfy Sonesse, Rollease) are invisible and last 12–18 months per charge. They're not as seamless as hardwired but are a genuine option.
💡 Smart Home Note

Somfy TaHoma, Rollease RYSE, and Automate all offer HomeKit and Google Home bridges. You don't need a $10,000 custom integration — these work with standard smart home setups at reasonable cost. Tell your supplier upfront which platform you're using.

6. The Mistakes People Make

After furnishing thousands of Melbourne new builds, these are the patterns we see:

  1. Trusting the builder's blinds allowance. It's usually $2,000–$2,500 for a $800,000 home. That buys roller blinds for maybe 8 windows. The allowance is a negotiating tool, not a real solution.
  2. Ordering before practical completion. Window dimensions can shift by 5–10mm during final fit-out. Custom blinds made 3 months early can come back undersized.
  3. Choosing based on display room lighting. Showrooms use flattering light. Ask to see fabric samples in natural daylight, ideally in-home, before committing.
  4. Forgetting the alfresco. Your outdoor entertaining area is an extension of the home — and Melbourne weather demands it. Outdoor blinds or a zip-track system for the pergola should be in the budget from day one.
  5. Skipping motorisation on high windows. Manual chains on 3m-high windows are a daily frustration. Motorise anything above standing reach.
  6. Buying cheap for the whole house. Entry-level products on large new build windows look worse than they do on small windows. Invest in at least mid-tier quality — the size magnifies any cut corners.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I order blinds for a new build? ↓
Book a measure at practical completion — once plaster, architraves, and window frames are fully installed but before you move in. Typically 2–6 weeks before settlement. Ordering too early risks measurement changes; waiting until settlement means 3–6 weeks without privacy while custom products are made.
What is a realistic budget for blinds in a 4-bedroom Melbourne home? ↓
For a standard 4-bedroom Melbourne home with 20–25 windows: roller blinds throughout $4,500–$8,000; plantation shutters throughout $12,000–$22,000; mixed approach (shutters in living areas, blockout rollers in bedrooms) $7,000–$13,000. Prices depend heavily on window sizes and product choices.
Can I use the builder's window furnishing allowance for custom blinds? ↓
Typically no — builder allowances usually only apply to the builder's nominated supplier. However, you can often opt out of the allowance and source independently. Confirm this in your contract before signing. Independent suppliers like iSeekBlinds typically deliver higher quality for equivalent or lower cost.
Should I motorise blinds in a new build? ↓
If you're building, now is the best time. Electricians can pre-wire motorised blinds power points ($60–$120 per point) before walls close. Retrofitting later costs significantly more. For floor-to-ceiling windows, high windows, or skylights — motorisation is practically essential, not optional.
What blinds work best with large windows in new builds? ↓
Large windows over 2.4m wide require either motorisation, a centre split, or specific products. Panel glide blinds work for very wide openings. Dual rollers with linked fascia cover 2.4–4.5m spans. Plantation shutters on bi-fold tracks work beautifully for large stacker door openings. Avoid heavy manual chain systems on anything wider than 2.1m.
