01 — The Basics

Day vs Night Privacy — The Distinction Most People Miss

Here's the thing most people don't realise until they've already installed their blinds: daytime privacy and nighttime privacy are completely different problems.

During the day, light hits the outside of your windows. Anyone looking in sees a bright surface — even a sheer fabric blocks most visibility. Your home is naturally private.

At night, the equation reverses. Your interior lights turn your windows into illuminated screens. Suddenly that light-filtering roller blind that seemed perfectly private during the day becomes semi-transparent. Silhouettes are visible. Colours bleed through. The street can see in.

☀️ Daytime

Light Works In Your Favour

  • Sunscreen rollers: Good privacy — outdoor light masks interior
  • Sheers: Surprisingly effective — haze blocks direct view
  • Light filtering: Solid daytime privacy
  • Venetians (tilted): Excellent — adjustable angles control view
🌙 Nighttime

Light Works Against You

  • Sunscreen rollers: Poor — silhouettes clearly visible
  • Sheers: Very poor — almost transparent when backlit
  • Light filtering: Moderate — some silhouette bleeding
  • Venetians (closed): Good — but light leaks at edges
The Ground Floor Rule

Privacy needs increase dramatically on ground floors and street-facing windows. A bedroom on the second floor can get away with light-filtering blinds. The same room at street level needs blockout or dual rollers for any real nighttime privacy.

Sunscreen ≠ Privacy at Night

The most common regret we see: homeowners install sunscreen rollers in bedrooms or living rooms, expecting full privacy. During the day they look great. Then evening comes and the neighbours can watch TV with you. If a room is used at night, sunscreen-only is never enough.

03 — Daytime Privacy

Best Blinds for Daytime Privacy

If your primary need is daytime privacy — blocking the view from the street while still letting natural light flood in — these options give you exactly that:

Sunscreen Roller Blinds

Sunscreen fabric is a micro-perforated mesh. From outside, people see a textured surface. From inside, you still get a view out and plenty of natural light. Openness factors range from 1% (more private) to 10% (more transparent). For street-level windows, choose 1–3% openness.

Plantation Shutters with Tilted Louvres

Angle the louvres upward and passers-by can't see in from street level, but light still enters and you maintain partial views. This is why shutters remain the gold standard for ground-floor street-facing windows in Melbourne's older suburbs — they solve privacy without sacrificing the façade or natural light.

Light-Filtering Honeycomb Blinds

The cellular structure does double duty: it diffuses incoming light beautifully while creating enough visual obstruction that people outside can't see through. Top-down/bottom-up honeycomb blinds let you lower the top portion for light while keeping the bottom closed for privacy — genuinely the best of both worlds.

Pro Tip: Top-Down/Bottom-Up

If daytime privacy with natural light is your main goal, ask for top-down/bottom-up operation on honeycomb or pleated blinds. You lower from the top to let light in through the upper glass while the lower half stays covered. It's the single most under-used privacy feature in window treatments.

04 — Nighttime Privacy

Best Blinds for Nighttime Privacy

At night, only opaque materials truly block visibility. No amount of "light filtering" prevents silhouettes when your lounge room is lit. Here's what actually works:

Blockout Roller Blinds

The definitive nighttime privacy solution. A properly fitted blockout roller in inside mount (or with side channels) blocks 100% of light and visibility. No silhouettes. No glow. Complete privacy. The fabric coating — usually a multi-layer polymer backing — is what does the work. Check that the fabric is genuinely blockout-rated, not just "dim-out."

Blockout Honeycomb Blinds

Same blockout performance as rollers, with the added benefit of thermal insulation. The air pockets in cellular fabric provide R-values of 0.4–0.7, meaning your privacy solution is also cutting your heating bill. Melbourne homes with south-facing bedrooms benefit enormously.

Heavy Blockout Curtains

Lined curtains with a blockout backing (or a separate blockout lining) provide excellent nighttime privacy. The overlap where curtains meet eliminates the centre gap that venetians and some roller blinds can have. For formal living areas and master bedrooms, layered curtains with blinds provide the most complete solution.

05 — All-Day Coverage

Best for Both Day & Night Privacy

Most rooms need privacy around the clock. These options handle both without compromise:

Dual Roller Blinds — The Standout Choice

Two fabrics on one bracket: a sunscreen or light-filtering blind for daytime, and a blockout behind it for night. During the day, lower the sheer layer — you get light, views, and privacy. At night, drop the blockout. Total privacy, zero silhouettes.

Dual rollers have become the default recommendation for living rooms, bedrooms, and any street-facing window in Melbourne. The slight increase in cost over a single roller pays for itself in daily quality of life.

Plantation Shutters + Blockout Curtains

For homes where aesthetics matter as much as function — particularly Victorian and Edwardian homes — shutters handle daytime privacy and light control, while floor-length blockout curtains close over them at night. This combination dominates in Melbourne's inner suburbs for good reason.

Honeycomb Blinds (Top-Down/Bottom-Up, Blockout)

Top-down for daytime — open the top for light, keep the bottom private. Fully closed at night with blockout fabric — complete privacy plus insulation. The only trade-off: you lose the outward view that sunscreen rollers offer during the day.

Our Verdict

If we had to choose one blind type for all-round privacy in a Melbourne home, it would be dual roller blinds. They're the most versatile, reasonably priced, and solve the day/night problem in a single system. For specific rooms, see our picks below.

07 — Real Situations

Common Privacy Scenarios & Solutions

🏘️

Close Neighbours Looking In

Side windows where the neighbouring house is 2–3 metres away. Standard blinds leave gaps at edges. Solution: inside-mount blockout with side channels eliminates all light leaks and sight lines.

→ Blockout roller with side channels
🚶

Street-Level Front Windows

Pedestrians pass at eye level. You want light during the day but absolute privacy at night. The classic Melbourne problem.

→ Dual roller (sunscreen + blockout)
🏢

Apartment — Overlooked by Towers

Upper-floor apartments where neighbouring buildings look down into your space. Regular blinds don't help because the viewing angle is steep. Shutters with louvres angled upward block this specifically.

→ Plantation shutters or top-down honeycomb
🚪

Bifold / Sliding Door Privacy

Large glass doors where you need coverage across 3–5 metres. Panel glide blinds or vertical blinds in blockout fabric provide the span. Large window guide →

→ Panel glide (blockout) or vertical blinds
🛁

Bathroom — Always Private

You don't want to operate a blind every time you shower. A fixed frosted film on the lower half + a sunscreen roller on top gives permanent lower privacy with controllable upper light.

→ Frosted film + sunscreen roller
💻

WFH Video Calls

You need diffused background light for camera quality but don't want the street to see your desk setup. Light-filtering honeycomb or a dual roller with the sheer layer gives you the professional background without the exposure.

→ Dual roller (sheer layer down)
08 — Watch Out

Privacy Mistakes to Avoid

1. Assuming Sunscreen = Private at Night

The single most common error. Sunscreen fabric is designed to reduce glare and UV — it's not designed to block visibility when backlit. If the room is used after dark, sunscreen-only is insufficient. Pair it with a blockout layer or upgrade to a dual roller.

2. Ignoring Light Gaps

A perfectly opaque blind with 10mm gaps at the sides still lets people see in. For serious privacy, choose inside mount with side channels (for rollers) or outside mount with 50mm overlap each side. Ask your installer about gap elimination.

3. Choosing by Looks Alone

Sheer curtains look beautiful in a showroom. But they provide almost zero nighttime privacy. If privacy is a requirement, assess the fabric's opacity first, aesthetics second. You can always find an attractive option within the privacy-rated fabrics.

4. Forgetting the Top

Standard roller blinds leave a gap at the top when fully lowered (the rolled fabric). From certain angles — especially from elevated neighbouring buildings or balconies — this gap is a sight line into the room. If you're overlooked from above, consider a fascia pelmet or face-fix bracket that closes the top gap.

5. Not Testing at Night

If you're visiting a showroom, ask to see the fabric samples backlit. Hold the fabric up to a bright light source and look through it. If you can see shapes, so will the street. A 30-second test prevents years of regret.
