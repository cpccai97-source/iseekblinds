The Real Risk: Why This Isn't Alarmism

⚠ Critical Hazard

Blind cord strangulation can happen silently and in under two minutes. Young children — particularly those aged 7 months to 4 years — become entangled in looped cords while sleeping, climbing, or playing near windows. Because they cannot free themselves, and because parents may be in another room, the outcome is often fatal before anyone is aware something is wrong.

The ACCC has recorded more than 25 corded blind-related deaths in Australia since 2001, with three or more occurring every year. This is not an edge-case hazard; it is a well-documented, recurring tragedy. The good news: it is entirely preventable.

The two key risk factors are accessible cord loops and proximity of furniture to windows. Children climb onto beds, cots, chairs, and toy boxes to reach windows — giving them access to cords that would otherwise be out of reach.

The single most important thing you can do today: Move any cot, bed, chair, or climbable piece of furniture at least 500mm away from any window with a corded blind. This alone eliminates the most common route of access.

What Australian Law Requires

Window coverings in Australia are subject to a mandatory safety standard enforced by the ACCC. All corded internal window coverings supplied from 30 December 2010 must comply with the Competition and Consumer (Corded Internal Window Coverings) Safety Standard 2014.

Mandatory requirements for compliant products

Requirement Details Who's Responsible
Warning label on product A permanent label must be affixed to the blind itself, warning of strangulation hazard Manufacturer / Supplier
Warning label on packaging Retail packaging must carry a hazard warning and safety information Manufacturer / Retailer
Safety device supplied A cord cleat or cord tension device must be supplied with every corded blind Manufacturer / Supplier
Installation instructions Written instructions for installing the safety device must be included Manufacturer / Supplier
Maximum free loop length When properly installed with safety device, no cord/chain may form a loop longer than 220mm Installer / Homeowner
Cord accessible height Operating cords must be kept at minimum 1600mm from floor when attached to safety device Installer / Homeowner
Inner cords Inner lift cords (inside the blind) must not be accessible when blind is fully raised or lowered Manufacturer / Installer
Important: The mandatory standard applies to supply — meaning blinds that pre-date 2010 are in many homes and are not legally required to be replaced. However, the ACCC strongly recommends replacing all pre-2010 corded blinds, particularly in rooms where children sleep or play.

For the full text of the standard, visit the ACCC Product Safety website or contact Consumer Affairs Victoria for local guidance.

Your Room-by-Room Home Audit

Walk through every room in your home and check each window against this checklist. Print this page for a physical walkthrough.

⚠ Prioritise first

Bedrooms and playrooms first — these are where children spend time unsupervised. Then lounge rooms and family areas. A kitchen blind can wait; a bedroom blind cannot.

  • No cord or chain accessible below 1600mm Measure from floor to lowest accessible point of any operating cord, chain loop, or inner cord.
  • Cord tension device installed (chain loop blinds) Should be anchored to wall or floor, holding the chain taut — not loose, not just clipped.
  • Cord cleat installed and in use (wand/pull-cord blinds) Cleat should be at ≥1600mm. Cord should be wound around cleat when blind is in position.
  • Inner cords not accessible when blind is fully raised Raise the blind fully — can you pull the internal lift cords? If yes, this blind is a risk.
  • No furniture within 500mm of any window Move cots, beds, chairs, toy boxes. Children use furniture to climb to window height.
  • Warning label visible on blind Compliant blinds (post-2010) must have a permanent warning label. No label = likely pre-standard.
  • Looped chains cannot slip over a child's head A loop with less than 220mm total slack is the standard; any larger loop must be restrained by the CTD.
  • Bedroom & playroom blinds replaced with cordless/motorised Best practice — not just legal minimum. Corded blinds managed well are safer; no cord is safest.

Safe Installation: Step by Step

Whether you're installing new blinds or retrofitting existing ones, these steps ensure ACCC compliance. This applies to every corded window covering in your home.

1

Choose the mounting location carefully

For rooms with children, mount blinds as high as structurally possible. The headrail height determines the natural cord position — a higher mount means the operating cord hangs at a safer height from the outset.

2

Install the cord tension device or cleat immediately

Don't wait. The safety device must be installed at the time the blind goes up — not as an afterthought. For chain loop blinds, the CTD anchors to the wall or floor and pulls the chain taut. For pull-cord blinds, a cleat at ≥1600mm allows cord winding.

3

Test the maximum loop/cord length

Operate the blind to its maximum position, then measure the lowest accessible point of any cord or loop. If it falls below 1600mm, the safety device needs adjustment. If inner cords become accessible, consult us about replacement options.

4

Anchor all safety devices to fixed structure

A CTD clipped loosely to a skirting board is not compliant. Devices must be screwed into a stud, masonry anchor, or solid substrate — a child's weight pulling on a cord must not dislodge the anchor. Use appropriate fixings for your wall type.

5

Never tie back cords to the blind itself

A common mistake: looping the excess cord around the headrail or tying it to the bracket. This is not a safety measure — the cord can come loose and form an accessible loop. Use only the supplied cleat or CTD anchored independently to the wall or floor.

6

Move furniture and re-check annually

As children grow, furniture arrangements change and children get taller. Re-audit all windows at least once a year — and any time furniture is moved near a window. A cord that was safely out of reach of a 12-month-old may be accessible to a two-year-old who can now climb.

What to Do with Older Corded Blinds

If you have blinds that pre-date 2010, or newer blinds where the safety devices weren't installed, here's your action plan:

Option 1: Retrofit with cord safety devices (short-term fix)

Cord cleats and cord tension devices are available from hardware stores for $5–$15 each. This brings older blinds closer to compliance, but note:

  • It does not fix inner cord exposure on venetian or honeycomb blinds when raised
  • It requires the devices to be properly anchored — not loosely attached
  • It's a legal minimum, not best practice for children's rooms

Option 2: Replace with cordless or motorised (permanent solution)

For any room where a child sleeps, plays, or spends unsupervised time, we strongly recommend replacing corded blinds outright. The cost of a motorised or cordless blind — typically $150–$450 per window installed — is a one-time investment in permanent safety.

Priority replacement order: (1) Child's bedroom cot/bed windows first; (2) Playroom windows; (3) Living and family room windows; (4) Any room where a young child can access unsupervised. Hallways and adult spaces last.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are blind cords dangerous for children?

Yes. Blind and curtain cords — particularly looped chains and inner lift cords — are a leading cause of strangulation in Australian children under five. The ACCC and the Window Shading Association of Australia both identify blind cords as a serious hazard. Children can become entangled in seconds, often silently, making supervision alone insufficient as a safety measure.

What are the legal requirements for blind cord safety in Australia?

All corded window coverings supplied in Australia from 30 December 2010 must comply with the mandatory safety standard: Competition and Consumer (Corded Internal Window Coverings) Safety Standard 2014. This requires warning labels on all corded products and packaging, a cord wind-up or cleat device supplied with the product, installation instructions detailing safe cord management, and cords that cannot form a loop longer than 220mm when attached to a safety device.

What are the safest blinds for children's bedrooms?

The safest options in order: (1) Motorised blinds — no cords at all, operated by remote, app, or voice; (2) Cordless spring-loaded roller blinds — push-up/pull-down with no operating cord; (3) Cordless honeycomb blinds — top-down/bottom-up without cords. If using a corded blind, it must have a properly installed tension device and all cords kept at minimum 1600mm from the floor.

Can I retrofit existing corded blinds to be child-safe?

Partially. You can install cord cleats and cord tension devices to keep cords out of reach — these are mandatory retrofits for compliant installation. However, older blinds with accessible inner lift cords (visible when the blind is raised) may not be fully fixable without replacement. For bedrooms and playrooms, we strongly recommend replacing with cordless or motorised options rather than relying on cord management alone.

How high should blind cords be from the floor?

The ACCC mandatory standard requires that cords and chains must not form a loop or free-hanging length below 1600mm from the floor when attached to their safety device (cleat or tension device). For rooms with children, we recommend keeping all cords at 1600mm+ and securing excess cord length with the supplied wind-up device or cleat.

What is a cord tension device and do I need one?

A cord tension device (CTD) is a small fitting anchored to the wall or floor that holds the bottom of a roller blind's chain loop taut, preventing it from forming a free loop. Any roller blind with a chain loop operating system MUST have a CTD installed as part of the mandatory Australian safety standard. All iSeekBlinds roller blinds include a cord tension device; professional installation ensures it's correctly anchored.
