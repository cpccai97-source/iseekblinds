Plantation shutters are the only window covering that adds measurable resale value to a Melbourne home. They're also the most permanently-fixed and most expensive — decisions made now stay with you for 20 years. That's why this guide exists.

We're not trying to sell you shutters. We're trying to give you the same briefing we give every client before a measure — the stuff that doesn't fit in a product brochure.

Materials: Timber, PVC & Aluminium

The material choice is more consequential than any other decision. Get this right first.

Painted Timber

Basswood or paulownia frames and louvres, factory-painted in your chosen colour (almost always white or off-white). The warmest, most furniture-like result. Can be repainted. Not suited to humid rooms.

Living Areas · Bedrooms · Studies

PVC / ABS Composite

Engineered polymer that looks indistinguishable from painted timber at normal viewing distance. Moisture-proof. Slightly heavier than timber — wide panels may need a central stile. Best value for wet areas.

Bathrooms · Laundries · Kitchens

Aluminium

Powder-coated aluminium frames and louvres. Used primarily outdoors or for oversized commercial windows where timber and PVC are structurally insufficient. More visible extrusion lines — clearly a different aesthetic.

Outdoor · Commercial · Large Spans

The Wet Area Decision

The single most common costly mistake in shutter installs: using timber in a bathroom. Steam from a daily shower will raise the grain, crack paint, and eventually cause delamination. Timber shutters in bathrooms are not covered under warranty by any reputable manufacturer.

PVC composite looks essentially identical to painted timber and will outlast the bathroom. For any room with a shower, bath, steam, or consistent humidity, specify PVC without exception.

Timber Species

Most Australian plantation shutters use basswood — it's lightweight, takes paint well, and has a fine, stable grain. Some higher-end ranges use paulownia (very light, excellent grain structure) or hardwood (heavier, more durable, popular for heritage homes). The species matters less than paint quality and the manufacturer's drying process — poorly dried timber will move seasonally regardless of species.

Ask your supplier: what timber species are the louvres and what is the frame? Some manufacturers use different species for frame vs louvre to reduce weight on tall windows. Mismatched movement can cause squeaking.

Louvre Width: 63mm vs 89mm vs 114mm

Louvre width changes the whole look of a shutter — this choice is irreversible.

Width Aesthetic Best For Trade-off
47mm Traditional, fine-detail — looks dated in modern homes Period homes wanting authentic colonial detail More visible louvres, less light when open
63mm Classic residential — clean and proportional Standard windows in any style home The safe default; won't look wrong anywhere
89mm Contemporary, architectural — fewer louvres per panel Larger windows, open-plan homes, new builds Heavier panel weight; deeper stack when open
114mm Very modern, floor-to-ceiling glass feel Oversized windows, designer interiors, feature walls Only suits large windows; looks overscaled on small openings

The scale rule: louvre width should be roughly 1/10th of the window height. A 1.5m high window suits 89–114mm louvres. A 900mm high window looks best with 63mm. When in doubt, 89mm is the modern default — it suits most Melbourne homes built after 2000 and makes an average-sized window look slightly larger.

Check louvres in the showroom, not on screen. The visual difference between 63mm and 89mm is subtle in photos and dramatic in person. Ask your supplier for a sample panel in both widths before committing.

Inside vs Outside Mount

Both are common in Melbourne homes — each has specific requirements.

Inside Mount (Recess Fit)

  • Shutter fits within the window reveal
  • Clean, architectural look — preferred by most designers
  • Requires minimum 60mm recess depth (with frame)
  • Recess must be square — measure diagonals (within 3mm)
  • Light bleed at frame edges is minimal but present
  • Handles and architrave don't interfere

Outside Mount (Face Fix)

  • Frame fixed to wall surface around the window
  • Can correct for out-of-square reveals
  • Requires flat mounting surface (check for protruding architrave)
  • Needs 50–75mm overlap on each side for coverage
  • Better light seal — frame blocks most edge light
  • Shutter appears part of the wall — suits heritage homes

The Recess Depth Requirement

Inside mounting a shutter requires adequate recess depth for the frame to sit without protruding past the architrave. Minimum is typically 60mm for a standard timber frame; 90mm if the window includes a window sill that creates a lip. Measure your reveal depth before requesting a quote — if you're borderline, an outside mount is cleaner than a shutter that sits proud of the wall.

Architrave: The Hidden Complication

Melbourne's older homes (Victorian, Edwardian, Federation) have deep decorative architrave mouldings that can be 30–60mm proud of the wall. This prevents a clean face-fix outside mount without a sub-frame spacer. When measuring Victorian homes, always check whether the proposed shutter frame will sit flat. Some installers use a shadow-line sub-frame to bridge the architrave gap — adds cost but gives a professional result.

Panel Configurations

Plantation shutters don't just fold open — they come in several configurations with different hardware and fold paths.

Bi-fold (Most Common)

Two to four panels hinged together, folding against the wall beside the window when fully open. Works for most standard windows. Panel width is constrained — individual panels wider than about 600mm become heavy and flex under their own weight. Wide windows typically split into multiple sets of bi-fold panels with a central stile between sections.

Single Panel (Café Style)

A single fixed panel covering the lower half of a window, leaving the upper half open. Very popular in Melbourne terrace homes and apartments where street views and privacy need to coexist — you get privacy at seated height while standing occupants can see out above. Not a light-control solution; more a screening device.

Full Height vs Split Rail

A split-rail (mid-rail) configuration divides the shutter panel horizontally — typically at 900–1100mm from the floor. This allows the bottom louvres and top louvres to be angled independently. Popular in bedrooms where you want light from above without visibility from outside. Adds cost (~$80–$150 per panel) but is a genuinely useful feature on windows facing public areas.

Bay Windows

Each facet of a bay window is fitted as a separate section. The frame is mitred to follow the bay angle. Bi-fold panels fold back against each facet face when open. Bay window shutters are a specialist installation — verify your supplier's experience before ordering and ask for photos of completed bay installations. Lead times are typically 1–2 weeks longer for bays due to complex angle measurement.

Arched & Shaped Tops

Arched windows require a fixed upper shutter section (matching the arch shape) with operable louvres in the rectangular lower portion. The arch section is typically solid painted panel with no louvres — full arched louvres are available but significantly more expensive. For historic accuracy in period homes, this is the correct approach.

Room-by-Room Suitability

Shutters work brilliantly in some rooms and are the wrong choice in others.

Living Room

Ideal. Shutters provide street privacy without blocking diffused light. 89mm louvres at 45° give excellent ambient light. Bi-fold panels fold away for full openness when wanted.

Bedroom

Good with a caveat: add a blockout roller blind behind if you need full darkness. Shutters alone don't achieve blackout. Split-rail configuration lets you angle top and bottom louvres independently.

Bathroom

Excellent — specify PVC only. Shutters provide privacy while allowing ventilation with louvres open at the top. More attractive than aluminium venetians and moisture-proof.

Kitchen

Good for dry areas over a kitchen sink. If steam is heavy (above a stove or near a dish washer), specify PVC. Shutters wipe clean easily — wand-tilt louvres have no cords to harbour grease.

Home Office

Usable, but roller blinds often serve better — roller sunscreens reduce screen glare while maintaining view. Shutters give coarser light direction control at a higher cost.

Nursery

Shutters are inherently cord-free and child-safe. However, they don't provide blockout — most parents find a blockout roller or curtain more practical for nap times. Consider shutters + blockout combination.

Honest Trade-offs

Where shutters win
  • Adds tangible resale value — noted by buyers and agents
  • Lifespan of 20–40 years vs 5–10 for fabric blinds
  • Inherently cord-free — AS/NZS compliant without extra hardware
  • No dry cleaning, no replacement fabric — wipe clean
  • Works with any interior style — classic or contemporary
  • Significant daytime privacy without sacrificing light
  • PVC options are genuinely humidity-proof
Where they fall short
  • Highest upfront cost of any window covering category
  • No blockout — need a second layer for full darkness
  • 4–8 week lead time (custom made to measure)
  • Fixed to the building — you can't take them if you move
  • Wide panels on tall windows need structural stiles (aesthetic impact)
  • Inferior insulation vs honeycomb blinds for pure thermal performance
  • Complex windows (bay, arch, heritage) add cost and planning time

The investment property question. For rental properties, shutters are generally not the right call — the premium is hard to recoup in rent differential, and tenants won't pay for shutters the way owner-occupiers do. For a home you're living in and plan to sell, the calculus is usually positive. Rule of thumb: if the home's land value justifies it, shutters are a worthwhile fixture.

What Drives Price Up

  • Window height: cost scales with panel area — tall windows cost significantly more
  • Number of panels per window: wide windows split into multiple bi-fold sections, each with its own hardware
  • Paint colour: standard white/off-white is included; custom RAL colours add $150–$400 per window
  • Complexity: arched tops, heritage sub-frames, bay mitre fitting
  • Motorisation: motorised louvre control adds $300–$600 per window

For accurate project budgeting, ask your supplier for a per-window itemised quote. A good supplier will measure every window individually — prices can vary by 30–40% based on window height even at the same width.

The three major categories serve different needs — this is where the decision often sits.

The most popular Melbourne combination: plantation shutters on street-facing windows (privacy, kerb appeal, light control) + blockout roller behind in the bedroom (full darkness when needed). This maximises the shutter's strengths while filling the one gap.

Measuring & Ordering

Plantation shutters are the most measurement-sensitive window covering — errors are expensive because the product is custom-manufactured and cannot be adjusted once made.

Always Get a Professional Measure

Unlike roller blinds, which have relatively forgiving tolerances, plantation shutters require exact measurements of frame reveal depth, squareness, and diagonal measurements. Most Melbourne shutter suppliers offer a free in-home measure — use it. A professional measurer will catch out-of-square reveals, handle or tile obstructions, and confirm mount type before production begins.

Key Things the Measurer Will Check

  • Reveal depth (minimum 60mm for inside mount)
  • Diagonal squareness — both diagonals must be within 3mm of each other
  • Window handle or winder clearance (common on casement windows)
  • Architrave profile and depth (important for outside mount)
  • Tile or skirting obstructions at sill level
  • Bay or angle measurements if applicable

For a printable measurement worksheet, see our how to measure for plantation shutters guide.

Lead Times

Standard painted timber shutters: 4–6 weeks. Custom colour: 6–8 weeks. Imported ranges: 8–12 weeks. Bay or arch windows: add 1–2 weeks. Confirm lead time at point of order, not at time of quote — timber availability and factory schedules change.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I paint plantation shutters myself to change the colour?

Yes, with caveats. Timber shutters can be repainted — use a water-based satin or semi-gloss paint and work slat-by-slat to avoid louvre-to-frame adhesion. Remove shutters from the window first if possible. Sand lightly between coats. The factory finish is very fine; DIY repainting is noticeably coarser up close. For a colour change, most suppliers also offer a professional repaint service.

Will plantation shutters help in Melbourne summers?

Shutters provide moderate heat reduction — closed louvres block direct solar gain, and the air gap between the shutter and glass provides some insulation. For Melbourne's extreme summer heat events, they won't match honeycomb blinds for thermal performance. The most effective hot-summer combination is external shutters or external blinds (to block heat before it hits the glass) plus internal plantation shutters for privacy and aesthetics.

What warranty should I expect on plantation shutters?

Quality Australian-assembled timber shutters typically carry a 5-year structural warranty and 2-year finish warranty. PVC shutters: 5–10 years. Imported shutters with shorter warranties (1–2 years) are a red flag. Ask specifically about the louvre mechanism warranty — the staple/pin tilt mechanism that moves all louvres together is the component most likely to need service.

Can I take plantation shutters when I move?

Technically yes, but practically no. Shutters are sized exactly to the windows they're installed in — they're worthless in a different home unless the windows are identical. They're normally treated as a fixture and left with the property. Budget for them as a fixed improvement, not portable furniture.

How do I clean plantation shutters?

Weekly: dust with a dry microfibre cloth, wiping each louvre from the centre toward the tip (direction of louvre's natural droop when released). Monthly: wipe with a slightly damp cloth — PVC and painted timber are both fine with mild soapy water. Don't soak timber louvres. Annual detail clean: use a louvre brush (twin sponge heads that clamp each louvre) — available from hardware stores for about $15 and dramatically faster than a cloth. Check the louvre mechanism is moving smoothly; if it stiffens, a small drop of silicone spray on the pin keeps it operating correctly.
