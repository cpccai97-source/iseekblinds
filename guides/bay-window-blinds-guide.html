Bay Window Types & What They Mean for Blinds

Not all bay windows are the same. The geometry determines what clearances you have at the corners — which in turn determines what blind types will actually work.

Angled Bay (135°) — most common in Victorian & Edwardian homes

The side panels are set at 135° to the wall. This is the easiest to cover — the generous angle gives comfortable clearance between headrails at the corners. Most blind types work well here.

Box Bay (90°) — common in 1970s–90s brick veneer homes

Side panels are perpendicular to the wall. The 90° corner is the tightest configuration. Roller blinds must be inside-mounted carefully, and venetian blinds need extra planning to ensure slat rotation clears the adjacent blind.

Square / Flush Three-Panel Bay

Three panels roughly in the same plane, with small angles between them. Usually the simplest to cover — treats almost like three separate windows in a row.

Which Blinds Work Best on Bay Windows

The short version: roller blinds are the safest choice across all bay types. But the "best" depends on your priorities.

Blind Type Bay Suitability Notes
Roller Blinds Excellent Slim headrails (42mm) fit tight corners. Most popular choice. Best light control options.
Plantation Shutters Excellent Premium look. Panels fold inward — check folded stack doesn't block sightline. Need 60mm+ recess depth.
Roman Blinds Good Soft, elegant look. Requires adequate recess depth (50mm+). Fabric stack can be bulky on large panels.
Sheer / S-Fold Curtains Good Soft and layered. Works well with separate rods per panel meeting at corners.
Venetian Blinds Caution Slats rotate outward — need extra clearance (20–25mm) at corners. Fine for angled bays; tight on box bays.
Vertical Blinds Limited Vanes stack to one side — awkward at corners. Better for box bays where each panel is independent.
Pro tip

For Melbourne's west-facing bay windows, combine a sunscreen roller blind (for daytime glare reduction) with a blockout panel — a dual roller system handles this in a single headrail without crowding the corners.

How to Measure Bay Windows — Panel by Panel

The golden rule: each panel is a separate window. Never copy measurements from one panel to the next.

1

Label your panels before measuring

Draw a quick floor plan sketch. Mark each panel: Left (L), Centre (C), Right (R). For a five-panel bay: L1, L2, C, R1, R2. This prevents mix-ups when ordering — mismatched panels are an expensive mistake.

2

Measure width at three heights

For inside mount: measure the recess width at the top, middle, and bottom of each panel. Write all three down. You'll order from the narrowest measurement — the manufacturer applies their standard 2–4mm clearance per side.

3

Measure drop at three widths

Measure the height of each recess at the left edge, centre, and right edge. Use the tallest measurement so the blind reaches the sill. For outside mount, measure to your desired drop from the intended bracket position.

4

Measure recess depth

From the front face of the architrave to the window glass. You need minimum: 45mm for rollers, 50mm for venetians, 60mm for plantation shutters. If you're short on depth, outside mount is your answer.

5

Measure corner gap

At each corner, measure the distance between the inner edges of adjacent panel recesses. This is your available clearance. See the next section on what clearances different blind types need.

Important

Always use a steel tape — fabric tapes stretch. Measure in millimetres, not centimetres. A 5mm error on a bay window often means one panel doesn't fit.

Corner Clearance: The Detail That Matters Most

This is the single thing most DIYers get wrong on bay windows. When two blinds meet at a corner, they need to operate independently without hitting each other.

Minimum clearances by blind type

  • Roller blinds (inside mount): 10mm between headrails at the corner. Most 42mm headrail rollers handle this easily on angled bays.
  • Venetian blinds: 20–25mm — slats rotate outward when tilted, so even when the headrail clears, the slats may contact the adjacent blind during operation.
  • Plantation shutters: panels fold inward. Measure the folded stack depth (usually 45–65mm for a standard 2-panel). The folded shutter must not protrude into the adjacent panel's operating space.
  • Roman blinds: fabric stack at top when raised. Allow 50–80mm stack height and check it doesn't brush the adjacent panel.
Box bay tip

On 90° box bays, standard inside-mount roller blinds can be tricky. Consider outside mount (face-fit) on the side panels — bracket on the wall, not in the recess — to give you more operating clearance at the corner.

Installation Approach by Blind Type

Roller Blinds — inside mount

The most straightforward install for bay windows. Fit each panel independently, starting from the centre and working outward. Use a spirit level to ensure the headrail is horizontal — even small angles are visible when multiple panels are side by side.

  1. Mark bracket positions in pencil — double-check clearance from adjacent brackets
  2. Drill pilot holes and secure brackets (use wall anchors if drilling into plaster or fibrous cement)
  3. Clip headrails into brackets starting with centre panel
  4. Test operation before hanging side panels — easier to adjust now
  5. Install side panels, checking corners are clear

Plantation Shutters — bay install

Shutters are usually supplied as a full frame + panels system. For bay windows, the frame wraps around each panel's recess. Installation is best left to professionals for bay windows, as the diagonal corner joins require precision mitring.

If DIY installing: allow the shutter supplier to fabricate the frame precisely to your measurements. The frame goes in first; panels clip in last.

Curtains & Tracks — per-panel approach

Use a bay window curtain rod kit (available from most hardware stores) — these are jointed systems that follow the bay angles. Alternatively, run separate tracks per panel with a small bracket gap at each corner. This is easier to install and still looks intentional when done neatly.

Safety note

On two-storey homes with bay windows near a stairwell, ensure cords or chains are kept clear of the balustrade area. Use motorised or cordless blinds — iSeekBlinds supplies child-safe cordless and motorised options for all product types.

Bay Window Curtains — Rods, Tracks & Styling

Bay windows and curtains are a classic pairing — they emphasise the architectural feature without fighting it. The main decisions are: single sweeping treatment vs per-panel treatment.

Single Bay Rod (spanning all panels)

A curved bay rod follows the angle of the bay. Fabric pools softly at the corners. Works beautifully for sheer curtains. For heavier blockout curtains, stack-back can be an issue — fabric bunches awkwardly at the angles. Best for: light filtering sheers, decorative panels, living rooms where you want drama over function.

Per-Panel Rods or Tracks

Each panel gets its own short rod, meeting at a bracket at the corner. This allows each blind or curtain to operate independently. Better for bedrooms where full blackout matters. Looks clean and deliberate when brackets are matched.

Layered Treatment (most popular for Melbourne living rooms)

Roller blind or sheer on each panel for everyday light control, plus a statement curtain rod spanning the full bay width for decoration only — curtains pulled to the sides permanently. Creates depth and the illusion of a larger bay.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the best blinds for bay windows in Melbourne?

Roller blinds are the most practical — slim headrails fit tight corners and you have full fabric choice (sunscreen, blockout, light filter). Plantation shutters are the premium choice for period homes. For a softer look, Roman blinds or per-panel sheer curtains work beautifully.

Do I need to measure each panel separately?

Yes, always. Even panels that look identical often vary by 5–15mm. Bay windows shift and settle over time, especially in older Melbourne homes. Measure each panel at three points and record them separately with a label.

Can I hang one long curtain rod across the whole bay?

You can use a bay rod kit that follows the angles of the window — this looks elegant with sheers. For heavier curtains, per-panel rods usually give a cleaner result since the fabric doesn't bunch at the corners.

How much clearance do I need at bay window corners?

Roller blinds (inside mount): minimum 10mm between headrails. Venetian blinds: 20–25mm because the slats rotate outward. Plantation shutters: measure your folded panel stack and ensure it doesn't block the adjacent panel's opening.

Inside mount or outside mount for bay windows?

Inside mount gives the cleanest look when each panel has sufficient recess depth (minimum 45mm for rollers). Outside mount is better when the recess is shallow, the angles are tight (under 120°), or you want to maximise light blockage. On box bays, outside mount on the side panels often gives better corner clearance.

How long does it take to install bay window blinds?

A standard three-panel roller blind install typically takes 45–90 minutes for an experienced installer. Plantation shutters for a bay window take 2–4 hours due to the frame mitring at corners. DIY installs take longer — allow a full morning for a first-time bay window roller blind install.
