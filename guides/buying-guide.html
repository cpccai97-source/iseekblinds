Before You Start: Three Questions That Matter

Every blinds purchase eventually comes down to three trade-offs. Get clear on these before you look at products:

The Three Trade-Offs

1. Light control vs view. Do you need total blackout (bedroom), glare reduction while keeping the view (living room), or pure privacy (bathroom)?

2. Budget vs longevity. Cheap blinds last 3–5 years. Quality custom blinds last 10–15. Plantation shutters can outlast the house. What's the real cost per year?

3. Style vs function. Some blinds look beautiful but are impractical for wet areas. Others are bulletproof but won't win design awards. Know which matters more for each window.

Most homes need a mix. Roller blinds in some rooms, curtains in others, maybe shutters for the front of house. There's no single "best" blind — only the best blind for each specific window and its job.

Every Blind Type Compared

Roller Blinds
From $80–$250 per window (custom)

The workhorse. Clean lines, minimal stack, available in sunscreen, light-filtering, or blockout fabrics. Fit virtually any window. The most popular choice in Australian homes for good reason.

Budget-friendly Low maintenance Easy to motorise Limited texture Any room
Best for: people who want something that works everywhere, looks clean, and doesn't break the budget. Especially good as a first-home or rental property solution.

Venetian Blinds
From $100–$350 per window (custom)

Tilting slats give you precise light control — angle them to bounce light toward the ceiling, block direct sun, or open fully. Available in aluminium (budget), faux wood (mid), and real timber (premium).

Precise light control Classic aesthetic Dust magnets Noisy in wind Kitchen · Study · Living
Best for: rooms where you want to control light angle throughout the day without fully raising the blind. Timber venetians add warmth to any space.

Honeycomb (Cellular) Blinds
From $150–$400 per window (custom)

The insulation champion. Air pockets in the cellular structure trap heat in winter and block it in summer. Double-cell models can cut window heat loss by up to 40%. Cordless and top-down-bottom-up options available.

Best insulation Cordless options Quiet operation Hard to repair Bedroom · Nursery · Living
Best for: energy-conscious homes, bedrooms that overheat in summer, south-facing rooms that freeze in winter. The top-down-bottom-up feature is brilliant for bathrooms — privacy below, light above.

Plantation Shutters
From $400–$1,200 per window (custom)

The premium choice. Timber, PVC, or aluminium louvres in a hinged frame. They become part of the architecture — more like cabinetry than a window covering. Virtually maintenance-free and can add to your home's resale value.

Adds property value 20+ year lifespan Low maintenance Highest upfront cost 4–8 week lead time Living · Front-of-house · Bedroom
Best for: street-facing windows, living areas, and anywhere you want a permanent, high-end look. The cost-per-year over their lifespan is often lower than replacing cheap blinds twice.

Curtains
From $200–$800+ per window (custom, incl. track)

Nothing matches the softness and warmth of fabric curtains. Eyelet, pinch pleat, S-fold, or ripple fold — each creates a different look. Often layered with a roller blind behind for complete light control.

Warmth & texture Good insulation Endless fabric choice Require stack space Need professional cleaning Living · Bedroom · Dining
Best for: creating a finished, interior-designed look. Living rooms, bedrooms, and dining areas where you want the room to feel warm and complete. Pair with sunscreen rollers for day/night versatility.

Zebra (Dual) Blinds
From $120–$300 per window (custom)

Two layers of alternating sheer and solid fabric that slide past each other. Align the solid strips for privacy, offset them for a filtered-light effect. A modern alternative to venetians with a much sleeker profile.

Modern aesthetic Flexible light control Slim profile Not full blockout Living · Study · Bedroom
Best for: modern interiors where you want light control without the horizontal-slat look of venetians. Particularly good in open-plan living areas with large windows.

Flat when lowered, stacking into soft horizontal folds when raised. Bridges the gap between the clean look of a roller blind and the warmth of curtains. Available in every fabric imaginable.

Elegant folds Fabric flexibility Structured or relaxed Stack height blocks light Higher maintenance Bedroom · Kitchen · Dining

Best Blinds by Room

Every room has different demands. Here's what actually works — not just what looks good in a showroom.

Bedroom

Full blockout is non-negotiable. Melbourne summer sunrise at 5:30am will wake you without it. Consider motorised with a timer — blinds open gradually as your alarm goes off.

Top picks: Blockout roller · Honeycomb · Curtains with blockout lining

Living Room

Balance is everything. You want natural light during the day but glare control for TV watching. Layer sunscreen rollers behind curtains for maximum flexibility.

Top picks: Sunscreen roller + curtains · Plantation shutters · Zebra blinds

Kitchen

Moisture, grease, and splashes. Fabric blinds near the stove are asking for trouble. Aluminium venetians clean easily; roller blinds in PVC-coated fabric work too.

Top picks: Aluminium venetian · PVC roller · Roman blind (away from stove)

Bathroom

Moisture resistance is critical. Timber warps, fabric moulds. PVC shutters and aluminium venetians handle humidity without issue. Top-down honeycomb blinds give privacy below, light above.

Top picks: PVC shutters · Aluminium venetian · Top-down honeycomb

Home Office

Glare on screens is the main enemy. Sunscreen rollers cut glare while maintaining your view. Position matters — a west-facing office needs more aggressive filtering than south-facing.

Top picks: Sunscreen roller · Zebra blind · Light-filtering venetian

Nursery

Full blockout for daytime naps. Cordless operation is mandatory — cord safety is an Australian standard. Motorised or cordless honeycomb blinds are the safest option.

Top picks: Cordless blockout honeycomb · Motorised roller · Plantation shutters

Custom-Made vs Off-the-Shelf

Off-the-shelf blinds from hardware stores cost less upfront. But here's what you're giving up:

  • Fit. Standard sizes rarely match real windows. Gaps leak light, look cheap, and reduce insulation. Custom blinds fit to the millimetre.
  • Fabric range. Hardware store blinds offer 10–15 options. Custom gives you hundreds — specific opacity levels, textures, and colours.
  • Warranty. Most off-the-shelf blinds have 1-year warranty. Custom blinds typically carry 5–10 year warranties on components and fabric.
  • Longevity. Better mechanisms, thicker brackets, higher-grade fabrics. Custom blinds last 2–3× longer in real-world use.
⚠️ The Hidden Cost of "Cheap"

We regularly replace off-the-shelf blinds that are only 2–3 years old. Broken chains, stripped gears, faded fabric. The customer ends up spending more than if they'd gone custom the first time. Read more in our custom vs off-the-shelf comparison.

Motorisation — Is It Worth the Extra?

Motorised blinds add $150–$400 per window on top of the blind cost. That's significant for a whole house. Here's when it genuinely makes sense:

  • Hard-to-reach windows — skylights, high clerestory windows, above bathtubs
  • Child safety — no cords whatsoever, meeting the strictest Australian standards
  • Large or heavy blinds — wide roller blinds (over 2.4m) are hard to operate by hand
  • Smart home integration — schedule blinds to open at sunrise, close at sunset, or respond to sun sensors
  • Accessibility — anyone with mobility limitations benefits enormously

When it's probably not worth it: small standard windows you walk past daily. The cord or chain takes two seconds to operate. Save the motorisation budget for windows where it solves a real problem.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the cheapest blinds in Australia?
Roller blinds are the most affordable option, starting from around $80–$150 per standard window for custom-made. Off-the-shelf rollers can be cheaper but rarely fit properly and look noticeably worse. Read our budget blinds guide for more options.
Which blinds are best for insulation?
Honeycomb (cellular) blinds are the clear winner. Their air-pocket structure traps heat in winter and blocks it in summer. Double-cell honeycomb blinds can reduce heat loss through windows by up to 40%. See our honeycomb energy savings analysis.
How long do custom blinds take to arrive?
Most custom blinds in Melbourne take 2–4 weeks from order to installation. Plantation shutters can take 4–8 weeks due to more complex manufacturing. Motorised blinds with smart home integration may take 3–5 weeks depending on the motor system.
Are motorised blinds worth the extra cost?
They add $150–$400 per window but offer real convenience: scheduled open/close times, hard-to-reach window access, child safety (no cords), and smart home integration. They're particularly worth it for skylights, large windows, and homes with young children. Our motorised blinds cost guide breaks down the numbers.
Should I choose inside mount or outside mount?
Inside mount looks cleaner and uses less material, but requires at least 50mm recess depth (varies by product). Outside mount provides better light block and covers the full window frame. Not sure? Our free measure service checks this for every window.
