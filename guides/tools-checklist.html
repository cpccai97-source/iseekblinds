Before you order your blinds, assemble your toolkit. There's nothing more frustrating than unpacking a beautiful set of roller blinds only to realise you don't have the right drill bit for your brick windowsill. This checklist is organised by task — measuring first, installing second — with notes on what changes depending on your wall surface and blind type.

ℹ️ Quick Version

For a standard inside-mount roller blind into a timber window frame, you need: steel tape measure, pencil, cordless drill with 3mm bit, Phillips screwdriver, spirit level, and a step ladder. Everything else below is for specific situations.

Measuring Tools

Get this right before anything else. Wrong measurements → blinds that don't fit → a frustrating (and expensive) reorder. See our measuring guides for step-by-step process, but these are the tools you need.

📏
Steel Tape Measure
3m minimum; 5m for large windows or rooms. Locking blade essential. Never use a fabric sewing tape — they stretch.
Essential
✏️
Pencil
For marking measurements on your notepad. A pen works, but graphite is easier to erase from painted surfaces during installation.

Essential

Step Ladder
Needed to reach the top of the window recess. Standard A-frame; position so you can reach the top corner without stretching dangerously.
Essential

Depth Gauge / Ruler
For measuring recess depth (front of frame to glass). A standard ruler works; a dedicated depth gauge is faster across many windows.

Installation Tools

🔌
Cordless Drill
12V minimum for timber frames; 18V+ if you're drilling into brick. Variable speed is essential — don't go full speed into timber or you'll split it.
Essential
🪛
Phillips Screwdriver
PH2 size for most bracket screws. Useful even if you have a drill — for final tightening in tight spots where the drill won't fit.
Essential
⚖️
Spirit Level
400mm minimum. Critical for making sure brackets sit level — even 1–2° off and the blind won't roll evenly. Bubble level preferred over phone apps.
Essential
✏️
Pencil (for marking)
Mark bracket positions on walls or frames. Soft pencil marks are easy to remove; avoid permanent marker on window frames.
Essential
🔦
Stud Finder
Only needed for outside-mount installations into plasterboard walls. Magnetic type finds screws in studs; electronic finds edges of studs.
Useful
📌
Wall Plugs / Anchors
Required for brick, concrete, or plasterboard without stud. Ribbed plastic plugs for brick; cavity anchors (Drywall plugs) for plasterboard.
Varies
🧲
Drill Bit Set
3mm HSS for timber/timber frames; 5–6mm masonry for brick/concrete. See surface guide below for specifics.
Essential
✂️
Tape / Masking Tape
Wrap around the drill bit to mark drilling depth — prevents over-drilling into cavity walls or tiled surfaces. Cheap and effective.
Optional

Bracket Mounting — By Surface Type

The right fixings depend entirely on what you're drilling into. Timber is easiest; no-stud plasterboard is the trickiest. When in doubt, use bigger anchors than you think you need.

Wall Surface Guide

The included screws are sized for timber window frames — the most common situation. Here's what to use for everything else:

Surface Drill Bit Fixing Notes Difficulty
Timber window frame 3mm HSS Included screws Standard setup. Pilot hole prevents splitting. Never overtighten. Easy
Aluminium frame 3–4mm HSS Self-tapping screws Aluminium is soft — use gentle, constant pressure. Self-tapping screws grip better than standard wood screws. Easy
Brick (with mortar) 6mm masonry Ribbed plastic wall plugs + 5mm screws Drill into mortar, not the brick face — mortar is softer and you won't crack the brick. Hammer drill mode helps. Medium
Concrete 6mm masonry SDS Nylon wall plugs + 5mm screws Needs a hammer drill. Standard drills won't penetrate concrete effectively. SDS-type bits preferred. Harder
Plasterboard (into stud) 3mm HSS Included screws Find the stud first with a stud finder. A 75mm zone either side of power points is a no-drill zone. Easy (with stud finder)
Plasterboard (no stud) 10mm HSS or hole saw Cavity anchors (toggle bolts or Drywall anchors) Weight limit per anchor is ~15kg. A standard roller blind headrail is 1–3kg — well within limits. Medium
Tiles 6mm carbide-tipped Tile plugs + screws Mark with masking tape first (prevents drill skipping). Use zero rotation (drill mode only, no hammer). Very slow speed. Hardest

🪟 Curtains & Tracks

Core toolkit (above)
Level — extra important for tracks spanning wide windows
Stud finder (tracks often mount into wall, not frame)
Hook pliers (for S-hooks on eyelet curtains)
Hemming tape (if shortening curtain length without sewing)

⚡ Motorised Blinds

Core toolkit + level
Electrician (hardwired motors — licensed work in Australia)
USB-C cable (for charging battery motors — included)
Smartphone with home hub app (Somfy, Rollease, etc.)
⚠️ Melbourne Building Note

Hardwired motorised blind installations must be done by a licensed electrician under AS/NZS 3000 (Wiring Rules). Battery and rechargeable motors are fully DIY-safe. If you're not sure which motor type you have, check the product spec sheet or ask us before booking an electrician.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a stud finder to install blinds?
Only if you're outside-mounting into a plasterboard wall. Most window frames — timber or aluminium — are solid enough that a stud finder isn't needed. For ceiling mounts or wide outside-mount blinds into bare wall, find the stud or use cavity anchors.
What size drill bit should I use for blind brackets?
Timber window frames: 3mm HSS pilot hole. Plasterboard into stud: 3mm HSS. Brick or concrete: 5–6mm masonry bit. Always drill a pilot hole smaller than your screw shaft — bracket screws are typically 3.5–4mm diameter.
Can I install blinds without a drill?
Into soft timber frames with pre-drilled pilot holes, it's possible to hand-drive screws with a Phillips screwdriver — but it's slow and risks splitting the wood. A cordless drill makes the job much faster and cleaner. Avoid hammer drills on tile — standard drill mode only at very slow speed.
What comes included with the blinds?
Mounting brackets, screws (sized for timber frames), child-safety tension device (AS 2999 compliant), and headrail end caps. For motorised blinds: remote and receiver. You provide the drill, appropriate bits for your surface, spirit level, pencil, and ladder.
My walls are brick. What do I need?
A hammer drill (or a drill with hammer mode), a 5–6mm masonry bit, and ribbed plastic wall plugs (available at Bunnings for under $5 per pack). Drill into the mortar joint between bricks where possible — mortar is softer and won't crack or split like the brick face might. Check the installation guide for your blind type for exact steps.
