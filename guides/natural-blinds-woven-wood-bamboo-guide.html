Natural Blinds: Woven Wood, Bamboo & Jute for Australian Homes

Everything you need to choose the right natural material blind — how each material behaves, what light control to expect, which liners you need, and what you'll actually pay in Melbourne.

Updated March 2026 12 min read Covers: bamboo · woven wood · jute · seagrass · rattan

Natural material blinds are one of the few window treatments that look equally at home in a coastal Mornington Peninsula retreat, an inner-city Melbourne terrace, or a heritage bungalow in Hawthorn. The trick is knowing which material to pick — and what to pair it with.

What Are Natural Blinds?

Natural blinds are made from plant-derived fibres rather than synthetics. The most common materials used in Australian homes are:

Bamboo

Fast-growing, durable grass (technically not wood). Uniform texture, smooth finish, excellent light-filtering. Most popular natural blind material in Australia.

Woven Wood / Reed

Mixed-fibre weaves of bamboo, wild reeds, grasses and timber. Organic texture variation, warm depth. Each panel is slightly unique.

Jute & Hessian

Loose, open-weave texture with golden-brown tones. Strong Boho / coastal aesthetic. Softer light diffusion. Best for decorative use.

Seagrass & Rattan

Coarser weave, excellent durability. Natural greeny-beige palette. Suits coastal, tropical and relaxed rural-inspired interiors.

Light Control: What to Expect

This is where most people get surprised. Natural blinds are not blockout by default. The weave gaps — even in tightly woven bamboo — allow diffused light through.

Bedroom tip: If you need genuine blockout — you're a light sleeper, shift worker, or have young children — order your bamboo or woven wood blind with a factory-fitted blackout liner. Retrofit liners exist but don't sit as neatly behind the natural face fabric.

Liner Options

Liners transform natural blinds from decorative to functional. The main options:

  • No liner: Maximum natural texture visible, softly diffused light, some privacy loss at night. Good for sunrooms, studies, rooms you don't sleep in.
  • Light-filtering liner: Reduces glare, adds privacy, retains warmth of natural material look. Most popular choice.
  • Blackout liner: Full light block. Fabric back is typically white or cream — only the natural face is visible from inside. Best for bedrooms.
  • Thermal/insulating liner: Adds energy efficiency. Useful for large sun-facing windows in Melbourne homes — helps in both summer heat and winter cold.

Which Rooms Work Best?

Living Room

Natural blinds thrive here. A woven wood Roman blind in a living room creates warmth without feeling heavy. Pair with sheer curtains for a layered window treatment look — natural blind for daytime texture and privacy, sheers for softer evenings.

Bedroom

Works well with a blackout liner. Bamboo roller blinds are practical in bedrooms — they roll fully clear of the window frame when open, maximising morning light. Woven wood Romans add softness to a bedroom but sit lower when raised (the fold stacks reduce clearance).

Dining Room

One of the best applications. A woven wood Roman in a dining room looks intentional and textural — more interesting than fabric, less fussy than curtains. Works with timber furniture, rattan chairs, pendant lighting.

Study / Home Office

Bamboo venetians are excellent for studies — the adjustable slat angle gives you fine control over glare on screens while maintaining the natural material aesthetic. See our home office blinds guide for full recommendations.

Bathroom

Possible — but use treated or lacquered bamboo only. Untreated bamboo will mould and warp in a steam-heavy bathroom. Keep windows open after showers. Better alternatives for wet bathrooms are aluminium venetians or treated PVC rollers.

Outdoor / Patio

Bamboo treated with outdoor lacquer or UV-resistant sealant works well on covered patios and alfresco areas. Not suitable for full weather exposure. For full outdoor use, see our outdoor blinds guide.

How to Choose: A 5-Step Process

Decide on light control first

Do you need blockout? If yes, bamboo roller with blackout liner. If filtered light is fine, all materials work. This is the most important decision.

Pick your operating style

Roller for practicality. Roman for luxury look. Roll-up for casual/outdoor. Venetian slat for adjustable light control.

Choose your material for texture

Bamboo: clean and uniform. Woven wood/reed: organic and variable. Jute: soft and rustic. Seagrass: coastal and durable.

Consider the room's humidity

High humidity rooms (bathrooms, laundries) need treated or lacquered natural blinds — or a different material entirely.

Get samples first

Natural blinds vary significantly between suppliers. The colour, weave density, and finish look different in person than online. Always request samples before ordering.

Care & Maintenance

Natural blinds are easier to care for than most people expect:

  • Dust: Weekly dusting with a dry microfibre cloth or vacuum brush attachment. Always wipe with the weave direction, not against it.
  • Spot clean: Damp cloth with mild soap for spots. Dry immediately — don't soak natural fibres.
  • Sun fade: Natural fibres will lighten over years with UV exposure. Use UV-protective liners or apply a UV-resistant fabric spray to slow fading.
  • Insects: In Queensland and tropical areas, moths can be attracted to natural fibres. In Melbourne this is rarely an issue, but cedar blocks near stored blinds help if concerned.

Can Natural Blinds Be Motorised?

Yes — bamboo rollers use standard tubular motor systems, same as fabric rollers. Motorisation works well for bamboo because the material is relatively lightweight. Heavy woven reed or rattan may require a higher-torque motor.

Motorised natural blinds work well with smart home systems — they're a popular choice for automated sun control in north-facing Melbourne living rooms, where you want the blind down during peak afternoon heat without thinking about it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do bamboo blinds block light completely?

Not on their own. Even tight-weave bamboo allows diffused light through the gaps. For bedroom blockout, add a blackout liner at the time of order — retrofit liners are less tidy.

Are natural blinds suitable for Melbourne's humidity and heat?

Yes for most rooms. Melbourne's climate is far less humid than Brisbane or Cairns, so untreated bamboo holds up well in living areas and bedrooms. Kitchens and bathrooms need treated or lacquered finishes. Direct rain exposure requires outdoor-rated bamboo with UV sealant.

What's the difference between bamboo blinds and woven wood blinds?

Bamboo blinds are made from bamboo reeds or slats. Woven wood is a broader category that includes bamboo plus jute, seagrass, reeds, grasses and other plant fibres woven together. Woven wood has more texture variation; bamboo is more uniform.

How much do natural blinds cost in Melbourne?

Bamboo rollers: $160–$350/window. Woven wood Romans: $280–$600/window. Add $50–$120 for blockout liners. Contact us for a free in-home quote.

Can natural blinds be motorised?

Yes. Bamboo roller blinds motorise using standard tubular motors. Romans can also be motorised. Best suited to lighter-weight weaves rather than heavy rattan or chunky reed.
