Quick Summary

Honeycomb blinds are one of the most forgiving blind types to measure — the cellular fabric compresses slightly at the edges, absorbing small variations. Choose your cell type first (single or double), then check recess depth. Measure width at three points (use narrowest), height at three points (use longest). Always use millimetres. Don't deduct clearance — we handle that. For top-down bottom-up, you need extra recess depth (65mm minimum).

Why Honeycomb Blinds Are Easy to Measure

Good news: honeycomb blinds are among the most forgiving window coverings to measure for. Unlike venetians with their rigid metal headrail or plantation shutters with zero tolerance for error, honeycomb fabric compresses slightly at the edges. A measurement that's 2mm off won't leave you with visible gaps or a blind that won't fit.

That doesn't mean you can be sloppy. The headrail is still rigid, and the cellular structure needs room to fold and stack properly — especially if you're going with double cell or top-down bottom-up. The process is the same three-point method we use for rollers, but with different recess depth requirements depending on your chosen cell type.

The unique advantage of honeycombs: minimal light gaps. The cellular structure wraps around the edges of the headrail, blocking light far better than flat fabric or slats. This makes inside mount particularly effective — you get near-blockout performance without needing outside mount overlap.

Choose Your Cell Type First

Cell type determines the look, insulation performance, and critically, how much recess depth you need. The cellular structure is what makes honeycombs special — air trapped inside the cells acts as thermal insulation, reducing heat transfer through your windows.

Slim & Versatile

  • Min recess depth: 35mm
  • Best for small–medium windows
  • Clean, minimal stack when raised
  • Good thermal insulation
  • Lightest weight — easy to operate
  • Most affordable honeycomb option

Maximum Insulation

  • Min recess depth: 50mm
  • Best for large windows & energy efficiency
  • Two layers of cells trap more air
  • Superior thermal & acoustic performance
  • Slightly thicker stack when raised
  • Ideal for south-facing & exposed windows

Ultimate Flexibility

  • Best for bedrooms & street-facing windows

Go double cell for south-facing windows, poorly insulated rooms, or if energy efficiency is a priority. Top-down bottom-up is worth the extra cost for bedrooms and street-facing living rooms. Inside Mount vs Outside Mount

Honeycomb blinds look excellent either way, but inside mount really shines here — the cellular fabric wraps around the headrail edges, naturally blocking light without the gaps you'd get with slats or flat fabrics.

Inside Mount (Recess Fit)

  • Headrail sits inside window recess
  • Honeycomb cells block light at edges — minimal gaps
  • Clean, built-in look
  • Requires adequate recess depth (varies by cell type)
  • Fabric compresses slightly — very forgiving fit
  • Best for: all rooms, especially where you want a clean look

Outside Mount (Face Fit)

  • Headrail mounts above the window opening
  • Complete light blockout with side overlap
  • Works on any window depth
  • Hides uneven or damaged frames
  • No minimum recess depth required
  • Best for: shallow recesses, bedrooms needing total blockout

Check Your Recess Depth

This is the critical honeycomb-specific step. Different cell types and configurations need different depths — and it's the main reason people end up switching from inside to outside mount.

Top-view cross-section: check recess depth matches your cell type before measuring width and height.
⚠️ Motorised honeycomb blinds need an extra 10–15mm of recess depth beyond the standard minimums above. The motor housing inside the headrail is thicker. If your recess is borderline, outside mount avoids the issue entirely.

Inside Mount — Step by Step

Inside mount: measure recess width at three points (use narrowest) and height at three points (use longest).
1

Check Recess Depth

Using your steel tape, measure from the front edge of the recess straight back to the glass (or window frame). Match against the minimums for your cell type:

  • Single cell (25mm): at least 35mm
  • Double cell (38mm): at least 50mm
  • Top-down bottom-up: at least 65mm
  • Motorised (any cell): add 10–15mm to the above

If your recess is too shallow, switch to outside mount — no depth limitation.

2

Measure Width at Three Points

Measure the internal width of the recess at:

  • Top — across the top of the opening, right below the lintel
  • Middle — halfway down
  • Bottom — across the sill or bottom of the opening

Record all three. Use the narrowest measurement. The headrail must fit through the narrowest point.

💡 Honeycomb advantage: Because the cellular fabric compresses slightly at the edges, small width variations (2–3mm) are absorbed naturally. You'll still get a clean look even if your recess isn't perfectly square — the cells flex to fill minor gaps.
3

Measure Height at Three Points

Measure the internal height from the top of the recess to the sill:

  • Left side
  • Centre
  • Right side

Record all three. Use the longest measurement. The fabric needs to cover the full drop — honeycomb pleats sit flush against the bottom rail, so any shortfall is visible.

⚠️ Don't deduct clearance. Give us the exact recess width. We deduct the 3–4mm headrail clearance during manufacturing. Honeycomb headrails are slimmer than venetian or roller headrails, so the clearance is smaller — but if you also deduct, you'll end up with a blind that's too narrow.
4

Check for Square

If your three width measurements differ by more than 5mm, the recess isn't square. Here's the good news: honeycomb blinds handle this better than most. The fabric compresses at the wider points and expands at the narrower ones.

For recesses more than 5mm out of square, outside mount is still recommended. Or talk to us — we've fitted honeycomb blinds in plenty of older Melbourne homes with character windows.

Outside Mount — Step by Step

Outside mount: add 40–60mm overlap each side, 50mm above and below the frame for honeycomb blinds.
1

Measure Frame Width

Measure the outside width of your window frame — outer edge to outer edge of the architrave. No frame? Measure the opening itself.

2

Add Side Overlap

Add 40–60mm on each side. Honeycomb blinds need less overlap than venetians or rollers because the cellular structure blocks light at the edges naturally. Even a 40mm overlap gives you excellent light control.

Your order width = frame width + 80 to 120mm total overlap

3

Decide Drop Height

Measure from where you want the headrail bracket — typically 50mm above the frame — down to your desired finish:

  • To sill: bottom of window frame
  • Below sill: add 50mm for better light block
  • To floor: measure to 15mm above floor (honeycomb bottom rails are lighter than venetians — they need less clearance)
4

Check Mounting Surface

Honeycomb blinds are lighter than timber venetians, so mounting requirements are less demanding:

  • Timber frame or wall studs: ideal, standard screws
  • Plasterboard: standard wall anchors are fine for most honeycomb sizes
  • Brick or concrete: masonry plugs and screws
💡 Pro Tip: Honeycomb blinds with blockout fabric in outside mount are Melbourne's best bedroom window solution. The cells trap air (insulation), the fabric blocks light, and the overlap eliminates side gaps. Add a pelmet across the top and you've got a genuinely dark room — even in summer when dawn hits at 5:30am.

Special Configurations

Honeycomb blinds offer configurations that other blind types simply can't match. Each one has measurement implications you need to know about.

Top-Down Bottom-Up (TDBU)

  • Both headrail and bottom rail move independently
  • Open from the top for light while keeping bottom closed for privacy
  • Needs 65mm minimum recess depth (inside mount)
  • Width and height measurements are the same as standard
  • Available in single and double cell
  • Ideal for bedrooms and street-facing windows

Day/Night (Duo Shade)

  • Two fabrics in one blind — sheer on top, blockout below
  • Move the middle rail to adjust the ratio
  • Needs 65mm minimum recess depth (inside mount)
  • Thicker stack when fully raised — check headroom
  • Width and height measured identically to standard
  • Best for living rooms needing daytime light + evening privacy
📝 Motorised honeycomb blinds: Measurements are the same as manual, but the headrail is 10–15mm deeper to house the motor. Check that your recess depth accommodates this. Battery-powered motors are rechargeable (no electrician needed). Hardwired motors require a power point within 1.5m or in-wall cabling — plan this before measuring. See our motorised options.

6 Common Honeycomb Measuring Mistakes

1

Ignoring recess depth for TDBU

Standard single cell only needs 35mm — most recesses handle that easily. But people order top-down bottom-up without checking, and discover their 45mm recess can't fit the dual-rail mechanism. Always check depth against your specific configuration.

2

Deducting headrail clearance

Same trap as every blind type. You measure 1,100mm, deduct 5mm "to be safe," and end up with a blind that's too narrow. Honeycomb fabric compresses, so small gaps aren't as visible as with venetians — but why risk it? Give us the exact recess measurement.

3

Forgetting stack height

Honeycomb blinds fold into a compact stack at the top when raised — but that stack still takes up space. Double cell and day/night blinds have a thicker stack. On short windows, this can block 40–60mm of your view. Ask us about stack height for your specific window size.

4

Measuring only once

Three width points, three height points — not negotiable. The headrail is rigid metal even though the fabric is forgiving. If the width varies by 6mm between top and bottom, the headrail that fits at the bottom will jam at the top.

5

Not accounting for motorisation depth

You check recess depth for a single cell (35mm minimum), confirm your 40mm recess works, then add motorisation. The motor housing adds 10–15mm. Now your 40mm recess is too shallow. Decide on motorisation before measuring depth.

6

Using centimetres instead of millimetres

Millimetres only. "About 120cm" could mean anywhere from 1,195mm to 1,205mm. That 10mm range matters when the headrail needs to slide into the recess. Every measurement in mm, every time.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the minimum recess depth for honeycomb blinds?

It depends on cell type and configuration. Single cell: 35mm minimum. Double cell: 50mm. Top-down bottom-up: 65mm. Motorised versions need an extra 10–15mm on top of these. If your recess is too shallow, outside mount works with any depth.

Single cell vs double cell — does it affect measurements?

Width and height measurements are identical. The only difference is recess depth: single cell needs 35mm, double cell needs 50mm. Double cell also has a slightly thicker stack when raised, which can matter on shorter windows where the stack blocks some of your view.

Can I use inside mount for top-down bottom-up honeycomb blinds?

Yes, but you need at least 65mm recess depth. The dual-rail mechanism (both headrail and bottom rail move independently) is bulkier than a standard single-rail setup. Most modern Australian homes have adequate depth, but older homes can be tighter. Always measure depth first.

Do I deduct clearance from my honeycomb blind measurement?

No. Give us the exact recess width in millimetres. We deduct the 3–4mm headrail clearance during manufacturing. Honeycomb fabric compresses slightly, making them more forgiving than rigid-headrail blinds — but you should still provide exact measurements, not rounded-down ones.

Are honeycomb blinds good for irregular or out-of-square windows?

They're one of the best blind types for slightly out-of-square windows. The cellular fabric compresses and flexes at the edges, absorbing variations of 2–3mm without visible gaps. For windows more than 5mm out of square, outside mount is the safer option. For severely irregular windows (arched, angled), contact us for custom solutions.

Can I measure for motorised honeycomb blinds the same way?

Width and height measurements are identical. The difference is recess depth — motorised headrails are 10–15mm deeper to house the motor. Also plan your power source: battery motors are rechargeable (USB-C) with no electrician needed. Hardwired motors need a power point within 1.5m. Decide before measuring so you can check the correct depth requirement.
