Quick Summary

Choose your slat size first — it determines the minimum recess depth. Measure your window recess width at three points (top, middle, bottom) and take the narrowest. Measure height at three points and take the longest. Always use millimetres. Don't deduct anything — we handle headrail clearances. If your recess is too shallow for your chosen slat size, go outside mount.

Why Venetian Blinds Need Precise Measurement

Venetian blinds have a rigid aluminium headrail that must slide into your window recess with just millimetres of clearance on each side. Too wide and the headrail won't go in. Too narrow and the slats can't cover the opening — you get light bleed down both sides and a blind that looks like it belongs to a different window.

The other factor that makes venetians different from rollers or curtains: slat size matters. A 63mm timber slat needs significantly more recess depth than a 25mm aluminium one. Choose your slat before you measure, or you might discover your recess can't accommodate the look you want.

The process itself is straightforward. Steel tape, 15 minutes, this guide. We'll walk you through slat selection, both mount types, and the specific clearances for each venetian style.

💡 Pro Tip: Measure every window individually, even if they look identical. Australian homes — especially older Melbourne weatherboard and period homes — routinely have "matching" windows that differ by 5–10mm.

Choose Your Slat Size First

Slat width is the single most important decision before measuring. It determines the look, the light control, and critically, how much recess depth you need for inside mount. Here's the breakdown:

25
mm — Aluminium

Slim & Modern

  • Min recess depth: 50mm
  • Best for small–medium windows
  • Modern, minimal aesthetic
  • Lightweight — easy to operate
  • Good privacy, moderate light block
  • Most affordable option
50
mm — Timber / Faux Wood

Classic Warmth

  • Min recess depth: 65mm
  • Best for standard–large windows
  • Warm, natural timber look
  • Better outward view when open
  • Suits both modern & heritage homes
  • Faux wood ideal for wet areas
63
mm — Faux Wood

Bold Statement

  • Min recess depth: 75mm
  • Best for large windows & doors
  • Maximum view when open
  • Plantation shutter alternative
  • Excellent light control
  • Fewer slats = easier to clean
📝 Not sure which slat size? As a rule of thumb, match slat width to window size. Small bathroom or kitchen windows → 25mm. Standard bedroom and living room windows → 50mm. Feature windows, sliding doors, or when you want a shutter-like look → 63mm. Browse our full venetian range to see them in situ.

Inside Mount vs Outside Mount

Decide your mount type before measuring. This determines what you measure and where. Venetians look particularly sharp inside-mounted, but outside mount has real advantages for light control.

Inside Mount (Recess Fit)

  • Headrail sits inside window recess
  • Clean, built-in look
  • Doesn't cover architrave or frame
  • Requires adequate recess depth (see slat table above)
  • Recess must be reasonably square
  • Best for: living rooms, studies, modern fitouts

Outside Mount (Face Fit)

  • Headrail mounts above the window opening
  • Better light blockout at sides
  • Works on any window shape or depth
  • No minimum recess depth required
  • Hides uneven or damaged frames
  • Best for: bedrooms, west-facing windows, shallow recesses
Inside mount: measure recess width (narrowest of 3), height (longest of 3), and check recess depth against your slat size.

Inside Mount — Step by Step

1

Check Recess Depth

Using your steel tape, measure from the front edge of the recess straight back to the glass (or the window frame behind it). The minimum depth depends on your slat size:

  • 25mm aluminium slats: at least 50mm
  • 50mm timber/faux wood: at least 65mm
  • 63mm faux wood: at least 75mm

If your recess is too shallow, you have two options: choose a narrower slat, or switch to outside mount.

2

Measure Width at Three Points

Measure the internal width of the recess at:

  • Top — across the top of the opening, right below the lintel
  • Middle — halfway down
  • Bottom — across the sill or bottom of the opening

Record all three. Use the narrowest measurement. The headrail must fit through the narrowest point, or it won't go in.

⚠️ Don't deduct clearance. Provide the exact recess width. We deduct the 3–5mm headrail clearance during manufacturing. If you also deduct, you'll get a blind that's 6–10mm too narrow — visible light gaps on both sides.
3

Measure Height at Three Points

Measure the internal height from the top of the recess to the sill:

  • Left side
  • Centre
  • Right side

Record all three. Use the longest measurement. The bottom rail and slats need to cover the full drop to prevent light coming through underneath.

4

Check for Square

If your three width measurements differ by more than 5mm, the recess isn't square enough for inside mount. The headrail might fit at one point but jam at another.

Outside mount is the safer bet for out-of-square windows. Or talk to us — we can sometimes work with slightly out-of-square recesses on venetians by adjusting the bracket position.

💡 Pro Tip: For inside mount venetians, also check that the recess walls are flat — not bowed or textured. The mounting brackets press flush against the inside top of the recess. A bump or uneven plaster can stop the bracket sitting flat, leaving the whole blind tilted.

Outside Mount — Step by Step

Outside mount: add 50–75mm overlap each side, 75mm above, and 50mm below the frame for venetian blinds.
1

Measure Frame Width

Measure the outside width of your window frame — from the outer edge of the left architrave to the outer edge of the right. If there's no frame, measure the opening itself.

2

Add Side Overlap

Add 50–75mm on each side. This overlap stops light leaking around the slat edges — important because venetian slats tilt, so any side gap becomes very visible when the slats are angled.

Your order width = frame width + 100 to 150mm total overlap

3

Decide Drop Height

Measure from where you want the headrail bracket — typically 75mm above the frame — down to your desired finish:

  • To sill: bottom of window frame
  • Below sill: add 50mm for better light block
  • To floor: measure to 20mm above floor (the bottom rail is heavier on venetians than rollers — give it clearance)
4

Check Mounting Surface

Venetian blinds — especially timber 50mm and 63mm — are heavier than rollers. The mounting bracket needs to go into something solid:

  • Timber frame or wall studs: ideal, no special fixings needed
  • Plasterboard only: use heavy-duty wall anchors rated for the blind weight
  • Brick or concrete: use masonry plugs and screws
💡 Pro Tip: For bedrooms, outside mount with 75mm overlap on each side plus a pelmet or valance strip across the top gives venetians a near-blackout performance. The valance hides the headrail and closes the light gap that the tilting mechanism creates at the top.

Control Options

Unlike roller blinds with a single chain, venetians have two controls — one to raise/lower and one to tilt the slats. Decide these before ordering, as they affect the measurement and manufacture:

Cord Lift + Tilt Wand

  • Pull cord raises and lowers the blind
  • Separate wand tilts the slats open/closed
  • Most common configuration
  • Wand is child-safe (no loops)
  • Cord can have a tensioner for child safety

Cordless / Motorised

  • Lift by hand — push bottom rail up or down
  • Or motorised with remote control
  • Safest option for homes with children
  • Motorised ideal for hard-to-reach windows
  • Motorised needs power — plan cable routing

When recording your measurements, note which side you want the cord/wand. Consider:

  • Which side is easiest to reach from where you usually stand
  • Obstructions — furniture, door handles, other blinds
  • Consistency across the room: all controls on the same side looks tidier
🔒 Child Safety: Australian standards require cord safety devices on all corded window coverings. We fit cord tensioners or breakaway connectors as standard. For homes with young children, consider cordless or motorised options — no cords, no risk. See our child safety guide for details.

Checking for Obstructions

Venetians have a thicker headrail and protruding tilt mechanism compared to rollers. Obstructions that don't affect roller blinds can be a problem for venetians. Check each window for:

🔩

Window handles & cranks

Awning and casement windows have protruding winder handles. Measure how far they stick out. If they protrude beyond the front of the headrail, the slats will hit them when tilted. Outside mount with adequate clearance is usually the answer.

🧱

Tiles & splashback

Kitchen and bathroom windows often have tiles running into the recess. Measure from the tile surface — not the hidden frame behind. Tiles reduce your effective recess depth. Faux wood slats are the better choice for wet areas.

❄️

Air conditioning & vents

Ceiling vents and split-system units near windows can interfere with outside-mount headrails. Leave at least 60mm clearance from vents — venetian headrails are bulkier than roller brackets.

📦

Pelmet boxes & cornices

Existing pelmet boxes or decorative cornices can limit how high you mount an outside-fit venetian. Measure the clearance between the top of the frame and the pelmet/cornice.

6 Common Venetian Measuring Mistakes

1

Ignoring recess depth

The #1 venetian-specific mistake. You measure width and height perfectly, order a beautiful 50mm timber venetian, then discover your recess is only 55mm deep. The headrail fits, but the slats can't tilt without hitting the glass. Always measure depth first.

2

Deducting headrail clearance

Same trap as roller blinds. You measure 1,100mm, deduct 5mm "to be safe," and end up with visible gaps. Give us the exact recess measurement. We handle the clearance.

3

Forgetting window handles

Venetian slats tilt to different angles. A handle that clears the headrail can still foul the slats when they're angled. Test by holding a ruler at the planned slat angle and checking it clears the handle.

4

Measuring only once

Three width points, three height points — not negotiable. Venetian headrails are rigid metal. If the width varies by even 4mm between top and bottom, the headrail that fits at the bottom might jam at the top.

5

Using centimetres

Millimetres only. "About 120cm" could mean anywhere from 1,195mm to 1,205mm. That 10mm range is the difference between a perfect fit and a blind that won't mount.

6

Assuming all windows match

Even windows installed by the same builder at the same time can vary. Measure each one. Label them on a sketch — "Living room, left window," "Bedroom 2, north." This also helps us if you call with questions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum recess depth for venetian blinds?

It depends on slat width. 25mm aluminium: 50mm minimum. 50mm timber/faux wood: 65mm. 63mm faux wood: 75mm. If your recess is shallower, go with outside mount or choose a narrower slat.

Should I measure in millimetres or centimetres?

Always millimetres. Venetian headrails are rigid and must slide precisely into the recess. Even 3mm off can mean the headrail won't fit or rattles loosely.

What slat size should I choose?

25mm aluminium for small-to-medium windows and modern, minimal spaces. 50mm timber/faux wood for standard-to-large windows — the most versatile choice. 63mm faux wood for large feature windows or when you want a plantation shutter look without the cost. Wider slats give better outward views when open.

Do I deduct clearance from my measurement?

No. Give us your exact recess width. We deduct the required headrail clearance (typically 3–5mm total) during manufacturing. Double-deducting is the most common mistake we see.

Can I install venetian blinds on a tile splashback window?

Yes. Measure from the tile surface inward, not the hidden frame. Tiles reduce your effective recess depth, so check whether the remaining depth meets the minimum for your slat size. Faux wood slats are best for wet areas — real timber can warp with moisture.

Cord or wand — which control type is better?

Cord lift with tilt wand is the standard setup — cord raises/lowers, wand tilts slats. For homes with young children, cordless or motorised is strongly recommended. Cordless is lift-by-hand (push the bottom rail). Motorised adds remote control and smart home integration. Both eliminate cord hazards entirely.
