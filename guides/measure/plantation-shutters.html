Quick Summary

Plantation shutters are custom-made — measurements must be precise. For reveal fit (inside the recess): measure width at 3 points, height at 3 points, use the smallest of each, and check recess depth is at least 35–40mm. For L-frame (outside mount): measure the opening and add 30–50mm overlap each side. Don't deduct — we handle manufacturing tolerances. Millimetres only.

Before You Start

Plantation shutters are solid panels with adjustable louvres (blades) mounted in a rigid frame. Unlike blinds or curtains, they're precision-manufactured to fit your specific window — there's no fabric to gather, no chain to adjust. The frame is either fitted inside your window recess or mounted over it.

This means measurements need to be accurate to the millimetre. A blind that's 3mm too narrow will still function. A shutter that's 3mm too wide won't fit in the recess at all.

The good news: the process is straightforward once you understand the two mount types. And if you'd rather not measure yourself, we offer free in-home measuring across Melbourne.

Choose Your Mount Type

This is the first decision. Everything else follows from it.

Most Popular

Reveal Fit (Inside Mount)

  • Shutters sit inside the window recess
  • Clean, built-in look — the architrave stays visible
  • Requires minimum 35–40mm recess depth
  • Window must be reasonably square (diagonals within 5mm)
  • Best for: deep recesses, windows with nice architraves
Alternative

L-Frame (Outside Mount)

  • Frame mounts over the window opening
  • Covers the architrave or opening edges
  • Works on any recess depth (even zero)
  • Hides out-of-square windows
  • Best for: shallow recesses, uneven windows, flush walls
💡 Not sure? If your recess depth is 40mm or more and the diagonals are within 5mm, go reveal fit — it looks better. If either condition fails, L-frame is the safer choice.

Step-by-Step: Reveal Fit (Inside Mount)

Cross-section: the shutter sits inside the recess, in front of the glass. Minimum 35–40mm depth required.
1

Check Recess Depth

Measure from the front face of the window frame (room side) to the glass — or to any handle, latch, or obstruction, whichever is closest. Measure at all four corners and the centre.

You need at least 35mm clear. If your handle protrudes and reduces the depth below 35mm, you may need to recess the handle or switch to L-frame.

Measure width at three heights and height at three points. Record the smallest of each.
2

Measure Width

Measure the internal width of the recess at three points: top, middle, and bottom. Use the smallest measurement. This ensures the shutter frame fits at the tightest point.

Measure from the brick, plaster, or timber surface — not from any trim that could be removed.

3

Measure Height

Measure the internal height at three points: left side, centre, and right side. Again, use the smallest measurement.

If you have a tile sill (common in Melbourne bathrooms), measure from the top of the tile, not the original sill underneath.

4

Check for Square

Measure both diagonals of the recess — top-left to bottom-right, and top-right to bottom-left. Compare the two numbers.

  • Difference < 5mm: Fine for reveal fit. We build in tolerance.
  • Difference 5–10mm: Workable, but mention it on your order. We may need to add packing.
  • Difference > 10mm: Seriously consider L-frame. A reveal-fit shutter in a window this far out of square will have visible gaps.
Measure both diagonals. If the difference is under 5mm, your window is square enough for reveal fit.
5

Check for Obstructions

Look for anything inside the recess that might interfere:

  • Window handles/cranks — do they protrude past the frame? Measure how far.
  • Window locks/latches — some sit proud of the frame by 10–15mm
  • Tiles or splashback — especially in kitchens and bathrooms
  • Alarm sensors — reed switches on the frame

Note any obstructions — we can often work around them with a divider rail or modified panel layout.

Step-by-Step: L-Frame (Outside Mount)

L-frame is simpler to measure because the frame covers the opening rather than fitting inside it. There's more margin for error.

1

Measure the Opening

Measure the full width of the window opening (or the architrave outer edge to outer edge). Then measure the height.

2

Add Overlap

Add 30–50mm on each side. This gives the L-frame something to mount against and covers any uneven edges. Total overlap: 60–100mm on width, same on height.

If you want the shutters to cover the architrave completely, add enough overlap to extend past the outer edge of the architrave.

3

Check the Wall Surface

The L-frame mounts directly to the wall surface around the window. Check that it's:

  • Flat — no bulges, lumps, or uneven render
  • Solid — plasterboard is fine if you hit studs; brick is ideal
  • Clear — no power points, light switches, or vents in the mounting zone
4

Note Clearances

Check that the shutter won't interfere with anything when open — curtain rods above, furniture below, adjacent doors or handles.

Panel Configuration

The number of panels depends on the window width. Each panel should be between 400mm and 600mm wide for comfortable operation.

T-post vs no T-post: A T-post is a vertical support between panel pairs. It adds rigidity for wider openings and gives a clean closed look. For bi-fold configurations (panels fold to one side), a T-post at the centre keeps things stable. We'll recommend the right approach during your quote.

Divider Rail (Mid-Rail)

A divider rail splits the shutter into upper and lower sections, each with independently adjustable louvres. This lets you angle the top louvres for light while keeping the bottom closed for privacy — perfect for street-facing windows.

Measure divider rail position from the bottom of the opening to where you want the rail centre. Typically aligns with the window lock.

Where to position it:

  • Align with the window lock — this is the most common position and gives a clean look
  • One-third from the bottom — a good default if there's no obvious alignment point
  • At the transom bar if your window has one — matching architectural lines

Measure the position from the bottom of the opening to the centre of the rail. Note: a divider rail is optional. If you don't need independent louvre control, skip it for a cleaner look.

Bay Windows

Bay windows work beautifully with plantation shutters, but they're the most complex to measure. Each flat section gets its own shutter panel set.

  • Measure each section independently — width, height, depth for every flat face of the bay
  • Measure the angles between sections. Common configurations: 90° (box bay), 120° (angled bay), 135° (splayed bay)
  • Note which sections are operable windows — this affects hinge placement and panel fold direction
⚠️ Strong recommendation: For bay windows, book a free in-home measure. The angles, returns, and panel interactions add layers of complexity that are hard to get right without experience. We measure Melbourne bays every week — it's free, and it eliminates risk.

Common Measuring Mistakes

1

Making deductions yourself

Don't subtract "a few mm for clearance." Give us the exact recess measurement. We apply the precise manufacturing deductions (typically 3–5mm per side) during production.

2

Measuring only once per dimension

A single width measurement at the middle could miss a bulge at the top. Three measurements (top, middle, bottom) catches the tightest point. Same for height.

3

Forgetting to check recess depth

Width and height are right, but the recess is only 25mm deep. The shutter doesn't fit. Always check depth first — it determines your mount type.

4

Ignoring window handles

A handle that protrudes 20mm into the recess effectively reduces your usable depth by 20mm. Measure depth to the closest obstruction, not just to the glass.

5

Rounding measurements

"About 120cm" isn't a measurement. 1,207mm is. Shutters are precision products. Millimetres matter — every one of them.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the minimum recess depth for reveal-fit shutters?

You need at least 35–40mm of clear depth from the front of the window frame to the glass (or any handle). This allows the shutter frame and louvres to sit and operate without hitting the glass. If your recess is shallower, use an L-frame mount instead.

How do I measure for L-frame shutters?

Measure the full window opening width and height. Add 30–50mm overlap on each side for the L-frame to mount against and cover the edges. Check that the wall surface is flat and clear of obstructions in the mounting zone.

How many shutter panels do I need?

Single panel for windows up to 600mm wide, 2 panels (bi-fold) for 600–1200mm, 3 panels for 1200–1800mm, and 4 panels for 1800–2400mm. We'll recommend the best configuration during the quote process — panel count also depends on the room layout and which way you want the panels to fold.

Can plantation shutters go on bay windows?

Absolutely. Each flat section of the bay gets its own shutter set, measured independently. You also need the angles between sections. Bay shutters look stunning but are complex — we recommend a free in-home measure for these.

What if my window isn't square?

Measure both diagonals. If the difference is under 5mm, reveal fit works fine — we build in tolerance. Over 5mm, L-frame is usually better because it covers the irregular edges. For severely out-of-square windows (10mm+), a professional measure is strongly recommended.

Do I need to make deductions for reveal fit?

No. Give us the exact recess measurements — smallest width and smallest height. We apply the correct manufacturing deductions (typically 3–5mm per side) so the shutter fits with proper operating clearance. If you deduct yourself, the shutter may end up too small.
