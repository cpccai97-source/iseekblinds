How to Measure for Curtains

Quick Summary

Measure your window width frame-to-frame, then add 150–200mm overlap each side — that's your track/rod length. For fullness, we multiply by 2× (pinch pleat) or 2.5× (S-fold) during manufacturing — you don't need to calculate this. Drop is from the track/rod to your chosen finish point: sill, below sill, or floor (10–15mm clearance). Always millimetres. Every window individually.

Why Curtain Measurements Differ from Blinds

If you've measured for roller blinds before, you might assume curtains work the same way. They don't. Three things change the maths:

  • Fullness — curtain fabric is wider than the track it hangs on. A 2000mm track might need 4000mm of fabric. The ratio depends on your heading style.
  • Stack-back — when curtains are open, the gathered fabric stacks to the sides. If your track is exactly the window width, the stacked curtains will block part of the glass.
  • Heading style — pinch pleat, eyelet, S-fold, pencil pleat. Each has different fullness, different hardware, and slightly different measuring rules.

The good news: you only need to give us the track/rod length and the drop. We handle the fullness calculation and fabric ordering. This guide tells you how to get those two numbers right.

Choose Your Heading Style

Your heading style determines the fullness ratio, the look, and whether you need a track or a rod. Pick this before measuring — it affects your hardware choice.

2× fullness

Pinch Pleat

  • Classic, formal look
  • Fabric pinched into neat folds at the top
  • Suits tracks (hooks clip onto gliders)
  • Best for: living rooms, master bedrooms
1.6–1.8× fullness

Eyelet / Grommet

  • Casual, modern look
  • Metal rings punched through fabric, threaded on rod
  • Requires a rod (not compatible with tracks)
  • Best for: kids' rooms, casual living
2–2.5× fullness

S-Fold / Wave

  • Minimal, contemporary look
  • Continuous soft wave along the track
  • Needs a compatible S-fold track
  • Best for: modern homes, sheers, open-plan
2–2.5× fullness

Pencil Pleat

  • Versatile, traditional
  • Tightly gathered folds like a row of pencils
  • Works with tracks or rods
  • Best for: anywhere, especially rentals

Track or Rod?

This is the second decision to make before measuring. It determines where your curtains mount and how they move.

Curtain Track

  • Mounted on ceiling or wall
  • Discreet — can be hidden by a pelmet or the curtain heading
  • Smoother glide, especially for heavy curtains
  • Can bend around bay windows
  • Compatible with: pinch pleat, pencil pleat, S-fold
  • Best for: floor-length, formal, bay windows

Curtain Rod

  • Wall-mounted with decorative brackets
  • Visible — the rod is part of the look
  • Finials (end caps) add character
  • Simpler installation
  • Compatible with: eyelet, pencil pleat, tab top
  • Best for: casual, feature windows, eyelets

Measuring Width

Your track extends beyond the window frame on both sides. This overlap lets curtains stack off the glass when open.
1

Measure the Window Opening

Measure the width of your window from the outside edge of the left frame to the outside edge of the right frame. If you don't have a frame (e.g. plasterboard opening), measure the full opening width.

2

Add Overlap Each Side

Add 150–200mm on each side of the window. This overlap serves two purposes:

  • It gives the curtain fabric somewhere to stack when the curtains are open, keeping the glass clear
  • When closed, the curtains extend past the window edges for better light block and insulation

For maximum insulation or bedroom blockout, use 200mm. For decorative curtains in living areas, 150mm is fine.

3

This Is Your Track/Rod Length

Track/rod length = window width + overlap (both sides)

Example: 1800mm window + 200mm left + 200mm right = 2200mm track.

Give us this number. Don't try to calculate fabric width — we do that based on your heading style and the fullness ratio.

⚠️ Don't give us fabric width. If you tell us "I need 4000mm of curtain," we'll assume that's your track length and multiply by the fullness ratio again. You'd end up with curtains wide enough to furnish two rooms. Give us the track/rod length only.
4

Check Wall Space

Make sure you have enough wall on each side of the window for the overlap. Check for:

  • Adjacent walls or corners — you may need to reduce one side
  • Power points or light switches under the bracket position
  • Air vents or ducted heating returns

If wall space is tight on one side, it's fine to use asymmetric overlap (e.g. 200mm left, 100mm right).

Measuring Drop

The drop is the vertical measurement from where the curtain hangs to where it finishes. Where you measure from depends on your hardware.

Four drop options: to sill, below sill, floor length (with clearance), or puddled on floor.
1

Measure From the Track/Rod Position

If you already have a track or rod installed, measure from the top of the track (or the top of the rod). If not yet installed, mark where it will go — typically 100–150mm above the window frame, or at the ceiling for a floor-to-ceiling look.

2

Choose Your Finish Point

  • Sill length: To the bottom of the window sill. Good for kitchen or bathroom windows where you don't want fabric near the floor.
  • Below sill: 150mm below the sill. Creates a more finished look while staying off the floor. Good for dining rooms.
  • Floor length: 10–15mm above the floor. The most popular choice. Looks intentional and clean.
  • Puddle: 20–30mm of extra fabric resting on the floor. Dramatic and luxurious — best in formal rooms that don't get heavy foot traffic.
3

Eyelet Curtains — Special Rule

Eyelet curtains sit differently. The fabric hangs from the rod, not from above it. The top of the curtain panel is level with the top of the rod, and the eyelet rings sit above.

So measure from the top of the rod down to your finish point. This is your drop measurement — the fabric will begin at rod level.

💡 Melbourne tip: For floor-length curtains in rooms with ducted heating vents along the floor, make sure the curtains sit above or clear of the vent. A curtain draped over a floor vent blocks airflow and can discolour the fabric.

Understanding Stack-Back

Stack-back is the space your curtains occupy when fully open. It's the most commonly overlooked factor in curtain measurement — and the reason so many curtains end up blocking part of the window.

When open, each curtain panel stacks to roughly one-third of the track length. The track should extend past the window so the stack sits off the glass.

As a rule of thumb, each curtain panel stacks to about one-third of the track length when fully open. Sheers stack less (the fabric is thinner); heavy lined curtains stack more.

This is why the overlap we add in Step 2 matters. If your track is exactly the window width with no overlap, the stacked curtains will cover 15–20% of your glass when open.

💡 Great view? If your window looks out to a garden, the city, or anything worth seeing, extend the track further — 250–300mm per side. The curtains will stack entirely on the wall, leaving every millimetre of glass clear.

Bay Windows

Bay windows are the most complex curtain job in any home. They require multiple track sections joined at angles, and every panel needs its own measurement.

  • Measure each flat section independently — width and drop for every panel
  • Record the angles — standard bays are 90° or 135°, but nothing is standard in Melbourne houses built before 1980
  • Allow for track bends — if using a bendable track, measure along the path the track will follow, not straight-line

5 Common Curtain Measuring Mistakes

1

Giving us fabric width instead of track length

If you measure and multiply by the fullness yourself, we'll multiply again. The result? Curtains for a window twice the size of yours. Just tell us the track/rod length.

2

Forgetting stack-back

If your track matches your window width exactly, the stacked curtains will permanently cover 15–20% of your glass on each side. Extend the track 150–200mm past the frame.

3

Wrong measurement point for eyelets

Eyelet curtains hang from the rod, not above it. If you measure from the bracket or the wall above the rod, your curtains will be too long. Measure from the top of the rod.

4

Not checking floor-level consistency

Floors aren't always level, especially in older homes. For floor-length curtains, measure the drop at the left, centre, and right. Use the shortest measurement, then subtract 10mm for clearance.

5

Assuming bay windows are simple

Each angle adds complexity. Each section needs its own measurement. Bend allowances, return bends, overlap at joins — it adds up. For bays, we genuinely recommend a professional measure.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much fullness do I need for curtains?

It depends on your heading style. Pinch pleat: 2× the track length. Eyelet: 1.6–1.8×. S-fold/wave: 2–2.5×. Pencil pleat: 2–2.5×. More fullness creates a richer, more luxurious drape. You don't need to calculate this — give us the track length and we'll handle the rest.

Should curtains touch the floor?

Standard floor-length curtains sit 10–15mm above the floor. This small gap looks intentional and keeps the fabric clean. For a dramatic "puddle" look, add 20–30mm of extra length. Avoid anything in between — a 40mm gap just looks like a measuring error.

Can I use a track with eyelet curtains?

No. Eyelet curtains have metal rings punched through the fabric that need to thread onto a rod. For tracks, choose pinch pleat, pencil pleat, or S-fold headings instead. If you've already got a track installed and want the eyelet look, we can suggest alternatives that achieve a similar aesthetic.

How far should the curtain track extend past the window?

150–200mm each side is standard. This gives the stacked curtains room to sit off the glass when open. If your window has a view, go wider — 250–300mm per side means the curtains clear the glass entirely.

Do I need to measure for lining separately?

No. Just tell us if you want unlined, standard lined, or blockout lined. The lining is sewn directly into the curtain during manufacturing — no separate measurement needed.
