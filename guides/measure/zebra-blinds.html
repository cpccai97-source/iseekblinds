How to Measure for Zebra Blinds

Before You Measure

Zebra blinds — also sold as day-night blinds, vision blinds, or banded roller shades — operate by sliding two layers of alternating sheer and solid fabric across each other. When the bands align, you get filtered light; rotate them to blackout. That dual-fabric mechanism adds a few measurement considerations you won't find with standard roller blinds.

Tools you need

  • Steel tape measure — minimum 3m, not a fabric or plastic tape (they stretch)
  • Pencil and pad — or a phone note; never trust memory across multiple windows
  • Spirit level — optional but useful to check if the sill or reveal is square
  • Torch — helpful for seeing into deep recesses and spotting handles or obstructions
⚠️

Measure every window individually. Even windows that look identical in the same room often differ by 5–20mm. Order based on actual measurements, never by copying a neighbour window's dimensions.

Decide: inside or outside mount?

This is the first decision because it changes what you measure. The quick rule:

  • Inside mount (recess fit) — blind sits inside the window reveal, clean flush look. Requires ≥ 60mm of recess depth.
  • Outside mount (face fix) — blind sits on the wall or frame face above the window. No minimum recess depth. Better light blockout.

Inside Mount — Recess Fit

Inside mount gives zebra blinds their most polished look — the cassette sits flush with the wall face and the fabric falls cleanly within the reveal. However, it demands enough depth and a reasonably square recess to work properly.

  1. 1

    Check recess depth first

    Measure from the wall face into the recess (horizontally inward). You need a minimum of 60mm for a standard zebra cassette. Motorised versions need 70mm minimum. If your depth is borderline, add 5mm buffer — blinds in tight recesses can bind on the wall or architrave.

    1 min
  2. 2

    Measure width — three heights

    Measure the recess opening width at top, middle, and bottom. Older homes especially can have recesses that taper. Record all three figures, then use the narrowest measurement as your order width. Supply this exact figure — the factory applies a 2–4mm deduction for operating clearance.

    2 min
  3. 3

    Measure drop — three widths

    Measure height from the top of the recess to the sill at left, centre, and right. Use the longest measurement (so the blind reaches the sill). The factory deducts up to 10mm for hem clearance above the sill. For a floating look (blind stopping 10–15mm above sill), tell your supplier and they'll factor it in.

    2 min
  4. 4

    Check for obstructions

    Look for handles, espagnolette bolts, tilt mechanisms, fly screens, or security locks inside the recess. Any handle projecting more than 20mm from the frame face will likely foul the fabric. If present, use outside mount or ask for a slim-cassette version (not always available).

    1 min

Inside mount: measure width between reveal walls (3 heights), drop from recess top to sill (3 widths), and confirm recess depth ≥ 60mm

💡

If the recess has no sill: Measure drop to the point you want the blind to finish — typically flush with the bottom of the reveal or just past it. Specify clearly when ordering.

Outside Mount — Face Fix

Outside mount is the more forgiving option and gives better light blockout. The cassette sits on the wall (or the face of the timber architrave) above the window, and the blind covers the entire frame opening plus an overlap on each side.

  1. 1

    Decide your fixing point

    Determine where the top of the cassette will sit — typically 80–120mm above the top of the frame (or above the architrave). Higher placement makes the window look taller. Mark this height on the wall with a pencil.

    1 min
  2. 2

    Measure width with overlap

    Measure the window frame width (or architrave-to-architrave if present). Add 50–80mm each side (total 100–160mm extra) to block side light and achieve a proportionate look. If you have tight wall space with a light switch or adjacent window nearby, add whatever the space allows — a minimum 30mm each side is the absolute floor.

    2 min
  3. 3

    Measure drop from fixing point

    Measure from your marked fixing point down to your desired finish position. Options: sill level (tidy), 50mm below sill (more light block), or 10–15mm above floor if no sill exists. Remember to add the 50–60mm cassette height into your total if the supplier asks for overall blind length vs fabric length — clarify with them at order time.

    2 min
  4. 4

    Check the wall surface and stud positions

    For outside mount, the cassette brackets fix to the wall face. Check that you have a solid surface at the fixing point — plasterboard between studs needs wall anchors; brick needs masonry anchors; timber frame is ideal. Locating a stud or using solid-wall fixings is covered in the installation guide.

    2 min

Outside mount: blind width = window/architrave width + 50–80mm each side. Drop = fixing point to desired finish.

💡

No architrave? Measure the window frame outer edge to outer edge and add your side overlap from there. Mark your top fixing point with a pencil and measure down from that mark.

Stripe Alignment — the Detail That Matters

Standard roller blinds have uniform fabric — it doesn't matter much if the drop is a little short because there's no pattern to align. Zebra blinds are different. The alternating sheer/solid stripes follow a fixed pattern repeat (typically 80–100mm per stripe pair), and a blind cut to a random length may end mid-stripe — meaning the bottom edge shows a partial stripe that looks unfinished.

What to do about stripe alignment

  • Most quality manufacturers automatically size to the nearest full stripe repeat — ask them to confirm.
  • If you have a specific drop that's non-negotiable (e.g., you're tiling under the blind), tell the supplier so they can start the stripe sequence from the top at a position that lands cleanly at the bottom.
  • For multiple windows in the same room, order all blinds in a single batch so the stripe repeat starts at the same position on each blind — this is especially visible when blinds sit side by side.

Left: blind cut to a non-repeat dimension leaves a partial stripe. Right: manufacturer aligns cut to full stripe repeat.

Special Situations

Double roller combinations

Zebra blinds are often paired with a blockout roller blind in the same cassette system (dual-roller). For these, the cassette is deeper (usually 95–120mm) — check minimum recess depth accordingly. Measure only once, but specify clearly that you're ordering a dual-roller system.

Motorised zebra blinds

The motor adds depth to the cassette — minimum 70mm recess depth for tubular motors (inside-cassette design). External wired motors may need even more depth. Ask for the specific cassette dimensions when getting quotes. Otherwise, width and drop measurements are identical to manual blinds.

Skylight and angled windows

Zebra blinds are not recommended for steeply angled skylights — the dual-fabric system relies on gravity to maintain tension between the layers. On any angle over about 30° from vertical, the two fabric layers can slip out of alignment. Use a purpose-made skylight blind instead.

Bay windows and corner windows

Measure each facet of a bay window independently. Zebra blinds don't bend — each blind covers one flat facet only. For a corner configuration, leave 5–10mm clearance at the corner so adjacent blinds don't overlap or clash. Outside mount usually works better in bay and corner situations.

Double-glazed windows

Most double-glazed window frames sit flush with or close to the wall face — recess depth is often less than 60mm. Measure the actual depth between the glazing/frame face and the wall. If under 60mm, default to outside mount.

📏

Minimum width for zebra blinds: Most manufacturers have a minimum blind width of around 300mm. Below this, the stripe pattern doesn't have enough room to function visually. For very narrow windows (e.g., slimline windows beside a door), check minimum width specs with your supplier.

Common Measurement Mistakes

  • For inside mount, you need the clear opening between the recess walls — not the frame or architrave. The frame is usually 10–20mm smaller than the recess. Measuring the frame gives you a blind that's too narrow to fit inside the recess properly.
  • If you supply the widest measurement, the manufactured blind may not fit into the narrowest part of the recess. Always supply the narrowest measurement for inside mount — the factory's 2–4mm deduction then provides clearance at all points.
  • Reputable blind manufacturers factor in operating clearance during fabrication. If you also deduct 10–15mm before supplying your figure, the final blind will be undersized. Supply your exact measured dimension unless the supplier explicitly tells you otherwise.
  • A 55mm recess ordered as inside mount will have the cassette protruding beyond the wall face. It won't sit flush, looks awkward, and may interfere with opening shutters or adjacent elements. Always check depth first — takes 10 seconds with a tape.
  • Adding only 20–30mm each side looks awkwardly proportioned and allows significant light bleed from the sides. The minimum for good light blockout is 50mm each side. If wall space allows, 60–80mm each side is ideal and looks much more substantial.
  • Soft tapes stretch and sag, especially over distances above 1m. A 5mm error on a 2m window width is easy to make with a fabric tape. Use a steel tape measure — hook-end against one wall, pull taut across to the other. Re-measure once to confirm.
