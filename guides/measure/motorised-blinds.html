Quick Summary

Measuring for motorised blinds follows the same width-and-height process as manual blinds, but you need three extra considerations:

  • Recess depth: 50mm minimum (single) or 70mm (double) — motor housing is wider than a chain mechanism
  • Motor side: choose the side closest to your power point, or either side for battery/solar
  • Cable route: plan how the power cable reaches the motor (hardwired) or where to access the motor for recharging (battery)

Why Motorised Blinds Need Extra Attention

A motorised blind has a tubular motor inside the roller tube. It's a neat piece of engineering — you tap your phone and the blind rolls smoothly into position. But that motor adds about 5–10mm to the bracket depth and creates a bulge at one end of the headrail.

This means the recess depth requirement is higher than for manual blinds, and you need to think about where the motor sits relative to your power source. Get these details right at the measurement stage and installation is straightforward. Miss them and you're repositioning power points or switching to outside mount after the blind arrives.

The actual width and height measurement process is identical to measuring for roller blinds — three widths, three heights, narrowest width, longest height. This guide adds the motorised-specific steps on top.

Power Options for Motorised Blinds

The power source you choose affects your measurement requirements. There are three options, each with different depth and clearance needs.

🔌

Hardwired (240V)

Permanent power via a dedicated cable to a transformer or power point. Set-and-forget — never needs charging.

  • Needs nearby power point or electrician
  • Cable exits one end of headrail
  • Best for new builds & renovations
  • No battery degradation over time
🔋

Rechargeable Battery

Built-in lithium battery. Charge via USB-C every 6–12 months depending on daily use.

  • No wiring required at all
  • Motor end needs to be accessible
  • Best for retrofitting existing homes
  • Charge indicator via app
☀️

Solar Panel

Small solar panel on the window charges the battery continuously. Truly wire-free and self-sustaining.

  • Panel sits on window glass (suction cup)
  • Needs sun-facing window
  • Best for hard-to-reach windows
  • Panel cable runs to motor end
📝 Note: For hardwired systems, the cable typically exits from the motor-side end cap and needs a discreet path to the power source. If you're building or renovating, ask your electrician to install a recessed power point inside the window recess — this gives the cleanest finish.

Choosing the Motor Side

The motor sits inside one end of the roller tube. You need to decide which side before measuring, because it affects:

  • Cable routing — for hardwired, the cable exits from the motor end
  • Charging access — for battery, you need to reach the motor end to plug in USB-C
  • Recess depth — the motor-side bracket is slightly deeper than the idle side
The motor adds ~50mm depth at its end. For hardwired, place it nearest the power point. For battery, pick the most accessible side.
💡 Consistency matters: If you're motorising multiple windows in the same room, keep the motor on the same side for all blinds. This keeps the headrail profiles symmetrical and simplifies wiring if you're using hardwired motors.

Inside Mount vs Outside Mount

The same two mounting options apply as manual blinds, but with adjusted depth requirements for the motor housing.

Inside Mount (Recess Fit)

  • Blind sits inside the window recess
  • Clean, integrated look
  • Minimum 50mm recess depth (single)
  • Minimum 70mm recess depth (double)
  • Cable exits into recess — needs concealment
  • Best for: living rooms, home offices

Outside Mount (Face Fit)

  • Blind sits on the wall or frame face
  • Better light blockout at edges
  • No minimum recess depth
  • Easier cable routing behind fascia
  • Motor housing concealed by pelmet
  • Best for: bedrooms, west-facing windows

Inside Mount — Step by Step

1

Check Recess Depth (Motor Side)

This is the critical difference from manual blinds. Measure the recess depth at the top, specifically on the side where the motor will sit. You need:

  • 50mm minimum for a single motorised roller blind
  • 70mm minimum for a double motorised roller blind

If your recess is shallower, you have two options: switch to outside mount, or choose a battery motor with a slimline profile (some are as thin as 45mm).

⚠️ Common trap: The recess depth is often different at the top versus the bottom, especially in older Melbourne homes with timber frames. Measure at the top — that's where the bracket sits.
2

Measure Width at Three Points

Measure the internal recess width at top, middle, and bottom. Record all three. Use the narrowest measurement.

Don't deduct anything — we handle manufacturing clearances. For motorised blinds, this is typically 3–5mm per side.

Inside mount: same three-point measurement, but check 50mm+ depth at the motor end. Motor shown on the left — choose whichever side suits your power source.
3

Measure Height at Three Points

Measure the internal height from the top of the recess to the sill at left, centre, and right. Use the longest measurement.

4

Check for Square

If your three width measurements differ by more than 5mm, inside mount may not work well. The motorised blind needs consistent clearance to run smoothly — a skewed recess can cause the fabric to rub on one side.

In this case, outside mount is the safer choice.

5

Note Power Point Location

For hardwired motors: sketch the position of the nearest power point relative to the window. Note the distance and whether the cable can run behind the architrave, through the wall cavity, or along the skirting board.

For battery motors: confirm you can reach the motor end with a USB-C cable for recharging. If the window is above a staircase or very high, consider solar charging instead.

Outside Mount — Step by Step

Outside mount: add 50–75mm overlap per side, 100mm above. Motor shown top-left with cable routing down the wall.
1

Measure Frame Width

Measure the outside width of your window frame, from outer edge to outer edge.

2

Add Side Overlap

Add 50–75mm on each side. For bedrooms or blockout motors, use the full 75mm. Your order width = frame width + 100–150mm total overlap.

3

Decide Drop Height

Measure from where the bracket will sit — typically 100mm above the frame — down to your finish point:

  • To sill: bottom of window frame
  • Below sill: add 50mm for better light block
  • To floor: measure to 10mm above floor level
4

Check Clearance Above

The motorised bracket projects further from the wall than a manual bracket. Check that:

  • There's no cornice or ceiling moulding within 100mm above the frame
  • No vents, air conditioning units, or smoke detectors are in the bracket path
  • The wall surface is solid enough for screws (plasterboard may need wall plugs)
💡 Pro Tip: Outside mount is actually easier for motorised blinds in many cases. A fascia or pelmet covers the motor housing completely, the cable can run behind it, and you don't need to worry about recess depth at all. It's the preferred option for most Melbourne bedrooms we fit.

Cable Routing for Hardwired Motors

If you've chosen a hardwired motor, the cable route needs planning at the measurement stage — not after the blind arrives. Here are the three most common approaches:

A

Recessed Power Point (Cleanest)

An electrician installs a power point inside the window recess or behind the pelmet. The cable plugs straight in with zero visible wiring. This is the standard approach for new builds and major renovations.

What to measure: the exact position where the motor end cap sits, so the power point aligns.

B

Cable Channel Along Architrave

The cable runs along the window architrave and down to a standard power point. A colour-matched cable channel keeps it tidy. Best for retrofitting where you don't want to open the wall.

What to measure: the distance from the motor end cap to the nearest power point, following the architrave path.

C

Through-Wall Feed

The cable passes through a small hole in the wall near the bracket and connects to a transformer in the ceiling cavity or behind the wall. Invisible from the room. Requires an electrician and wall access.

What to measure: wall thickness and confirm there are no pipes or wires in the drilling path. A stud finder helps.

🔒 Safety: All hardwired motor installations in Australia must comply with AS/NZS 3000 (Wiring Rules). The motor transformer and any in-wall cabling must be installed by a licensed electrician. Don't DIY the electrical work — measure and plan, then get a sparky in.

Smart Home Integration

Most motorised blind motors support smart home control. Here's what works with what — mention your system when quoting so we recommend the right motor.

🏠

Google Home

Wi-Fi motors work natively. Voice control via "Hey Google, close the blinds."

🍎

Apple HomeKit

Requires a HomeKit-compatible bridge (e.g. Automate Pulse 2). Full Siri and Home app control.

📡

Amazon Alexa

Most Wi-Fi and Zigbee motors support Alexa skills. Group blinds by room for single-command control.

🔧

Home Assistant

Zigbee and MQTT motors integrate directly. Full automation with sensors, schedules, and scenes.

📝 Wi-Fi note: Check that your Wi-Fi reaches the window where the motor will be installed. Thick walls, double glazing, and metal window frames can weaken the signal. If needed, a Wi-Fi extender near the window solves this.

6 Common Motorised Blind Mistakes

1

Forgetting the motor housing depth

A standard roller blind bracket needs 45mm. A motorised bracket needs 50mm. Seems small — but it's the difference between a blind that fits flush and one that sticks out of the recess.

2

No power point planning

The blind arrives, the motor is on the left, and the nearest power point is 3 metres away on the right. Plan the cable route before ordering — or choose battery/solar to avoid wiring entirely.

3

Wrong motor side

Once manufactured, the motor side can't be swapped. Specify it at order time. If in doubt, our team will recommend based on your window photos.

4

Weak Wi-Fi at the window

Smart motors need a stable connection. Test your Wi-Fi signal at the window before ordering. Metal window frames and double glazing can kill the signal.

5

Mixing motor types across one room

Using different motor brands or protocols in the same room creates a control mess. Standardise on one motor and one app for all blinds in a space.

6

Ignoring maximum width limits

Motorised roller blinds typically max out at 3,000–3,600mm width depending on the motor torque. Beyond that, you need a higher-torque motor or two blinds with a mullion join. Measure wide windows carefully and mention the width when quoting.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum recess depth for motorised blinds?

Single motorised roller blinds need at least 50mm recess depth (vs 45mm for manual). Double motorised rollers need 70mm minimum due to the motor housing and dual bracket system.

Can I install motorised blinds without a nearby power point?

Yes — battery-powered motors (rechargeable lithium) eliminate wiring entirely. They last 6–12 months per charge depending on usage. Solar-panel chargers can extend this indefinitely for sun-facing windows.

Which side should the motor go on?

For hardwired motors, put the motor on the side closest to your power point. For battery motors, either side works — choose the side that's easiest to access for recharging. For consistency, keep the same side across all windows in a room.

Do motorised blinds work with Google Home and Apple HomeKit?

Most modern motorised blind motors support Wi-Fi or Zigbee and integrate with Google Home, Amazon Alexa, and Apple HomeKit (via bridge). Mention your smart home system when requesting a quote so we can recommend compatible motors.

Is measuring for motorised blinds different from manual blinds?

The width and height measurement process is identical — measure at three points, use narrowest width and longest height. The difference is recess depth (50mm minimum vs 45mm for manual) and planning for the motor housing position, power source, and cable routing.

How noisy are motorised blinds?

Modern tubular motors run at around 35–40 dB — roughly the volume of a quiet library. You'll hear a soft hum for the few seconds the blind is moving, then silence. Older or cheaper motors can be louder, so stick with quality brands.

What's the maximum width for a single motorised roller blind?

Most standard motors handle up to 3,000mm width. Higher-torque motors can go to 3,600mm. Beyond that, we recommend two blinds with a centre join or a commercial-grade motor. Tell us the width when quoting — we'll recommend the right motor.
