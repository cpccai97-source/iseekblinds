How to Measure for Roman Blinds

Roman blinds are one of the most measurement-sensitive window coverings — get the width wrong by 10mm and you'll have light gaps or a blind that won't fit. This guide covers inside mount, outside mount, stack height, and the two numbers most people forget to record.

Inside Mount vs Outside Mount

This is the first decision — everything else flows from it. Roman blinds can be mounted inside the window recess (sitting flush within the architrave) or outside on the wall above (covering the frame and surrounds).

Inside Mount

Recess Fit

  • Clean, architectural look
  • Blind sits within the window reveal
  • Requires ≥ 40mm recess depth
  • Shows architrave on all sides
  • Slight light gaps at edges (normal)
  • Best for: feature windows, new builds
Outside Mount

Face Fit

  • Covers window frame + surrounds
  • Makes windows appear larger
  • Works with any recess depth
  • Better light blockout
  • Headrail visible from outside
  • Best for: bedrooms, privacy rooms

Left: Inside mount fits within the recess — clean look, slight edge gaps. Right: Outside mount covers the frame — maximum light blockout.

⚠ Check your recess depth first

Before choosing inside mount, measure how deep your window recess is — from the face of the glass to the front of the architrave. You need a minimum of 40mm for a standard Roman blind headrail. If it's shallower than that, you must use outside mount.

Step-by-Step Measuring

1

Check Recess Depth (Inside Mount Only)

Push your tape measure into the window recess from the face of the glass/frame outward to the front edge of the architrave. This is your recess depth.

Standard Roman blind: minimum 40mm
Motorised Roman blind: minimum 65mm

If your recess is borderline (40–50mm), choose outside mount — a tight fit makes the blind look bulky and the fabric can scuff the architrave as it folds.

2

Measure Width

Measure in millimetres, not centimetres or inches. Use a steel tape — fabric tapes stretch and give inaccurate readings.

Measure width at top, middle and bottom of the recess. Use the narrowest reading.

Inside Mount

Measure the inside width of the recess at three points: near the top, at the middle, and near the sill. Write down all three. Use the narrowest measurement.

Do not deduct anything. The manufacturer will reduce the width by 8–12mm to allow the blind to hang freely without binding.

Outside Mount

Measure the full width of the window frame (including architrave). Then add 60–80mm on each side for overlap. Total = frame width + 120–160mm.

Example: 1000mm frame + 70mm each side = 1140mm wide blind. This extra width blocks the side light gaps.

Pro Tip

If your window has a handle or winder on the side, make sure the blind width doesn't foul it when closed. Measure to 20mm short of any obstacle.

3

Measure Drop (Height)

Drop is the vertical distance from where the headrail will sit to where you want the blind to finish.

Measure drop at left, centre, and right. Use the longest reading so the blind reaches the sill everywhere.

Inside Mount

Measure from the top of the recess (where the headrail bracket will sit) down to the window sill. Take three readings — left, centre, right — and use the longest.

Outside Mount

Decide where you want the headrail to sit above the window frame (standard: 100–150mm above the frame top) and where the blind should finish (sill level, or 50–100mm below sill to eliminate the underframe light gap). Measure that full distance.

Make Windows Look Taller

Mounting the headrail high (150–200mm above the window frame) and dropping the blind well below the sill creates a dramatic full-height look. Popular in Melbourne apartment renovations.

4

Stack Height — The Number Most People Forget

When a Roman blind is raised, the fabric folds stack up at the top of the window. The height of this folded stack determines how much of your view is obscured when the blind is open.

When raised, folded fabric stacks at the top. A 1.8m drop blind stacks ~250–300mm high — partially blocking the window.

If maximising the view when open matters — gardens, water views, high-rise outlook — consider whether the stack height is acceptable, or discuss Roman blind alternatives like blockout roller blinds which stack to only 80–100mm.

5

Note Cord Side and Child Safety

Roman blinds use a cord or chain lift mechanism. You need to specify which side you want this on — left or right when standing facing the window.

Consider the following:

  • Which side is easier to reach from where you usually stand?
  • Is there furniture, bedhead, or curtaining on either side that would obstruct cord reach?
  • For multiple windows, consistent cord placement (all left, or all right) looks tidier

⚠ Child Safety — AS 2999

Australian standard AS 2999 requires that all corded window coverings have safety devices to prevent strangulation hazards for children under 9. iSeekBlinds supplies all Roman blinds with:

  • Cord cleats or tensioners (keeps cords taut and out of reach)
  • Safe cord positioning — minimum 1600mm from floor, or secured to wall
  • Child safety warning labels

If ordering for a home with young children, ask about motorised Roman blinds — fully cordless and child safe by design.

6

Check for Obstructions

Before finalising, do a quick check for anything that might interfere with installation or operation:

  • Window handles or cranks: Will the blind foul them when raised/lowered? Measure the clearance.
  • Architrave depth: For outside mount, you need a flat mounting surface — check that the architrave is at least 30mm wide on the top edge.
  • Curtain tracks: If adding Roman blinds in front of existing curtain tracks, allow at least 60mm of clearance between the track and the planned blind headrail position.
  • Window air-conditioning unit: Any protruding AC unit will determine the minimum outside mount height.
7

Record and Label Every Window

Never rely on memory. Even "identical" windows in the same house can vary by 5–10mm. Measure every window individually and label each one clearly.

Recommended format per window:

  • Room + position (e.g. "Master Bedroom — Left Window")
  • Mount type: Inside or Outside
  • Width: Xmm (narrowest)
  • Drop: Xmm (longest, inside) or from headrail to finish (outside)
  • Cord side: Left or Right
  • Fabric: [your fabric choice]
  • Notes: handles, obstructions, motorise Y/N

5 Common Measuring Mistakes

!

Deducting clearance yourself

The most common mistake. You measure the recess as 950mm wide, then think "I'd better take off 10mm each side" and send in 930mm. The manufacturer then takes their 10mm off 930mm and makes a 920mm blind. Result: a blind 30mm narrower than it needs to be, with visible light gaps.

Fix: Send the exact measured dimension. The manufacturer's deduction table handles clearance — they do it professionally every time.

!

Using the widest measurement for inside mount

Windows are rarely perfectly square. If you measure 957mm at top, 960mm at middle, and 954mm at bottom, you use 954mm — not the average, not the widest. A blind made to the widest measurement will jam in the narrowest part of the recess.

!

Ignoring stack height for bedroom windows

You have a 2.1m ceiling and your window runs from 900mm height to 2050mm (floor to nearly ceiling). When fully open, the Roman blind stack (roughly 380mm) covers the top of the window. You now have an open view only through the bottom 770mm of glass. For a bedroom this might be fine — but check before ordering a bold fabric.

!

Forgetting that each window is different

Old houses especially. Three seemingly identical windows in the same room can easily vary by 15–20mm due to house settlement. Always measure every window individually. Never copy measurements from one window to another.

!

Using a cloth tape or folded-up metal tape

Cloth sewing tapes stretch. A kinked or bent metal tape gives a reading up to 10mm off at 1200mm spans. Use a rigid steel tape in good condition, and hold it taut across the opening without sag.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum recess depth for inside mount Roman blinds?
Standard Roman blinds need at least 40mm of recess depth for inside mount. Motorised Roman blinds require 65mm minimum to accommodate the motor housing. If your recess is shallower than 40mm, choose outside mount — the headrail mounts on the wall or architrave above the window. Trying to force a blind into a too-shallow recess causes the fabric folds to scrape the architrave and limits how flat the blind hangs.
Should I measure before or after deciding on inside vs outside mount?
Decide your mount type first, then measure. The dimensions are completely different: inside mount uses exact recess dimensions; outside mount uses the frame width plus overlaps. If you measure without a decision made, you'll be taking two sets of measurements — or guessing wrong.
How much does the fabric stack when a Roman blind is raised?
As a rule of thumb: allow 200–250mm for a 1.0–1.5m drop blind, and 250–350mm for a 1.5–2.0m drop. Linen and heavier fabrics stack higher than lightweight polyester. If the view when raised matters (garden, water, street level), consider this carefully or ask about a roller blind alternative.
Do I deduct anything from my measurements for inside mount?
No. Give the manufacturer your exact recess dimensions in millimetres. Roman blind manufacturers apply a standard deduction of 8–12mm from your width measurement to ensure the blind operates freely in the recess without binding. If you pre-deduct, the blind will be too narrow and have light gaps at the sides.
Can Roman blinds be wider than 2400mm?
Most Roman blinds are limited to 2400mm as a single blind. For wider windows, two blinds side-by-side is the standard approach. At the centre join, a 10–15mm light gap will be visible — unavoidable with two separate blinds. Some manufacturers can go to 3000mm on custom order, but fold consistency becomes harder to guarantee at that span.
How far above the window should I mount a Roman blind for outside mount?
Standard recommendation is 100–150mm above the window frame or top of the architrave. Mounting higher makes the window appear taller and reduces the light gap at the top when the blind is closed. For a ceiling mount (popular in new builds), measure from the ceiling down and check the stack height doesn't obscure important views.
