How to Measure for Sheer Curtains

Sheers seem forgiving — they're not. A few millimetres off in the wrong place and the whole window looks off. Here's exactly what to measure and what to give us.

⏱
10–15 minutes
🎯
Easy difficulty
📐
Steel tape measure + pencil
🆓

Step 1 — Choose Your Heading Style First

The heading style determines the track type, fullness ratio, and what measurements you actually need. Choose before you pick up the tape measure.

Heading Style Track or Rod Fullness Ratio Best For
S-Fold / Wave Track only 2.0–2.5× Modern living areas, continuous soft ripple
Pencil Pleat Track or rod 2.0–2.5× Traditional and transitional interiors
Eyelet Rod only 1.5–1.8× Relaxed, contemporary look
Tab Top Rod only 1.5–2.0× Coastal, rustic, relaxed styling
Pinch Pleat Track or rod 2.0–2.5× Classic, formal, layered looks
💡 Tip: You only give us the finished track or rod width and the drop in mm. We calculate fabric width from the fullness ratio. Never try to calculate fabric cut width yourself.

The four main sheer heading styles — choose before measuring

Step 2 — Decide Where the Track or Rod Goes

Sheer curtains hung too low look like an afterthought. Most designers mount them high — ideally 100–200mm above the window frame, or flush with the ceiling for maximum drama.

Track or rod position options — measure drop from the track carrier bottom (or rod top for eyelets)

💡 Tip: For ceiling-mounted S-fold sheers, the track sits flush with the ceiling. Sheers hung this way need no cornice or pelmet — the fabric starts right at the ceiling and reads as intentional.
⚠️ Double tracks: If you're layering sheers over blockouts, measure the outer (sheer) track — it's usually 50–80mm longer than the inner blockout track to allow both curtains to stack independently.

Step 3 — Measure the Track or Rod Width

This is the finished width you give us — the track length, not the fabric width. We apply the fullness ratio on our end.

  1. 1

    Mark your track start and end points

    For wall-to-wall sheers, start and end at the wall brackets. For window-span sheers, start 100–150mm outside the window frame on each side. Mark both points with a pencil.

  2. 2

    Measure between your marks

    Hold the tape measure taut from one mark to the other. Record in millimetres. This is your track length / finished width.

    Tip: For S-fold curtains, the track needs extra length for stack-back if you want the curtains to clear the glass when open. Stack-back is roughly ⅓ of track length — half per side. Extend your track past the window frame to account for this.
  3. 3

    Measure again if using a double track

    Give us two separate widths: Sheer track width and Blockout track width. The sheer track runs in front and is typically 40–60mm longer on each side than the blockout track to stack separately.

Sheer track extends 100–150mm past the window frame on each side; blockout track is shorter so both can stack independently

Step 4 — Measure the Drop

Drop = from the bottom of the track carrier (where the curtain hooks in) down to your chosen finish point. For eyelet curtains on a rod, measure from the top of the rod.

Finish What to Measure Notes
Sill length Track carrier → window sill top Practical for kitchens, kids' rooms
Below sill Track carrier → sill top + 150mm Classic curtain look, covers radiators
Floor clearance ✓ Track carrier → floor − 10 to 15mm Most popular for sheers. Measure at 2–3 points
Puddle / pool Track carrier → floor + 100 to 150mm Romantic, intentional. Add extra for linen fabrics
⚠️ Always measure drop at two points: Measure at the left, centre, and right of the window. Australian homes regularly have 10–20mm floor variation across a single window. Use the longest measurement if you want a consistent floor clearance — adjust downward if you prefer the shorter side to just clear.

Step 5 — Record and Submit

We need five things for every sheer curtain order:

  1. Track or rod width (mm) — the finished track length
  2. Drop (mm) — from track carrier to finish point
  3. Heading style — S-fold, pencil pleat, eyelet, tab top, pinch pleat
  4. Track or rod type — track (S-fold/pencil pleat) or rod (eyelet/tab top)
  5. Fabric selection — or ask us to recommend based on your room
💡 Multiple windows: Sketch a quick floor plan and label each window (e.g. Living Left, Living Right, Master Bed). Sheer curtains made for one window rarely work on another — even if they look the same size.

Common Measurement Mistakes

❌ Mistake: Measuring the window glass, not the track span.
✓ Fix: Always measure the intended track or rod length — which should extend well past the glass.
❌ Mistake: Measuring drop in centimetres, not millimetres.
✓ Fix: Give us millimetres. 2450mm, not 245cm. Saves calculation errors.
❌ Mistake: Using the same drop measurement for all windows in a room.
✓ Fix: Floors are rarely level. Measure each window's drop separately.
❌ Mistake: Forgetting stack-back allowance on the track width.
✓ Fix: S-fold sheers stack to roughly ⅓ of total track length when open. If you want the curtains fully off the glass, extend the track past the window frame before measuring.
❌ Mistake: Ordering sheers and blockouts on the same track length.
✓ Fix: Double tracks need two different widths: a longer sheer track (front) and a shorter blockout track (back). Give us both separately.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • How much fullness do sheer curtains need?

    S-fold and wave sheers need 2.0–2.5× the finished track width. Pencil pleat: 2.0–2.5×. Eyelet: 1.5–1.8×. More fullness means a richer, more layered look — most of our sheer ranges default to 2.5× fullness. You only give us the track width; we calculate the fabric cut width.

  • Should sheer curtains touch the floor?

    For most living areas, a 10–15mm clearance looks intentional and keeps hemlines clean. Bedroom sheers are often floor-length or slightly puddling (+80–150mm) for a romantic effect. In dining areas or near pet traffic, clearance is more practical.

  • Can I hang sheers and blockout curtains on the same window?

    Yes — use a double track system. The front (inner) track carries the sheer; the back (outer) track carries the blockout. Measure them separately: the sheer track is typically 80–120mm wider than the blockout track so each panel can stack independently.

  • Do I measure the fabric width or the track width?

    Always the track width (finished width). We multiply by the fullness ratio for your heading style to determine fabric cut width. If you try to calculate fabric width yourself, you'll almost certainly over- or under-order.

  • What if my ceiling isn't level?

    Check with a spirit level before installing a ceiling-mounted track. An unlevel track makes every curtain hang at a subtle diagonal — it's very noticeable with sheers. If your ceiling is off by more than 5mm over the track span, consider a wall-mounted track instead and adjust the height manually.

  • How do I measure for sheer curtains in a bay window?

    Measure each bay section separately — width and drop. Bay window tracks need to be bent to follow the angle; straight tracks don't work. Tell us it's a bay window and give us the angle and each section width so we can cut and bend the track correctly before delivery.
