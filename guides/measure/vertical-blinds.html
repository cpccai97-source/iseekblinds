How to Measure for Vertical Blinds

Vertical blinds cover sliding doors and large windows differently from any other blind. Get the stacking clearance wrong and your door is permanently blocked. This guide covers inside/outside mount, slat deductions, and split-draw — all in millimetres.

What Makes Vertical Blinds Different

Most blinds measure width first, drop second — and that's it. Vertical blinds add a third variable: stacking clearance. When you open vertical blinds, the slats bunch up to one or both sides of the opening. If you don't account for that stacked width, you'll either block access to your sliding door or lose light where you don't want to.

Vertical blinds are most commonly used on:

  • Sliding doors — the most common application in Australian homes; requires careful stacking planning
  • Wide windows — bay windows, feature windows over 1800mm wide
  • Bi-fold door openings — large spans needing maximum light control
  • Floor-to-ceiling glazing — modern homes with full-height windows
Australian context

In Australia, the standard sliding door opening is typically 1800–2100mm wide and 2000–2400mm high. Always measure your specific opening — don't assume standard dimensions, even in a new build. Frames vary and floors are rarely perfectly level.

Inside vs Outside Mount

Before you measure a single number, decide which mount type you're using. This affects every measurement you take.

Inside Mount (Recess Fit)

Blind fits inside the window frame

The headrail mounts to the ceiling of the window recess or on the reveal face. Slats hang within the window opening. Gives a flush, built-in look.

  • ✓ Cleaner appearance
  • ✓ Shows architraves
  • ✓ Doesn't protrude into room
  • — Requires 80mm+ recess depth
  • — Light gaps at sides and top
  • — Not suitable for most sliding doors
Outside Mount (Face Fit)

Blind mounts above and over the frame

The headrail fixes to the wall or ceiling above the window or door frame. Blind covers the full frame and overlaps the wall on each side.

  • ✓ Works on sliding doors
  • ✓ Better light blockage
  • ✓ No recess depth needed
  • ✓ Makes windows appear larger
  • — Covers architraves
  • — Needs clear wall space above
⚠ Sliding doors → Outside mount only

Sliding door frames are typically only 30–40mm deep. You cannot inside-mount vertical blinds on a standard sliding door. The headrail (65–80mm deep) physically won't fit. Always use outside mount on sliding doors — fix the headrail to the ceiling or the wall above the door frame.

Inside Mount Measurements

Inside mount is only practical on windows with a recess depth of 80mm or more. If your recess is 60–79mm, you can still inside-mount but the headrail will protrude slightly — ask your supplier about low-profile headrail options.

1

Measure the recess depth first

Hold your tape measure horizontally from the front face of the window reveal to the back wall (or glass surface). If this measurement is less than 80mm, switch to outside mount.

Standard vertical blind headrails need 80–90mm clear depth from the front fixing point to the back wall. Check this number before anything else.

⏱ 2 minutes
2

Measure width at three points

Measure the inside width of the recess (left reveal face to right reveal face) at three heights:

  • Near the top of the recess
  • At mid-height
  • Near the bottom

Record the narrowest measurement. This is your exact opening width. Your supplier will deduct 10–15mm for clearance — give the exact measurement, let them make the deduction.

⏱ 3 minutes
3

Measure the drop at three points

Measure from the ceiling of the recess (where the headrail will fix) to the floor or sill at left, centre, and right positions. Record the longest measurement.

Deduct 10–15mm from the longest drop to ensure the bottom chain clears the floor. This prevents the slats dragging.

⏱ 3 minutes

Outside Mount Measurements

Outside mount is the standard approach for sliding doors and any window where inside mount isn't practical. The blind covers the frame and overlaps the surrounding wall.

1

Measure the full door or window frame

For a sliding door, measure edge-to-edge of the door frame (not the glass — the full frame including the aluminium surround). This is your base width. Record in mm.

⏱ 2 minutes
2

Add side overlaps

Add overlap to each side to ensure full frame coverage and light blockage:

  • Minimum overlap: 50mm each side
  • Better light control: 75mm each side
  • Full blackout overlap: 100mm each side

Example: Frame width 1800mm + 75mm × 2 sides = order width 1950mm

⏱ 1 minute
3

Decide ceiling vs wall fix for the headrail

The headrail can fix to:

  • Ceiling (most common): Measure drop from ceiling to floor. Deduct 10–15mm so slats clear the floor.
  • Wall above door: Measure from your intended fixing height to the floor. Position the headrail at least 50–100mm above the door frame.

Ceiling fix is generally neater — the headrail disappears against the ceiling.

⏱ 2 minutes
4

Record as W × H in mm

Write down your finished dimensions — Width × Height (Drop) in millimetres. For outside mount, these are the dimensions you order. Your supplier will not make deductions from outside-mount measurements.

Example: 1950 × 2380mm (ceiling fix, 10mm floor clearance already deducted)

⏱ 1 minute

Stacking Clearance — The Critical Calculation

Stacking clearance is the width the slats occupy when drawn fully open. This is the most commonly miscalculated element of vertical blind measuring.

As a rule of thumb: Stack width ≈ 15–20% of total blind width per side.

Total Blind Width Single Stack (one side) Split Stack (each side) Traffic clearance remaining
1800mm ~320mm one side ~160mm each side 1480mm (single) / 1480mm (split)
2100mm ~375mm one side ~190mm each side 1725mm (single) / 1720mm (split)
2400mm ~430mm one side ~215mm each side 1970mm (single) / 1970mm (split)
3000mm ~540mm one side ~270mm each side 2460mm (single) / 2460mm (split)

Single Stack (all to one side)

All slats draw to one side. Best when you only need access from one end of the door. Stacking is thicker but doesn't encroach on the opposite side at all.

Split Draw (centre open)

Slats divide and stack equally to both sides. Symmetrical look; blind opens from the centre. Ideal when you want even access across a wide opening or prefer balanced stacking.

Sliding door rule

For a standard 1800mm sliding door, plan the stack to go on the fixed panel side (the side that doesn't open). This way the stacked slats never obstruct the sliding panel. If your sliding panel is on the right, specify stack right when ordering.

Draw Direction

When ordering, you must specify how the blind draws open:

  • Left draw: Slats draw to the left (stack on the left side)
  • Right draw: Slats draw to the right (stack on the right side)
  • Split draw / centre open: Slats draw to both sides equally from the centre

Stand inside the room, facing the window or door. "Left" and "right" are from your perspective standing inside. This is the standard Australian convention — confirm with your supplier if in doubt.

Slat Width & Technical Deductions

The standard vertical blind slat width in Australia is 89mm. Some systems use 127mm (5 inch) slats — wider slats have a more modern appearance and are increasingly common on large glazing. Check which slat width your supplier offers for your chosen fabric.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Measuring the glass area instead of the frame — for outside mount, you need frame width as your baseline, then add overlaps. Glass-only measurements always result in under-coverage.
  • Not accounting for stacking clearance — ordering a 2400mm blind for a 2400mm opening with left-draw means ~430mm of slats blocking the door when open. Factor this in before you decide on draw direction.
  • Measuring only once — always measure three points for width and three for drop. Window frames are rarely perfectly square, especially in older homes.
  • Ordering without specifying draw direction — a blind ordered without a specified draw direction is often defaulted to split or left-draw. Confirm with your supplier.
  • Forgetting the recess depth for inside mount — the most common reason inside mount fails is insufficient recess depth. Measure it first.
  • Using centimetres instead of millimetres — a 10mm error is significant on vertical blinds. Always measure and order in mm.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I measure the whole sliding door frame or just the glass?
For outside mount vertical blinds on a sliding door, measure the full door frame width (edge to edge of the frame, not just the glass). Add at least 50mm on each side to ensure the blind covers the frame entirely and blocks light gaps. For total light block, add 75–100mm per side.
What is stacking clearance and why does it matter?
When vertical blinds are drawn open, the slats stack up on one or both sides of the opening. This stacked width is approximately 15–20% of the total blind width per side. If the blind stacks across the sliding door panel you use, you won't be able to open the door without battling through the slats. Plan stacking to the non-traffic side, or use split-draw for balanced stacking.
What's the minimum recess depth for inside mount vertical blinds?
The headrail for vertical blinds is typically 65–80mm deep. You need a minimum clear recess depth of 80mm for a standard single headrail — measured from the front face of the window reveal to the back wall or glass. If your recess is shallower than 80mm, outside mount is the better option.
How do I measure for vertical blinds on a corner window or bay window?
Corner and bay windows typically need separate blinds for each pane, joined at the corner with a specific 90° or angled bracket. Measure each opening individually — the corner bracket takes up 25–30mm on each side. Discuss the corner configuration with your supplier before ordering; some systems handle 90° and 135° angles differently.
Should I measure in millimetres or centimetres?
Always measure and order in millimetres (mm) in Australia. Centimetres introduce rounding errors that result in ill-fitting blinds. A 10mm gap on a vertical blind is very visible. Write measurements as W × H — for example 2150 × 2400 — and double-check before submitting.
Can vertical blinds be wider than 4 metres?
Most manufacturers have a maximum single-headrail width of 4000–5000mm. For wider openings, a joined or multi-section headrail can be used. The join point is typically positioned at a structural post or dividing mullion. Discuss large opening configurations with your supplier — stacking clearance and draw direction become critical for very wide setups.
