Complete Guide

S-Fold & Wave Fold Curtains

The modern curtain heading that's taken over Australian interiors — and for good reason. Here's everything you need before you order: how the fold works, which track to use, fabric selection, Melbourne pricing, and honest comparisons to pinch pleat and eyelet.

12 min read Updated March 2026 Curtains · S-Fold · Wave Fold · Melbourne

Walk into almost any new Melbourne home or apartment built in the last five years and there's a strong chance the curtains hang in long, even, sinuous waves. That's S-fold — also marketed as wave fold, ripple fold, and S-wave. The names are different; the mechanism is identical.

S-fold curtains aren't complicated, but there are specific requirements: a dedicated glider track (rods won't work), a precise fullness ratio, and fabric that has enough body to hold the wave. Get those right and you get a result that looks genuinely expensive. Get them wrong and you get curtains that sag between carriers and look nothing like the showroom sample.

This guide covers all of it — including the parts most suppliers gloss over.

How S-Fold Curtains Work

Traditional curtain headings — pinch pleat, eyelet, pencil pleat — create their shape at the top of the fabric. The heading gathers or folds the material, and the rest of the curtain hangs below.

S-fold is different. The shape is engineered by the track system, not the fabric heading. Here's how:

  • The glider track has carriers spaced at fixed intervals (typically 80–120mm apart, depending on the track brand and chosen fullness ratio).
  • The fabric is sewn with loops or hooks at the same spacing, so when hung, each hook attaches to a carrier.
  • The carriers alternate offset positions — slightly in front of the track, then slightly behind — forcing the fabric to curve in an S-shape between each point.
  • The result is a uniform, repeating wave that looks the same open or closed, at floor-to-ceiling height, every single time.

Key point: The "wave" is created by the track, not the curtain fabric. This means the same fabric can look completely different on an S-fold track vs a pinch pleat heading. If a supplier shows you a fabric sample but not the track system, you're not seeing the full picture.

S-Fold vs Wave Fold vs Ripple Fold — What's the Difference?

Practically nothing. They all describe the same heading style and track mechanism. The variation is in branding:

  • S-Fold — the most common generic trade name in Australia, describing the S-shape of each wave
  • Wave Fold — used by several Australian retailers and fabric houses; often associated with a slightly softer, wider wave
  • Ripple Fold — originally a registered US trademark (Hunter Douglas); now used loosely as a generic term by many suppliers
  • S-Wave — a specific track brand name; technically a brand, not a style
  • Silent Gliss Wave — premium European track system (Silent Gliss 6600 series), popular in high-end Melbourne projects

When quoting or ordering, the distinction that actually matters is the carrier spacing and fullness ratio specified by the track manufacturer — not the marketing name. Always confirm these specs with your supplier before ordering fabric.

Choosing the Right Track

The track is half the product. An S-fold curtain on a poor track will never hang properly. Here's what to look for:

Aluminium Glider Tracks

The standard for S-fold in Australian homes. Extruded aluminium channels accept plastic glider carriers that snap in and slide smoothly. Quality ranges considerably — cheap tracks flex, have uneven carrier spacing, and the gliders bind over time.

What to specify: Look for tracks with a rated load (at least 30kg/m), smooth pre-lubricated channel, and carriers with a positive locking snap (not friction fit). Brands like Forest, Integra, and Silent Gliss are well-regarded in the Melbourne trade.

Ceiling-Fix vs Face-Fix

  • Ceiling fix — track mounts directly to the ceiling (or a ceiling-height batten). Clean, minimal look. Requires solid fixing into joists or a blocking batten.
  • Face fix with brackets — brackets mount to the wall above the window frame. More flexible for installation but brackets are visible unless the curtain fully covers them when closed.
  • Recessed ceiling channel — track sits inside a plastered recess in the ceiling for a completely flush look. Requires planning during construction or renovation; not a retrofit option in most cases.

Motorised Tracks

S-fold curtains pair exceptionally well with motorisation — the smooth glider mechanism requires less motor torque than pinch pleat or eyelet heading, and the consistent wave pattern means motorised open/close looks polished. Somfy, Dooya, and Becker are common motor brands used in Melbourne. See our smart home & motorised curtains guide for integration options.

Pro tip: If you're planning any smart home automation now or in future, install a motorised-compatible track from day one even if you're not motorising immediately. Retrofitting a motor later on a track not designed for it is expensive and often impossible.

Fabric Selection

Not all fabrics suit S-fold. The even wave needs fabric with enough body to hold its shape between carriers. Too limp, and the fabric collapses between hooks. Too stiff, and it won't form a smooth curve — it'll crease or fold awkwardly.

🌿
Linen Blend Excellent body, natural texture, slight slub adds character. Most popular for living areas.
💧
Polyester Sheer Light, airy, holds wave well in heavier sheers. Great for layering with blockout roller behind.
🖤
Velvet Dramatic, excellent drape weight. S-fold accentuates the light/dark play in velvet pile.
🌾
Cotton Sateen Smooth face, medium weight, takes dye well. Good blockout performance when fully lined.
✨
Textured Weave Slub weaves, basket weaves, boucle — all work well. Texture adds depth to the wave shadow.
☀️
Blockout Lining S-fold blockout versions use an integrated blockout backing. Adds weight, which helps the wave.

What Fabrics to Avoid

  • Very lightweight voile or chiffon — too soft to hold between carriers unless the window is very sheltered from air movement
  • Stiff canvas or duck cotton — won't curve smoothly; better suited to flat-fold or Roman blind applications
  • Heavyweight interlined fabric — can work, but the added bulk means you need a higher-rated track and strong ceiling fixing

Fullness Ratio

Unlike pinch pleat (where you choose fullness), S-fold fullness is fixed by the track's carrier spacing. Most Australian S-fold tracks are designed for 2× to 2.5× fullness. Your fabric order is calculated from the track length and this ratio — not adjustable independently. When ordering, confirm the required fullness ratio with the track supplier before cutting any fabric.

The biggest practical advantage of S-fold over all pleated headings: stack-back. Pinch pleat and pencil pleat curtains take up considerable width on each side when open. S-fold stacks much tighter — roughly 10–15% of track length per side vs 25–35% for pinch pleat. On a sliding door or wide opening where you want maximum glass exposure when open, this matters significantly.

Which Rooms Suit S-Fold Best

Living Rooms & Open Plan Areas

This is where S-fold dominates. Floor-to-ceiling panels create height and drama. A sheer S-fold on a ceiling-fix track with a blockout roller blind recessed behind it is the most-specified combination in contemporary Melbourne new builds — full flexibility from open/bright to complete privacy without compromising the look of either product.

Bedrooms

Blockout S-fold curtains work very well in bedrooms. The even wave means light gaps are predictable and manageable — unlike eyelets, which often let in more light between rings. Pair with a complete blockout layer behind for total darkness.

Sliding Doors

S-fold's minimal stack-back makes it one of the best options for bifold and sliding door openings where you need maximum clear aperture. For very wide openings (>4m), a centre-split with two tracks stacked is worth considering. See our tricky windows guide for wide opening solutions.

Less Ideal Uses

  • Very short windows — the wave pattern needs height to read well; curtains shorter than 1.5m can look odd
  • DIY ceiling fix with no solid structure — S-fold tracks require reliable ceiling anchors; plasterboard alone isn't sufficient
  • Budget installs — cheap glider tracks undermine the whole look; if budget is tight, eyelet on a decent rod is often a better result

Measuring for S-Fold Curtains

S-fold curtains are measured differently from most other curtain heading styles. The key principle: you measure the track length, not the window width. The fabric calculation flows from the track.

  1. Decide track position — ceiling fix (most common for S-fold) or face fix above frame. Mark the start and end points of the track run.
  2. Measure track length — include any extension past the window frame for stack-back clearance. Allow 150–200mm per side beyond the frame edge as a minimum.
  3. Measure the drop — from the underside of the track to your chosen finish. Floor-level drops: subtract 10mm clearance. Sill length: measure to bottom of sill.
  4. Confirm fullness ratio — your track supplier specifies this (e.g. 2× or 2.25×). Fabric width = track length × fullness ratio. This is what you order in fabric.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between S-fold, wave fold, and ripple fold curtains?
They are all the same heading style — just different brand and trade names used by different suppliers. S-fold describes the S-shaped silhouette. Wave fold and ripple fold are alternative names. All three use the same glider track mechanism with evenly spaced carriers that force the fabric into consistent waves.
Can S-fold curtains be hung on a curtain rod?
No. S-fold requires a specific glider track with evenly spaced snap-in carriers. A standard curtain rod cannot produce the even wave pattern. If you need rod-hung curtains, eyelet heading is the standard choice.
How much do S-fold curtains cost in Melbourne?
Supply and install ranges from $180–$320 per panel for sheer fabrics, $280–$480 for blockout, and $400–$700 for premium linen or velvet. Track cost is additional: $80–$180 per linear metre for a quality aluminium glider track. Motorised track systems add $350–$650 per opening.
What fullness ratio do S-fold curtains use?
Typically 2× to 2.5×, meaning the total fabric width is 2 to 2.5 times the track length. The exact ratio is fixed by the carrier spacing on your specific track — it's not adjustable. Always confirm the required fullness with your track supplier before ordering fabric.
Are S-fold curtains suitable for Melbourne homes?
Yes — they're one of the most-specified curtain styles in Melbourne's contemporary new builds and renovations. For bedrooms and north-facing rooms, choose a blockout or interlined version for thermal performance. Sheer S-fold in a living area softens light beautifully. For Melbourne's mixed weather, layering a sheer S-fold with a blockout roller behind is the most flexible setup.
Can S-fold curtains be motorised?
Yes, and they're one of the best curtain styles for motorisation. The smooth glider track requires less motor torque than pleated headings. Specify a motorised-compatible track from the start — retrofitting later is difficult. Compatible with Somfy, Dooya, and Becker motor systems, and integrates with Google Home, Apple HomeKit, and Amazon Alexa via compatible hubs.
How do I clean S-fold curtains?
Most S-fold curtains, particularly lined and blockout versions, are dry-clean only. Sheer S-folds in polyester can often be machine washed on a delicate cold cycle — always check the fabric care label. Remove fabric from the carriers (leave the track in place), clean, then rehang. Avoid tumble drying as heat can distort the fabric and cause hook positions to shift.
