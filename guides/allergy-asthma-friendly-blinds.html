Best Blinds for Allergy & Asthma Sufferers
What Actually Collects Dust — and What Doesn't

Not all window coverings are equal when it comes to harboring dust, pollen and mites. Here's a straight comparison to help Melbourne allergy sufferers choose window treatments they won't regret.

The Short Answer: Dust Accumulation by Product

The more surface area, texture and fabric weave a window treatment has, the more dust it traps. Here's how the main product types compare:

Roller Blinds

✓ Best Choice

Flat, smooth surface. Minimal dust accumulation. Wipe clean with a damp cloth in under a minute.

Aluminium Venetians

✓ Good Choice

Slats collect dust but clean easily. Commit to a weekly wipe and they're very manageable.

PVC Plantation Shutters

~ Acceptable

More surface area than venetians but easy to wipe. PVC doesn't harbour mould; timber can if it gets damp.

Zebra / Dual Roller

~ Acceptable

Polyester fabric wipes down reasonably well. Avoid if you have severe allergies — fabric retains more than hard surfaces.

Honeycomb Blinds

✗ Use Caution

Fabric cells trap dust inside pleats. Difficult to clean thoroughly without professional help.

Heavy Fabric Curtains

✗ Worst Choice

Dust mites thrive in heavy fabrics. Floor-pooling styles are especially problematic. Require hot-wash every 2–3 months.

Quick Rule of Thumb

The allergy hierarchy is simple: hard, smooth surfaces > tight-weave fabrics > open-weave fabrics > heavily textured fabrics. Wipeability matters more than whether a product is labelled "hypoallergenic."

Detailed Breakdown by Product Type

1. Roller Blinds — The Clear Winner

For allergy and asthma sufferers, roller blinds are the best-performing window treatment. Here's why:

  • Minimal surface area — flat fabric on a roll; no slats, pleats or pockets
  • Easy cleaning — unroll fully, wipe with a damp microfibre cloth, let dry before rolling
  • Blockout fabrics offer the tightest weave, meaning dust sits on the surface rather than embedding in fibres
  • PVC-coated fabrics are even better — essentially wipeable like a hard surface

Avoid loosely woven sunscreen fabrics (e.g. 3% openness factor) — the open holes trap airborne particles. If you want light filtering, choose 5% openness or tighter.

2. Aluminium Venetian Blinds — Practical and Manageable

Aluminium venetian blinds have individual horizontal slats that do collect dust — but aluminium is non-porous, so dust sits on top rather than embedding. A microfibre duster sleeve (the kind that slips over the slats) takes 30 seconds per blind.

The challenge is thoroughness: you need to wipe both sides of the slats. If that feels unrealistic week-to-week, roller blinds are a better fit.

Avoid timber venetians if you're allergy-sensitive. Natural wood is porous and can harbour mould spores in humid rooms (kitchens, bathrooms).

3. Plantation Shutters — Durable, but More Surface Area

Plantation shutters have more surface area than venetians due to the wider louvres and frame. But the flat, painted PVC surface wipes clean easily. The key factors for allergy sufferers:

  • PVC shutters: resistant to moisture and mould — good choice
  • Timber shutters: can absorb moisture in humid rooms, potentially encouraging mould — use only in dry rooms
  • Cleaning frequency: fortnightly with a dry microfibre cloth keeps dust at bay

4. Honeycomb Blinds — The Trade-Off

Honeycomb (cellular) blinds are excellent for thermal insulation, but the fabric cell structure that makes them insulating also makes them dust traps. Dust settles inside the pleats and is nearly impossible to remove without professional cleaning.

If you need both insulation and allergy management, consider: roller blockout blind + thermal lined curtain (washed regularly), rather than honeycomb alone.

Important for Asthma Sufferers

Dust mites thrive in warm, humid environments and in fabric fibres. Melbourne's warm summers and variable humidity (especially in bayside suburbs) make this particularly relevant. Aim for window treatments you can clean on a regular, realistic schedule.

5. Curtains — Can Work, With the Right Fabric

Curtains aren't automatically bad for allergies — it depends on fabric choice and maintenance. Polyester and microfibre curtains are far less hospitable to dust mites than cotton, velvet or linen. For allergy sufferers who prefer curtains:

  • Choose tightly woven polyester or microfibre
  • Avoid floor-pooling styles — they accumulate far more dust
  • Hang curtains with enough clearance to vacuum beneath
  • Machine wash in hot water (60°C) every 2–3 months to kill dust mites
  • Vacuum with a HEPA attachment monthly between washes

Cleaning Schedule for Allergy Sufferers

The best blind choice is useless if you don't clean it. Here are realistic cleaning frequencies:

Roller Blinds (Monthly routine)

  1. Unroll the blind to its full length
  2. Wipe both faces with a damp, wrung-out microfibre cloth (no spray needed)
  3. For stubborn marks, a drop of mild dish soap diluted in water
  4. Let dry fully before rolling up — rolling damp causes mould

Aluminium Venetians (Weekly routine)

  1. Close the slats to horizontal position
  2. Run a microfibre duster sleeve along each slat from left to right
  3. Flip slats to the opposite angle, repeat on the reverse side
  4. Monthly: fully immerse in bathtub with warm soapy water, rinse, hang to dry

Plantation Shutters (Fortnightly routine)

  1. Open louvres to 45° angle
  2. Wipe each louvre with a dry microfibre cloth, top to bottom
  3. Wipe the frame and stiles
  4. In bathrooms or humid areas, quarterly wipe with diluted white vinegar to prevent mould on seals

Melbourne-Specific Considerations

Melbourne's climate creates specific challenges for allergy sufferers:

  • Pollen season (Oct–Feb): Keep windows closed on high-pollen days and use roller blockout or venetians with a tight seal to minimise pollen entry around edges
  • Humid summers: Avoid fabric window treatments in west-facing rooms that get afternoon heat — moisture + warmth + fabric = dust mites and mould
  • Port Phillip Bay influence (bayside suburbs): Salt air can accelerate corrosion on aluminium venetians — wipe down more frequently if you're in Brighton, Sandringham or St Kilda
  • Bushfire season ash: A fine particulate that coats every surface. Roller blinds and venetians are easiest to clean after a smoke event

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the best blinds for allergy sufferers?

Roller blinds on a smooth, wipeable PVC or blockout fabric are the best option for allergy sufferers. They have minimal surface area, no slats or pleats to trap dust, and can be wiped clean with a damp cloth. Aluminium venetian blinds are a good second choice if you commit to weekly wiping.

Are curtains bad for asthma?

Heavy fabric curtains can harbour dust mites and allergens, especially if they pool on the floor. If you prefer curtains, choose tightly woven fabrics (polyester or microfibre), keep them off the floor, and machine-wash every 2–3 months in hot water (60°C+) to kill dust mites.

Do plantation shutters collect dust?

Plantation shutters accumulate dust on the louvre surfaces, but they're easy to wipe clean with a dry microfibre cloth. PVC shutters are better than timber for allergy sufferers because they don't absorb moisture (which encourages mould and dust mite growth).

Are honeycomb blinds bad for allergies?

Honeycomb blinds have fabric cells that can trap dust inside the pleats — these are difficult to clean without professional equipment. They're not the best choice for severe allergy sufferers, though light-filtering options with tighter weaves perform better than open-weave fabrics.

How often should allergy sufferers clean their blinds?

For allergy sufferers, roller blinds should be wiped monthly; venetian slats weekly (or bi-weekly with a dust-trapping sleeve); plantation shutter louvres fortnightly; curtains vacuumed monthly and machine-washed every 2–3 months.
