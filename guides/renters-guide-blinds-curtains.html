With roughly one in three Melbourne households renting, blinds in rental properties is a genuinely fraught topic. The property might have nothing on the windows. The existing blinds might be broken and mouldy. Or you might want something better — and not know what you're allowed to do.

This guide cuts through the uncertainty. Whether you're a renter wanting decent window coverings or a landlord trying to stay compliant, here's what actually applies.

1. Landlord Obligations — What the Law Requires

Victorian Law (2025–26)

Under the updated Victorian Rental Minimum Standards, landlords must provide window coverings in every bedroom and living area that provide privacy and can reasonably block light. This is not optional — a property without adequate window coverings is non-compliant and cannot be let.

In practical terms, this means:

  • Bedrooms must have coverings that block enough light for sleep (roller blockout or equivalent)
  • Living areas must have privacy coverings (sheers, Holland blinds, or similar)
  • Bathrooms — required where a window faces a public area or neighbour's line of sight
  • Kitchens and laundries — generally not required

If your rental doesn't have adequate window coverings, you can raise this as an urgent repair (privacy and security grounds) or a minimum standards breach with Consumer Affairs Victoria.

Practical Tip

Before signing a lease, photograph every window. If coverings are absent or broken, ask the property manager to confirm in writing that they will be replaced before your move-in date. This avoids disputes later.

2. State-by-State Rules at a Glance

🏙️ Victoria
Minimum standards: landlords must provide window coverings for privacy and light blocking in all bedrooms and living areas. Updated 2025.
🏖️ Queensland
RTA requires privacy coverings in rooms likely used as bedrooms or living areas. Must be closeable and block light. Applies to new tenancies from 2023.
🌉 New South Wales
No statewide minimum standard for window coverings (as of 2026), but landlords have a general duty to provide a "reasonably fit" property. Lack of coverings in bedrooms may qualify as uninhabitable.
🌞 Western Australia
Minimum standards regulations include window furnishings — bedrooms must have coverings that provide privacy and block light. Effective July 2023.
🌿 South Australia
Window coverings required where the room faces a public area. Specific blockout requirement for bedrooms from 2024 amendments.
🏔️ ACT
Fit-for-habitation standards require adequate window coverings where privacy is expected. Most inspectors apply a bedroom-specific standard.
Note

State legislation changes frequently. Always confirm current requirements with your state's tenancy authority (Consumer Affairs Victoria, Fair Trading NSW, RTA Queensland). This guide reflects the law as understood in March 2026.

3. No-Drill Blinds: Options That Need Zero Permission

The key distinction in tenancy law is between fixtures (require written landlord permission) and non-permanent fittings (generally no permission needed). No-drill blinds fall into the second category because they leave no trace when removed.

Tension-Mounted Inside Recess Roller Blinds

These fit friction-tight into the window recess using spring tension — no screws, no adhesive. They look identical to drilled roller blinds. Width accuracy matters: order within 2–3mm of your recess width.

Best for: standard rectangular windows with a recess depth of at least 20mm. Available in sunscreen, light-filter, and blockout fabrics.

Pressure-Fit Cellular / Honeycomb Blinds

Cellular blinds with a tension-fit cassette are among the cleanest no-drill options. The honeycombed fabric also provides insulation — useful in Melbourne's variable climate. Inside mount only.

Tension Rod Curtains

Spring-loaded curtain rods press against the window frame. Pair with eyelet or ring-top curtains for a soft, layered look. Work best on windows up to about 120cm wide (wider spans can bow).

Adhesive Hook + Curtain Panel Systems

Heavy-duty 3M Command strips (rated for the weight of your curtain fabric) can support lightweight sheer panels. Not suitable for blockout curtains — the fabric weight will eventually pull the adhesive.

Measure First

For tension-mounted blinds to hold securely, measure the inside width of your window recess in three places (top, middle, bottom) and use the narrowest measurement. A blind that's even slightly too wide won't tension correctly.

4. How to Request Blind Installation (and Get a Yes)

Most landlords will approve a tenant installing blinds at their own expense — it adds value to the property. The trick is how you ask.

What to Put in Your Written Request

  • The specific rooms and window count
  • Product type and how they'll be mounted (brackets into the timber reveal, not into walls)
  • That you'll repair any holes to original condition on vacating (standard practice)
  • Whether you're offering to leave the blinds behind as an improvement (often helps get faster approval)
Victorian Tenants' Rights (2025)

Under Victorian law, landlords must respond to requests for minor modifications within 14 days. They can only refuse on reasonable grounds (e.g. heritage listing, structural issues). Installing standard blinds into window reveals is generally considered a minor modification that cannot be unreasonably refused.

If Your Landlord Refuses

If a landlord refuses a reasonable modification request, tenants can apply to VCAT (Victoria) or their state's tenancy tribunal for an order allowing the modification. In practice, most disputes around window coverings are resolved before reaching this stage.

6. Removing Blinds When You Move Out

If you drilled to install blinds, you have a general obligation to restore the property to its original condition (fair wear and tear excepted). In practice:

  • Remove brackets and fill screw holes with appropriate filler
  • Sand flush and touch up paint if required by the lease
  • Document the restored condition with photos before your final inspection
Leave-Behind Offer

Many landlords will agree to let you leave the blinds behind (especially if they're quality) — this saves you removal work and patches. Get it in writing before you install. The landlord gets an improvement, you get to skip the patch job.

Move-Out Blind Checklist

  • Photograph all windows before and after removal
  • Fill all screw holes with appropriate filler (PVA-based for plaster, timber filler for reveals)
  • Sand flush when dry, touch-up paint if wall colour matches
  • Clean blinds/curtains you're taking — mould or grease can be a bond deduction
  • Return any hardware supplied by the landlord
  • Confirm leave-behind agreement in writing (if applicable)

7. Frequently Asked Questions

My rental has broken blinds. Who is responsible for replacing them?

Broken blinds provided by the landlord are the landlord's responsibility to repair or replace. Lodge the request in writing via your property manager. In Victoria, if it affects privacy or security, it may qualify as an urgent repair requiring a response within 24 hours.

Can I install motorised blinds in a rental?

Motorised blinds that retrofit onto existing brackets (battery or plug-in motor units) generally only need permission if new brackets are required. Some tension-mount motorised roller options exist, but are expensive. Most renters opt for manual tension-mount and add motorisation only after getting written permission.

The landlord installed terrible blinds. Can I replace them?

Not without permission — even if their blinds are ugly. You'd need written consent to remove and store the existing blinds and install yours. Include a commitment to reinstall the originals on vacating. Most reasonable landlords will agree if you're upgrading to something better.

Do no-drill blinds actually hold properly?

Yes, for standard-sized windows. Tension-mount rollers hold very securely in a recess up to about 120cm wide. For wider windows, a centre support system or conventional mounting is safer. Pressure-fit honeycomb systems are rated for heavier fabric and slightly wider spans.

I'm a landlord — are plantation shutters worth installing in my rental?

For a medium-to-long-term investment property, yes. Shutters eliminate the replacement cycle of cheaper blinds, increase perceived value, and can justify a small rent premium. Our guide on whether plantation shutters are worth it covers the ROI in detail.
