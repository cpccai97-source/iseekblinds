Installation Guide

How to Install Sheer Curtains

Track vs rod, ceiling vs wall mount, and double track layering with blockouts — everything you need to hang sheers that look exactly right.

⏱ 30–45 minutes
🔧 Difficulty: Medium
🏠 S-fold, pencil pleat & eyelet
📍 Melbourne tips included

What You'll Need

Power drill + 6mm bit
Spirit level
Steel tape measure
Pencil
Stud finder
Step ladder
Screwdriver (Phillips)
Hacksaw (to trim track)
Wall plugs / toggle anchors
Fabric ties (for dressing)
Before You Begin
Choose Your Track Type
⚠️ Important S-fold and pencil pleat sheers require a curtain track — not a rod. The glider system is what allows the fabric to pull cleanly. Only eyelet-heading sheers work on a rod. If you're unsure what heading your sheers have, check the top of the panel before buying hardware.

Left: track with gliders for S-fold/pencil pleat sheers. Right: rod with eyelets — only suitable for eyelet-heading sheers.

Mount Position — Wall or Ceiling?

The higher the mount, the taller the room feels. Ceiling mounting is especially effective in standard 2.4m Melbourne homes where extra visual height makes a real difference.

Wall mount (left): 100–150mm above frame. Ceiling mount (right): maximum visual height — the preferred option for low-ceiling Melbourne homes.

Installation
Step-by-Step
1

Mark bracket positions

Mark the first bracket 80mm from each end of the track. Space remaining brackets every 500–600mm across the span. Use a spirit level to run a level pencil line across all bracket positions — an unlevel track makes every panel hang diagonal.

For a double track, mark both rows at the same time. The outer track (blockout) sits 80–100mm further from the wall than the inner track (sheer), so both panels can pass each other independently.

Bracket spacing: 80mm from each end, then every 500–600mm across the span.

2

Drill and fix brackets

Drill pilot holes at each mark. Wall type determines your fixing:

  • Brick/concrete — 6mm masonry bit, raw plugs + screws
  • Plasterboard hitting a stud — timber bit, 50mm timber screws direct
  • Plasterboard between studs — toggle anchors (butterfly or spring toggle)
  • Ceiling (concrete) — 6mm masonry bit with 50mm+ screws into plug

Sheer fabrics are light (typically 100–200g/m²), so fixing loads are minimal — but always use the right anchor for your wall type.

💡 Tip Check all brackets with a spirit level before you drill all holes. It's much easier to shift a pencil mark than to re-plug and re-drill.
3

Load gliders and clip track into brackets

Do this before you clip the track into its brackets. Slide all gliders into the track channel from one open end. You cannot add gliders after the end stops are fitted.

Count your gliders: roughly 1 glider per 80–100mm of finished panel width. For a 2400mm wide pair of S-fold sheers, you'll need approximately 24–30 gliders total (12–15 per panel).

Once gliders are loaded, clip the track into the wall brackets. Trim track length with a hacksaw if needed before loading gliders. Fit end stops at both ends.

⚠️ Common Mistake Fitting end stops before all gliders are loaded. End stops lock the channel — you'll have to remove them and start over.
4

Attach hooks and hang the panels

Attach curtain hooks into the sewn hook tape or pleat pockets on the top of each sheer panel. Hook spacing:

  • S-fold / wave — follow the manufacturer's hook tape spacing (typically every 80–100mm)
  • Pencil pleat — insert hook into every 3rd or 4th pocket depending on desired fullness
  • Eyelet (rod) — simply thread the rod through each eyelet ring

Hang each hook onto a glider. Work from the centre outward for S-fold panels so the wave pattern distributes evenly from the middle.

5

Dress and train the fabric

This step separates a professional-looking result from an amateur one. Sheer fabric has memory — you need to train it.

  1. Pull panels to the fully closed position
  2. Starting at the top, shape each fold or S-wave by hand, guiding the fabric into the correct wave direction
  3. Work downward to the hem, keeping folds consistent and parallel
  4. Loosely tie each panel in the closed position using fabric ties, cable ties, or strips of the fabric packaging — every 300–400mm from top to bottom
  5. Leave tied for 24–48 hours
💡 For maximum effect Lightly steam or mist the fabric while shaping — heat relaxes the fibres and the trained wave will hold significantly better after steaming.
6

Check operation and hem clearance

After training, release the ties. Test the panels across the full travel — they should glide smoothly with no hooks catching or panels bunching at the end stop.

Check the hem at multiple points across the window. Floors are rarely perfectly level: if the hem is floating at one end, adjust the hook at that point (remove and insert one pocket lower on pencil pleat, or add an extender hook for S-fold).

Standard clearances: 10–15mm above floor for practical use; 0–5mm for floor-grazing look; +100mm for puddle effect.

Layering
Double Track — Sheer + Blockout

Installing sheers alongside blockout curtains on the same window requires a double track system. The two tracks run parallel — sheer on the inner (glass-side) track, blockout on the outer track.

Cross-section: inner track (sheer) mounted 80–100mm closer to glass than outer track (blockout). Both run independently on separate glider sets.

💡 Tip When ordering a double track system, provide two separate track widths: the inner sheer track at your finished width, and the outer blockout track at the same width (or up to 40mm wider per side if you want maximum light block at the edges).
Troubleshooting
Common Problems & Fixes
Problem Cause Fix
S-fold waves look flat Fabric not trained, or hooks too far apart Re-dress and tie for 48 hrs; check hook spacing matches manufacturer spec (every 80mm, not 120mm)
Panels won't glide smoothly Track twisted or bracket misaligned Unclip from brackets and check track is straight; re-clip and check level across all brackets
Hooks popping off gliders Wrong hook type for track system Check hook dimensions match the glider slot; most tracks need a specific hook depth (1.5–2.5mm neck)
Hem not level across window Floor not level, or hooks inconsistently placed Measure drop at 3 points; adjust hooks at high end by inserting one pocket lower on pleat tape
Track sagging in middle Bracket spacing too wide (over 600mm) Add additional bracket at midpoint; for wide windows over 3m, use heavy-duty track and 400mm spacing
Sheers don't sit close to wall Bracket standoff too deep Choose a minimal-projection bracket (15–25mm from wall) for a flush look, especially for ceiling mount
Questions
Frequently Asked
Can I install sheer curtains on a rod instead of a track?
Only for eyelet-heading sheers. S-fold and pencil pleat sheers must be hung on a curtain track — the glider system is what creates that smooth, even wave. Hanging them on a rod will cause bunching and uneven folds. If you want the rod look, choose eyelet sheers specifically designed for rod installation.
How do I install sheers with blockout curtains on the same window?
Use a double track system. The inner track (closer to the glass) carries the sheer; the outer track carries the blockout. Both tracks mount from the same or adjacent bracket sets. The outer track sits 80–100mm further from the wall so both panels slide past each other independently.
How far apart should curtain track brackets be?
80mm from each end, then every 500–600mm across the span. On plasterboard without hitting a stud, use toggle anchors. Sheer fabrics are lightweight, so bracket loading is minimal — but tight spacing prevents track sag over wide windows (3m+).
How do I get S-fold sheers to wave evenly?
Train the fabric. After hanging, close the curtain fully, shape each S-wave by hand, and loosely tie the panels in position using fabric ties every 300–400mm. Leave for 24–48 hours. When released, the fabric remembers the wave. Rushing this step is the most common reason S-fold sheers look flat.
Do sheer curtains need ceiling mounting to look right?
Not necessarily, but ceiling mount (or 50–100mm below the ceiling) makes rooms feel taller and windows look larger — especially effective in 2.4m Melbourne homes. Wall mounting above the frame is also fine; just aim for at least 100mm above the frame so the track doesn't crowd the window opening.
