Before You Start

Plantation shutters are heavier and more precise than blinds or curtains. A successful install depends on preparation — rushing to drill without checking the basics is how frames end up crooked or panels that won't close. Run through this checklist first:

  • Confirm measurements match your order. Measure the delivered frame pieces against your original order confirmation. Manufacturing tolerances are tight (±1mm), but shipping damage can warp pieces. If anything is off, contact your supplier before you start drilling.
  • Check which frame type you ordered. Reveal fit (Z-frame), L-frame, or direct mount (frameless) — each has a different installation process. If you're not sure, check the paperwork or look at the frame cross-section (see frame types below).
  • Identify your wall type. Brick, plasterboard, timber frame, or aluminium — this determines your drill bit, plug type, and screw length. See the wall types section.
  • Check for obstructions. Open the window fully. Are there handles, winders, locks, or trickle vents that will interfere with the frame or panel swing? Note anything that protrudes more than 15mm into the recess.
  • Clear the area. Remove existing blinds, curtain tracks, and any hardware from the window. Fill old screw holes with filler and let it set before you start.
  • Read the manufacturer's instructions. This guide covers general principles — your specific shutter system may have variations (hinge type, frame connection method, catch style). Always defer to the manufacturer where they differ.

Frame Types

Your shutters will use one of three frame types. The frame type determines how the shutters attach to your window and how they'll look when finished. If you're unsure which you have, check the cross-section of the frame piece — the profile shape is distinctive for each type.

Reveal Fit

Z-Frame (Inside Recess)

The frame sits inside the window recess. A small Z-shaped lip overlaps the edge of the reveal, hiding any gap between frame and wall. The most common choice for standard windows with a recess depth of 60mm or more.

✓ Clean, flush appearance — nothing protrudes beyond the wall
✓ Maximises panel size within the opening
✓ Hides minor imperfections in the reveal edges
✗ Requires a reasonably square, deep recess (≥60mm)
✗ Won't work if handles or winders protrude into the recess
L-Frame

L-Frame (Outside Mount)

The frame mounts onto the wall face surrounding the window, with an L-shaped profile that covers the window frame edge. Best for shallow recesses, uneven reveals, or where you want the shutters to appear larger than the window.

✓ Works on any recess depth, including flat windows
✓ Covers uneven or damaged window frames
✓ Adds a visual border that frames the window
✗ Protrudes from the wall by 30–40mm
✗ Requires clear wall space around the opening
Direct Mount

Direct Mount (Frameless)

No separate frame — hinges screw directly into the window reveal or architrave. The simplest, most minimal option, but requires a perfectly square recess with a solid, flat reveal surface.

✓ No frame to assemble — fastest installation
✓ Most minimal appearance
✓ Lowest cost (no frame pieces needed)
✗ Requires a perfectly square, solid reveal
✗ No margin for error — gaps are visible
✗ Not suitable for plasterboard or soft reveals

Wall Types

Shutter frames are heavier than blind brackets — the fixing needs to hold the weight of the frame plus fully-loaded panels (a standard 1200×1500mm two-panel set weighs 8–12kg). Use the right fixing for your wall type:

🧱

Brick / Masonry

6mm masonry bit, 50mm depth, hammer drill mode. Use nylon wall plugs. Drill through the frame's pre-drilled holes into the brick — avoid mortar joints where possible as they're softer and plugs can work loose over time.

Hammer Drill
📋

Plasterboard (into stud)

3mm pilot bit, 50mm depth, no hammer. Use a stud finder to locate timber studs behind the plasterboard. Always fix into studs — shutter frames are too heavy for hollow-wall fixings alone. If no stud is available, use a timber batten behind the plasterboard.

Stud Required
🪵

Timber Frame

2.5mm pilot bit, 40mm depth, no hammer. Screw directly — no wall plugs needed. Pre-drill to prevent splitting, especially near edges. Hardwood frames may need a slightly larger pilot hole (3mm).

Direct Screw
🔩

Aluminium Window Frame

4mm metal bit, slow speed, no hammer. Use self-tapping metal screws. Don't over-tighten — aluminium strips easily. Apply masking tape over the drill point to prevent the bit skating across the surface.

Metal Bit
🔲

Tile / Stone

Use a tile bit at low speed first to penetrate the glaze, then switch to masonry bit for the substrate behind. Tape over drill points to prevent cracking. For thick stone reveals, use long wall plugs (60mm+).

Tile + Masonry Bit
💡 Melbourne Homes

Most pre-1960s Melbourne homes have double-brick reveals — you'll be drilling into solid brick. Newer homes (1990s+) typically have plasterboard on timber stud, even around windows. If you're unsure, tap the reveal with your knuckle — solid brick sounds dead, plasterboard sounds hollow.

Installation Steps

Work through these eight steps in order. The total time is roughly 45–60 minutes for a standard two-panel hinged shutter. Allow extra time for your first window while you learn the process.

1

Unbox & inspect all panels and frame pieces

Lay everything out on a clean, flat surface — a carpeted floor or a drop sheet on tiles works well. Identify each piece: top frame rail, bottom frame rail, two side frame stiles, shutter panels (each should be labelled L or R), hinges (usually pre-fitted), screws, wall plugs, filler strips, and magnetic catches.

Inspect every piece for transit damage: dents in the frame, cracked louvers, chipped paint, bent hinge pins. Check louvers tilt freely on each panel — gently move the tilt rod up and down. Any defects should be reported to your supplier before you proceed.

💡 Label Check

Multi-window orders are labelled with window reference numbers (e.g., "W1-L", "W1-R"). Match these to your order sheet and install the correct set in each window. Panels are not interchangeable between windows — even 2mm difference matters.

⏱ 3 min
2

Dry-fit the frame — assemble on the floor

Connect the four frame pieces together on the floor without screws. Most frames use corner keys (loose-fitting tongue and groove joints) or mitred corners with alignment pins. Push the pieces together and check:

  • All four corners meet cleanly with no gaps
  • The overall frame dimensions match your window opening (for reveal fit: the frame should be slightly smaller than the recess to allow shimming)
  • No pieces are bowed or warped — lay a straight edge along each rail
  • Measure both diagonals — they should be equal (within 2mm). If they're not, the frame is racking and won't sit square
⏱ 5 min
3

Install the frame into the recess or onto the wall

Reveal fit (Z-frame): Lift the assembled frame into the window recess. The Z-lip should overlap the front edge of the reveal. Press the frame gently into position — it should sit with a small gap (2–3mm) around all four sides for shimming. Use masking tape across the corners to hold it temporarily while you work.

L-frame (outside mount): Hold the frame against the wall surrounding the window. The L-lip should reach into the recess, covering the edge of the window frame. Position it so the overlap is even on all four sides. Use masking tape or a helper to hold it while you mark the screw positions through the pre-drilled holes.

Direct mount: Skip frame installation — you'll be hinging panels directly into the reveal in Step 7. For now, mark the hinge positions on the reveal face using the hinge hardware as a template.

⚠️ Heavy Frames

Frames for windows over 1500mm are heavy and unwieldy. Get a second person to hold the frame in position while you mark and drill. Trying to hold a large frame with one hand while drilling with the other is a recipe for crooked holes.

⏱ 15 min
4

Check frame is square and level

This is the most critical step. A frame that's even slightly out of square will cause panels that won't close properly, gaps on one side, and louvers that look crooked from across the room.

  • Spirit level: Check all four sides — top, bottom, left, right. Every one must read level (horizontal rails) or plumb (vertical stiles)
  • Diagonal check: Measure corner to corner in both directions. Equal diagonals = square frame. If they differ by more than 2mm, adjust with shims
  • Shims: Insert plastic or timber shims between the frame and the wall/reveal wherever there's a gap or where you need to push the frame into alignment. Shims are your friend — most window openings are not perfectly square
💡 Older Melbourne Homes

Victorian and Edwardian homes in Melbourne's inner suburbs often have window reveals that are 5–10mm out of square. This is normal — it's exactly why shims exist. Don't try to force the frame to match an out-of-square reveal.

⏱ 5 min
5

Secure frame permanently

With the frame checked square and shimmed, it's time to fix it permanently. Work through the pre-drilled holes in the frame:

  • Fix the top rail first — one screw at each end, leaving them slightly loose
  • Fix the bottom rail — again, one screw at each end
  • Re-check square before tightening. Adjust shims if needed
  • Fix the side stiles — typically 2–3 screws per side depending on frame height
  • Tighten all screws firmly but don't over-torque. Over-tightening can bow the frame inward — use hand pressure on the last quarter-turn, not the drill's full torque

For masonry walls: drill through the frame hole into the wall, insert a wall plug, then drive the screw. For timber: drill a pilot hole and screw directly.

⏱ 10 min
6

Fill screw holes and gaps

Most shutter frames come with colour-matched filler strips that press into the screw recesses. If your frame uses countersunk screws without recesses, use wood putty in the matching colour — apply, let it set for 30 minutes, then sand flush with fine-grit sandpaper (240 grit).

For gaps between the frame and wall (common with reveal fit), use colour-matched silicone caulk or the supplied filler strips. Run a clean bead of caulk and smooth with a damp finger. Wipe excess immediately — dried silicone is difficult to remove cleanly.

If the frame is painted and you've scratched it during installation, use the supplied touch-up paint. Apply with a small artist's brush — don't try to brush large areas or you'll get visible brush marks.

⏱ 5 min
7

Hang the shutter panels

This is the satisfying part. Most modern plantation shutters use a pin-and-cup hinge system — the pin is pre-fitted to the panel stile, and the cup is pre-fitted to the frame. All you need to do is align and drop the pins into the cups.

  • Start with the bottom hinge — tilt the panel at an angle, engage the bottom pin into its cup, then swing the panel upright and drop the top pin into its cup
  • For tall panels with three hinges, engage the bottom first, then middle, then top
  • Once engaged, the panel should swing freely. Test by opening and closing — it should move smoothly without binding

If hinges are stiff or don't align, check that the frame is perfectly square (Step 4). Even 1mm of frame distortion can misalign hinges.

⏱ 10 min
8

Adjust louver tension and fit magnetic catches

Louver tension: Each panel has a tension adjustment — usually a small grub screw on the tilt rod mechanism or inside the stile. The louvers should stay at any angle you set them, without drifting. If they droop under their own weight, tighten the tension screw a quarter-turn at a time until they hold position.

Magnetic catches: Where two panels meet in the centre (or where a single panel meets the frame), fix the supplied magnetic catches. One half screws into the frame, the other into the panel stile. Align them so the panels pull closed with a satisfying click. The catch should be strong enough to hold the panel closed but easy enough to pull open with one finger.

Final check: Open and close every panel. Tilt all louvers fully open and fully closed. Check that nothing binds, scrapes, or catches. Stand back and eyeball the alignment — all panels should sit at the same height and the louvers should line up horizontally across the window.

⏱ 5 min

Bi-Fold & Sliding Shutters

Standard hinged shutters open like a door — each panel swings out from the frame. Bi-fold and sliding configurations give you different options for wider openings or where swing clearance is limited.

Bi-Fold Shutters

Bi-fold shutters use pairs of panels connected by hinges. When opened, the panels fold flat against each other like a concertina. They're popular for wide openings (servery windows, room dividers) where standard hinged panels would protrude too far into the room.

  • Track installation: Bi-fold systems run on a top track that screws into the top rail of the frame (or directly into the lintel for trackless frames). The track carries a sliding pivot that the lead panel rides on. Fix the track dead level — any sag will cause the panels to drift open or closed.
  • Panel connection: Connect panel pairs with the bi-fold hinges before hanging. Lay them flat, engage the bi-fold hinges, then hang the assembly into the top track and bottom guide.
  • Bottom guide: A small guide pin or channel at the bottom prevents the panels from swinging. This screws into the bottom rail or the sill. Align it directly below the top track pivot point.

Sliding Shutters

Sliding shutters run on a horizontal track — panels slide left or right to open, rather than swinging. They're ideal for stacking doors, very wide windows, or anywhere you don't want panels protruding when open.

  • Top track: A heavy-duty aluminium track screws into the lintel or top frame rail. Use at least 4 fixings for tracks over 2m. The track has one or two channels depending on whether panels bypass (overlap when open) or stack to one side.
  • Bottom guide rail: A shallow guide rail on the sill keeps panels aligned. For timber sills, screw directly. For tile sills, use adhesive or countersunk screws with masonry plugs.
  • Rollers: Each panel has top-mounted rollers that ride in the track. Adjust the roller height screws to ensure the panel hangs plumb and clears the bottom rail by 2–3mm.
⚠️ Professional Installation Recommended

Bi-fold and sliding shutter systems are significantly more complex than standard hinged panels. Misaligned tracks cause binding, derailing, and premature wear. If you're not confident with precision fixing, we recommend booking professional installation for these configurations.

Special Situations

Bay Windows

Bay window shutters use multiple frame sections joined at angles. Each flat face of the bay gets its own frame and panels — the frames are connected with angled mullion posts or corner connectors.

  • Angle connectors: Bay kits include 90°, 120°, or 135° connectors depending on your bay geometry. These bolt between adjacent frame sections. Get the angle right before drilling — test-fit all sections on the floor first.
  • Installation order: Start with the centre section of the bay, then work outward to the angled side sections. This ensures the angular connections line up.
  • Measure each face independently. Bay windows are notorious for having different-sized openings on each face. Each section must be measured and ordered separately.

Arched & Shaped Windows

Arched tops, circular windows, and other shaped openings require custom-built panels that follow the curve. These are always factory-made to match a template of the actual opening.

  • Fixed arch section: Most arched shutters have a fixed (non-opening) arch section at the top with radiating louvers, and standard hinged panels below. The arch section is screwed directly into the frame.
  • Template first: The manufacturer will have made a template of your arch during the measure visit. The finished product should match this template — hold it up and check before drilling.

French Doors

Shutters on French doors are hinged to a narrow frame that's fixed to the door itself — the shutters open and close with the door. Key considerations:

  • Weight: Adding shutters to a door increases its weight. Check that the door hinges can handle the extra load — upgrade to heavy-duty hinges if the door starts to sag.
  • Clearance: The shutter frame adds depth. Make sure the door still closes into the door frame without the shutter frame catching. Some systems use a very slim frame (12mm) specifically for French door applications.
  • Handle cutout: Panels will need a cutout around the door handle. This is factory-made — the louver layout is adjusted to avoid the handle zone.

Troubleshooting

Most plantation shutter problems trace back to frame alignment or hinge adjustment. Work through these before calling for a service visit:

Frame isn't square — diagonals don't match
Loosen all screws slightly (don't remove them). Insert shims on the long-diagonal side to push the frame back into square. Re-measure the diagonals after each adjustment. When they match within 2mm, re-tighten. If the reveal itself is severely out of square (10mm+), the frame should have been ordered with a compensation allowance — contact your supplier.
Panels don't close flush — gap at top or bottom
This is almost always a frame squareness issue, not a panel issue. Check frame diagonals. If the frame is square but panels still gap, check the hinge lateral adjustment screw — most hinges have a small Allen-key screw that moves the panel in or out by 1–2mm. Adjust both hinges on the affected panel in the same direction.
Louvers won't stay in position — they drift or droop
The louver tension is too loose. Find the tension adjustment — it's either a grub screw on the tilt rod staple (inside the stile, accessible from the edge) or a friction pad on the pivot pin. Tighten the grub screw a quarter-turn clockwise. Test. Repeat until the louvers hold at any angle. Don't over-tighten or the tilt rod will be too stiff to operate.
Hinge is too stiff or too loose
Most shutter hinges have a tension screw (see diagram above). Clockwise = tighter, anti-clockwise = looser. Adjust in quarter-turns. The panel should swing with light finger pressure but stay open at any angle without drifting. If the hinge is stiff even when fully loosened, check that the hinge pin isn't bent — this can happen from forcing a misaligned panel during installation.
Gap between panels when closed
Check that the magnetic catches are properly aligned — one half on the frame, one on the panel stile, both at the same height. If catches are aligned but there's still a gap, the panels may need lateral hinge adjustment to bring them closer together. Adjust both panels inward by 0.5–1mm each. A thin foam weatherstrip on the meeting stiles can also close a persistent small gap.
Frame pulling away from the wall
The fixings aren't adequate for the wall type. For plasterboard, you must be into a stud — toggle anchors alone won't hold a shutter frame long-term. For masonry, the wall plugs may be in mortar joints instead of brick. Remove the affected screws, reassess the wall type, re-drill into solid material, and use appropriate fixings. Fill the old holes with filler before re-drilling nearby.
Panels sag over time — bottom edge drops
The hinges are bearing the full panel weight. This is more common on tall panels (1800mm+) or heavier timber shutters. Check that all hinge screws are tight — both on the panel side and the frame side. If screws have pulled out, fill the holes with timber dowels and PVA glue, let them set overnight, then re-drill and re-screw. For persistent sagging, add a third hinge midway between the existing two.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install plantation shutters myself?
Yes — most standard hinged plantation shutters are a DIY-friendly project if you're comfortable with a drill and spirit level. A second person makes it much easier for holding the frame in position. Bi-fold and sliding configurations are more complex and benefit from professional installation.
What is the difference between reveal fit and L-frame shutters?
Reveal fit (Z-frame) sits inside the window recess with a thin lip overlapping the edge. L-frame mounts onto the wall surrounding the window and covers the entire frame. Reveal fit gives a cleaner, flush look but requires a square, deep recess (≥60mm). L-frame is more forgiving of uneven openings and shallow recesses.
How long does it take to install plantation shutters?
Allow about 45–60 minutes per window for a standard two-panel hinged shutter. The first window takes the longest while you learn the process — subsequent windows typically take 30–45 minutes each. Bay windows and bi-fold configurations take longer.
Do plantation shutters need a frame?
Not always. Direct mount (frameless) installation is possible if your window recess is perfectly square and the reveal face is smooth enough to accept hinge screws. However, a frame gives a much cleaner finish, hides uneven reveals, and provides a solid mounting surface for the hinges. We recommend a frame for most installations.
How do I fix shutters that won't close flush?
This is usually caused by a frame that isn't perfectly square, or by hinge adjustment. First check your frame diagonals — if they're unequal, the frame is racking and needs shims. If the frame is square, adjust the hinge screws: most shutters have a lateral adjustment screw that moves the panel left or right by 1–2mm.
Can plantation shutters be installed on bay windows?
Yes. Bay window shutters use angled frame connectors (typically 90°, 120°, or 135° depending on the bay geometry) to join frame sections at the corners. Each flat section of the bay gets its own frame and panels. The angled connectors are supplied as part of the bay window shutter kit. We recommend professional installation for bays.
