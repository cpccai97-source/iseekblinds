Before You Start

Curtain installation is straightforward — but getting the preparation right makes the difference between curtains that glide effortlessly and ones that stick, sag, or look wrong. Run through this checklist before reaching for the drill.

  • Confirm your measurements — check that the track or rod length matches your window width (plus any stack-back extension). If you haven't measured yet, see our curtain measurement guide first.
  • Identify your wall or ceiling type — rendered brick, plasterboard, timber frame, and concrete all need different fixings. Curtain tracks carry considerable weight when fabric is hung, so correct fixings are critical.
  • Check for obstructions — window handles that protrude, air vents above the frame, downlights near the ceiling, and existing pelmets can all affect bracket placement. Check the swing path of your curtain before fixing anything.
  • Count your parts — unbox everything and lay it out. Check brackets, gliders, hooks, end stops, screws, and plugs against the packing list.
  • Have a second person available for tracks over 2m or ceiling installations where you need to hold the track while fixing brackets.
⚠️ Important

Never drill into areas with hidden pipes or electrical cables. Use a stud finder and cable detector before drilling, particularly for ceiling mounts. Melbourne homes with ducted heating often have vents positioned near ceiling height — check clearance before marking bracket positions.

Parts

Parts supplied with your curtain track or rod should include:

  • Curtain track or rod (cut to length, or to be cut on site)
  • Brackets — ceiling-fix or wall-fix depending on your order
  • Gliders (for tracks) or rings (for rods)
  • End stops (2×)
  • Screws and wall plugs
  • Finials (for rods only)
  • Overlap arm / master carrier (for tracks with centre-opening curtains)
💡 Tip

Always install gliders into the track before fitting the end stops. Once end stops are in place, you can't add more gliders without removing them. Count your hooks/gliders against the number of hook points on your curtain header tape.

Track vs Rod

The first decision is whether you need a curtain track or a curtain rod. This depends on your curtain heading style and the look you want.

🔧 Curtain Track

For Pinch Pleat & S-fold

Aluminium or PVC channel that conceals the hardware. Gliders run inside the track for smooth, quiet operation. Can be ceiling or wall mounted. Ideal for a clean, modern look.

✓ Smooth, silent glide
✓ Ceiling or wall mount
✓ Can be bent for bay windows
✓ Hidden hardware — minimal visual profile
✗ Less decorative than rods
✗ May need cutting to size
✦ Curtain Rod

For Eyelet & Tab Top

Decorative pole in timber, metal, or acrylic. Curtains thread directly onto the rod via eyelets or fabric loops. Wall mount only. Makes a style statement.

✓ Decorative — a design feature
✓ Easy to hang eyelets and tab tops
✓ Simple bracket installation
✗ Wall mount only — can't ceiling fix
✗ Can sag on wide spans without centre support
✗ Not suitable for bay windows

Ceiling Mount Track

Ceiling-mounted tracks give the cleanest look — the hardware virtually disappears, and floor-to-ceiling curtains make any room feel taller. This is our most popular installation method for new builds and renovations.

Diagram

Ceiling Mount Bracket Spacing

1

Mark bracket positions

Measure and mark the first bracket 100mm from each end of where the track will sit. Then space remaining brackets evenly at 500–600mm intervals. Use a chalk line or long spirit level to ensure all marks are in a straight line.

2

Check for joists

Use a stud finder to locate ceiling joists. Joists are typically 450mm apart. Try to position at least some brackets into joists for maximum holding power. For brackets between joists, use ceiling-rated toggle anchors.

3

Drill and plug

Drill pilot holes at each mark. For timber joists, drill directly. For plasterboard between joists, install toggle anchors rated for the total curtain weight (fabric + track can easily exceed 10kg for a wide window).

4

Fix brackets and click track in

Screw all brackets firmly to the ceiling. Most ceiling track brackets have a clip mechanism — slide or click the track into place. Check the track is straight and can't be pulled out of the clips.

5

Cut track if needed

If your track is longer than needed, mark the cut point and use a hacksaw with a fine-tooth blade. File any burrs smooth so gliders can pass freely. Always cut from the same end — never from the centre.

6

Fit end stops and attach gliders

Slide all your gliders into the track channel. Then press-fit or screw the end stops into each end of the track. Test that gliders slide freely from end to end with no sticking points.

Wall Mount Track

Wall-mounted tracks sit on brackets fixed to the wall above the window frame. This is the easier option if you can't access ceiling joists or prefer the track to be visible (some decorative tracks are designed for wall mount).

Diagram

Wall Mount Height Positioning

1

Mark height above frame

Measure 100–150mm above the window frame and mark a level line. This height gives curtains enough room to hang without catching on the frame top, while keeping the track close enough to look proportional.

2

Space brackets along the line

Mark the first bracket 100mm from each end of the track. Space remaining brackets every 500–600mm. Use the spirit level to verify every mark sits on the same horizontal line.

3

Drill, plug, and fix

Drill at each mark, insert wall plugs (masonry) or screw into studs (plasterboard). Drive the bracket screws firmly. Check brackets are level before clipping the track in.

4

Clip track and fit end stops

Click or slide the track into the bracket clips. Add gliders, then press-fit end stops at each end. Test that the track is secure and gliders run smoothly across the full length.

Installing Curtain Rods

Curtain rods are the classic choice for eyelet and tab top curtains. The rod itself is a visible design element, so bracket placement matters for both function and aesthetics.

1

Mark bracket positions

Mark brackets 150–200mm beyond the window frame on each side. This extension is critical — it creates stack-back space so curtains clear the glass when open, making the window appear wider. Mark the height at 100–150mm above the frame.

2

Check level and fix brackets

Use a spirit level to confirm both brackets are at the same height. Drill, plug, and screw the brackets to the wall. Tug firmly to confirm they're secure — rods carry significant cantilever load at the ends.

3

Thread rod and place finials

Thread the rod through your curtain eyelets or rings before placing it in the brackets. Rest the rod in the brackets and secure with grub screws if provided. Screw finials onto each end of the rod.

4

Add centre support (if needed)

For rod spans over 1500mm, install a centre support bracket to prevent sagging. For spans over 2400mm, use two centre supports spaced evenly.

💡 Tip

For eyelet curtains, make sure you thread the rod through the eyelets before placing the rod in the brackets. You can't add eyelets after the rod is up — unlike track gliders which slide in from the end.

Bay Window Tracks

Bay windows are one of the trickiest curtain installations — but with a flexible track, the result is stunning. Melbourne's heritage homes are full of beautiful bay windows that deserve proper curtain treatment.

Diagram

Bay Window Track Bending — 90° and 135° Angles

Bending Flexible Tracks

  • Flexible aluminium tracks can be bent by hand to follow bay angles. Bend slowly and evenly — don't kink the track.
  • For tight 90° bends, lightly score the back of the track with a hacksaw to help it bend without crimping. Three or four light cuts across the back face at the bend point.
  • For 135° angles (the most common bay window angle in Melbourne homes), a gradual hand bend is usually enough.
  • Add extra brackets within 100mm either side of each bend to prevent the track lifting under fabric weight at the corners.

Hanging Styles

How you hang your curtains depends on the heading style. Each type connects to the track or rod differently.

Diagram

Four Hanging Styles Compared

Track System

Pinch Pleat

Insert pin hooks into the pockets on the header tape — typically every 80–100mm. Clip the hook ends into the track gliders. Pinch pleat gives a formal, tailored look with deep, structured folds. Best for living rooms and bedrooms.

Track System

S-fold (Wave / Ripple)

S-fold uses evenly spaced hooks at 60mm or 80mm intervals (matching the glider spacing on the track). The fabric forms a continuous, gentle wave. The result is contemporary and relaxed — our most popular style for modern Melbourne homes.

Rod System

Eyelet (Grommet)

Thread the curtain rod directly through the metal-ringed eyelets in the fabric. Creates uniform, deep folds. The rod is visible between each eyelet — choose a rod that complements your room. Can't be used with tracks.

Rod System

Tab Top

Fabric loops (tabs) sewn into the header wrap over the curtain rod. Casual, relaxed aesthetic — popular for kids' rooms and informal spaces. Tabs can make the curtain harder to slide, so best for curtains you don't open/close frequently.

Stack-back & Clearance

Stack-back is the space your curtains occupy when fully open. Getting this right means your curtains won't block light or cover the glass when drawn back.

The One-Third Rule

A good rule of thumb: curtains stack back to roughly one-third of the window width on each side. So for a 1800mm wide window, expect about 300mm of stack-back per side. This means your track or rod needs to extend at least 300mm beyond the window frame on each side to keep the glass fully clear.

  • Heavier fabrics (blockout, velvet, lined) stack thicker — allow more space.
  • Sheers and lightweight fabrics stack very thin — you can get away with less extension.
  • S-fold curtains stack slightly more than pinch pleat due to the continuous wave pattern.
  • Eyelet curtains stack moderately — the deep folds compress reasonably well.
💡 Tip

If wall space is limited on one or both sides of the window, consider a one-way draw curtain (all fabric stacks to one side) rather than a centre-opening pair. This gives you maximum glass exposure from the clear side.

Clearance from Wall

Curtain tracks should sit at least 50mm from the wall to allow fabric to hang freely without catching on window handles, sills, or architraves. Most ceiling-mount brackets provide 50–80mm projection. Wall-mount brackets come in various projection depths — choose one that clears any obstructions beneath the track.

Child Safety

Curtain cords and tie-backs can pose a strangulation risk to young children. Australian Standard AS/NZS 13120 requires all internal window coverings to meet strict cord safety requirements.

  • Avoid exposed cords — choose cord-free curtain tracks where possible. Modern track systems operate by hand without any cord mechanism.
  • Motorised tracks eliminate cords entirely and can be operated by remote or smart home integration. This is the safest option for homes with young children. See our motorised curtain options →
  • Tie-backs should be fixed at a height of at least 1600mm from the floor — well out of reach of young children. Avoid long, looped tie-backs that could form a noose. Use fixed holdback hooks instead of fabric tie-backs where possible.
  • Cord cleats — if your curtain system has any pull cords (for traverse rods), install a cord cleat at 1600mm height and wrap the cord securely.
⚠️ Child Safety Warning

No cord or chain should form a loop longer than 150mm at any height accessible to children. For comprehensive guidance, see our child safety guide for window furnishings.

Troubleshooting

Most curtain installation problems have straightforward fixes. Work through these before calling for a service visit:

Curtains don't glide smoothly along the track
Check for burrs or rough spots inside the track channel — sand with fine sandpaper if needed. Ensure gliders are the correct type for your track profile. Apply a tiny amount of silicone spray lubricant inside the track channel. Check that the track isn't warped or bent at any bracket point — a bracket screwed too tight can pinch the track and create a sticking point.
Bracket pulling away from ceiling
The fixing isn't suited to your ceiling type. If you've screwed into plasterboard between joists, the weight of the curtain can pull standard screws straight out. Remove the bracket, install a ceiling-rated toggle anchor (also called a butterfly anchor or spring toggle), and re-fix. For heavy curtains, always try to relocate brackets to hit a joist — the difference in holding power is enormous.
Curtains won't close fully — gap in the middle
Check that your overlap arm or master carriers (the special gliders at the centre of the track) are meeting properly. If the curtains are too narrow for the track, the fabric simply can't reach the centre. Confirm your curtain width — each curtain panel should be at least 1.5× the width of the half-track it covers (2× for generous fullness). If the track is longer than needed, remove it and cut to size.
Uneven drape — one side hangs longer than the other
First check that the track or rod is perfectly level — even a few millimetres of tilt will show in the hem. If the track is level but the curtain still hangs unevenly, the hook heights within the curtain header may be inconsistent. Re-pin the hooks at the shorter side, lowering them by the amount of the discrepancy. Steam or press the hem fold if it's uneven.
Curtain rod sagging in the middle
The rod span is too wide for the rod diameter and curtain weight. Install a centre support bracket — this is the most common fix and should be standard for any rod over 1500mm. If a centre bracket still leaves sag, either upgrade to a thicker rod (28–35mm diameter) or switch to a track system, which distributes weight across multiple brackets and doesn't rely on the structural rigidity of a single pole.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install a curtain track on the ceiling?
Yes — ceiling mount is one of the most popular options for a modern, streamlined look. It maximises drop length, hides the track hardware, and works brilliantly for floor-to-ceiling curtains. Locate ceiling joists with a stud finder, or use rated ceiling toggle anchors between joists.
How far apart should curtain track brackets be?
Place the first bracket 100mm from each end of the track, then space the remaining brackets every 500–600mm. For heavier curtains (lined, blockout, or pinch pleat), stay closer to 500mm. Lightweight sheers can use 600mm spacing.
Can I bend a curtain track for a bay window?
Yes — flexible aluminium or PVC tracks can be hand-bent to follow bay window angles. For tight 90° bends, lightly score the back of the track with a hacksaw to prevent kinking. Pre-bent bay tracks are also available from us in standard configurations.
Do I need a stud finder for curtain installation?
Highly recommended, especially for heavy curtains and ceiling mounts. Curtain tracks and rods carry significant weight when fabric is hung. Fixing into a stud or joist provides far more holding power than plasterboard alone. For ceiling mounts, a stud finder is essential.
How do I stop my curtain rod from sagging?
Add a centre support bracket for any rod span over 1500mm. For very wide windows (over 2.4m), use two centre supports spaced evenly. Choose a rod with a larger diameter (28–35mm) for heavy curtains. If sagging persists, switch to a track system which distributes weight across multiple brackets.
Can I install curtains without drilling?
Tension rods (spring-loaded) work for lightweight curtains in narrow openings under 1.5m. They press against the inside of the window reveal without fixings. However, they're not suitable for heavy curtains, wide spans, or ceiling-mount tracks. For a secure, long-term installation, drilling is always recommended.
