Operation Types

Honeycomb blinds come in three main operation styles. Understanding which one you have determines how you'll test it, mount it, and what child-safety measures apply.

🤚

Cordless

Push the bottom rail up to raise, pull down to lower. The internal spring mechanism holds the blind at any height. No cords, no chains — the simplest and safest system.

Safest for kids
🔗

Corded (Loop)

A continuous cord loop hangs from one side of the headrail. Pull one side to raise, the other to lower. Requires a cord cleat fixed to the wall for child safety compliance.

Most common
↕️

Top-Down Bottom-Up

Two independent rails — lower from the top for privacy while letting light in above, or raise from the bottom like a standard blind. Can be set to any combination of open positions.

Premium option
💡 Tip

Not sure which type you have? Check the bottom rail. Cordless blinds have a smooth bottom rail with a finger grip. Corded blinds have a loop hanging from the headrail. TDBU blinds have two separate rails — a middle rail and a bottom rail — both movable.

Tools & Parts

You'll need standard tools and whatever came in the box. Honeycomb blinds are lighter than roller or venetian blinds, so the install is less physically demanding.

Parts in the Box

  • Honeycomb blind (headrail + fabric + bottom rail assembled)
  • 2× mounting brackets (type varies — see bracket types)
  • Screws and wall plugs
  • Cord cleat (included with corded systems only)
  • Centre support bracket (for blinds over 1.5m wide)
💡 Tip

Count everything before you start. Honeycomb blinds are fragile when unpackaged — the pleated fabric can crease permanently if bent or stepped on. Keep the blind on a flat surface until you're ready to clip it in.

Bracket Types

Different honeycomb blind manufacturers use different bracket systems. Identify yours before drilling — the mounting method differs slightly for each.

🔲

Snap-In Bracket

The most common type. The headrail hooks onto the front lip of the bracket and clicks into the rear catch. To release, press the tab on the bracket front and tilt the headrail out.

Most common
📎

Spring Clip Bracket

Used for shallow recesses. A spring-loaded clip grips the headrail when pressed in. Lower profile than snap-in brackets — ideal when recess depth is tight (35–45mm).

Shallow recess
📐

L-Bracket

An L-shaped metal bracket used for heavy or wide blinds. The headrail sits on the horizontal face and is secured with a screw through the bracket into the headrail. More robust but requires screwing into the headrail.

Heavy / wide blinds

Choosing Your Mount Type

Inside mount gives a cleaner look but requires minimum recess depth. Outside mount is more forgiving and blocks more light. You should have already decided this when measuring.

📐 Inside Mount

Recess Fit (Inside the Window Frame)

Brackets are fixed inside the recess at the top. The blind sits within the window reveal. Requires a minimum recess depth of 35mm for single-cell and 45mm for double-cell honeycomb blinds.

✓ Clean, architectural look
✓ Doesn't intrude into room
✓ Works well with curtains layered outside
✗ Some light gap around edges
✗ Minimum recess depth required
🪟 Outside Mount

Face Fix (Outside the Window Frame)

Brackets are fixed to the wall above the window. The blind overlaps the window opening. Fabric should extend 40–60mm beyond the frame on each side for maximum light block.

✓ Maximum light blockage
✓ Makes window appear larger
✓ Works on any window
✗ More visible when raised
✗ Requires clear wall space above

Know Your Wall Type

The correct fixing depends on what you're drilling into. Honeycomb blinds are lightweight, but the bracket still needs to hold securely over years of daily use.

🟫

Plasterboard (Gyprock)

Hit a stud and screw directly. Between studs, use toggle anchors rated for the blind's weight. Most common in newer Melbourne homes and apartments.

Hit stud or toggle anchor
🧱

Brick / Rendered Masonry

Use a 6mm masonry bit, drill 40mm deep, insert a plastic wall plug before screwing. Standard in most Melbourne houses built before 2000.

Masonry bit + plug
🪵

Timber Frame / Architrave

Drill a pilot hole slightly smaller than the screw, then drive directly. No wall plug needed. Avoid splitting by staying clear of edges.

Pilot hole, no plug
🏗️

Steel Lintel / Frame

Use a metal drill bit at low speed. Self-tapping metal screws work best. Pre-drill a pilot hole slightly smaller than the screw diameter.

Metal bit + self-tap screw

Installation Steps

These steps cover all honeycomb blind types — cordless, corded, and TDBU. For TDBU-specific setup after installation, see the TDBU setup section.

Diagram

Inside Mount — Bracket Positioning
1

Unbox & check components

Remove the blind from its packaging carefully — honeycomb fabric creases easily. Lay it flat on a clean surface. Check you have the headrail with fabric attached, bottom rail, mounting brackets, screws, wall plugs, and cord cleat (if corded). Compare the blind width against your window to confirm it's the right size.

⏱ 2 min
2

Test the mechanism before mounting

Hold the blind up (or rest it on a table) and test the operation. Cordless: push the bottom rail up and release — it should hold at any height. Corded: pull the cord to raise and lower. TDBU: test both rails move independently. Confirm everything works before you drill a single hole.

⏱ 1 min
3

Mark bracket positions — 50mm from each end

Inside mount: Hold a bracket against the top face of the recess, positioned 50mm from the end of the headrail. Mark screw holes with a pencil. Repeat on the other side. Use a spirit level across both marks to verify they're perfectly level.

Outside mount: Position brackets 50mm from each end of the headrail width, at your desired height above the window (typically 80–100mm above frame). Level-check across both marks.

💡 Level Check

Even 2mm of misalignment will show as a visible tilt on honeycomb blinds. The pleated fabric makes any unevenness more obvious than on a roller blind. Take the extra minute to get this right.

⏱ 3 min
4

Drill pilot holes

Select the correct drill bit for your wall type (see wall types). Drill each pilot hole to the depth recommended for your fixings. Keep the drill perpendicular to the surface.

  • Brick / masonry: 6mm masonry bit, 40mm depth, hammer mode
  • Plasterboard into stud: 3mm pilot bit, 50mm depth, no hammer
  • Timber frame: 2.5mm pilot bit, 40mm depth
  • Steel frame: 4mm metal bit, slow speed, no hammer

Clear dust from each hole before inserting wall plugs.

⏱ 3 min
5

Fix brackets to wall

Tap wall plugs flush into drilled holes (if using). Hold each bracket in position and drive screws firmly. Don't over-torque — plastic plugs will spin if overtightened.

Once both brackets are fixed, hold a spirit level across them one final time. If one is off, loosen slightly, adjust, and re-tighten.

⏱ 3 min
6

Clip headrail into brackets

Engage the front hook first — tilt the front lip of the headrail into the front edge of the bracket. Then push the rear of the headrail upward until you hear a definitive click. The headrail should now be locked in and not wobble.

Give it a firm tug downward to confirm it's seated. If it feels loose, press the back edge harder until it clicks.

⚠️ Don't Force It

If the headrail won't click in, check that the brackets aren't too far apart or too close together. The headrail should slide in smoothly. Forcing it can crack the bracket or headrail endcaps.

⏱ 2 min
7

Test operation fully

Raise and lower the blind completely 3–4 times. Watch for:

  • Smooth, even fabric movement with no catching or bunching
  • Bottom rail staying level (not tilting to one side)
  • Blind holding position when released (cordless) or when cord is locked (corded)
  • Both rails moving independently (TDBU)
⏱ 2 min
8

Install cord cleat (corded systems only)

If your blind uses a cord loop, fix the cord cleat to the wall at a minimum height of 1.6m from the floor. This is a mandatory safety requirement under Australian standards. Wrap excess cord around the cleat when not in use to prevent dangling loops.

For cordless blinds, skip this step — no cord cleat is needed. For TDBU blinds, see the TDBU setup section for cord management.

⏱ 2 min

Top-Down Bottom-Up Setup

TDBU (top-down bottom-up) honeycomb blinds have two independently movable rails — a middle rail and a bottom rail — each controlled by separate cords or handles. This gives you precise control over privacy and light.

Diagram

TDBU Operation — Three Positions

How the Two-Rail System Works

A TDBU blind has two sets of internal cords running through the honeycomb cells:

  • Inner cords (rear): Control the top rail (middle rail). Pulling these lowers the top of the blind, creating an opening at the top of the window.
  • Outer cords (front): Control the bottom rail. Pulling these raises the bottom, creating an opening at the bottom — just like a standard blind.

On corded TDBU systems, the cords are typically colour-coded or separated left/right. On cordless TDBU, each rail has its own handle — simply push or pull the rail you want to move.

💡 Tip

After installing a TDBU blind, spend a minute testing every combination: fully closed, top-down only, bottom-up only, and both partially open. This confirms both cord systems are routing correctly through the cells and nothing is tangled.

⚠️ Important

If a TDBU blind has cords, both cord sets need a cleat or cord management. Don't leave any cord loop hanging — each one is a potential strangulation hazard. Fix a cord cleat at 1.6m+ for each cord set.

Child Safety

Honeycomb blinds offer some of the safest options on the market — but only if installed correctly. Here's what you need to know under Australian mandatory safety standards.

  • Cordless is safest: No cords, no chains, no loops. Cordless honeycomb blinds are the recommended choice for nurseries, children's bedrooms, and any room where young children spend time.
  • Cord cleat at 1.6m+: For corded systems, fix a cord cleat to the wall at a minimum height of 1.6m. Wrap all excess cord around the cleat when not in use.
  • TDBU cord management: TDBU systems with cords have two sets — both must be cleated. Don't assume one cleat handles both.
  • No furniture near windows: Keep beds, chairs, cots, and climbable items at least 1.5m from the blind.
  • Australian Standards: AS/NZS 13120 covers internal window coverings and child safety. All corded blinds must have a functioning cord restraint device installed.

For homes with young children, we strongly recommend cordless honeycomb blinds throughout. See our full child safety guide for a room-by-room assessment, or browse our cordless honeycomb blind range.

Troubleshooting

Most honeycomb blind issues have simple fixes. Work through these before calling for a service visit:

Blind won't stay up (drops slowly after raising)
The internal cord tension needs resetting. Remove the blind from the brackets. With the blind fully lowered on a flat surface, locate the cord lock mechanism on the headrail. Most have a small reset button or require you to pull the cord fully through and re-lock. Refer to your manufacturer's specific instructions — the cord lock design varies between brands. If the cord lock is worn out, a replacement cord lock assembly is available from us.
Fabric is uneven or bunching on one side
Usually caused by one internal cord being slightly tighter than the other. Lower the blind fully and check that the bottom rail sits level. If one side is higher, gently tug the lower side of the bottom rail down while holding the higher side. This redistributes cord tension. If the problem persists, the cord may be caught on an internal cell — remove the blind and carefully inspect the cord routing through the cells.
Light gaps around the edges (inside mount)
Some light gap is inherent to inside-mount honeycomb blinds. Minimise it by: (1) ensuring the blind was ordered with minimal side deductions (2–4mm each side), (2) considering outside mount for bedrooms where darkness matters most, (3) adding optional side channels that create a seal between the blind and the window frame — we offer these as an accessory for our honeycomb range.
Bracket won't click — headrail doesn't seat
Check that both brackets are mounted at exactly the same distance from the window frame edge. If they're misaligned by even 2–3mm, the headrail won't engage both clips simultaneously. Also check that the brackets are the right way around — most snap-in brackets have a distinct front and back. The front lip (where the headrail hooks first) should face toward the room.
Headrail sags in the middle (wide blinds)
Blinds wider than 1.5m need a centre support bracket. This clips onto the headrail between the two end brackets and screws into the mounting surface. If your blind didn't come with one, contact us — we can supply a compatible centre bracket. For blinds over 2m, two evenly-spaced centre brackets are recommended.
TDBU blind slipping — won't hold position
The cord lock mechanism that holds each rail in place may be worn or the cords may have stretched. First, try the cord tension reset: remove the blind, lower both rails fully, and pull each cord set through the cord lock firmly before re-locking. If the blind still slips, the cord lock assembly likely needs replacing. On cordless TDBU systems, the internal spring mechanism may need re-tensioning — this usually requires professional service.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install honeycomb blinds without a drill?
Adhesive no-drill brackets exist for lightweight honeycomb blinds on smooth surfaces, but they're only reliable for blinds under 900mm wide. For anything wider or heavier, a drill provides far more secure fixing. Rentals can use tension-fit systems for some window types.
How long does it take to install honeycomb blinds?
About 20–35 minutes per window for a first-time DIYer. Once you've done a couple, expect 15–20 minutes each. TDBU (top-down-bottom-up) systems take slightly longer due to testing both rail directions.
What's the minimum recess depth for inside mount?
Most honeycomb blinds need a minimum of 35mm recess depth for inside mount. Double-cell (Duette-style) blinds may need 45mm. Check your specific product specs — if your recess is too shallow, outside mount is the better option.
Are cordless honeycomb blinds safe for children?
Yes — cordless honeycomb blinds are the safest option for homes with young children. They have no cords, chains, or loops that could pose a strangulation risk. They're recommended by Australian safety standards for nurseries and children's bedrooms.
My TDBU blind won't stay in position — how do I fix it?
TDBU blinds use internal cord tension to hold position. If the blind slips, the cord tension mechanism may need resetting. Remove the blind from brackets, locate the cord lock mechanism on the headrail, and re-tension according to your manufacturer's instructions. If it still slips, the cord lock may need replacement.
Do honeycomb blinds need a centre bracket for wide windows?
Yes — for blinds wider than 1.5m, a centre support bracket prevents the headrail from sagging under the fabric weight. The centre bracket clips onto the headrail between the two end brackets. For blinds over 2m, two centre brackets evenly spaced are recommended.
