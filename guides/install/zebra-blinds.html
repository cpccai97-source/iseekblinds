How to Install Zebra Blinds

Also called day-night blinds or vision blinds. Same bracket system as roller blinds, with one extra consideration: getting those stripes to align. This guide covers everything.

⚠️

Child safety (AS 2999): Zebra blinds with chain operation must have a chain tensioner fixed to the wall or sill. The chain loop must not be large enough for a child to put over their head. All iSeekBlinds zebra blinds include child-safety tensioners — install them as described in Step 7.

What You'll Need

Everything should arrive in the box — you just need the tools.

Tools
  • Power drill with 6mm masonry bit
  • Phillips head screwdriver
  • Steel tape measure (3m+)
  • Spirit level (400mm minimum)
  • Pencil
  • Step ladder
In the Box
  • Cassette headrail with zebra fabric pre-loaded
  • Clip-in mounting brackets (2 or 3)
  • Screws + wall plugs
  • Chain or cordless operating mechanism
  • Chain tensioner with fixing screw
  • Bottom bar (pre-attached to fabric)

Anatomy of a Zebra Blind

Understanding the parts before you start makes installation much easier. Here's what you're working with.

Inside Mount vs Outside Mount

The choice depends on your window recess depth and the look you want. Both use the same brackets.

Inside Mount (Recess Fit)

  • Cleaner, flush look inside the window reveal
  • Brackets screw into the top of the recess (ceiling fix)
  • Requires minimum 60mm recess depth for the cassette
  • Leaves architraves and window frame visible
  • Small light gaps at edges — acceptable for most rooms
Best for: living rooms, studies, modern interiors where you want a neat, recessed look.

Outside Mount (Face Fit)

  • Blind mounts on the wall or frame above the window
  • Covers the entire window opening — better light block
  • Works when recess is too shallow (<60mm)
  • Can make windows appear larger
  • Hides damaged or uneven reveals
Best for: bedrooms, shallow recesses, wide windows, or when maximum light blockout is needed.

Installation Steps

Seven steps, about 25 minutes per blind. Read all steps before drilling your first hole.

1

Unpack & Inspect

Remove the zebra blind from its packaging. Lay it on a clean surface and check all components are present: cassette headrail (fabric pre-loaded), mounting brackets (2 for blinds under 1800mm, 3 for wider), screws, wall plugs, chain tensioner, and operating chain.

Unroll the fabric partially and inspect for transport damage. The alternating sheer and opaque stripes should be evenly spaced with no creasing, tears, or marks.

Tip: Leave the fabric rolled until the cassette is mounted. Handling unrolled fabric on the floor risks marking or creasing the sheer stripes.
2

Mark Bracket Positions

Inside mount: Hold the cassette inside the recess to confirm it fits. Mark the bracket screw holes on the ceiling of the recess, positioning brackets 50–80mm from each end of the cassette. For blinds over 1800mm, mark a centre bracket position equidistant from each end bracket.

Outside mount: Measure and mark the centre point of the window. From centre, measure half the blind width in each direction to locate bracket positions. Mark the height — the bottom of the cassette typically sits 50–80mm above the window opening.

Important: Use a spirit level across both bracket marks before drilling. A 3mm error here becomes a visible slope when the blind is half-open.
3

Drill Pilot Holes

Drill pilot holes at your marked positions. Match your drill bit to your wall type:

Timber frame/reveal: 2–3mm bit directly into timber. No wall plugs needed.
Plasterboard: Locate studs with a stud finder. Into studs: 3mm bit. Mid-bay: use plasterboard toggle anchors (rated 15kg+).
Brick/masonry: 6mm masonry bit with hammer drill setting. Insert wall plugs flush.

Tip: Most Melbourne window recesses have timber reveals (the sides and top of the frame). Tap it — if it sounds solid, you're drilling into timber. That's the easiest case.
4

Fix Brackets to Surface

Screw the brackets into the pilot holes using the supplied screws. For inside mount (ceiling fix), the clip mechanism should face downward. For outside mount (face fix), the clip faces outward from the wall.

Tighten until firm — don't overtighten, especially into plasterboard. The bracket should sit flat against the surface with no wobble.

Tip: Before tightening the second bracket fully, hold the cassette up to check alignment. Easier to adjust before the screw is fully driven.
5

Click Cassette Into Brackets

Hold the cassette headrail up to the brackets. Most zebra blind brackets use a snap-in system — press one end into the bracket until it clicks, then press the other end. You should hear a definite click on each side.

Give the cassette a firm downward tug to confirm it is locked. It should not slide or shift. If it feels loose, the cassette may not be fully seated — push it upward firmly until the clips engage.

6

Test Operation & Check Stripe Alignment

Operate the chain (or cordless mechanism) to lower the fabric fully. The front and back layers of fabric should create clean, aligned stripes across the full width. Then raise it fully and lower again — the stripes should settle consistently.

To adjust light filtering: move the chain slightly to shift the front layer relative to the back layer. When opaque stripes overlap, light is blocked. When opaque aligns with sheer, light filters through.

Stripes not aligning? Check the cassette is level. If brackets are level but stripes are still off, the fabric tube may need a slight rotation — see the Stripe Alignment section below.
7

Install Chain Tensioner

This is a legal requirement under Australian Standard AS 2999 for all corded/chained window coverings.

Fix the chain tensioner to the wall or window sill approximately 100mm below the chain guide mechanism. Screw it into place with the supplied screw. Thread the chain through the tensioner and adjust until the chain is taut enough that it cannot form a loop large enough to pass over a child's head.

Non-negotiable: The chain tensioner must be fixed to the wall — not left loose. If your blind came without one, contact your supplier immediately. Cordless and motorised zebra blinds do not require a tensioner.

Stripe Alignment: The Three Positions

Zebra blinds work by shifting two layers of alternating stripes. The chain moves the front layer up or down relative to the back layer, giving you three distinct positions.

Open Position

Sheer stripes of both layers align — soft, filtered daylight enters. Privacy from outside while still seeing silhouettes.

Transitional

Stripes partially overlap — a middle ground between open and closed. Reduces glare while keeping some view.

Closed Position

Opaque stripes of both layers fully overlap — near-complete light blockout and full privacy. Best for bedrooms.

Troubleshooting

Common issues and how to fix them — most are quick adjustments, not reinstalls.

🔄

Stripes Won't Align Evenly

Check the cassette is level. If level is fine, the fabric tube may need micro-rotation. Unclip the cassette, rotate the tube 1–2mm at the idle end (non-chain side), and re-mount. Some models have a tension adjustment screw at the idle end.

📐

Blind Sits at an Angle

One bracket is lower than the other. Remove the blind, check level across both brackets, and re-drill the lower bracket. Fill the old hole with Polyfilla. Even 2mm difference shows when stripes are misaligned.

🔊

Chain Mechanism Feels Stiff

The cassette may be slightly compressed by the brackets. Loosen the bracket screws by a quarter turn — the cassette should rotate freely. Also check that no packaging material is caught between the fabric layers.

💡

Light Gaps at the Edges (Inside Mount)

This is normal for inside mount — the blind sits inside the recess with 2–4mm clearance each side. For bedrooms needing full blockout, consider outside mount or add a pelmet above and side channels.

↕️

Fabric Sagging in the Middle

For blinds wider than 1800mm, the tube sags without a centre support bracket. If your blind didn't include one, contact iSeekBlinds for a centre bracket. This also causes stripes to misalign in the centre of the blind.

🧹

Cleaning Zebra Blind Fabric

Use a feather duster or soft microfibre cloth. For marks, dab gently with a damp cloth — never scrub or use chemicals. The sheer stripes are delicate. For deep cleaning, remove the cassette and take it to a blind cleaning specialist.

Common Questions

Are zebra blinds hard to install? +
No. They use the same clip-in bracket system as standard roller blinds. Installation takes 20–30 minutes per blind. The only additional consideration is checking stripe alignment after mounting, which is a simple visual check and minor chain adjustment.
What's the minimum recess depth for inside-mount zebra blinds? +
60mm minimum for the cassette headrail. Measure the depth from the wall face into the recess at all four corners. If any corner is less than 60mm, use outside mount instead. Some compact cassette models require only 50mm — check your product specs.
My zebra blind stripes aren't aligning — what do I do? +
First, check your brackets are level with a spirit level. If they are level but stripes still don't align, lift the cassette off, rotate the fabric tube slightly by hand (1–2mm at the idle end), and re-mount. Some models have a tension adjustment screw at the idle end of the tube. If nothing works, the fabric may have been wound incorrectly during manufacturing — contact your supplier.
Can I install zebra blinds without drilling? +
For narrow blinds under 900mm, adhesive brackets can work. But zebra blind cassettes are heavier than standard rollers, so we recommend proper screw-fixing for anything wider. Renters: check with your landlord — most allow bracket holes in timber window frames as normal wear.
Do zebra blinds need a centre bracket? +
For blinds wider than 1800mm, yes. A centre support bracket prevents tube sag, which causes stripe misalignment in the middle. Most manufacturers include one for wide blinds. If yours didn't come with one, contact iSeekBlinds.
