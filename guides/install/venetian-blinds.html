How to Install Venetian Blinds

⏱ 25–40 minutes 🔧 Difficulty: Easy–Medium 📅 Updated March 2026

Whether you've ordered aluminium or timber venetians, installation follows the same core process: fix two brackets, clip in the headrail, test the tilt and lift. This guide covers both inside and outside mount with SVG diagrams, wall-type fixings, and real troubleshooting for when things don't behave.

Timber vs Aluminium Venetians

The installation process is identical for both materials — the difference is weight and bracket strength. Understanding which you have affects bracket choice and whether you need a second pair of hands.

Aluminium Venetian Timber / Faux-Timber Venetian
Slat width 25mm (standard) 50mm or 63mm
Weight (1.2m wide) ~1.5 kg ~4–6 kg
Max recommended width 2.4m 2.1m (timber) / 2.4m (faux)
Min recess depth (inside mount) 40mm 55mm (50mm slat) / 70mm (63mm slat)
Bracket type Clip-in (spring-loaded) Twist-lock or heavy-duty clip
Best for Kitchens, bathrooms, wet areas Living rooms, bedrooms, studies
Second person needed? No (most sizes) Yes, if wider than 1.5m

Parts supplied with your venetian blind should include:

  • Headrail with slats, ladder tapes/cords, and lift cords assembled
  • 2× mounting brackets (3 brackets for blinds over 1.8m wide)
  • Bottom rail with end caps
  • Tilt wand (or tilt cord on some aluminium models)
  • Screws (usually 2× per bracket)
  • Wall plugs
  • Cord cleat (child safety device)
  • Valance and valance clips (if ordered)
💡 Tip

Count all components before you start. Venetian blinds have more small parts than rollers — particularly check you have all valance clips, the tilt wand, and the cord cleat. Missing a cord cleat means you can't complete the install safely.

Bracket Types

Venetian blinds use one of three bracket styles. Check which came with your blind before you start — the fixing process is the same, but how the headrail engages is different.

📎

Clip-In (Spring-Loaded)

Most common for aluminium venetians. The headrail pushes in from the front and the spring clip holds it. Release by pressing a tab on the bracket front.

🔄

Twist-Lock

Common for timber venetians. The headrail slides in, then a twist tab rotates to lock it in place. More secure for heavier blinds. Unlock by rotating the tab back.

🔩

Screw-Fix (L-Bracket)

Used for very heavy or wide blinds. The headrail sits in the bracket and is secured with a small set-screw. Most secure but slowest to install and remove.

⚠️ Centre Bracket

Blinds wider than 1.8m require a centre support bracket to prevent the headrail from sagging. This bracket goes at the midpoint and should be the same type as the end brackets. Mark and fix it during the bracket installation step — not after the blind is hung.

Choosing Your Mount Type

You should have decided this when measuring. If you haven't, here's the quick summary — but don't change your mind at this stage unless you're willing to re-order the blind to different dimensions.

📐 Inside Mount

Recess Fit (Inside the Window Frame)

Brackets are fixed to the top face of the recess. The blind sits entirely within the window reveal. Requires minimum recess depth of 40mm (aluminium) or 55mm (timber 50mm slat).

✓ Clean, integrated look
✓ Doesn't protrude into the room
✓ Works well with curtains layered outside
✗ Small light gaps at sides
✗ Requires adequate recess depth
🪟 Outside Mount

Face Fix (Outside the Window Frame)

Brackets are fixed to the wall or architrave above the window. The blind overlaps the window opening. Order width 30–50mm wider than the opening on each side for full coverage.

✓ Better light block (overlap coverage)
✓ Makes windows appear larger
✓ Works on any window regardless of recess depth
✗ Headrail and valance visible on wall
✗ Requires clear wall space above

Know Your Wall Type

Wrong fixings are the number one reason brackets fail. Venetian blinds — especially timber ones — exert more lateral force than rollers because raising and lowering creates side-load on the brackets. Match your wall type carefully.

🧱

Brick / Rendered Masonry

Use a masonry bit. Drill 40mm deep. Insert a 6mm plastic wall plug before screwing. Most Melbourne homes have brick — this is the most common scenario.

Masonry bit + plug
🟫

Plasterboard (Gyprock)

Hit a stud if possible — screw directly in. Between studs, use toggle anchors rated for the blind's weight. Timber venetians over 1.2m wide must hit at least one stud.

Hit stud or toggle anchor
🪵

Timber Frame / Architrave

Drill a pilot hole slightly smaller than the screw diameter. Drive the screw directly — no plug needed. Inside mount into a timber recess is the easiest venetian install possible.

Pilot hole, no plug
🪟

Aluminium Window Frame

Use a metal drill bit at low speed. Self-tapping metal screws work well. Be careful not to puncture through to the frame cavity — stop drill depth at 8–10mm.

Metal bit + self-tap screw
🏗️

Ceiling (Plasterboard)

Locate joists with a stud finder — typically 450mm apart. Fix into joists where possible. Between joists, use ceiling-rated toggle anchors with dynamic load rating.

Joist preferred, toggle otherwise
⬜

Tiles (Kitchen / Bathroom)

Use a tile/glass bit at low speed with no hammer. Stick masking tape over drill points to prevent slipping. Only for aluminium venetians in wet areas.

Tile bit, slow speed

Installation Steps

These steps cover wall-mount installation for both inside and outside mount. For ceiling mount, see the ceiling mount section below.

Diagram

How the Headrail Clips Into a Venetian Bracket
1

Unbox and identify components

Remove the venetian blind from its packaging. The slats should already be threaded onto the ladder tapes/cords and connected to the headrail. Identify:

  • Headrail — the metal or timber channel at the top
  • Tilt wand — the clear or white rod that hangs from one end (rotates slats)
  • Lift cords — thin cords that run through holes in each slat (raises/lowers the blind)
  • Bottom rail — weighted bar at the base
  • Mounting brackets — 2 or 3 depending on blind width
  • Valance — decorative front cover (sometimes pre-clipped)

Lay the blind flat on a clean surface. Never stand the blind upright while unboxed — the slats will cascade and tangle.

⏱ 3 min
2

Mark bracket positions

Inside mount: Hold each bracket flush against the top face of the window recess, positioned 50–80mm from each end of the headrail. The bracket opening should face downward (toward the floor). Mark all screw holes with a pencil.

Outside mount: Position brackets on the wall or architrave at your desired height — typically 80–100mm above the top of the window frame. Measure both marks from the floor to ensure they're exactly level. Mark screw holes for both brackets.

If you have a centre bracket (blinds over 1.8m), mark its position at the midpoint between the two end brackets.

💡 Level Check

Use a spirit level to draw a faint horizontal line across both bracket positions before marking screw holes. Even 2mm off will cause the blind to hang crooked — and venetian slats make any tilt painfully obvious.

⏱ 5 min
3

Drill pilot holes

Select the correct drill bit for your wall type (see wall types section). Drill each pilot hole to the depth recommended for your fixings:

  • Brick / masonry: 6mm masonry bit, 40mm depth, hammer drill mode
  • Plasterboard into stud: 3mm pilot bit, 50mm depth, no hammer
  • Timber frame: 2.5mm pilot bit, 35mm depth, no plug needed
  • Aluminium frame: 4mm metal bit, slow speed, 8–10mm depth

Keep the drill perpendicular to the mounting surface. Clear dust from each hole with a puff of air before inserting plugs.

⏱ 5 min
4

Fix brackets and verify level

Tap wall plugs flush into each hole (masonry and plasterboard only). Position each bracket over the plugs and drive screws through the bracket holes into the plugs. Tighten firmly but don't over-torque — spinning plugs won't hold.

With both brackets fixed, hold a spirit level across the bracket faces to confirm they're perfectly horizontal. If one is off, loosen its screws slightly, adjust, and re-tighten.

⚠️ Don't Skip This

Brackets that are even slightly misaligned will cause the blind to bind when raising/lowering and the slats to tilt unevenly. Fix it now — it's much harder to fix with the blind hanging.

⏱ 8 min
5

Clip headrail into brackets

Open all bracket clips (lift the front tab or twist the lock tab to the open position). Hold the blind horizontally at bracket height — for heavy timber venetians, have a second person hold one end.

Clip-in brackets: Push the headrail up into the bracket opening from below. Push firmly until the spring clip snaps closed. You should feel and hear a definite click.

Twist-lock brackets: Slide the headrail into the bracket slot from the front, then rotate the locking tab 90° to secure.

Engage all brackets before releasing the blind. Give the headrail a firm downward tug to confirm it's fully locked in every bracket.

⏱ 3 min
6

Test tilt and lift mechanisms

Tilt test: Rotate the tilt wand (or pull the tilt cord) to open and close the slats fully. All slats should rotate together, evenly, through the full range. If some slats don't tilt or tilt at a different angle, check the ladder tapes — one may be twisted or caught.

Lift test: Pull the lift cords to raise the blind to half height. Release the cords at a slight angle to engage the cord lock. The blind should hold at that position without dropping. To release, pull the cords straight down briefly, then guide the blind down.

If the cord lock doesn't hold: The cords may not be fully engaged in the lock mechanism. Pull up firmly, then release at a 45° angle toward the window. If it still slips, the cord lock inside the headrail may need adjustment — see troubleshooting.

⏱ 5 min
7

Install cord cleat (mandatory)

The cord cleat anchors excess lift cord to the wall, preventing a dangling loop that poses a child strangulation hazard. This is legally required under Australian safety standards for all corded blinds.

With the blind fully lowered, note where the lift cords hang at their lowest. Fix the cord cleat to the wall at a minimum height of 1.6m from the floor. Use a single screw into a wall plug or stud. Wrap the excess cord around the cleat in a figure-8 pattern so no loop hangs free.

🚨 Child Safety

A dangling cord loop is a strangulation risk. Always install the cord cleat immediately — don't leave it as a "later" task. If your cleat is missing, contact us for a free replacement before using the blind.

⏱ 3 min
8

Fit valance (if included)

The valance is a decorative strip that clips to the front of the headrail, concealing the mechanism. Most valances use small plastic clips that hook over the headrail front edge.

If separate valance clips were supplied, clip them onto the headrail first (usually two per metre). Then press the valance onto the clips — it should snap on from the front. Check both ends are flush.

Not all venetians come with a valance. Timber venetians often have a timber valance matching the slat finish; aluminium venetians may use a thin metal or fabric strip. Some designs look better without one — especially inside-mounted blinds where the headrail sits in the recess.

⏱ 3 min

Ceiling Mount Installation

Ceiling mounting venetian blinds works well for floor-to-ceiling windows and bay windows where wall mounting isn't practical. The process follows the same steps, with these adjustments:

Diagram

Ceiling Mount — Bracket Orientation for Venetian Blinds
  • Rotate brackets 90° — most venetian brackets have a ceiling-mount orientation. The screw holes face upward, and the headrail clips in from below.
  • Locate joists with a stud finder. Fix into joists where possible — timber venetians are heavy enough that plasterboard-only fixing is risky.
  • Between-joist fixing: Use ceiling-rated toggle anchors (minimum 30kg working load per anchor). Standard wall plugs in ceiling plasterboard will eventually fail.
  • Cord cleat placement: For ceiling mount, fix the cord cleat to the adjacent wall at ≥1.6m height. The cord will need to angle from the headrail to the cleat — this is normal and doesn't affect operation.
💡 Pro Tip

For ceiling mounts without convenient joist positions, fix a strip of 18mm ply or timber batten to the ceiling (screwed into two joists), then mount your venetian brackets to the timber. This gives unlimited positioning and distributes load across multiple joists.

Child Safety Compliance

Under Australian mandatory safety standards, all corded venetian blinds must be installed with a cord restraint system. This applies to both lift cords and any separate tilt cord (not tilt wands — wands are inherently safe).

  • Cord cleat: Fix at minimum 1.6m height and wrap all excess cord in a figure-8
  • No free-hanging loops — any cord loop that can fit over a child's head is dangerous
  • Keep furniture away — no beds, chairs, or climbable items within 1.5m of the window
  • Check cord condition — frayed or worn cords should be replaced promptly; a snapped cord mid-raise drops the full weight of the blind

For children's bedrooms and nurseries, we strongly recommend cordless venetians (spring-loaded raise/lower) or motorised blinds that eliminate cords entirely. See our childproof window blinds guide for a full room-by-room safety assessment.

Troubleshooting

Venetian blinds have more moving parts than rollers, so there are more things that can go slightly wrong. Most are easy fixes:

Slats won't tilt evenly — some lag behind others
A ladder tape or cord is twisted or caught between slats. Lower the blind fully, identify the stuck slat, and gently straighten the ladder tape on both sides. If the tilt mechanism drum inside the headrail has uneven cord routing, pop off the headrail end cap and re-route the ladder strings evenly on the drum.
Blind won't stay raised — cord lock doesn't hold
The cord lock relies on the cords being pulled at a slight angle to engage. Pull the cords straight up, then release at a 45° angle toward the window. If the lock still doesn't engage, the lock mechanism inside the headrail may be worn or have dust buildup. Remove the headrail end cap, locate the cord lock, and clean or replace it.
Blind raises unevenly — one side higher than the other
The lift cords are at different lengths. Lower the blind fully, pull all lift cords together until the bottom rail is perfectly level, and re-knot or re-clamp the cords at even lengths. If one cord is significantly shorter, check whether a slat hole is snagging that cord — slat holes can develop burrs that add friction to one side.
One or more slats are bent (aluminium)
Bent aluminium slats can often be straightened. Lay the slat on a flat surface and run a plastic ruler or the edge of a credit card along the bend with firm pressure. For severe bends or creased slats, replacement slats can be ordered individually from us — send us the colour code from the headrail label.
Headrail wobbles or feels loose in brackets
The clip hasn't fully engaged. Remove the headrail, check that the bracket clip is clean and undamaged, then re-insert the headrail with a firm push until the clip snaps closed. For twist-lock brackets, ensure the lock tab is rotated fully to the locked position. If the bracket itself is loose on the wall, tighten the fixing screws or upgrade to larger wall plugs.
Slats stick to each other when opening
Static buildup, especially in dry weather or air-conditioned rooms. Wipe slats with a damp microfibre cloth or use an anti-static spray. For timber venetians, apply a light furniture polish — it reduces static and makes slats move freely. Avoid silicone sprays on timber as they attract dust.
Lift cord has snapped
This requires re-threading a new cord through all slat holes. It's fiddly but doable: lower the blind, remove from brackets, lay flat, thread new cord from the bottom rail up through each slat hole and into the headrail lock mechanism. Use the old cord as a guide if possible. Replacement cord is available from us — specify the cord diameter (usually 1.4mm for aluminium, 1.8mm for timber).

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install venetian blinds without drilling?
Adhesive no-drill brackets exist for lightweight aluminium venetians under 900mm wide on smooth surfaces. For timber venetians or wider blinds, drilling is essential — adhesive brackets can't handle the weight and lateral force of raising/lowering.
How long does it take to install venetian blinds?
A single venetian blind takes 25–40 minutes to install. Once you've done the first window, subsequent blinds typically take 15–20 minutes each. Timber venetians are heavier — budget a second person for windows wider than 1.5m.
Should venetian blinds be inside or outside mount?
Inside mount gives a cleaner, architectural look and is preferred for most installations. Outside mount is better when the recess is too shallow (less than 40mm for aluminium, 55mm for timber), the window frame is uneven, or you want maximum light block with overlap beyond the window edges.
What's the minimum recess depth for venetian blinds?
Aluminium venetians need 40mm minimum. Timber venetians with 50mm slats need 55mm; with 63mm slats need 70mm. If your recess is shallower, go with outside mount or consider a slim-profile aluminium venetian.
How do I fix slats that won't tilt evenly?
Uneven tilting is usually a tangled or misrouted ladder tape/cord inside the headrail. Open the headrail end cap, check the tilt mechanism drum, and ensure the ladder strings are evenly routed on both sides. A single twisted ladder cord will cause lopsided tilting.
Can venetian blinds be ceiling mounted?
Yes — most brackets rotate 90° for ceiling fix. Locate ceiling joists with a stud finder and fix into joists where possible. Between joists, use rated ceiling toggle anchors. This works well for floor-to-ceiling windows and bay window installations.
