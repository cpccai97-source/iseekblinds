How to Install Roman Blinds

Mount your Roman blinds correctly with our step-by-step guide. Covers headrail bracket systems, face fix vs top fix, cord threading, and child safety compliance — with SVG diagrams for every step.

Before You Start

Roman blinds are different from roller blinds. Instead of a roller tube, Roman blinds use a flat headrail (aluminium or timber batten) from which the fabric hangs. The fabric folds into soft horizontal pleats when raised, using cords or chains that run through rings sewn on the back.

This means installation is about mounting a headrail — not clipping in a tube. The bracket system is simpler, but you need to think about stack height (how much space the folded fabric occupies when raised) and cord management for child safety.

Roller blinds roll around a tube — brackets hold the tube. Roman blinds fold into pleats — brackets hold a flat headrail. The headrail is wider (typically 30–50mm deep) and needs a different bracket style. Don't try to use roller blind brackets for Roman blinds.

Check that your blind came with:

  • Headrail with fabric attached (pre-strung with cords through back rings)
  • Mounting brackets (2 minimum, 3 for wider blinds over 1.5m)
  • Screws and wall plugs
  • Cord lock mechanism (mounted on the headrail — pull to lower, tug to lock)
  • Cord cleat for wall mounting (mandatory safety device)
⚠️ Missing Parts?

If the cord lock or cord cleat is missing, do not install the blind. Contact your supplier for replacements. Installing a Roman blind without a cord restraint is illegal in Australia and a genuine strangulation hazard for children.

Mounting Types

Roman blinds support three mounting methods. Your choice depends on window style, recess depth, and how much of the glass you want exposed when the blind is raised.

Method Best For Min. Recess Depth Mounting Position
Face Fix (Outside Mount) Shallow recesses, maximum light block, making windows look taller N/A — mounts on wall Wall above window, 80–150mm above frame
Recess Fix (Inside Mount) Clean built-in look, showcasing architraves 45mm minimum Top face of window recess
Top Fix (Ceiling Mount) Floor-to-ceiling windows, bifold door openings N/A — mounts on ceiling Ceiling above window

Diagram

Face Fix vs Recess Fix — Bracket Positions
💡 Which Mount to Choose?

Face fix is the most popular for Roman blinds. It hides the stack height above the window frame, maximising glass exposure. Recess fix looks cleaner but the folded fabric will block the top portion of your glass. If your recess depth is under 45mm, face fix is your only option.

Wall Types & Fixings

Identify your wall type before drilling. Using the wrong fixing will result in brackets pulling out under the weight of the fabric.

🧱

Brick / Masonry

Use 6mm masonry drill bit with hammer drill mode. Insert 6mm wall plugs. Drill 35–40mm deep. Most reliable fixing for heavy Roman blinds.

Masonry bit + hammer drill
📋

Plasterboard (into Stud)

Use stud finder to locate timber studs (usually 450mm apart). Drill 3mm pilot hole into stud, 40mm deep. No wall plug needed — screw directly into timber.

3mm pilot, no plug
📋

Plasterboard (No Stud)

Use spring toggles or gravity toggles rated for 20kg+. Do NOT use basic plastic wall plugs in unsupported plasterboard — they will pull through under load.

Toggle anchors, 20kg+ rated
🪵

Timber Frame / Architrave

Drill 2.5mm pilot hole, 30mm deep. Screw directly — no plug needed. Excellent holding strength. Common for inside-recess mounting.

2.5mm pilot, direct screw
⬜

Concrete / Rendered

Similar to brick — 6mm masonry bit, hammer drill. Rendered walls may need a longer plug (50mm) to get past the render layer into solid substrate.

Masonry bit, longer plugs
🔲

Ceiling (Plasterboard)

For top fix / ceiling mount: locate joists with stud finder. Between joists, use rated ceiling toggle anchors. Standard wall plugs WILL fall out of ceilings.

Joist preferred, toggle otherwise

Installation Steps

These steps cover face fix (wall mount) installation — the most common method. For recess fix and ceiling mount, see Top Fix / Ceiling Mount. The bracket process is similar; only the orientation changes.

Diagram

Roman Blind Headrail & Bracket Assembly
1

Unpack and inspect the blind

Remove the Roman blind from its packaging. Lay it flat on a clean surface, face up. Check the following:

  • Headrail is intact — no bends or damage
  • Fabric is attached to the headrail (usually via Velcro strip or clips)
  • Cords run through all the rings on the back of the fabric and converge at the cord lock
  • Bottom bar / weight rod is inserted in the bottom hem pocket
  • All brackets, screws, wall plugs, and cord cleat are present

Identify the front (fabric face you see) and back (cord rings, dowels in fold pockets). The cord lock will be on one side of the headrail — this determines which side the operating cord hangs.

⏱ 5 min
2

Mark bracket positions

Face fix: Measure your desired mounting height above the window frame — typically 80–150mm. Use a spirit level to draw a faint horizontal pencil line at this height, extending 50mm beyond the window frame on each side.

Hold each bracket against the wall at the line. The outer brackets go 30–50mm from the ends of the headrail. For blinds over 1.5m wide, add a centre bracket. Mark all screw holes with a pencil.

Recess fix: Hold brackets against the top face of the window recess, 20mm from the front edge (so the fabric hangs clear of the window frame). Mark screw holes pointing upward.

💡 Pro Tip

Hold the headrail up against the marks before drilling. Have someone step back and check it looks level and centred. Much easier to adjust pencil marks than re-drill holes.

⏱ 5–8 min
3

Drill pilot holes

Select the correct drill bit for your wall type (see wall types section). Drill each pilot hole perpendicular to the wall surface, to the depth recommended for your fixings:

  • Brick / masonry: 6mm masonry bit, 35–40mm deep, hammer drill on
  • Plasterboard into stud: 3mm pilot, 40mm deep, no hammer
  • Plasterboard no stud: Drill a clearance hole for your toggle anchor
  • Timber: 2.5mm pilot, 30mm deep

Clear dust from each hole before inserting plugs. A quick blow or vacuum does the job.

⏱ 5 min
4

Fix brackets to wall

Insert wall plugs (if applicable) and tap flush with a hammer. Hold each bracket in position and drive screws through the bracket into the plugs. Tighten firmly — Roman blinds are heavier than roller blinds (fabric + dowels + headrail), so brackets must be rock-solid.

After fixing all brackets, hold a spirit level across them and verify they're perfectly horizontal. If one is off, loosen it, adjust, and re-tighten.

⚠️ Weight Check

A large Roman blind (2m wide × 2.4m drop) can weigh 5–8kg. Each bracket must be rated and fixed to handle this weight with a safety margin. If you're mounting into plasterboard without studs, use toggle anchors rated for at least 20kg each.

⏱ 8 min
5

Mount the headrail

How the headrail attaches depends on your bracket type:

  • Clip-in brackets: Push the headrail up into the bracket clip and rotate forward until it clicks into place. You'll feel and hear a firm snap.
  • Screw-through brackets: Rest the headrail on the bracket shelf, then drive a small set-screw through the bracket into the headrail to lock it.
  • Velcro batten: If your Roman blind uses a timber batten wrapped in Velcro (common on handmade/custom blinds), the batten sits on top of the L-brackets and is secured with screws through the batten into the bracket.

Once mounted, the headrail should feel completely rigid — no wobble, no play. Give it a firm downward tug to test.

⏱ 3–5 min
6

Test the cord operation

With the blind mounted, pull the operating cord slowly to raise the blind. Watch the fabric carefully:

  • Folds should form evenly and horizontally
  • The blind should rise straight (not pull to one side)
  • The cord lock should hold the blind at any height when you stop pulling

To lower: give the cord a short sharp tug to release the cord lock, then guide the fabric down by easing the cord out slowly.

If folds are uneven: One cord may be threaded incorrectly or have slipped off a ring. Lower the blind fully and check each cord route from bottom to top — every cord must pass through every ring in its column.

⏱ 3 min
7

Install cord cleat (mandatory)

The cord cleat mounts on the wall next to the window. Its purpose is to secure the loose cords when the blind is raised, preventing a hanging loop that children could become entangled in.

Mounting position: At least 1.6m above the floor — out of reach of children. On the same side as the operating cord, within comfortable arm's reach for adults.

Drill and fix the cord cleat to the wall using the included screws. When the blind is raised, wrap the excess cord firmly around the cleat in a figure-eight pattern.

⚠️ This is Mandatory

Under AS/NZS 13120, all corded internal window coverings in Australia must have a cord restraint device. A cord cleat costs nothing and takes 2 minutes to install. There is no excuse for skipping it.

⏱ 3 min

Ceiling / Top Fix Installation

Top fix is used for recess mounting (screws go up into the top of the window recess) or true ceiling mounting (for floor-to-ceiling windows). The process is identical to face fix except:

Diagram

Top Fix — Bracket Orientation (Recess & Ceiling)
  • Rotate brackets 90° so the flat mounting face points upward. Most Roman blind brackets are dual-purpose — check which holes to use for top fix.
  • Recess top fix: Position brackets 20mm from the front edge of the recess. This sets the fabric back slightly so it hangs within the recess without catching on the frame.
  • Ceiling fix: Locate joists with a stud finder. If joists don't align with your desired bracket positions, use rated ceiling toggle anchors (20kg+ working load per anchor).
  • Don't use plastic wall plugs in ceilings. Gravity works against you. Toggle anchors or direct-into-joist fixing only.
💡 Timber Batten Method

For ceiling mounts where joists don't align: fix a 30mm × 18mm timber batten across the ceiling, secured into two joists. Then mount the Roman blind brackets into the timber batten at whatever spacing you need. This gives unlimited positioning flexibility.

Understanding Stack Height

Stack height is the vertical space the Roman blind fabric occupies when fully raised. Unlike roller blinds (which roll into a compact tube), Roman blinds fold into horizontal pleats that stack on top of each other. This means the top portion of your window will be obscured when the blind is open.

Approximate stack height = Number of folds × Fold depth (typically 200–250mm per fold)

A typical 2m drop Roman blind has 7–8 folds, giving a stack height of about 280–350mm when fully raised.

This is why face fix (mounting above the frame) is recommended — the stack sits above the window glass rather than blocking it.

Diagram

Stack Height — Raised vs Lowered Position
💡 Minimising Stack

If maximum glass exposure is important, consider: (1) Face fix mounting so the stack sits above the frame, (2) A Roman blind with closer fold spacing (smaller folds = tighter stack), or (3) A flat-fold Roman style instead of cascading soft folds.

Child Safety Compliance

Roman blinds pose a higher cord-entanglement risk than roller blinds because they typically have multiple hanging cords rather than a single chain loop. Australian safety standards are strict — and rightly so.

  • Cord cleat: Mandatory. Fixed at minimum 1.6m height. All cords wrapped around it when the blind is raised.
  • No free-hanging loops: If your Roman blind has multiple pull cords, they must be tied together below the cord lock and secured to the cleat. No individual cord should be able to form a loop a child could get tangled in.
  • Minimum cord height: When the blind is fully lowered, any free cord must still be at least 1.6m above the floor.
  • Furniture placement: No beds, cots, highchairs, or climbable furniture within 1.5m of the window.
⚠️ For Nurseries & Children's Bedrooms

We strongly recommend cordless Roman blinds (chain-drive or motorised) for any room where children sleep or play unsupervised. A cordless Roman blind has a chain-operated headrail (like a roller blind) instead of hanging cords — with a chain tensioner for safety. Or go fully motorised with zero cords. See our childproofing guide for room-by-room recommendations.

Troubleshooting

Roman blinds are mechanically simple — most problems come down to cord routing, bracket alignment, or fold stiffener positioning.

Blind raises unevenly — one side lifts before the other
The most common issue. Check that all cords are threaded through every ring in their column. A missed ring means that cord has slack — causing uneven lifting. Lower the blind fully, inspect the back, and re-thread any cords that have slipped off a ring. If cords are correct, check that the cord lock isn't jammed — sometimes one cord gets caught in the lock mechanism.
Folds don't stack neatly — fabric bunches instead of folding
Check that the dowels (stiffener rods) in each fold pocket haven't shifted. Each fold has a horizontal pocket on the back that holds a thin dowel — this keeps the fold crisp. If a dowel has slipped sideways, push it back to centre. If a fold pocket is empty, contact your supplier for replacement dowels. Also ensure all ring positions are vertically aligned — if a ring is sewn off-centre, the fold will twist.
Cord lock won't hold — blind slides down after releasing
The cord lock mechanism may be worn or the cords may be too slippery for it to grip. Try pulling the cord at a steeper angle (more toward the wall, not straight down). If it still slips, the cord lock may need replacing — these are standard parts available from blind hardware suppliers. As a temporary fix, use the cord cleat to hold the blind at your desired height.
Headrail won't clip into brackets
Check that you're inserting the headrail the right way — the clip channel is usually on the back (wall side) of the headrail. Try pressing one end in first, then pushing the other end until it clicks. If the headrail is slightly bowed (common on longer blinds), flex it gently while clicking in. Some brackets need a slight upward push followed by a forward rotation to engage.
Fabric sags or bows between brackets
Your blind needs a centre bracket. Any Roman blind over 1.5m wide should have at least three brackets. Add a centre bracket and the sag will disappear. For very wide blinds (2m+), use four brackets spaced evenly across the width.
Bottom bar is crooked when the blind is lowered
Check that the weight bar / bottom rod is fully inserted and hasn't shifted to one side. Some bottom bars are adjustable — slide the bar so it protrudes equally from each end. Also check brackets are level — hold a spirit level across both bracket faces.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install Roman blinds myself?
Yes — Roman blind installation is a straightforward DIY job if you can use a drill and spirit level. The headrail bracket system is simpler than curtain tracks. Allow 30–45 minutes for your first window, 15–20 minutes for each additional window.
What brackets do Roman blinds use?
Most use universal L-brackets or clip-in brackets that work for both face fix (wall) and top fix (ceiling/recess). Custom or handmade Roman blinds often use a Velcro-wrapped timber batten that sits on simple L-brackets. Your blind supplier should include the correct brackets.
How far above the window should I mount a Roman blind?
For face fix: 80–150mm above the window frame. Higher = more dramatic look and more glass exposed when raised. The key is mounting high enough that the stack height (folded fabric) sits above the glass line — otherwise you block light even when the blind is "open."
Do Roman blinds need a cord cleat?
Yes — it's a mandatory safety requirement under Australian standards (AS/NZS 13120). The cord cleat must be fixed at minimum 1.6m height, with all cords wrapped around it when the blind is raised. Alternatively, choose a cordless chain-drive or motorised mechanism.
What is Roman blind stack height?
Stack height is the vertical space the folded fabric occupies when the blind is fully raised. Typically 250–350mm depending on drop length and fold spacing. This matters because it determines how much glass is blocked when the blind is open. Face fix mounting positions the stack above the frame to maximise light.
Can I wash a Roman blind?
Most Roman blinds can be spot-cleaned or gently vacuumed with a soft brush attachment. Some have removable fabric (detaches from headrail via Velcro) that can be hand-washed or dry-cleaned. Always check the fabric care label. Never machine wash unless the manufacturer specifically says it's safe — the dowels and rings need to be removed first.
