How to Install Blinds on Bifold Doors

Bifold doors need a blind system that works with the door mechanism, not against it. This guide covers the three main approaches — vertical blinds, panel glide tracks, and perfect fit clip-in systems — with SVG diagrams, bracket positions, and troubleshooting for each.

01 — Choose Your Blind System

The most important decision for bifold door blinds is where the blind is mounted, not which fabric you choose. Unlike standard windows, a bifold door opening is wide (often 3–5m) and the door mechanism needs to fold freely. There are three approaches:

System How It Works Opening Width Drilling? Best For
Vertical Blinds Most Popular Track fixed above opening; louvres hang down and draw to one side Up to 6m Yes (wall/ceiling) Indoor/outdoor rooms, large openings, light control
Panel Glide Wide fabric panels on a sliding multi-channel track above opening Up to 5m Yes (wall/ceiling) Contemporary interiors, sheer/blockout fabric options
Perfect Fit Blind clips into glass bead of each door panel — no screws into frame Per panel only No Rentals, privacy on fixed panels, uPVC frame doors
💡 Pro Tip

For most Australian homes with 3–5m bi-fold openings to a deck or courtyard, vertical blinds are the right choice. They draw fully to one side (matching the door's own stacking direction), handle wide spans without sagging, and come in outdoor fabrics suited to covered alfresco areas.

Before ordering, confirm: (1) where the blind track will be fixed — wall above, ceiling, or timber soffit; (2) which side the blind will stack when the doors are fully open; and (3) your exact opening width including any architrave overlap.

⚠️ Wall Substrate

The wall above a bifold door opening is often a load-bearing lintel zone. Check what you're drilling into before starting — brick veneer, double brick, and timber frame each need different plugs and screw sizes. When in doubt, use a professional installer.

03 — Installing Vertical Blinds

Vertical blinds are the most DIY-friendly choice for bifold doors. The headrail / track mounts above the opening and the louvres hang from rotating carriers inside the track.

Diagram

Vertical Blind — Bracket Positions & Clearances
1

Mark the track centre line

Draw a level pencil line on the wall 50–75mm above the top of the bifold door frame. Use your longest spirit level and check against a laser level if available. The track must be perfectly horizontal — even 3mm of tilt will cause louvres to hang unevenly across a wide opening.

⏱ ~10 min
2

Mark bracket positions

Mark end brackets first — 20mm in from each end of the track. Then space intermediate brackets evenly, with no gap exceeding 600mm. For a 4m track you'll need at least 7 brackets. Mark all positions clearly before drilling anything.

  • End brackets: 20mm from each track end
  • Intermediate spacing: max 600mm apart
  • Mark with pencil X through the bracket screw holes
⏱ ~10 min
3

Drill and fix brackets

Drill pilot holes at each mark. Use a 6mm masonry bit for brick/plaster, timber bit for timber frames. Insert appropriate plugs (red plastic plugs for plaster over brick; no plug needed for solid timber). Drive screws firmly — each bracket should not move under hand pressure.

⏱ ~20 min
4

Cut track to length (if required)

If your track came in a standard length and needs trimming, cut it to opening width + 80mm (40mm overhang each side). Use a hacksaw with a fine-tooth metal blade. Cut squarely and file the end smooth to prevent carriers catching on sharp burrs.

⏱ ~10 min
5

Clip track to brackets

Most vertical blind tracks clip up into face-fix brackets from below. Start at one end, engage the back lip first, then press the front down until it clicks. Work along each bracket in sequence. Confirm the track doesn't flex or bow — if it does, add another intermediate bracket.

⏱ ~10 min
6

Load carriers and louvres

Slide all carriers into the open end of the track. Insert each louvre stem into a carrier, aligning the clip groove. Load louvres from the stacking side first — they should be numbered or colour-coded by the manufacturer. Once all louvres are loaded, thread the bottom chain through each and clip the chain together to keep louvres aligned.

⏱ ~20 min
7

Test operation with doors open and closed

Draw the blind fully open and fully closed. Check: louvres hang plumb, rotate smoothly with the wand/cord, and the bottom chain lies flat. Then open the bifold doors fully — confirm the blind stack sits clear of the door handles and door stack. Adjust the stack depth or louvre spacing if needed.

⏱ ~10 min

04 — Installing Panel Glide Blinds

Panel glide systems use wide fabric panels (typically 600–1000mm wide) that slide across a multi-channel track. They give a cleaner, more architectural look than vertical louvres and work well with sheer, textured, or blockout fabrics.

Diagram

Panel Glide — Track Channels & Panel Stack Clearance

The key difference from vertical blinds is that panel glide systems need stack clearance — space beside the opening where the panels bunch up when fully open. Each panel is roughly 600–900mm wide, so a 3-panel system needs 600–900mm of clear wall space on the stacking side. Plan this before you order.

1

Confirm stack clearance and track length

Track length = opening width + stack clearance. With 3 panels at 700mm each, you need at least 700mm of extra wall beside the opening. If wall space is limited, choose fewer, wider panels or consider vertical blinds instead.

2

Mark and fix bracket positions

Panel glide tracks are heavy when loaded — space brackets at maximum 400mm intervals. The track must be absolutely level; even 2mm over a 4m span causes panels to drift. Use a 2m spirit level and check both ends independently.

3

Load panels in correct channel sequence

Panel glide wagons (the carriers) are numbered — each goes in a specific channel. Load the rear-most panel first (deepest channel), then work forward. Insert the wagon at the open end of the track and slide it to the stacking side before loading the next panel.

4

Hook fabric panels onto wagons

Clip the panel's top velcro or hook strip onto the wagon's bottom hook bar. Ensure fabric is straight before clipping. Add the bottom hem bar weight to each panel to ensure they hang taut. Adjust spacing until panels overlap correctly when closed (typically 20–30mm overlap at each join).

5

Adjust and test

Pull the draw cord to close the panels. Check joins are aligned and panels hang plumb. If a panel leans forward or backward, adjust the wagon tilt screw (usually an Allen key adjuster on the wagon body). Open fully and confirm panels stack neatly and bifold doors clear the panel stack.

05 — Installing Perfect Fit Blinds

Perfect fit blinds clip directly into the glass bead channel around each bifold door panel — no drilling, no screws, no damage to the door frame. They're ideal for uPVC bi-fold doors, rental properties, or when you want privacy on individual panels without a track across the full opening.

⚠️ Compatibility Check

Perfect fit systems require a specific glass bead channel depth (usually 14–20mm deep, 10–12mm wide). Measure your door frame channel before ordering. They do not work on timber doors with exposed rebate frames, doors with flat aluminium beads, or doors with decorative glazing bars.

1

Measure each panel individually

With bifold doors, each panel opening is the same size only if the doors were manufactured to exact tolerances — which they often aren't. Measure each panel's glass bead channel width and height separately. Order each blind to those exact dimensions.

2

Clean the glass bead channel

Wipe out any dust, sealant residue, or debris from the bead channel. The clip-in brackets need firm contact with the inside of the channel to stay secure. A clean channel is essential for a clip that won't work loose.

3

Fit the frame clips

Push the four corner clips (or continuous frame sections, depending on system) into the glass bead channel. They should snap in firmly. Test by pulling the clip outward — it should resist firmly. Apply any supplied wedge pieces if the fit feels loose.

4

Click the blind cassette in

Snap the roller blind cassette (or pleated blind frame) into the clips from the glass side. It should click into place with firm hand pressure on each corner. The blind now moves with the door — when the panel opens, the blind opens too.

5

Test blind operation and door movement

Raise and lower the blind, then open the door panel. The blind should travel smoothly with the panel and not bind at any hinge point. If the blind fouls on the adjacent panel or door frame, check the clip position — most allow ±2mm adjustment.

06 — Troubleshooting

Common issues and their fixes for bifold door blind installations:

Louvres won't rotate — wand turns but slats stay put
A carrier has disengaged from the gear strip inside the headrail. Slide all louvres to one end, locate the wayward carrier (it won't turn with the others), press it firmly down into the track so the gear engages, then test rotation. If the gear strip itself is cracked, that section of headrail needs replacement.
Blind track sags in the middle under the weight of louvres
You need more intermediate brackets. For tracks over 2.4m, add brackets at every 400–500mm. A sagging track puts uneven load on the central louvres and causes them to hang at an angle. This is the most common installation mistake on wide bifold openings.
Panel glide panels don't overlap properly when closed
The wagons aren't positioned correctly in the track. Each wagon has a fine-adjust screw that shifts it left or right by a few mm — use this to align overlaps. If the gap is large, check that you loaded panels in the correct numbered sequence (panel 1 in channel 1, etc.) per the manufacturer instructions.
Perfect fit clips won't stay in — blind keeps falling out
The glass bead channel may be too wide for your clips. Some uPVC systems use a wider-than-standard channel. Contact your supplier with the exact channel depth and width; most manufacturers offer an extended-clip version for wider channels. Do not use tape or adhesive as a substitute.
Bifold door catches on the blind when opening
The blind track is too low — the door handle is hitting the track or the bottom of the louvres are catching the door top rail. Raise the track (or reposition screws) so there's at least 10mm clearance between the door top rail and the lowest point of the track. For vertical blinds, also check that the bottom chain isn't resting on the door top edge.
Louvres splay out in the wind / outdoor use
The bottom chain is missing or incorrectly threaded. Every louvre should be connected via the bottom weight chain in a continuous loop. Ensure the chain passes through the small hole at the bottom of each louvre and the chain clips are engaged. For covered outdoor areas with regular wind, upgrade to heavier louvres (120mm+ width) with a heavier bottom chain.

07 — Pro Tips for Bifold Door Blinds

💡 Stack Direction

Your blind should stack to the same side as your bifold doors fold to. If the doors fold to the left, the blind should also draw left. This way, when the doors are fully open, both the door stack and the blind stack are on the same side — maximising your opening width.

💡 Outdoor Areas

If your bifold opens to an alfresco or outdoor area, choose UV-stabilised outdoor-rated louvre fabric (PVC or solution-dyed acrylic). Standard indoor fabrics deteriorate rapidly in direct sun and moisture. Outdoor vertical blinds use heavier 89mm or 127mm louvres with weighted bottoms to resist wind movement.

💡 Motorisation

Wide bifold door blinds are excellent candidates for motorisation. A motorised vertical or panel glide system lets you control a 5m wide blind from your phone or voice assistant — ideal when the doors are open and you can't reach the wand. Most Australian motorised systems work with Google Home, Amazon Alexa, and Apple HomeKit.

⚠️ Child Safety

Vertical blind chains are a strangulation hazard for children under 6. Always install the supplied chain tensioner or wind-up device at the wall so the chain loop cannot hang below 1.6m. If your blind came without a tensioner, contact the supplier — it's legally required in Australia under AS/NZS 1935.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about installing blinds on bifold doors.

What is the best type of blind for bifold doors?
Vertical blinds are the most popular choice for bifold doors in Australia — they draw completely to one side (like the doors themselves), don't obstruct the door mechanism, and handle wide openings easily. Panel glide systems offer a more contemporary look. Perfect fit clip-in blinds are ideal when you want privacy on individual panels without any drilling.
Can I install a single blind across the full bifold opening?
Yes — vertical blinds and panel glide systems are specifically designed to span wide openings of 3–5m. The blind is installed at the top of the doorway (not on the door itself), so it remains stationary while the bifold doors open and close underneath.
Do bifold door blinds need to be ceiling mounted?
Not necessarily. You have three options: face fix to the wall above the opening (most common), ceiling/soffit mount inside the recess if there's enough depth, or mount to the door frame header if the architrave is deep enough. Ceiling mount gives the cleanest look but requires a solid ceiling substrate.
Will my bifold doors still open fully with blinds installed?
Yes, if you choose a system designed for bifolds. Vertical blinds and panel glide systems mount above the opening so the blind stack sits at the side when doors are fully open. Confirm the blind's stacking width doesn't block door handles or obstruct the folded door stack.
Can I use roller blinds on bifold doors?
Roller blinds can work but are not ideal unless you're mounting one blind per panel and don't need to open all panels at once. The blind must be mounted on the door itself, which means it moves with the door — fine for fixed panels, awkward for frequently operated ones. For best results, use a vertical blind or panel glide across the full opening.
