How to Install Blackout Blinds
Without Light Gaps

⏱ 30–45 minutes
🔧 Medium difficulty
👤 1–2 people
📐 Drill required

A blackout blind that lets light creep in at the edges defeats its purpose. This guide covers bracket fitting, side channel installation, and the finishing details that make the difference between 80% blackout and true darkness.

Before You Start

⚠️ Child safety (mandatory): All blinds with an operating chain must have a child-safety tensioner or cleat fixed to the wall. This is required under Australian Standard AS 2999. Your blind should be supplied with one — do not skip this step.

Blackout blinds are usually roller blinds with a thicker, coated fabric. The installation process is similar to a standard roller blind, but light control is the priority — so you need to think carefully about mount type and whether to use side channels.

Inside mount vs outside mount — which is better for blackout?

Outside mount wins for blackout performance. When the blind extends well beyond the window frame on all sides, there's simply less opportunity for light to get around it. Inside mount looks cleaner but requires side channels to prevent edge leakage.

About side channels

Side channels (also called guide tracks or side rails) are U-shaped plastic or aluminium profiles fixed to the inside walls of the window recess. The blind fabric runs inside them, sealing the edges. They're the best solution for inside-mount blackout blinds.

Not all blackout blinds come with side channels — check your order confirmation. If yours didn't include them, they can usually be ordered separately or sourced from a hardware store.

What's in the Box

Tools & Materials

  • Power drill with 6mm bit (masonry for brick/render, timber for wooden frames)
  • Spirit level — essential for keeping channels and brackets plumb
  • Steel tape measure
  • Pencil for marking positions
  • Phillips head screwdriver
  • Step ladder
  • Wall anchors/plugs — use appropriate type for your wall (masonry plugs for brick, plasterboard anchors for stud walls)
Tip: For plasterboard walls, locate the nearest stud with a stud finder before drilling. A properly anchored blind bracket is much more secure than a plug in plasterboard alone.

Step-by-Step Installation

Unbox and identify all parts

Lay everything on a flat surface. Find the pin end (a rounded pin that spins freely) and the chain end (square socket with the chain attached) of the roller. The brackets are handed — they're not interchangeable.

If side channels are included, identify which is left and which is right (the channel opening faces inward toward the blind fabric).

Install side channels (if applicable)

This must be done before fitting the brackets. Hold each channel against the side wall of the window recess (or the wall, for outside mount). The channel track faces inward — toward where the blind will travel.

Use a spirit level to ensure the channel is perfectly plumb (vertical). Mark all screw positions, drill pilot holes, insert plugs, and screw firmly. Repeat for the other side.

⚠️ Level matters: If the channels aren't perfectly plumb, the fabric will bind or pull to one side as you lower the blind. Take your time here.

Mark bracket positions

Inside mount: Hold each bracket flush with the front face of the recess. The bracket should sit directly above the channel (not on top of it). Mark your screw holes with a pencil. Both brackets must be at the same height — use your tape measure to confirm, not your eye.

Outside mount (no channels): Mark bracket positions at your desired height above the window frame. The blind should extend at least 60mm beyond the frame on each side, and at least 100mm above the top of the glass.

Drill and fix the brackets

Drill pilot holes at your marked positions. Insert appropriate wall anchors. Screw brackets firmly — each bracket carries a significant portion of the blind's weight, and a loose bracket will cause the blind to sag or tilt.

For masonry (brick, render, concrete): use masonry plugs. For timber frames: self-tapping screws directly into timber. For plasterboard: use plasterboard anchors rated for the blind's weight, or find a stud.

Clip in the roller blind

With the brackets fixed, slide the pin end into its bracket (the round one) first, then press the chain end firmly into its bracket until you hear it click. Give the blind a gentle side-to-side tug to confirm it's seated and won't fall.

Tip: Before lowering the blind, manually confirm which way the fabric rolls. Standard roll is fabric coming off the front of the roller (toward you). Reverse roll (fabric off the back) is sometimes used for inside mount to sit flatter against the window — check your blind's specifications.

Feed fabric into side channels

Lower the blind slowly — about 300mm — and check that both edges of the fabric are sitting inside the channel tracks. The fabric should slide freely with light resistance. If it's bunching or one side is falling out, gently guide it back into the track and lower again.

Lower all the way to the sill. The bottom bar should reach the sill or sit just above it (within 5mm) for best blackout at the base.

Fix the child-safety chain tensioner

The chain tensioner must be fixed to the wall (or skirting board) at a point that keeps the chain loop taut. In Australia this is a legal requirement under AS 2999. Screw the tensioner anchor firmly — don't rely on adhesive.

Hook the chain loop through the tensioner. The chain should be taut enough that a child cannot easily create a loop larger than 220mm.

Test for light gaps

Close the blind fully. Turn off any lights, close the door, and wait for your eyes to adjust. Look specifically at:

  • Top of blind — between headrail and ceiling/recess top. A cassette fascia or foam strip fixes this.
  • Side edges — fabric should sit inside channels with no visible gap
  • Bottom bar — should sit against the sill; add foam draught seal if gap persists
✓ True blackout: If the room is genuinely dark with the door closed and the blind down, you've nailed it. Light leaks are fixable — see the troubleshooting section below.

Sealing Remaining Light Gaps

Even a well-installed blind can have small gaps. Here are targeted fixes:

Top gap (between headrail and ceiling/recess)

A cassette or pelmet is the premium fix — it completely encloses the headrail and blocks light at the top. Alternatively, self-adhesive foam draught-seal tape (5–8mm) pressed to the top of the recess works for small gaps.

Side gaps (edges leaking past channels)

If light leaks despite having side channels, check that the channel is fully closed and the fabric is genuinely inside the track. Some channels have an adjustable track width — check if yours can be tightened. If the channel has warped slightly, shimming behind it with cardboard and re-screwing can help.

Bottom gap (under the bottom bar)

A strip of self-adhesive foam seal on the window sill creates a light-tight contact point. Choose a density that allows the blind to reach the sill without excessive resistance on the chain.

When to Call a Professional

DIY installation is fine for most standard windows. Consider professional installation if:

  • Your window is wider than 2400mm (large blinds are heavy and difficult to align solo)
  • You have heritage-listed windows and can't drill into the frames
  • The wall is double-brick with no stud access and you're not comfortable with masonry work
  • Your blind is motorised with hardwired power (requires an electrician)
  • You've attempted installation and have persistent light gaps you can't resolve

FAQ

Do blackout blinds need side channels?
Not always, but side channels dramatically improve blackout performance. Without them, light leaks in at the edges — especially with inside mount. If you're using blackout blinds for shift workers, babies, or home cinema, we strongly recommend side channels or a cassette/flasher option.
What's the minimum recess depth for blackout blinds with side channels?
You need at least 55mm of recess depth to fit a standard blackout roller blind with side channels. If your recess is shallower, choose outside mount instead.
How do I stop light coming in at the bottom?
A weighted bottom bar helps, but if light still leaks through the gap between the bottom bar and the sill, add a small strip of self-adhesive foam draught seal to the sill. Alternatively, order a blind with a floor-to-ceiling drop so the bottom rests on the sill.
Can I install blackout blinds without drilling?
Tension-fit no-drill brackets work in some uPVC window frames, but they can't support the side channels needed for full blackout. For a true no-light-gap installation, drilling is required. If you can't drill, consider outside mount with a large overlap instead of side channels.
My blackout blind is crooked after installation. What went wrong?
The most common cause is one bracket sitting higher than the other. Remove the blind, re-check both bracket heights with a spirit level, and refit the lower bracket. A second common cause is the pin end not fully seating in its bracket — push it in firmly until it clicks.
Can I use a blackout blind in a bathroom or kitchen (high humidity)?
Standard blackout blinds can warp in very humid environments because the PVC coating may respond to moisture. Choose a blind specifically rated for wet areas — these use a moisture-stable backing. Ask your supplier before ordering.
