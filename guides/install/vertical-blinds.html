How to Install Vertical Blinds

Ideal for sliding doors and wide windows — vertical blinds use a track-and-louvre system operated by a wand or cord. This guide covers wall mount, ceiling mount, and recess mount with full bracket diagrams and troubleshooting.

What's in the Box

Before you start, lay everything out on a clean flat surface and check all components against your order. Vertical blinds have more individual parts than most other blinds — it's worth counting now rather than discovering a missing louvre halfway through installation.

  • Headrail — aluminium track that houses the carrier mechanism. Check the length matches your measurement.
  • Mounting brackets — 2–4 depending on your track width. Includes end brackets and centre support brackets for wider tracks.
  • Centre support bracket — for tracks wider than 1.5m. Prevents mid-span sag and keeps the carriers running smoothly.
  • Louvre carriers — hook clips that travel along the track. Each louvre hangs from one carrier. Count should match your louvre count.
  • Louvres / blades — fabric, PVC, or aluminium vertical strips. Count these carefully — they should match your order quantity exactly.
  • Bottom weights — small metal or plastic inserts sewn into pockets at the bottom of each louvre. Keep the louvres hanging straight.
  • Bottom chain — links the louvres together at the bottom for even spacing and prevents flapping in a breeze.
  • Wand — clear or white plastic rod for rotating louvres and drawing them along the track.
💡 Tip

If anything is missing or damaged, contact us before drilling any holes — we can ship replacement parts same day within Melbourne metro.

Bracket Mounting Options

Vertical blinds can be mounted in three ways. Your choice depends on your window type, recess depth, and the look you're after. Sliding doors almost always use ceiling mount.

🪟 Wall / Face Mount

Outside Recess

Brackets fixed to the wall above the window frame. The track and louvres sit in front of the window opening.

✓ Covers full window, hides frame imperfections
✓ Works with shallow or no recess
✓ Easy to install — simple wall fix
✗ Protrudes from wall
✗ Takes up more space when louvres are open
⬆️ Ceiling Mount

Inside or Outside Recess

Brackets fixed to the ceiling. Ideal for floor-to-ceiling windows, sliding doors, and maximising drop length.

✓ Clean, seamless look
✓ Maximises drop length
✓ Perfect for sliding doors
✗ Must hit joists or use toggle anchors
✗ Harder to align — need two people
📐 Top-of-Recess Mount

Inside the Window Recess

Brackets fixed to the top of the window recess. The track sits within the reveal for a flush, integrated look.

✓ Neat, flush appearance
✓ Doesn't protrude from wall
✓ Clean architectural look
✗ Needs min. 75mm recess depth
✗ Louvres may not clear window handles

Know Your Wall / Ceiling Type

Getting the right fixing for your surface is the single most important factor in a long-lasting installation. The wrong plug or anchor is the most common reason brackets pull out.

🪵

Timber Stud / Plasterboard

Use a stud finder to locate timber studs behind plasterboard. Screw directly into the stud — no wall plug needed. If between studs, use toggle anchors rated for the blind's weight.

Stud finder + direct screw
🧱

Masonry / Brick

Use a masonry drill bit (5mm). Drill 35–40mm deep. Insert plastic wall plugs before screwing. Most Melbourne homes have brick — this is the most common scenario.

Masonry bit + wall plug
🏗️

Concrete Ceiling

Requires a hammer drill with concrete-rated bit. Use concrete anchors (expansion bolts or Dynabolts). Pre-drill to the exact depth specified for your anchors.

Hammer drill + concrete anchors
⬜

Plasterboard Ceiling

Always try to locate a joist (typically 450mm apart). If mounting between joists, use spring toggle anchors rated for dynamic load. Standard wall plugs will pull through plasterboard ceilings.

Joist or spring toggle anchors

Step-by-Step Installation

These 9 steps cover the full installation process from unboxing to final testing. Allow 20–40 minutes per window depending on your wall type and experience.

1

Unpack and check components

Lay out all parts on a clean, flat surface. Count that your louvres match the quantity on your order confirmation. Check the headrail length matches your measurement — hold a tape measure along it to verify.

Identify each component: headrail (track), brackets, louvre carriers, louvres/blades, wand, bottom weights, and bottom chain. Set aside any pelmet/fascia for the final step.

⚠️ Before Drilling

If anything is wrong — wrong length, missing louvres, damaged track — contact us immediately. Don't drill holes for a blind that won't fit.

⏱ 5 min
2

Mark bracket positions

Wall mount: Position brackets 50–100mm above the top of the window frame. The first and last brackets should sit within 100mm of the headrail ends.

Ceiling mount: Position brackets so the headrail sits flush with the front edge of the window recess (or 50mm in front of the glass for face-mounted ceiling installs).

Centre support: For tracks over 1.5m, add a centre support bracket every 600–900mm. A 2.4m sliding door track typically needs 3–4 brackets total.

💡 Level Check

Use a spirit level to draw a faint horizontal line at your bracket height. All brackets must sit on this line — even a 3mm discrepancy will show as tilted louvres across a wide track.

⏱ 5 min
3

Drill pilot holes and fix brackets

Select the correct drill bit for your wall or ceiling type. Drill each pilot hole to the recommended depth — typically 35–40mm for masonry. Keep the drill perpendicular to the surface.

  • Masonry / brick: 5mm masonry bit, wall plugs, hammer drill mode
  • Plasterboard into stud: 3mm pilot bit, screw directly, no plug
  • Plasterboard (no stud): toggle anchors or spring toggles
  • Concrete ceiling: hammer drill + concrete anchors

Insert wall plugs (if needed), then screw brackets firmly into place. Check level across all brackets before moving on.

⏱ 8 min
4

Click headrail into brackets

Most vertical blind brackets have a clip mechanism. Push the headrail up into the brackets and click or snap them closed. You should hear a firm click as the headrail locks in place.

Give the headrail a gentle downward pull to confirm it's secure in all brackets. If it wobbles or shifts, re-check each bracket clip — one end likely hasn't seated fully.

💡 Wide Tracks

For tracks over 2m, have a second person hold one end while you engage the other. Rest the far end on a step ladder temporarily if you're working alone.

⏱ 3 min
5

Attach louvre carriers

Louvre carriers clip into the track channel inside the headrail. Slide each carrier into the channel opening (usually at one end of the track) and space them evenly along the rail.

Most custom-ordered vertical blinds come with carriers already loaded in the headrail — in this case, simply check they're evenly distributed and slide freely along the track.

⏱ 3 min
6

Hang louvres onto carriers

Hook each louvre onto its carrier. The plastic hook at the top of each louvre clips into the carrier slot — push it up and you'll hear/feel a small click as it engages.

Important: All louvres should face the same direction. The seam or textured side should all face the same way. If one is backwards, the louvres won't close flush against each other.

💡 Tip

Start from one end and work systematically to the other. This is much faster than jumping around — and you're less likely to skip a carrier.

⏱ 5 min
7

Attach bottom weights and chain

Check that each louvre has its bottom weight inserted into the sewn pocket at the bottom. Weights keep the louvres hanging straight and prevent them from curling or twisting.

Thread the bottom chain through the small slots or hooks on each weight, linking all louvres together. The chain maintains even spacing between louvres and prevents them from flapping in a breeze or when the door/window is open.

⏱ 5 min
8

Test operation — wand rotate and draw

Rotate the wand to open and close the louvres. They should all tilt together in unison — if one lags behind, its carrier clip may need re-seating.

Draw the wand sideways to stack the louvres to one end (or split-draw to both ends, depending on your track type). Check for smooth operation — the carriers should glide freely without catching or jamming.

⚠️ Don't Force It

If the track feels stiff, don't force the wand. Check for carriers that aren't properly seated, or debris in the track channel. A light spray of silicone lubricant along the track can help.

⏱ 3 min
9

Fit pelmet / fascia if applicable

If your vertical blind came with a decorative pelmet or fascia, clip it over the headrail to conceal the brackets and track mechanism. Most fascias engage from the front — press the bottom edge in first and rotate up to click into the headrail channel.

Fit end caps to both ends for a clean finish. Some pelmets attach via a separate mounting strip — if included, this strip should already be screwed to the brackets before clipping in the headrail.

⏱ 3 min

Wand vs Cord Draw

Vertical blinds use two main control methods. In Australia, wand control is by far the most common — and for good reason.

✓ Child-Safe

Wand Control

A single rigid wand handles both rotation (twisting the louvres open/closed) and drawing (sliding louvres along the track to stack them). No loose cords or chains hang from the headrail.

  • Single wand does both rotate and draw
  • No loose cords — inherently child-safe
  • Most common system in Australia
  • Simple, intuitive operation
  • Wand clips directly to the lead carrier
⚠️ Cord Safety Required

Cord / Chain Draw

Separate cord or chain for drawing the louvres, with a separate wand for rotation only. Less common in Australia and not considered child-safe without a safety device fitted.

  • Separate cord for draw + wand for rotate
  • Free-hanging cord is a strangulation hazard
  • Must fit cord cleat or tensioner (AS/NZS 2598.2)
  • Less common in modern Australian installations
  • Some older or imported vertical blinds use this system
⚠️ Safety

If your vertical blinds have a cord draw, you must fit a cord safety device — this is a legal requirement under Australian standards for all corded window coverings in homes.

Troubleshooting

Most vertical blind problems have straightforward fixes. Work through these before calling for a service visit:

Louvres won't rotate evenly
Check that each louvre carrier clip is properly seated in the track. Remove the affected louvre, unclip the carrier, and re-seat it. If one carrier's rotation gear is worn, it may need replacing — these are inexpensive and available from most blind suppliers. Also check the wand connection to the lead carrier is firm.
Track is stiff or hard to draw
Clean the track channel with a dry cloth to remove dust and debris. Apply a light spray of silicone lubricant along the full length of the track channel. Avoid WD-40 or oil-based lubricants — they attract dust and will make the problem worse over time. If individual carriers are stiff, remove them and check for cracked or worn rollers.
Louvres hang at different heights
Check that all bottom weights are present and properly inserted into their pockets. A missing weight will cause that louvre to hang shorter. Also check carrier spacing is even — uneven spacing can make louvres appear to hang at different heights. If louvres are different lengths, they may have been cut incorrectly — contact your supplier.
Headrail sags in the middle
The track is too long for the number of brackets supporting it. Add a centre support bracket — for tracks over 1.5m, you should have a support bracket every 600–900mm. The centre support clips onto the headrail and screws into the wall or ceiling above.
Louvres catch on window handle
The headrail is mounted too close to the window. You'll need spacer brackets (available from hardware stores) to push the track further out from the wall, or re-mount the brackets further forward. For inside-recess mounts, consider switching to a wall or ceiling mount to clear the handles.
Bottom chain keeps falling off
Check that the bottom weight pocket on each louvre is deep enough to hold the chain hook securely. If the pocket has stretched or the chain hook is bent, re-thread the chain and ensure each link sits firmly in its weight slot. For older louvres with worn pockets, a small cable tie through the weight slot can help hold the chain in place.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install vertical blinds on a sliding door?
Yes, ceiling mount is ideal for sliding doors. Ensure the track extends past both sides of the door opening so louvres can stack clear when drawn open. For a standard 2.4m sliding door, a track width of 2.7–2.8m allows full stack-back on one side.
How many brackets do I need?
Minimum 2 (one at each end). Add a centre support bracket for every 900mm of track width over 1.5m. A 2.4m sliding door track typically needs 3–4 brackets. Under-bracketing is the most common cause of headrail sag and carrier jamming.
Can I cut the headrail to fit?
Yes, aluminium headrails can be cut with a fine-tooth hacksaw. Measure twice, cut once. Deburr the cut end with fine sandpaper to prevent sharp edges. Remove the end cap before cutting and refit it after. You'll also need to remove any carriers beyond the new length.
Are vertical blinds child-safe?
Wand-operated verticals have no loose cords and are considered child-safe. If your vertical blinds have a separate cord draw, you must fit a cord safety device (cleat or tensioner) — this is required by Australian standards AS/NZS 2598.2. For maximum safety in children's rooms, always choose wand-operated systems.
My vertical blind louvres are bowing — what's wrong?
Bowing usually means the bottom weights are missing or have fallen out of their pocket. Check each louvre has its weight properly inserted. Also check the louvres aren't too long — they should clear the floor by 10–15mm. Louvres that rest on the floor will bow outward. If weights are present and length is correct, the fabric may have absorbed moisture — let them dry in a well-ventilated room.
Can I replace individual louvres?
Yes, individual louvres/blades can be replaced without removing the entire blind. Simply unhook the old one from its carrier and clip on the new one. Order matching fabric from your supplier — be aware that colour batches can vary, so provide the batch/colour code from your original order for the closest match.
