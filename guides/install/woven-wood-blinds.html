Installation Guide

How to Install Woven Wood & Bamboo Blinds

Step-by-step DIY guide with SVG diagrams — bracket placement, headrail mounting, liner handling, and cord safety for Australian homes.

⏱ Time 30–45 min
🔧 Difficulty Medium
🧰 Tools Drill, level, tape
📏 Material Natural weave / bamboo

Before You Start

Woven wood and bamboo blinds use the same headrail bracket system as roller blinds, but natural materials add a few extra considerations: the fabric is heavier than it looks, especially when lined; the weave is irregular so any tilt in your mounting will be visually obvious; and the cord lock mechanism can sit at either end of the headrail — check the label before you mark bracket positions.

⚠️
Check your wall type first.

Woven wood blinds — particularly jute, seagrass, and lined bamboo — are heavier than standard roller blinds. In plasterboard, always use toggle anchors or locate a stud. A bracket pulling out of plasterboard mid-fall will damage both the blind and the wall.

What's in the Box

Confirm all components before drilling. Standard woven wood blind kits include:

  • Headrail with cord lock mechanism (check which end the lock is on)
  • 2–3 mounting brackets (2 for blinds under 1200mm wide, 3 for wider)
  • Screws and wall plugs
  • Lift cord with wooden bead or tassel pull
  • Cord cleat for child safety compliance
  • Liner panel with attachment clips (if lined version)

Step-by-Step Installation

  1. 1

    Decide on mount type

    Inside mount: brackets fix inside the window recess — requires 65mm minimum recess depth. Outside/face mount: brackets fix to wall or ceiling above the window frame. For outside mount, plan at least 50mm overlap on each side of the glass for light control. Woven fabrics have natural light gaps in the weave, so a generous overlap is advisable.

  2. 2

    Mark bracket positions

    Place end brackets 50–75mm from each end of the headrail. For blinds wider than 1200mm, add a centre bracket at the midpoint. Hold each bracket at its intended position and mark all screw holes with pencil. Use a spirit level to confirm all brackets are perfectly level — natural weave amplifies any tilt far more visibly than a smooth fabric.

    ⚡ Pro tip: Mark brackets with tape and hang the headrail temporarily before drilling — it's much easier to adjust at this stage than after holes are drilled.
  3. 3

    Drill pilot holes and fix brackets

    Select drill bit for your wall type: 6mm masonry bit for brick, 4mm for plasterboard (use toggle anchors or stud), 3mm for timber. Drill pilot holes, insert plugs where needed, then screw brackets firmly. Re-check level on each bracket after fixing — plasterboard can compress slightly under screw pressure.

  4. 4

    Attach liner to headrail (if applicable)

    If your blind has a separate fabric liner, attach it to the headrail now — before mounting on the wall. Most liners clip onto hooks on the back of the headrail, or attach via a secondary batten. The liner hangs behind the woven fabric panel to reduce the natural light gaps inherent in bamboo and rattan weaves. Do this on the bench, not overhead.

    💡 Liners add weight. If your blind is 1500mm+ wide and lined, use a centre bracket even if the manufacturer spec only shows two.
  5. 5

    Clip headrail into brackets

    Most woven wood headrails snap or slide into the brackets from the front. Start at one end, engage the headrail into the bracket channel, then work along to the opposite bracket. Many systems include a thumbscrew or wingnut per bracket to lock the headrail in — tighten by hand only. Confirm the headrail is fully seated and level before proceeding.

  6. 6

    Test operation

    Pull the lift cord to raise the blind. Woven natural fabric will form irregular, slightly organic folds as it stacks — this is normal and is part of the aesthetic. If the blind rises unevenly left-to-right, the headrail is not level. Loosen the bracket screws slightly, adjust, and re-tighten. For new blinds, stiff natural fibres can cause uneven operation that improves after a few raises.

  7. 7

    Install cord cleat and secure cords

    Fix the cord cleat to the wall at a minimum height of 1.6m (AS/NZS 13120 — mandatory for all corded window coverings in Australia). Wrap the lift cord around the cleat when the blind is raised to keep the cord off the floor and out of reach of children. This is a legal requirement, not a suggestion.

Cord Safety — Legal Requirements

Under Australian mandatory safety standards (AS/NZS 13120), all corded window coverings must have a cord restraint device installed. For woven wood blinds:

  • Fix cord cleat to the wall at minimum 1.6m height
  • Wrap all excess cord around the cleat when the blind is raised
  • Cords must not form a loop accessible to a child when wrapped
  • Consider upgrading to a motorised version to eliminate cords entirely — particularly for rooms used by young children
⚠️
Child safety is mandatory, not optional.

Loose window blind cords are a strangulation hazard. Installing and using a cord cleat is required by law in Australia. If in doubt, contact iSeekBlinds about cordless or motorised options.

Troubleshooting

ProblemLikely causeFix
Blind rises unevenly / tilts to one side Brackets not level, or headrail not fully seated in both brackets Re-check level, adjust bracket heights, confirm headrail is clipped in at both ends
Headrail sags in the middle Missing centre bracket for wide blind Add a centre support bracket at the midpoint of the headrail
Cord lock won't hold — blind drops Cord not threaded correctly through lock mechanism, or worn lock Re-thread the cord ensuring it passes through the lock channel; replace cord lock if worn
Natural weave folds look uneven Normal for new blind — natural fibres stiffen unevenly Operate blind 10–15 times to loosen fibres; folds will even out. Persistent unevenness → check bracket level
Light gaps visible through weave Natural characteristic of woven materials Add a liner for better light control, or switch to outside mount with 50mm+ overlap for blackout effect
Brackets pulling out of plasterboard Inadequate anchors — natural blinds are heavier than they look Use toggle bolts or Molly bolts, or locate a stud behind the plasterboard

When to Call a Professional

DIY is absolutely feasible for most woven wood installations. But consider professional installation when:

  • Mounting into heritage or fibro walls where drilling could cause damage
  • Installing very large blinds (2400mm+ wide) where a second pair of hands is needed
  • Mounting motorised woven wood blinds — motor wiring requires an electrician
  • You're unsure about wall type or stud location
  • Rental property where you can't risk damage to walls

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install woven wood blinds myself?
Yes — the bracket system is identical to roller blinds. The main difference is weight: natural woven fabrics, especially lined versions, are heavier than standard roller blinds. Use appropriate wall anchors and add a centre bracket for wide blinds. Allow 30–45 minutes per window for a first install.
Do woven wood blinds need a centre bracket?
Yes, for any blind wider than 1200mm. Some heavier materials (jute, seagrass, thick bamboo) benefit from a centre bracket from 900mm width. Check the weight of your specific blind — if in doubt, add the bracket.
How do I install the liner on woven wood blinds?
Attach the liner to the headrail before mounting. Most liners clip onto hooks on the back of the headrail or attach to a secondary batten that hooks over the headrail. Always do this on a flat surface, not overhead. The liner hangs behind the woven panel to reduce natural light gaps in the weave.
Why do my woven wood blinds have light gaps?
Natural weave materials have inherent gaps between fibres — this is an aesthetic characteristic, not a defect. To reduce light gaps: use an inside liner, choose outside mount with generous overlap (50mm+ per side), or select a tighter weave fabric. Woven wood blinds are not designed to be blackout — for blackout performance, use a roller blind with blockout fabric.
What is the minimum recess depth for inside mount woven wood blinds?
65mm minimum for a lined blind, 55mm for an unlined blind. The headrail typically adds 35–45mm depth, and you need clearance for the blind to operate without the fabric rubbing on the window frame.
