Before You Start

Installing roller blinds is one of the more satisfying DIY tasks — you can do a standard window in under 20 minutes once you've done it a couple of times. Before you pick up the drill though, it's worth spending 5 minutes going through this checklist.

  • Confirm your measurements are right — check your order confirms against what's installed (not against the window). If you haven't measured yet, see our roller blind measurement guide first.
  • Check the chain side — which side did you order the chain on? Open the box and confirm it matches your room layout before drilling.
  • Identify your wall or ceiling type — rendered brick, plasterboard, timber frame, and aluminium windows all need different fixings. We cover this in the wall types section.
  • Check for obstructions — window handles, tiles, architraves, and existing hardware can all affect bracket placement.
  • Have a second person available for wide blinds (over 1.8m) or ceiling installations where you need to hold the blind while fixing brackets.
⚠️ Important

Never install roller blinds into areas with pipes or electrical cables hidden in the wall. If you're unsure, use a stud finder and pipe/cable detector before drilling any holes, particularly for ceiling mounts.

  • Roller tube with fabric wrapped
  • 2× brackets (pin bracket + chain bracket)

💡 Tip

Count your screws and plugs before you start. It sounds obvious, but discovering you're two plugs short halfway through is frustrating. If anything is missing, contact us before drilling — we can ship replacements same day.

Choosing Your Mount Type

The two primary installation options are inside mount (recess fit) and outside mount (face fix). You should have already decided this when measuring — the instructions below differ slightly depending on which you chose.

📐 Inside Mount

Recess Fit (Inside the Window Frame)

Brackets are fixed inside the recess, top face or side walls. The blind sits within the window reveal. Requires a minimum recess depth of 65mm for most roller blinds.

✓ Clean, architectural look
✓ Doesn't intrude into room
✓ Works well with shutters or curtains layered outside
✗ Some light gap around edges
✗ Recess depth minimum required
🪟 Outside Mount

Face Fix (Outside the Window Frame)

Brackets are fixed to the wall or above the window frame. The blind overlaps the window opening. Fabric should extend 25–50mm beyond the frame on each side.

✓ Maximum light block
✓ Makes window appear larger
✓ Works on any window regardless of recess depth
✗ Slightly more visible when raised
✗ Requires clear wall space above window

Know Your Wall Type

The correct fixing depends on what you're drilling into. Getting this wrong is the most common cause of brackets pulling out over time.

🧱

Brick / Rendered Masonry

Use a masonry bit. Drill 40mm deep. Insert a 6mm plastic wall plug before screwing. Most Melbourne homes have brick walls — this is the most common scenario.

Masonry bit + plug
🟫

Plasterboard (Gyprock)

If you can hit a stud behind the plasterboard, screw directly in. If between studs, use a toggle anchor (also called a butterfly anchor) rated for the blind's weight.

Hit stud or toggle anchor
🪵

Timber Frame / Architrave

Drill a pilot hole slightly smaller than the screw diameter, then drive the screw directly. No wall plug needed. Avoid splitting timber by not drilling too close to edges.

Pilot hole, no plug
🪟

Aluminium Window Frame

Use a metal drill bit. Be careful not to drill through to the inside of the frame cavity. Self-tapping metal screws work well; alternatively use pop rivets for a permanent fix.

Metal bit + self-tap screw
🏗️

Ceiling (Plasterboard)

Always try to locate a joist. Use a stud finder — joists are typically 450mm apart. If mounting between joists, use ceiling-rated toggle anchors (must be rated for dynamic load).

Joist preferred, toggle otherwise
⬜

Tiles (Kitchen / Bathroom)

Use a tile/glass drill bit at low speed with no hammer function. Mark with masking tape to prevent slipping. Be very careful — cracked tiles are expensive to replace.

Tile bit, slow speed

Installation Steps

These steps cover the standard wall-mount installation (both inside and outside mount). For ceiling mount, see the ceiling mount section. For double rollers, see double roller installation.

Diagram

Bracket Positions — Inside vs Outside Mount
1

Unbox and orient the blind

Remove the blind from the box. Identify the pin end (idle end — usually a round plastic cap) and the chain end (drive end — has the chain/bead cord hanging from it). The chain bracket has a slot through which the chain exits; the pin bracket is typically round.

Orient the blind so the chain will hang on your intended side. If the chain is on the wrong side, most modern rollers can be swapped — but contact your supplier to confirm before proceeding.

⏱ 3 min
2

Mark bracket positions

Inside mount: Hold a bracket flush against the top face (or side wall if side-fixing) of the recess. Position it 5mm from the front edge of the recess so the roller tube clears the frame when raised. Mark all screw holes with a pencil. Repeat for the other bracket.

Outside mount: Mark the bracket positions at your desired height — typically 80–100mm above the window frame. Use a tape measure to ensure both marks are exactly the same height from the floor. A 2mm discrepancy will show up as drift.

💡 Level Check

Before marking both brackets, use a spirit level or laser level to draw a faint horizontal line at your desired bracket height. Both brackets must sit on this line.

⏱ 5 min
3

Drill pilot holes

Select the correct drill bit for your wall type (see wall types section). Drill each pilot hole to the depth recommended for your wall plugs — typically 30–40mm into masonry. Keep the drill perpendicular to the wall for a flush bracket fit.

  • Brick / masonry: 6mm masonry bit, 40mm depth, use hammer drill mode
  • Plasterboard into stud: 3mm pilot bit, 50mm depth, no hammer
  • Timber frame: 2.5mm pilot bit, 40mm depth, no plug needed
  • Aluminium frame: 4mm metal bit, slow speed, no hammer

Clear drill dust from each hole before inserting plugs.

⏱ 5 min
4

Insert wall plugs and fix brackets

Tap wall plugs flush into each drilled hole using a hammer. Don't drive them too deep — the head should be level with the wall surface so the bracket sits flat.

Hold each bracket in position and drive the screws through the bracket and into the plugs. Tighten firmly but don't over-torque — over-tightening plastic plugs causes them to spin.

Once both brackets are fixed, hold a spirit level across the two bracket mounting points and verify they're perfectly horizontal. If one is high, loosen it slightly and re-set.

⏱ 8 min
5

Clip in the roller tube

Lift the roller and engage the pin end first: press the pin cap into the round socket on the pin bracket. Then swing the chain end down and press it into the chain bracket until you hear/feel a firm click. The chain should now hang freely through the slot.

Give the roller a firm downward tug to confirm it's fully engaged in both brackets. If it feels loose or wobbles, re-check both clips — one end likely hasn't seated fully.

⚠️ Weight Note

Wide blinds (1.8m+) are heavy when the tube is loaded with fabric. Have a second person hold the chain end while you engage the pin end, or rest the far end on a step ladder temporarily.

⏱ 3 min
6

Test operation and adjust spring tension

Pull the chain to lower the blind all the way. Watch the bottom bar — it should descend evenly. A slight tilt (one side lower) is normal on the first use and usually self-corrects after a few cycles.

If the blind rolls up too slowly (sags in the middle): Remove the roller, roll the fabric up 3–4 turns by hand to add spring preload, then re-clip.

If the blind shoots up too aggressively: Remove the roller, unroll 2–3 turns by hand to reduce preload, then re-clip.

⏱ 5 min
7

Install chain tensioner (mandatory)

The chain tensioner anchors the hanging chain loop to the wall, preventing it from forming a free-hanging loop that poses a child strangulation hazard. This is a mandatory safety requirement under Australian law for all corded blinds.

With the blind fully raised, note where the chain loop hangs at its lowest point. Fix the tensioner at this point — screw it into the wall at the base of the chain. The chain should sit in the tensioner's slot, held taut but still able to move freely when operated.

⏱ 5 min
8

Fit fascia (if applicable)

If your blind came with a decorative fascia or pelmet, this clips over the headrail to conceal the brackets. Most fascias engage from the front — press the bottom edge in first and rotate up to click the top into the headrail channel. Fit end caps to both ends.

Some fascias require a pilot strip that screws into the brackets first. If included, fit this strip before clipping in the roller tube.

⏱ 3 min

Ceiling Mount Installation

Ceiling mounting is popular for floor-to-ceiling windows, bifold door openings, and rooms where a seamless top-to-floor coverage is wanted. The process is similar to wall mount, but bracket orientation changes.

Diagram

Ceiling Mount — Bracket Orientation & Fixing Points
  • Rotate brackets 90° — most roller blind brackets have two fixing faces; the ceiling-mount face points upward. Check your bracket instructions for which orientation is the ceiling-mount mode.
  • Locate joists with a stud finder before marking. Joists are typically at 450mm centres. If possible, fix at least one bracket into a joist.
  • Between-joist fixing: Use rated ceiling toggle anchors (minimum 50kg working load per anchor for wide blinds). Do not use standard wall plugs in plasterboard ceiling — they will pull through.
  • Mark holes carefully — you can't easily patch and re-drill ceiling plasterboard. Measure twice before you drill.
  • Chain tensioner: For ceiling mount, fix the tensioner to the adjacent wall at the same height as the bracket, keeping the chain taut to the wall surface.
💡 Pro Tip

For ceiling mounts with no nearby joist, use a 30mm strip of timber (18mm ply works) fixed to the ceiling first. Screw the timber into two joists across the span, then fix your blind brackets into the timber. This distributes the load and gives you unlimited positioning flexibility.

Double Roller Blinds

Double roller systems let you have two blinds on the same window — typically a sunscreen for daytime privacy and glare control, and a blockout for night-time darkness. Both blinds run on the same dual bracket system.

Installation follows the same steps as a single blind, with these additions:

  • Dual brackets: Double roller brackets have two sets of fixing sockets — front-track for one blind and rear-track for the other. The blockout typically goes at the rear (closest to the window) and the sunscreen at the front.
  • Recess depth: Double rollers need a minimum recess depth of 130mm for inside mount. If your recess is shallower, you'll need to outside-mount or use a separate fascia pelmet system.
  • Two chains: Each blind has its own chain. Chains are typically on the same side but at different heights. Install the chain tensioner for both chains — two separate tensioners, both screwed to the wall.
  • Fascia: A wide fascia covers both headrails — this is strongly recommended for double rollers as the dual headrail looks cluttered when exposed.
💡 Tip

For a cleaner look on double rollers, consider motorising both blinds and operating them via a single remote or smart home scene. This eliminates four chains (two blinds × two ends) and is especially worthwhile for hard-to-reach or high windows.

Child Safety Compliance

Under Australian mandatory safety standards, all corded roller blinds must be installed with a functioning chain tensioner (also called a chain restraint or anchor). This is not optional — it's a legal requirement and a genuine safety measure.

  • Fix the tensioner at the lowest point of the chain loop, secured to the wall
  • No chain loop should hang free — the tensioner must keep the chain taut at all times
  • Minimum cord height — any free-hanging cord or chain must be kept above 1.6m from the floor when not in use
  • Keep furniture away — no beds, chairs, or climbable items within 1.5m of the window

For homes with young children, we strongly recommend replacing corded roller blinds in nurseries and children's bedrooms with motorised roller blinds — which have no chains or cords at all. See our full childproof window blinds guide for a room-by-room safety assessment.

Troubleshooting

Most roller blind problems have straightforward fixes. Work through these before calling for a service visit:

Blind rolls at an angle / bottom bar tilts
Almost always a bracket alignment issue. Remove the roller, hold a spirit level across both bracket faces, and identify which is higher. Loosen that bracket's screws slightly, adjust, and re-tighten. Even 2mm off horizontal will cause visible tilt. If brackets are level and it still drifts, try adjusting the pin bracket in or out by 1–2mm.
Blind shoots up too fast / won't stay down
Too much spring preload. Remove the roller and hold the chain end to prevent it spinning. Manually unroll the fabric 2–3 full turns while holding the tube. Re-clip and test. Repeat until the speed is comfortable.
Blind won't raise / chain feels stiff
Could be insufficient spring preload or a chain that's jumped off the drive mechanism. First, try rolling the blind up manually 2–3 turns to add preload. If the chain feels gritty or stiff, check that it's fully seated in the sprocket wheel on the chain bracket. Remove the chain bracket side cap (usually clips off), reseat the chain on the sprocket, and replace the cap.
Blind won't stay at set position (creeps up or down)
The chain mechanism relies on friction to hold position. If a blind creeps, the chain may be worn or the sprocket clutch inside the bracket is worn out. Try a new chain first (cheap fix). If the blind still creeps with a new chain, the clutch bracket assembly needs replacing — contact your supplier for a replacement mechanism.
Gap of light on one or both sides (inside mount)
This is inherent to inside mount — some light gap is normal. Minimise it by: (1) ordering the blind with minimum fabric deduction (2–5mm each side rather than 10mm), (2) using a blockout fabric that wraps slightly around the roller tube, (3) adding a side channel/reveal strip available from us as an optional accessory.
Bracket pulled out of wall
The fixing wasn't suited to the wall type. Re-assess the wall type (brick, plasterboard, stud location) and use the appropriate fixing — larger plugs in masonry, toggle anchors in plasterboard, or relocate to hit a timber stud. Fill old holes with filler before re-drilling nearby. Don't re-use the same holes with larger plugs — the hole is already oversized.
Fabric has a visible crease or fold
Lower the blind fully and leave it in the sun for a day — most creases fall out with warmth and gravity. For persistent creases, use a garment steamer held 20–30cm from the fabric (not a clothes iron — direct contact will melt most roller fabrics). Never fold or store roller blinds with the fabric creased.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a drill to install roller blinds?
For most installations yes — a drill creates far more secure fixings. Adhesive no-drill brackets exist for smooth surfaces (tiles, glass, painted plaster) but aren't recommended for blinds wider than 900mm. They're popular for rentals but check weight limits carefully.
Can I install roller blinds by myself?
Yes, for standard single rollers. A second person is helpful for blinds over 1.8m or ceiling-mount installations. The whole process takes 15–30 minutes per window once you've done the first one.
Which side should the chain go on?
You specify chain side when ordering — default is usually right. If you need to swap it, most rollers can be reversed by swapping tube inserts, but check with your supplier before disassembling anything.
How do I adjust spring tension?
Remove the roller from its brackets. Hold the chain end to prevent spin. Manually roll the fabric 2–3 turns by hand (up = add tension, down = reduce tension). Re-clip and test. Repeat until the speed and hold are right.
Can roller blinds be ceiling mounted?
Yes — most brackets rotate 90° for ceiling fix. Locate joists with a stud finder, or use rated ceiling toggle anchors between joists. Ceiling mount maximises drop length and gives a clean, seamless look for floor-to-ceiling windows.
How wide can a single roller blind be?
Standard single rollers are typically manufactured up to 2.4m wide. Above this, the fabric weight causes mid-span sag. For wider openings, use two blinds side by side on a single headrail, or a motorised system with a centre support bracket.
