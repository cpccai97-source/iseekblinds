Heritage Overlays — What They Actually Mean

Melbourne has more Victorian-era housing stock than anywhere else in Australia. Suburbs like Fitzroy, Carlton, South Yarra, Richmond, Malvern and Hawthorn are full of homes under heritage overlays — HO designations that protect streetscape character.

The good news for anyone shopping for blinds: internal window treatments almost never require planning permits. Heritage overlays primarily regulate what's visible from the street — external finishes, alterations to facades, demolition. What you hang inside your sash windows is, in nearly all cases, entirely your business.

Key point: Internal roller blinds, plantation shutters, venetian blinds, curtains and roman blinds are classed as "internal fittings" and sit outside heritage overlay control in all Melbourne metropolitan councils we're aware of. External roller shutters and awnings that alter the building's external appearance may require a planning permit — always check with your local council first.
When to check with council: If you're installing external awnings (cassette-style or otherwise), external shutters mounted to the facade, or any window structure that modifies the external window opening, contact your local council's planning department first. Requirements vary by council and HO level.

Melbourne's Period Architecture — What Suits Each Era

Getting window treatments right in a period home isn't just about compliance — it's about the look. Here's a quick orientation to the main eras and what window treatments were historically used (and what works today).

1850–
1890

Victorian — Terraces & Townhouses

Narrow sash windows, ornate plasterwork, dark timber. Historically: heavy drapes, lace curtains in layers. Today's best fits: plantation shutters (louvred shutters are historically accurate), timber venetians in dark tones, heavyweight linen roman blinds, or sheer voile for lace-curtain effect without maintenance headaches.

1901–
1920

Edwardian — Bungalows & Cottages

Wider windows, casement styles, leadlight accent panels. More light, more relaxed than Victorian. Works well with: timber venetians, natural-fibre roman blinds (jute, linen), light-filtering roller blinds in warm neutrals. The Edwardian palette runs to greens, creams and warm golds.

1910–
1940

Federation & Californian Bungalow

Generous proportions, verandas, bay windows, stained glass panels. The warm timber palette suits: dark-stained timber shutters, Roman blinds in earthy terracotta or olive, layered curtains with contrast banding. Avoid stark white or grey — it fights the warm timber tones.

1930–
1960

Art Deco & Inter-War

Geometric forms, steel-framed windows, horizontal banding. These homes suit clean, simple lines. Roller blinds in fabric solids work beautifully. Plantation shutters suit the geometric aesthetic. Avoid fussy fabric treatments that compete with the architecture.

Common Window Types in Melbourne Period Homes

The window type often determines your treatment options more than the era does. Heritage homes throw up some tricky configurations — here's what to know.

Double-Hung Sash

Most common in Victorian & Edwardian homes. Two panels slide vertically.

Best fits: Plantation shutters, roller blinds (inside face-fix), Roman blinds

Casement Windows

Open outward on hinges. Common in Edwardian and Federation homes.

Best fits: Roller blinds (ceiling or wall fix), roman blinds on ceiling track

Arched & Fanlight Windows

Arched tops or decorative fanlights above main pane. Very common in Victorian terraces.

Best fits: Custom shaped shutters, roller blind below fanlight only, sheer fabric panels

Bay & Oriel Windows

Projecting multi-panel windows. Common in Federation and late Victorian.

Best fits: Individual roller or Roman blinds per panel, shutters on each facet

Leadlight Panels

Coloured or textured glass accents, often at top of window. Should not be covered.

Best fits: Blinds installed below leadlight only, ceiling track to preserve glass panel

Tall Narrow Sash

High-ceilinged rooms, elongated proportions. Typical of Victorian terraces.

Best fits: Full-height plantation shutters, long Roman blinds, floor-to-ceiling drapes

Product-by-Product Breakdown

Plantation Shutters — The Heritage Classic

If one product was made for period homes, it's plantation shutters. Louvred timber shutters were used in colonial Australian architecture from the 1840s — they're not a trend, they're a restoration. Full-height shutters on tall Victorian sash windows look genuinely period-correct.

Timber shutters are the authentic choice and take paint or stain to match original joinery. PVC shutters cost less and handle humidity better (useful in bathrooms), though the look is slightly less warm. For listed heritage properties where authenticity matters most, timber is hard to beat.

Shutters also work around the leadlight issue — you can specify the shutter panel height to stop below any decorative glass, covering only the clear glazing beneath.

Roman Blinds — The Refined Fabric Option

Roman blinds fold into flat horizontal panels when raised — a clean, architectural form that suits period homes far better than gathered Austrian or balloon blinds (which scream 1980s). Use heavyweight linen, cotton velvet, or woven textiles in heritage-appropriate colours: deep greens, warm ochres, dusty pinks, off-whites.

For casement windows that open outward, ceiling-fix roman blind tracks keep the blind clear of the opening hardware. For sash windows, face-fix inside the reveal is usually neater.

Timber Venetian Blinds — Warm & Controllable

50mm timber slats in a natural stain or painted finish give a period-sympathetic look at a lower price than shutters. They offer precise light control — important in rooms where leadlight or stained glass creates interesting light patterns you don't want to block entirely. Dark-stained timber venetians (walnut, cedar tones) work particularly well in Victorian-era interiors with dark timber joinery.

Roller Blinds — Modern Performance, Appropriate Minimalism

A plain roller blind in a neutral linen-weave fabric is actually a defensible choice in period homes — it's simple enough not to compete with architectural detail. The trick is in the fabric choice and colour. Avoid anything that looks overtly modern (metallic coatings, stark white blockout in a Victorian sitting room). Natural-look fabrics in warm tones sit back and let the room's character lead.

Roller blinds also solve the bay window problem elegantly: each panel gets its own independent blind, measuring exactly to fit each facet. No swags, no complicated tracking systems.

Sheer & Curtain Layers — The Historical Approach

Victorian homes were draped in layers — lace sheers for privacy, heavy drapes for warmth, often with pelmets. That layered approach is period-correct and highly functional: sheers manage daytime privacy while letting light flood in; blockout drapes seal the room at night for sleep and insulation.

Modern ripple-fold curtains on ceiling tracks achieve this layered look cleanly, without the bulk and maintenance of traditional gathered drapes. Pair a fine-weave sheer with a lined linen drape in a warm neutral for maximum authenticity and performance.

Practical Installation Tips

The golden rule: Protect original timber frames and sills. Original joinery in a period home has character that can't be replicated cheaply. Use face-fix brackets outside the window recess when possible, or tension-fit systems that don't require drilling into heritage timber at all.

Mounting in Original Sash Windows

Double-hung sash windows present a specific challenge: the sashes slide vertically within a box frame, and the reveals (the recessed area inside the frame) are often quite shallow — sometimes less than 50mm. This rules out inside mounting for most blinds. The better approach is face-fixing just outside the window reveal, onto the wall above, which also visually enlarges the window.

Dealing with Out-of-Square Windows

Heritage homes settle. Expect windows to be slightly out of square — sometimes significantly. Measure at the top, middle and bottom of each window and use the narrowest width. For plantation shutters, our team will do a precise onsite measure to account for any racking before manufacture.

Leadlight and Stained Glass

Never cover original leadlight or stained glass panels. Position your blind or shutter to cover only the clear glass below. For sash windows with a small stained glass panel at the top, measure from the base of that panel to the sill — that's your blind drop. For shutters, specify a solid panel top rail that aligns with the base of the decorative glass.

Colour Selection for Period Homes

Period homes have warm palettes — cream joinery, warm timber floors, aged plaster. Avoid cool grey or stark white fabrics; they fight with the interior. Warm whites (linen, natural, chalk), earthy neutrals (stone, wheat, flax) and soft heritage colours (sage green, dusty blue, warm terracotta) all sit sympathetically. For shutters, matching the existing joinery colour is usually the safest choice.

Energy Performance: The Single Glazing Problem

Almost every heritage home in Melbourne has single-glazed sash windows. This is the single biggest source of heat loss in winter and heat gain in summer — double-glazing replacements are expensive and, in many cases, require heritage council approval since they change the window profile.

The window treatment is often the most practical upgrade available. Here's how the main options perform:

Honeycomb blinds provide the best insulation performance of any blind type. Their cellular structure traps a layer of still air between blind and glass, reducing heat transfer significantly. For back bedrooms or study where period aesthetics matter less than sleep comfort, honeycomb blinds are worth considering.

Lined roman blinds or curtains with a thermal interlining create a fabric layer that adds meaningful insulation. Heavy linen drapes lined with cotton thermal interlining can reduce cold air infiltration by 30–40% compared to a bare window — and they look appropriate in a Victorian room.

Plantation shutters provide moderate insulation via their air-gap effect when the louvres are closed, but not as much as fabric treatments. Their real value in heritage homes is aesthetic and light control, not thermal performance.

For maximum winter warmth in a heritage home: layer a sheer roman or roller blind close to the glass with heavy lined curtains outside. The air gap between the two layers dramatically reduces cold air infiltration.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need council approval to install blinds in a heritage home?
In most cases, internal window treatments like blinds and curtains do not require council approval, even in heritage overlay areas. Heritage overlays primarily govern changes to the external appearance of the property. However, if you plan to install external awnings or roller shutters that alter the streetscape appearance, you may need a planning permit. Always check with your local council — Melbourne councils vary in their requirements.
What blinds suit Victorian terrace houses?
Plantation shutters and timber venetian blinds are the most historically congruent choices for Victorian terraces. Roman blinds in natural linen or cotton also suit the era well. Modern roller blinds in neutral tones work fine internally — they're not visible from the street. Avoid motorised external roller shutters as they can clash with heritage character and may require permits.
Can I install plantation shutters in a heritage home?
Yes. Plantation shutters are one of the best choices for heritage homes — the louvred shutter design has genuine historical precedent in Australian colonial and Victorian architecture. They add to rather than detract from period character. Internal timber or PVC plantation shutters require no planning permit in any Melbourne council area we're aware of.
How do I cover sash windows without damaging original frames?
Tension-fit (no-drill) mounting brackets, face-fix mounting outside the window recess, or ceiling track systems all work well for protecting original sash window frames. Avoid drilling into heritage timber sills if possible. Our installation team is experienced with heritage homes and can advise on the right approach for your specific window configuration.
What are the best energy-efficient options for heritage homes with single glazing?
Honeycomb (cellular) blinds or heavy lined Roman blinds provide the best insulation for single-glazed windows. Adding a fabric layer plus a sheer creates a dead-air gap that significantly reduces cold air drafts in winter. For Melbourne's cooler months, layered curtain and blind combinations can dramatically reduce heating costs without touching the original glass.
