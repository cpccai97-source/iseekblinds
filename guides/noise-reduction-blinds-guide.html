Noise Reduction Blinds & Curtains
What Actually Works

An honest look at acoustic performance across blind and curtain types — with real numbers, not marketing promises.

📍 Melbourne, Australia ⏱ 8 min read 📅 Updated March 2026

The Honest Truth First

Blinds and curtains will not soundproof your apartment. If a busy road, train line, or noisy neighbour is your problem, window coverings are a partial solution — not a fix.

Reality Check

The single biggest factor in street noise is your windows. Single-pane glass does almost nothing acoustically. Good acoustic blinds or heavy curtains can reduce noise by 5–12 dB — enough to take the edge off, but not enough to silence a tram route.

That said, the right window treatment makes a real difference. Here's what the data actually shows.

How Noise Travels Through Windows

Sound enters through two paths: direct transmission through the glass, and indirect transmission through gaps — around frames, at sill level, and between curtain and wall. Most cheap solutions only address the first. A thin blind does almost nothing because it doesn't address either path well.

Sound enters primarily through the glass, with secondary paths through frame gaps. Blinds and curtains affect both to varying degrees.

Best Result

Layer double-cell honeycomb blinds (sealed with side channels) with heavy floor-to-ceiling curtains. This combination can achieve 12–18 dB reduction — approaching the performance of secondary glazing at a fraction of the cost.

Installation Makes or Breaks It

The most common reason acoustic window treatments underperform is poor installation. Sound finds every gap.

Left: inside-mount blind with side gaps — minimal acoustic benefit. Right: outside-mount with side channels + ceiling-hung curtain — maximum performance.

Installation Rules for Maximum Acoustic Performance

  1. Mount outside the frame, not inside. Cover the entire window plus 10–15cm on each side.
  2. Use side-tracking channels on honeycomb blinds. These seal the air gap between blind and wall. Without them, you lose 3–5 dB of benefit.
  3. Hang curtains from ceiling height or as close as possible — not from just above the window.
  4. Let curtains pool slightly at the floor to seal the bottom gap.
  5. Use a double rod — sheers behind, heavy curtain in front. The air gap between layers adds acoustic benefit.
  6. Seal window frame gaps with acoustic sealant before installing any treatment. This alone can reduce noise by 2–4 dB.

When You Need More: Double Glazing Comparison

If your noise problem is severe — a major arterial road, airport zone, or train corridor — window treatments alone won't solve it. Here's where double glazing fits in:

  • Single pane glass: ~26–28 STC
  • Standard double glazing: ~28–32 STC
  • Acoustic double glazing (laminated inner pane): ~38–42 STC
  • Good acoustic curtains (added to single pane): +5–12 dB improvement
When to Upgrade Windows Instead

If you can't carry on a normal conversation in your bedroom with windows closed, blinds and curtains won't fix that. Consider secondary glazing inserts or window replacement. For Melbourne rentals, you can still get significant improvement from the layered curtain approach.

Melbourne-Specific Considerations

Melbourne's housing stock creates specific acoustic challenges:

  • Victorian/Edwardian homes typically have single-pane sash windows with rattly frames — the frames pass as much noise as the glass. Sealing and heavy curtains give significant improvement here.
  • Inner-city apartments (Southbank, CBD, Fitzroy) near tram routes benefit most from double-cell honeycomb with side channels — quieter than curtains and fits modern interiors better.
  • Bayside and eastern suburbs — road noise is generally more manageable; standard acoustic curtains are usually sufficient.
  • New builds with double glazing — adding acoustic treatments provides smaller absolute gains, but layered curtains still measurably reduce internal echo and reverberation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do acoustic blinds actually reduce noise?
Yes, but with realistic expectations. Acoustic blinds and heavy curtains typically reduce noise by 5–12 dB. That's noticeable — it takes the edge off — but won't silence a busy road. They work best for mid-to-high frequency sounds (voices, traffic hum) rather than low-frequency bass (trucks, trains).
What blinds are best for noise reduction?
Double-cell honeycomb blinds with close-fitting side channels offer the best noise reduction among blind types. Heavy lined curtains hung from ceiling height are often even more effective. For maximum results, layer both.
Are honeycomb blinds good for noise?
Yes — their air-cell structure absorbs mid-range sound frequencies. Double-cell versions perform better than single-cell. Side-tracking channels that seal against the window frame significantly improve acoustic performance.
Can blinds replace double glazing for noise?
No. Double glazing achieves STC ratings of 35–45, while even the best curtains reach 15–20 dB improvement. Blinds and curtains are a cost-effective supplement, not a replacement for acoustic glazing.
Do thicker curtains block more noise?
Mass helps, but installation matters more. Floor-to-ceiling curtains hung outside the window frame are more effective than slightly lighter curtains installed badly. Acoustic lining — separate from standard thermal lining — adds meaningful mass.
