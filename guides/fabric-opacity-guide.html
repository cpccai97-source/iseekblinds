Blockout vs Sunscreen vs Light Filtering — Which Blind Fabric Do You Need?

The Three Fabric Types, Explained

Every roller, honeycomb, roman and vertical blind is available in at least two fabric options. Picking the wrong one is the single most common regret people have after installation. Here's what each fabric actually does.

Blockout (Opaque)

Blockout fabric is coated or laminated on the reverse side with an opaque layer — usually acrylic foam or PVC — that blocks 100% of visible light when fully closed. The room-facing side comes in hundreds of colours and textures; the reverse is typically white (for heat reflection) or matching colour.

Blockout provides complete day and night privacy. Nobody sees in, ever, regardless of interior lighting. It also offers the best thermal insulation of any single-layer fabric, because that backing acts as a radiant barrier.

The trade-off: when it's down, you lose your view entirely. There's no gentle glow, no filtered daylight — it's either up or it's dark.

Sunscreen (Mesh / Screen)

Sunscreen fabric is a woven mesh, typically made from PVC-coated fibreglass or polyester yarn. The weave creates thousands of tiny openings that filter UV radiation (blocking 90–99% depending on the fabric) while letting you see through to the outside.

The density of this weave is measured as openness factor (OF) — ranging from 1% (very tight, minimal see-through) to 10% (looser weave, clearer outward view). Most Australian homes use 3% or 5% OF.

⚠️ Night privacy warning Sunscreen fabric provides good privacy during daylight hours (when it's brighter outside than inside). At night with lights on, the effect reverses — outsiders can see silhouettes and movement. If the room faces a street or neighbours, you'll need a second layer for after dark.

Light Filtering (Translucent)

Light filtering fabric sits between blockout and sunscreen. It's a tightly woven, opaque-ish material that diffuses daylight into a soft, even glow — without letting you see shapes or detail from either side.

Unlike sunscreen, light filtering does provide basic privacy at night: no silhouettes, just a warm glow if lights are on. Unlike blockout, it lets a pleasant amount of ambient light through during the day — enough to read by, not enough to cause glare.

It's the middle-ground option, and often the right choice for living areas where you want soft light without needing a view.

Light Filtering

Diffused ambient light. No view, no shapes. Day & night privacy.

Understanding Openness Factor (OF%)

Openness factor only applies to sunscreen fabrics. It's the percentage of the fabric surface that's "open" — tiny gaps between woven threads. A lower number means a tighter weave.

1% Openness

Maximum glare reduction and UV block. You can still see general shapes outside in bright conditions, but detail is minimal. Best for west-facing windows that cop heavy afternoon sun, or rooms where glare control matters more than the view.

3% Openness (most popular)

The sweet spot for most Melbourne homes. Good outward visibility during the day, excellent UV rejection (typically 94–97%), and enough density to provide solid daytime privacy. Works with virtually any room orientation.

5% Openness

Clearer outward view, slightly less glare reduction. Popular for living rooms and offices where maintaining connection to the garden or street is important. Still blocks 90%+ UV. Daytime privacy is slightly reduced compared to 3%.

10% Openness

Maximum outward view, least filtering. Rarely used in residential — more common in commercial fitouts where views are the priority and internal lighting can be controlled. Privacy is noticeably reduced even during the day.

💡 Melbourne orientation tip North and west-facing windows get the most heat and glare. Use 1–3% OF for these. East-facing windows get morning sun (usually pleasant) — 5% is fine. South-facing windows rarely need sunscreen at all; light filtering is often the better choice.

Room-by-Room Fabric Recommendations

Every room has different light, privacy and comfort requirements. Here's what actually works — based on thousands of Melbourne installations.

🛏 Master Bedroom

Darkness for sleep. Full privacy always. Best with white-backed blockout for heat reflection in summer.

→ Blockout (or double roller: sunscreen + blockout)

🍼 Nursery / Kids Room

Absolute darkness for daytime naps. Child-safe operation is mandatory (cordless or motorised).

→ Blockout only — no compromise

🛋 Living Room

Soft light during the day, privacy at night. If you have a garden view you want to keep, sunscreen rollers with a blockout layer.

→ Sunscreen (3–5% OF) or Light Filtering

🍳 Kitchen

Moisture resistance matters. Sunscreen is PVC-coated and wipes clean. Avoid heavy blockout fabric near cooktops — it holds grease and odour.

→ Sunscreen (3–5% OF)

💻 Home Office

Screen glare is the enemy. You need to reduce direct sun without working in a cave. Light filtering or low-OF sunscreen.

→ Sunscreen (1–3% OF) or Light Filtering

🚿 Bathroom

Privacy all day, moisture tolerance. Light filtering works well — lets light in, blocks shapes. PVC-based fabrics resist mould. See our bathroom blinds guide.

→ Light Filtering (moisture-resistant fabric)

🍽 Dining Room

Soft ambient light for meals, enough privacy for evening entertaining. Light filtering gives the warmest glow.

→ Light Filtering or Sunscreen (5%)

🎬 Media Room

Zero light leakage. Period. Side channels and pelmets improve blockout performance by eliminating edge gaps.

→ Blockout with side channels

The Double Roller Solution

Can't decide between sunscreen and blockout? You don't have to. A double roller blind mounts two fabrics on a single bracket — sunscreen in front, blockout behind — each operating independently.

During the day, lower the sunscreen for UV protection and glare control while keeping your view. In the evening, pull down the blockout behind it for full darkness and privacy. It's the most versatile window treatment setup available, and increasingly the default choice for Melbourne bedrooms and living rooms.

📐 Recess depth for double rollers You'll need at least 110mm of recess depth for a double roller inside mount. Standard single rollers need 65–70mm. If your window frame is too shallow, face-mount (outside mount) is always an option — and often looks cleaner anyway. Check our roller blind measurement guide for details.

Double rollers cost roughly 50–70% more than a single roller in the same fabric — not double, because you're sharing a bracket and installation. For a standard 1500mm × 2100mm window in Melbourne, expect $250–$400 for a quality custom double roller installed.

Fabric & Energy Performance

Your blind fabric directly affects heating and cooling costs. In Melbourne's climate — hot summers, cold winters — the right fabric can cut energy loss through windows by 25–60%.

Summer (heat rejection)

West-facing windows are the worst offenders — they can push room temperature up 8–12°C on a 38°C Melbourne day. Fabric performance ranked:

  1. White-backed blockout — reflects up to 60% of radiant heat. The white backing acts as a mirror for infrared radiation.
  2. Honeycomb blockout — trapped air pockets add insulation on top of the reflective backing. Best overall thermal performer. See our energy efficiency guide for R-values.
  3. Sunscreen (light colour, 1–3% OF) — reflects 30–50% of heat while maintaining airflow and view. Lighter colours perform better.
  4. Light filtering — blocks direct radiation but traps heat between fabric and glass. Open the window or combine with ventilation.

Winter (heat retention)

Up to 40% of a home's heating energy escapes through windows. Closing blinds creates a still-air buffer that slows conduction. Blockout and honeycomb fabrics work best here — they create a tighter seal and more effective air pocket.

💡 Colour matters for energy On the room-facing side, choose any colour you like. On the window-facing side (the backing), white or silver reflects the most heat in summer. Avoid dark backing colours on west and north windows — they absorb heat and radiate it into the room.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Putting sunscreen in a bedroom and expecting sleep darkness. Sunscreen dims light — it doesn't block it. You'll still see street lights and morning sun. Use blockout, or add a blockout layer.
  2. Choosing blockout for a living room and never opening it. If blockout makes the room too dark and you leave it rolled up most of the time, you've wasted money. Sunscreen or light filtering would serve you better.
  3. Ignoring night privacy with sunscreen. The "I can see out but they can't see in" effect only works when it's brighter outside. Test at night with interior lights on before committing to sunscreen-only in street-facing rooms.
  4. Matching fabric colour to the room, ignoring the backing colour. The backing colour faces outside and affects energy performance. White backing is almost always the right choice — it reflects heat and looks neutral from the street.
  5. Assuming all sunscreen fabrics are the same. Openness factor, yarn type (fibreglass vs polyester), coating quality, and UV resistance vary enormously between brands. Cheap sunscreen fabric degrades in 3–5 years under direct sun. Quality fabric lasts 10–15 years.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between blockout and sunscreen blinds?

Blockout uses an opaque, coated fabric that blocks 100% of external light — ideal for bedrooms and media rooms. Sunscreen uses a mesh-weave fabric (typically 1–10% openness factor) that filters UV while preserving your view. Sunscreen gives daytime privacy only; blockout gives full day-and-night privacy.

Do sunscreen blinds provide privacy at night?

No. When interior lights are on and it's dark outside, people can see silhouettes and movement through sunscreen fabric. For night-time privacy, pair sunscreen blinds with blockout curtains or choose a double roller system with both fabrics.

What is openness factor in blind fabric?

Openness factor (OF) measures the percentage of a fabric's surface that's open weave — i.e. how much you can see through it. 1% OF means very tight weave (minimal see-through), 10% OF means clearer view but less filtering. Most Australian sunscreen fabrics are 3–5% OF.

Which blind fabric is best for energy efficiency?

Blockout fabrics with a reflective backing (white or silver-coated rear) offer the best thermal performance — reducing heat gain by up to 60%. Honeycomb blinds in blockout fabric outperform all others due to trapped-air insulation. Sunscreen also reduces UV heat gain by 60–80% but allows more radiant heat through.

Can I use sunscreen blinds in a bedroom?

Yes — if you pair them with a blockout layer. A double roller blind (sunscreen in front, blockout behind) is the most popular bedroom setup in Melbourne. During the day you get filtered view and UV protection; at night roll down the blockout for darkness and privacy.
