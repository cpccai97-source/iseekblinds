What Is a BAL Rating and Why Windows Matter

The Bushfire Attack Level (BAL) is a measure of the severity of a building's potential exposure to ember attack, radiant heat and direct flame contact. It's determined under AS 3959 Construction of Buildings in Bushfire-Prone Areas — the Australian Standard that governs how homes in fire-risk zones must be built or retrofitted.

Windows and glazing are consistently identified as the most common failure point in residential bushfire losses. Standard glass cracks under radiant heat. Ordinary fly screens don't block ember entry. And internal blinds — no matter the fabric — do nothing once embers are inside the building cavity.

The role of bushfire-rated window treatments is to close that gap: to stop embers passing through the window system and to provide a barrier that reduces radiant heat transmission into the home during the critical exposure period at your BAL level.

⚠️ Internal blinds are not bushfire protection

Roller blinds, venetians, curtains and any internal window treatment offer zero protection under AS 3959. Bushfire compliance requires external systems fitted to the outside face of the window or integrated into the window frame.

Types of Bushfire-Rated Window Protection

1. Ember Guard Screens

Ember guard (or ember mesh) screens are the most cost-effective bushfire window treatment. They consist of a fine steel or aluminium mesh with an aperture of 2mm or less — small enough to prevent burning embers from passing through while still allowing light and ventilation.

Ember guards are suitable at BAL-12.5 and BAL-19 as a primary protection measure, and can be used in conjunction with shutters at higher ratings. The mesh must be framed and installed to close all gaps around the window perimeter — a 3mm gap defeats the purpose entirely.

Key spec: AS 3959-2018 specifies ember screens must be non-combustible, maximum 2mm aperture, and installed to prevent gaps at edges. Stainless steel mesh is the most durable option; powder-coated aluminium is more common and suitable for most residential applications.

2. Aluminium External Roller Shutters (BAL-Rated)

Purpose-built aluminium external roller shutters are the most widely available bushfire-compliant window treatment for BAL-12.5 through BAL-40. When lowered, the shutter curtain forms a continuous non-combustible barrier across the window opening.

Critical specification differences from standard external roller shutters:

  • Slat-to-slat interlocking joints must be tight — standard shutters often have gaps that allow ember ingress
  • Bottom rail must seal to the sill with a contact seal or brush seal
  • Side guides must prevent wind-induced gaps to maintain the ember barrier
  • The entire system (slats, guides, bottom rail, housing) must achieve its claimed BAL rating as an assembly — component-level specs alone are insufficient
  • Look for manufacturer test reports to AS 3959 or AWTA (Australian Wool Testing Authority) fire testing results

BAL-40 shutters use heavier-gauge aluminium sections and tighter tolerances. BAL-FZ typically requires steel construction.

3. Steel Folding Shutters and Panels

At BAL-40 and BAL-FZ, steel folding panels and hinged screens are the standard solution. These are heavier, more expensive, and more complex to operate than aluminium rollers — but the thermal mass and structural integrity of steel is required to withstand the radiant heat exposure at these extreme ratings.

Steel shutters at BAL-FZ are typically custom-fabricated, powder-coated, and integrated into the building structure. They are not a retrofit product — they require engineering input and building permit approval.

4. Aluminium Louvred Shutters (Heritage Aesthetic)

A relatively recent development: aluminium louvred external shutters that close to a sealed panel position. In the open position they look similar to traditional plantation shutters. Fully closed, they form the non-combustible barrier required under AS 3959.

These suit Victorian homes and heritage-adjacent properties where a roller shutter box would look out of character. Most are rated to BAL-29; some manufacturers offer BAL-40 versions. The trade-off is higher cost — typically $1,400–$2,200 per window installed — versus $900–$1,500 for a conventional roller shutter of similar size.

ℹ️ What about timber plantation shutters?

Timber is combustible and cannot achieve a BAL rating regardless of treatment. Standard external timber shutters offer no bushfire protection under AS 3959 and are not compliant at any BAL level above BAL-Low. If your home has a heritage overlay that requires timber shutters, discuss this with your council and a BAL assessor — solutions usually involve ember screens behind the timber shutters.

Victoria-Specific Context

Victoria has a large number of properties in designated Bushfire Prone Areas (BPA) and Bushfire Management Overlays (BMO). Under the Building Act 1993 and Building Regulations 2018, new construction and significant renovations in these zones require building permit compliance with AS 3959.

Key areas in greater Melbourne and surrounds with significant BAL-12.5 to BAL-40 properties:

  • Dandenong Ranges: Ferntree Gully, Belgrave, Monbulk, Emerald — predominantly BAL-12.5 to BAL-29
  • Yarra Valley: Healesville, Warburton, Yarra Glen — BAL-12.5 to BAL-40 common
  • Mornington Peninsula: Red Hill, Main Ridge, Arthurs Seat — BAL-12.5 and BAL-19 common
  • Macedon Ranges: Macedon, Woodend, Hanging Rock area — BAL-19 to BAL-40
  • Kinglake / Whittlesea: Post-2009 rebuilds typically BAL-29 minimum

If your building permit was issued post-2012 (when the current version of AS 3959 took effect), your permit documents will specify the BAL rating and construction requirements that applied to your build. These are the requirements you must maintain in any window replacement or renovation.

Retrofitting Bushfire Protection to Existing Windows

Existing homes in Bushfire Prone Areas that predate current regulations are not legally required to upgrade window treatments retroactively — unless they're undertaking works that trigger a building permit. That said, retrofitting is strongly recommended given that ember ingress through windows and openings is the dominant cause of residential fire losses.

Practical retrofit considerations:

  • Ember guard screens are the most cost-effective starting point for BAL-12.5 and BAL-19 properties. They can be retrofitted to most existing window frames without structural changes.
  • External roller shutters can usually be retro-fitted using face-fix or cavity-fix brackets. The shutter housing is mounted above the window reveal. This works on most construction types but requires adequate structural fixing points.
  • Glazing upgrades are more invasive and expensive. If your windows are being replaced anyway, specify toughened or laminated glass appropriate to your BAL level at the same time.
  • Check with your council whether your retrofit requires a building permit. In most cases, like-for-like window covering changes don't require a permit, but structural alterations associated with shutter installation may.
✅ Prioritise high-risk elevations first

If budget is a constraint, prioritise windows on your bushfire-hazard-facing elevations (the side facing the most likely fire approach). For most Melbourne peri-urban properties this is the north and north-west elevation. A partial retrofit focused on the highest-risk openings delivers the majority of the protection benefit.

Maintenance and Operation

Bushfire shutters must be operable and in good condition to provide protection. A shutter that won't lower, or has damaged seals and gaps, is non-compliant in practice regardless of its certified rating.

Annual maintenance checklist:

  • Test motorised shutters under full load — motor failures often appear during extended operation
  • Check bottom rail seals for cracking, compression set or pest damage
  • Clean guide channels and lubricate with a dry lubricant (avoid oily lubricants that attract dust and debris)
  • Inspect slat interlocks for deformation or corrosion, especially in coastal areas
  • Verify manual operation backup is functional on motorised systems — power may be unavailable during a fire event

On high-risk fire days (FDI 50+), shutters should be closed as part of your home's fire preparation protocol — not left until flames are visible. Radiant heat begins causing damage well before direct flame contact.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are regular roller blinds bushfire rated?
No. Standard internal roller blinds — even blockout fabrics — offer no bushfire protection and don't comply with AS 3959. Bushfire protection requires external shutters or external screens rated for your property's BAL level. Internal blinds may actually increase fire risk if they're made from synthetic fabrics that ignite quickly.
What BAL rating do I need for my home?
Your BAL (Bushfire Attack Level) is determined by a formal assessment under AS 3959. Your local council, planning permit, or a registered BAL assessor can confirm your property's rating. In Victoria, many peri-urban properties in the Dandenong Ranges, Yarra Valley, Mornington Peninsula hinterland and Macedon Ranges sit at BAL-12.5 to BAL-29.
What window treatments are required at BAL-29?
At BAL-29, AS 3959 requires windows to be protected by either: (a) bushfire-rated external shutters that close to form a continuous barrier, (b) ember guard screens with maximum 2mm aperture (check manufacturer's compliance statement), or (c) glazing compliant with the BAL-29 specification. Window treatments must prevent ember entry and provide radiant heat resistance for the exposure duration at that BAL level.
Can plantation shutters be bushfire rated?
External timber plantation shutters are not BAL-rated — timber is combustible. BAL-rated external shutters are made from aluminium or steel with a non-combustible construction. Some manufacturers produce aluminium external shutters with a louvred appearance that mimic the plantation shutter aesthetic while meeting AS 3959 requirements up to BAL-29 or BAL-40.
How much do bushfire-rated shutters cost in Victoria?
Aluminium external roller shutters (BAL-12.5 to BAL-29) typically cost $850–$1,600 per window installed. Ember guard screens are $220–$480 per window. BAL-40 steel shutters range from $1,800–$3,500+ per window. A free measure gives you accurate pricing for your specific window sizes and BAL requirement.
