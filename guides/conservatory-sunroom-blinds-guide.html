Conservatory & Sunroom Blinds:
Heat Control Done Right

Australian sunrooms are beautiful in theory — until summer hits. Here's exactly which blinds survive the UV, control the heat, and keep the space usable year-round.

Reading time: 8 minutes
Updated: March 2026
Products covered: Sunscreen, Honeycomb, Roller, Roman, Venetian

Why Sunrooms Need Special Treatment

A conservatory or sunroom exposes blinds to conditions that destroy standard window treatments within months: sustained direct UV radiation from multiple angles, rapid temperature swings (sometimes 20°C in a single day), and — in older structures — humidity from poor sealing. Regular blinds fade, warp, and fail.

But the bigger problem is heat. Glass enclosures amplify solar gain dramatically. On a 35°C Melbourne summer day, an unshaded sunroom can hit 55°C. The right blinds don't just look good — they make the space liveable.

The Core Challenge

You want light in — but not heat. You want the view — but not the UV. Sunroom blinds have to solve a harder problem than any other window in the house.

Heat Control: How the Numbers Stack Up

Solar heat gain is measured by the Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC) — the lower the number, the less heat enters. The openness factor of sunscreen fabrics is the key variable: a 1% openness blocks more heat than a 10% openness fabric, though it also reduces visibility more.

Sunscreen Roller (5–10%) 85–92% Moderate Excellent Excellent

What "Openness Factor" Actually Means

Sunscreen fabric is rated by openness factor — the percentage of the weave that is open. A 1% openness fabric has tiny holes; a 14% fabric has a more open weave and lets you see through more clearly.

Choosing the Right Openness for Your Sunroom

  • 1–3% openness: Maximum UV and heat protection, still see the garden in daylight. Best for harsh north-facing sunrooms.
  • 5% openness: The most popular balance — good heat/UV reduction with clear daytime views. Works for most orientations.
  • 10–14% openness: More view, less protection. Suitable for cooler south-facing rooms or areas where view is paramount.
⚠️ Note on Night-Time Privacy

All sunscreen fabrics reverse visibility after dark — when your interior is lit and outside is dark, people outside can see in. For evening use, pair sunscreen blinds with a secondary blockout roller or ensure secondary lighting doesn't create this effect.

Fabric Durability: Why This Matters More in Sunrooms

A sunroom window can receive 6–10 hours of direct sun per day — compared to 2–3 hours for a typical interior window. This means standard blind fabrics that would last 10 years in a bedroom might fade or delaminate in a sunroom within 2–3 years.

The key specification to ask for: solution-dyed yarn. In solution-dyed fabrics, the colour is part of the fibre itself (not a surface coating), making it dramatically more UV-resistant. All quality sunscreen fabrics use solution-dyed yarns and are rated to 7–10+ years of direct sun.

What to Avoid in Sunrooms

  • Standard fabric rollers with non-UV-stable coatings — they yellow, crack, and lose their opacity
  • Timber venetians — expansion and contraction from heat will warp slats within a year
  • Fabric Roman blinds with non-UV-treated fabric — fading within 12–18 months
  • Plastic/PVC components that aren't heat-rated — they'll deform in a hot sunroom

Motorisation: Worth It for Sunrooms

Conservatories often have multiple blinds — sometimes on roof glazing that's impossible to reach manually. Motorised blinds solve this completely: all blinds raise and lower together via an app, remote, or temperature sensor. On a hot day, automated blinds can deploy at a set temperature threshold — reducing your cooling bill without you having to think about it.

For sunrooms specifically, look for motors with built-in sun and wind sensors. If you have an alfresco area adjacent to the sunroom, smart home integration lets you control indoor and outdoor blinds together.

Moisture & Humidity: The Overlooked Problem

Older conservatories and sunrooms can experience condensation on glass, particularly in Melbourne winters when cold exterior glass meets warm interior air. This creates a humid microclimate that attacks blinds — especially fabric ones.

Specify: anti-mould fabric treatments, aluminium or stainless headrails, and PVC chain weights rather than metal ones that can rust. Most quality sunscreen fabrics have inherent moisture resistance — but check the spec sheet.
