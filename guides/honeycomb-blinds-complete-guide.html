What Are Honeycomb Blinds?

Honeycomb blinds — also called cellular blinds or pleated shades — are window coverings made from a pleated fabric that forms hexagonal air pockets when stacked. Look at them in cross-section and the shape is unmistakably honeycomb-like. Those air pockets are the point: they trap still air, which is one of the best natural insulators available.

Unlike roller blinds, which stack around a tube at the top, honeycomb fabric concertinas into tight, neat pleats. This means even a large window can have a very slim stack — typically 50–80mm at the top. The face of the blind stays flat and clean, with no visible cords running through the fabric.

The category is popular in northern Europe and North America for cold-climate insulation, and has grown steadily in Australia as energy costs have risen. Melbourne's variable climate — genuinely cold winters and scorching summers — makes it one of the more logical Australian cities to invest in the category.

Single Cell vs Double Cell

This is the decision most people get confused by, because the marketing around it is deliberately vague. Here is the honest version:

Single Cell

One layer of air pockets. Good insulation, lighter fabric, better light diffusion, lower cost.

R-value ≈ 0.4–0.7

Better Insulation

Double Cell

Two stacked layers of air pockets. 15–20% better insulation than single cell, thicker stack.

R-value ≈ 0.8–1.2

The practical question is: does the extra insulation justify the cost? For most Melbourne homes with reasonable glazing (double-glazed or even decent single-glazed), single cell is sufficient in temperate zones. Double cell makes more sense if you have large, west-facing windows that cop afternoon summer heat, rooms used as year-round bedrooms in cool climates, or older homes with single-pane aluminium frames.

Real-world data point: A Reddit thread from Melbourne homeowners found one user had 9 windows and a sliding door done for $3,800 with single cell honeycomb. They reported the house stayed comfortable on 35°C days with blinds down throughout the day. That is a reasonable outcome — but it requires keeping the blinds closed, which affects light and view.

Opacity Options — Light Filtering, Blockout, Sheer

Honeycomb blinds come in three broad opacity categories. The fabric determines both how much light enters and how much insulation you get — denser fabrics generally insulate better.

Opacity Light Transmission Best For Privacy
Sheer / Translucent 20–40% Living areas, diffused light Daytime only
Light Filtering 5–15% Lounge, study, home office Good
Room Darkening 1–3% Media rooms, west windows Excellent
Blockout <1% Bedrooms, baby rooms Complete

One nuance often missed: even a "blockout" honeycomb blind will show a thin line of light at the side edges unless the window reveal is deep enough to mount inside. Side channel tracks solve this completely — they grip the blind edge and eliminate light gaps, at a cost of about $40–$60 per blind extra.

Energy Efficiency — What the Numbers Actually Mean

Honeycomb blinds are the most thermally efficient fabric window covering available. Here is where they sit relative to other options, using approximate Australian R-values for window coverings (not the window itself):

  • Uncovered single-glazed window: R-0.18
  • Roller blind (standard fabric): R-0.23–R-0.35
  • Curtain with pelmet: R-0.4–R-0.6
  • Single cell honeycomb: R-0.4–R-0.7
  • Double cell honeycomb: R-0.8–R-1.2
  • Double cell blockout with side channels: up to R-1.4

These numbers matter most on cold nights and hot afternoons. On a Melbourne winter night at 5°C outside, a well-fitted double cell blockout honeycomb can reduce heat loss through glass by 40% compared to no covering. The catch: the blind needs to be closed and the fit needs to be good. A blind with 10mm gaps at the sides loses most of that benefit.

Tip: The most cost-effective upgrade is always closing what you already have. If you have honeycomb blinds and leave them open all day, you have paid for insulation you are not using. Set a habit of lowering them at dusk in winter and before noon on hot summer days.

Operating Systems — Corded, Cordless, TDBU, Motorised

Standard Cord or Wand

Simplest and cheapest. A cord on the side raises and lowers the blind. Wand operation (a rigid rod that you push/pull) is standard for cordless compliance in newer builds. Works well for accessible windows.

Cordless Spring-Lift

Push the blind up to raise it, pull down to lower. No exposed cord at all. Clean look, very safe for children. The spring tension can weaken over time on very tall blinds — check the maximum blind height with your supplier.

Top-Down Bottom-Up (TDBU)

The most versatile operating system available for honeycomb blinds. Two independently-controlled rails: lower the top rail to bring light in from above while keeping the bottom portion closed for privacy. This is particularly useful in:

  • Street-facing bedrooms (privacy at lower half, sky light from above)
  • Bathrooms (let light in from top, keep lower half private)
  • Living rooms next to footpaths

TDBU adds approximately $80–$120 per blind. If you are buying honeycomb for a bedroom or bathroom, budget for this.

Motorised

A tubular motor drives the blind via app, remote, or smart home integration (Somfy, Rollease Acmeda, Tuya). Excellent for large or ceiling-mounted windows. Motorised honeycomb is fully child-safe with zero exposed cords. Add $150–$250 per blind for the motor, which can be scheduled to close at dusk automatically — a genuine energy saving feature, not just a luxury one.

Which Rooms Suit Honeycomb?

Bedrooms ✓

The best application. Blockout honeycomb with TDBU gives you sleep-quality darkness, morning privacy as you raise the top, and genuine thermal insulation on cold nights. If you budget for one room in the house, make it the bedroom.

Living Areas ✓

Light filtering in a living area diffuses glare beautifully without killing natural light. The flat face of the blind suits contemporary interiors. If you have west-facing windows that overheat in afternoon, room-darkening honeycomb you can lower from 2pm makes a genuine comfort difference.

Nurseries and Children's Rooms ✓

Blockout, cordless, child-safe. Honeycomb is one of the safest options for young children's rooms given the lack of exposed cords and the blackout capability for day-sleep.

Bathrooms ⚠️

Possible but requires care. Choose moisture-tolerant fabrics and ensure good ventilation. If your bathroom has no exhaust fan, a waterproof aluminium venetian or roller blind is easier maintenance.

Kitchens ✗

Not recommended. Cooking grease and steam penetrates the fabric cells and is difficult or impossible to remove. Roller blinds with wipe-clean PVC fabrics are the practical kitchen choice.

Pros & Cons

✓ Pros

  • Best insulation of any fabric blind
  • Excellent light diffusion (not harsh shadows)
  • Very neat, slim stack
  • Cordless options are genuinely child-safe
  • Top-down-bottom-up is uniquely versatile
  • Quiet operation — no slat noise
  • Modern, clean aesthetic

✗ Cons

  • More expensive than roller blinds
  • Fabric cells collect dust internally
  • Cannot wipe clean like roller or venetian
  • Not suitable for kitchens or high-humidity bathrooms
  • Blockout requires side channels for full seal
  • Less fabric/colour choice than rollers

Honeycomb vs Roller Blinds — Which to Choose?

Roller blinds are the most popular window covering in Australia for good reason: they are affordable, clean, and available in hundreds of fabrics. But honeycomb wins in specific situations:

  • Choose honeycomb if: insulation is a priority, you want TDBU for a bedroom or bathroom, you want a cordless child-safe option, or you need effective light diffusion without glare.
  • Choose roller if: you want the widest fabric selection, lower price, easier cleaning, or kitchen/laundry application.

Many Melbourne homes use both: roller blinds in kitchens, laundries, and living areas, honeycomb in bedrooms. This hybrid approach is common with clients and often the most cost-effective. See our energy efficiency guide for a full comparison of window covering insulation options.

Child Safety Compliance in Australia

All new window coverings sold in Australia since October 2023 must comply with AS/NZS 2080:2022. For honeycomb blinds specifically:

  • Cordless spring-lift: fully compliant, no exposed cords
  • Motorised: fully compliant
  • Wand operation: compliant (rigid wand does not form a loop)
  • Traditional pull cord: requires safety tensioner to be compliant

For any room used by children under 6, always specify cordless or motorised. Read our full child safety guide for installation requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between single cell and double cell honeycomb blinds?

Single cell has one layer of hexagonal air pockets — good insulation at a lower price. Double cell stacks two layers, increasing the R-value by roughly 50–80%. For most Melbourne homes, single cell is adequate; double cell is worth it for bedrooms or high-exposure west-facing windows.

Do honeycomb blinds actually save energy?

Yes, measurably — but only when closed. A double-cell blockout honeycomb with side channels can reduce heat loss through a window by up to 40% on a cold night compared to an uncovered window. The saving is real but requires the discipline to actually use the blind.

How much do honeycomb blinds cost in Melbourne?

Single cell supply and install: $180–$380 per window depending on cell type and fabric. Double cell: $250–$520. Add $80–$120 for TDBU, $150–$250 for motorisation.

Are honeycomb blinds suitable for bathrooms?

Possible in bathrooms with adequate ventilation. Moisture can accumulate in the cells in high-humidity environments. If your bathroom has no exhaust fan, a waterproof roller blind or aluminium venetian is more practical.

What is top-down bottom-up and why does it matter?

TDBU lets you lower the blind from the top or raise from the bottom independently — so you get natural light from above while maintaining privacy at eye level. Ideal for bedrooms, bathrooms, and street-facing windows. Budget an extra $80–$120 per blind for this feature.

Are honeycomb blinds child safe?

Cordless, wand-operated, and motorised versions are fully compliant with AS/NZS 2080:2022. For children's rooms, always specify cordless or motorised. See our child safety guide.

How do I clean honeycomb blinds?

Vacuum gently with a soft brush attachment — never wash, submerge, or steam clean. For spot stains, a barely damp cloth and mild detergent, blotted gently. For deep cleaning, a professional compressed-air service ($15–$30 per blind) is available from specialist blind cleaners in Melbourne.
