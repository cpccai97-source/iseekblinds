Best Blinds for Tricky Windows

Bay windows, arched windows, corners, skylights — standard blinds won't cut it. Here's what actually works, how to measure each type, and the mistakes that cost people money.

01

Bay Windows

3-panel, 5-panel, angled & curved configurations

Bay windows are Melbourne's most common "tricky" window. You'll find them in everything from Edwardian terraces in Fitzroy to new builds in Point Cook. The challenge is geometry: multiple window panels set at angles to each other, often with narrow mullions between them.

The key decision is whether to treat each panel individually or span the entire bay as one unit. Individual treatment (separate blinds per panel) gives cleaner lines and lets you control each section independently. A single spanning treatment (usually a curved curtain track) creates a softer, more unified look.

Measure each panel independently — W1, W2, W3 at top/middle/bottom. Height (H) at left/centre/right of each panel.

Measuring Tips

Measure each panel as a separate window — width at three points (top, middle, bottom), height at three points (left, centre, right). Use the narrowest width and tallest height for inside mount. Check the angle where panels meet: most Melbourne bay windows are 135° but older homes can be 120° or even custom angles. Record the angle — your supplier needs it for shutters.

⚠️ Common mistake Measuring the total bay width as one number and ordering a single wide blind. It won't sit flat against angled panels and looks terrible. Always measure panel by panel.

If your bay has a window seat or sill that protrudes, outside mount may not work — the blind would hang away from the glass. Inside mount into each recess is usually the cleaner option here. Check our roller blind measuring guide for inside mount depth requirements (minimum 45mm recess).

Corner Windows

Two windows meeting at 90°

Corner windows — where two windows meet at a 90° angle — are everywhere in Melbourne apartments and modern homes. The problem is the junction: standard blinds have brackets on both ends, and two sets of brackets can physically clash in the corner.

The corner junction is where brackets compete for space. Measure each window separately and note the gap (if any) at the corner.

Solutions That Actually Work

Best choice

Roller Blinds (Inside Mount)

If both windows have sufficient recess depth, inside-mounted rollers avoid the corner clash entirely. Each blind sits within its own frame.

Best choice

Corner Curtain Track

A single track that bends around the 90° corner. One continuous curtain pair covers both windows. Looks seamless and avoids bracket conflicts.

Alternative

Honeycomb Blinds

Compact headrails (35mm minimum recess) make honeycombs one of the easiest products to fit in tight corner recesses.

Measuring Corner Windows

Measure each window as if the other doesn't exist — standard width and height at three points each. Then measure the corner gap: the distance from Window A's nearest edge to Window B's nearest edge. If this gap is under 40mm, outside-mount brackets will clash. Inside mount or a corner curtain track is your solution.

💡 Pro tip For outside-mount corner blinds, ask your supplier about "shared corner brackets" — a single L-shaped bracket that holds both blinds. Not all products support this, but roller blinds from most Australian manufacturers do.
03

Arched & Shaped Windows

Half-moon, full arch, cathedral & custom shapes

Arched windows are gorgeous — they're also the single trickiest window type to cover. Standard blinds are rectangular. Arches are not. You have three real options, and which one makes sense depends on how much of the arch you want to cover.

Option A: Mount a standard blind at the arch's spring line and leave the curved section open. Option B: Custom-shaped shutter fills the entire opening.

Your Three Real Options

1. Cover only the rectangular section. Mount a roller blind, venetian, or curtain at the point where the arch begins (the "spring line"). The curved part stays open — this actually looks intentional and elegant, letting in feature light through the arch. This is the most affordable approach and works with any standard blind.

2. Custom-shaped plantation shutters. The premium option. Shutters can be manufactured to match any arch shape — half-round, gothic, segmented. The arched section is typically a fixed panel (non-operating), with operable shutters below. Cost is 30–50% more than standard shutters due to the custom manufacturing.

3. Shaped fabric blinds. Some specialty manufacturers offer fan-shaped pleated blinds that fill the arch. These collapse to the spring line when open. Limited colour options and longer lead times (6–8 weeks typical).

Best value

Standard Blind Below Arch

Any product — roller, venetian, honeycomb. Mount at the spring line. Arch stays uncovered. Looks clean, costs standard pricing.

Premium choice

Full coverage including the curved section. Fixed arch panel + operable rectangular shutters below. Heritage homes love these.

⚠️ Don't do this Ordering an oversized rectangular blind to cover the entire arch with outside mount. You'll have massive light gaps on either side of the curve, the blind will look awkward and bulky, and you've wasted money on extra fabric that blocks an architectural feature.

Measuring Arched Windows

For the rectangular approach: measure width and height only to the spring line (where the curve starts). For custom shutters: you need a precise template of the arch curve. This is one window type where we strongly recommend our free professional measure — getting the curve wrong means starting from scratch, and there's no quick fix.

Skylights & Roof Windows

Velux, custom skylights & angled roof windows

Skylights dump Melbourne's harsh summer sun directly onto you. In winter, they're a major heat loss point. Covering them is practical, not cosmetic — but they're on the ceiling, often at an angle, and manual operation means pulling a cord while staring up. This is the one window type where motorisation goes from luxury to near-necessity.

Skylight blinds must be tensioned on side channels to prevent sagging away from the glass at any roof pitch.

What Works for Skylights

Best choice

Motorised Honeycomb Blinds

Tensioned side channels keep them flat. Honeycomb structure insulates against heat and cold. Battery motor = no electrician. Control via remote or smart home app.

Brand-specific

Velux Branded Blinds

If you have Velux skylights, their own blinds clip directly into the frame with no drilling. Convenient but limited fabric and colour range.

Key considerations: the blind must have tensioned side guides or channels — without tension, any blind mounted at an angle will sag away from the glass and look terrible within weeks. Standard roller blinds without side channels do not work on angled skylights.

For measurement, you need the skylight frame's internal width and height (measure the actual glass opening, not the ceiling cutout below). Also record the roof pitch angle — your supplier needs this to set the correct spring tension. If you have a Velux or Fakro skylight, the model number stamped on the frame tells the supplier everything they need.

🏠 Smart home note Battery-powered skylight motors from Acmeda and Somfy integrate with Apple HomeKit, Google Home, and Alexa. Schedule them to close at noon in summer and open at sunrise — set once and forget. See our smart home blinds guide for platform comparison.

Bifold & Stacker Doors

Full-width openings that need to disappear when not in use

Bifold doors are meant to open your living space to the outdoors. Any window treatment needs to completely stack out of the way when the doors are open — otherwise you've defeated the purpose. The other challenge is width: a typical Melbourne bifold span is 3,000–5,000mm, sometimes wider.

Products That Stack Back Properly

Most popular

Roller Blinds (Outside Mount)

Mounted above the door frame, they roll up to a compact tube when doors are open. For wide spans, use 2–3 linked blinds with a shared pelmet. Max single width ~3,000mm.

View Roller Blinds →
Best insulation

Honeycomb Blinds

Compact stack height means they virtually disappear when raised. Excellent thermal barrier for those west-facing afternoon sun situations Melbourne's western suburbs know well.

View Honeycomb Blinds →
Alternative

Curtains on Ceiling Track

Stack to one or both sides. Ripplefold or S-fold curtains on a ceiling-mounted track. Needs 300–400mm stack-back space per side for a full-width span.

View Curtains →
⚠️ Never do this Inside-mounting blinds onto bifold door panels. When the doors fold open, the blinds crash into each other, brackets get ripped out, and you end up replacing both the blinds and the door hardware. Always mount above or beside the entire door frame.

Measuring for Bifold Doors

Measure the total opening — from one side of the outer frame to the other, not individual door panels. For outside mount (recommended), add 75–100mm overlap per side and 100mm above the frame. Check that there's a solid lintel or sufficient wall above the doors for bracket mounting — some builders leave a thin plasterboard strip that can't support blind brackets without additional blocking.

For spans over 3,000mm, you'll likely need multiple blinds with a continuous pelmet to hide the join. Make sure you note the door handle positions — the blinds need enough clearance to pass over them when lowered.

Floor-to-Ceiling Windows

Extra-tall drops, weight considerations & motorisation

Floor-to-ceiling glazing is standard in Melbourne high-rise apartments and increasingly common in new homes. Drops of 2,400–3,000mm are normal; penthouse levels can hit 3,600mm or more. The challenges are fabric weight (long drops = heavy blinds), operating effort (pulling a 3m chain gets old fast), and the visual impact of getting the proportions right.

Best Options for Tall Windows

Best choice

Motorised Roller Blinds

Eliminates the heavy-chain problem entirely. Available in blockout or sunscreen up to 3,600mm drop. Battery motors keep installation simple — no electrician required.

View Motorised Blinds →
Alternative

S-Fold Sheer Curtains

Floor-length sheers on a ceiling track create a light, elegant look. The fabric weight is distributed across the track rather than concentrated in a headrail. Pair with a blockout roller behind for bedrooms.

View Curtains →

For manual operation, consider a geared chain drive — these use a reduction gear in the chain mechanism that makes operating heavy blinds much easier. Standard chains with drops over 2,400mm become a genuine frustration within weeks. If budget allows, motorisation pays for itself in daily convenience.

💡 Fabric choice matters more at height Light-coloured sunscreen fabrics show every crease and wave at tall drops. Choose a fabric with some body and texture — woven sunscreens or translucent rollers maintain a flatter hang than thin polyester. Ask for a large sample and hold it vertically before committing.

Measuring Floor-to-Ceiling

Measure height from the ceiling line (or where you want the blind mounted) to 10mm above the floor — never touching the floor, or the blind will buckle. For apartments with floor ducts or skirting boards, measure to the top of the obstruction plus 5mm clearance. Width follows standard rules: inside mount uses narrowest width at three points; outside mount adds 50–75mm per side.

Highlight & Clerestory Windows

High windows above doors, other windows, or along rooflines

Highlight windows sit above standard windows or doorways, typically 2,400–3,500mm off the floor. They're designed to bring in extra light without sacrificing wall space — but when that extra light becomes glare or heat, you need to cover them without needing a ladder every time.

Practical Solutions

Best choice

Motorised Roller or Honeycomb

The only practical option for windows you can't reach. Battery motor, remote control, set-and-forget schedules. Install once, operate from the couch.

Budget option

Fixed Sunscreen Film

If you never need to adjust — tinted or frosted window film reduces glare permanently. No motor, no moving parts. But zero flexibility.

Some clerestory windows are narrow horizontal strips (200–400mm tall). For these, honeycomb blinds with a compact headrail are ideal — they practically disappear when raised and the fabric body fits the small opening without looking oversized.

🔧 Installation reality check Mounting brackets at 3+ metres requires a solid ladder, a second person for safety, and confidence with a drill above your head. If that doesn't sound like your Saturday afternoon, our installation team includes bracket mounting at height as part of the service — no extra charge for standard installations.

For measuring: you'll need a laser measure or a long steel tape and a helper. Measure from the top of the lower window frame (or door frame) to the ceiling/soffit above the highlight window, then the width of the highlight opening. Note whether there's a mullion dividing the highlight from the window below — this determines if you're mounting into a frame or onto a wall surface.

Frequently Asked

Tricky Window FAQs

Can you put blinds on bay windows?
Yes. The best options are individual roller blinds or venetians mounted inside each panel, or a curved curtain track that follows the bay's shape. Plantation shutters also work well for angled bays — each panel gets its own shutter frame.
What blinds work for arched windows?
Custom-shaped plantation shutters are the gold standard — they can be manufactured to match the exact curve. Alternatively, mount a standard blind below the arch and leave the curved section exposed. This looks intentional and lets in feature light.
Do I need motorised blinds for skylights?
Strongly recommended. Manual skylight blinds exist but require reaching the ceiling every time you adjust. Battery-powered motorised blinds with remote or smart home control are the practical choice — no electrician needed for battery models.
How do you measure corner windows where two frames meet?
Measure each window independently, exactly as you would a standard window. The critical measurement is the gap where the two windows meet at the corner. If brackets would clash, inside mount or a corner curtain track solves it.
What blinds are best for bifold doors?
Roller blinds or honeycomb blinds mounted above the door frame (outside mount) are most popular — they roll up completely out of the way when the doors are open. Never inside-mount anything on bifold door panels.
Should I measure tricky windows myself or get a professional?
For standard bay windows and corner windows, confident DIYers can measure successfully. For arched windows, skylights, and unusual shapes, we strongly recommend a free professional measure — custom shapes have zero margin for error.
