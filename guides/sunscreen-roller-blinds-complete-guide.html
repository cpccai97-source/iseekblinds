What Are Sunscreen Roller Blinds?

Sunscreen roller blinds — also sold as screen blinds, solar blinds, or sheer roller blinds in Australia — are made from an open-weave mesh fabric. The weave has thousands of tiny perforations that diffuse incoming sunlight rather than blocking it. The result: significantly reduced glare and UV exposure, a clear view to the outside, and a room that stays bright but comfortable.

The key technical characteristic is the openness factor — the percentage of the fabric that is, literally, open holes. A 1% fabric has tighter weave than a 10% fabric. This single number drives most performance differences: glare reduction, UV blocking, view quality, and nighttime privacy.

In Melbourne's climate, sunscreen blinds are one of the most popular blind choices for north-facing and west-facing living rooms, where afternoon sun is intense but occupants still want to see the garden.

Quick terminology

"Sunscreen", "screen", "solar", and "sheer roller blind" are used interchangeably by Australian retailers. They all refer to open-weave roller blind fabric. Some suppliers use "sheer" specifically for softer, more translucent fabrics — but the function is the same.

Openness Factor: The Number That Matters Most

Openness factor (OF) is expressed as a percentage and describes the ratio of open area to total fabric area. Industry-standard ratings in Australia run from 1% to 14%, with 1%, 3%, 5%, and 10% being the most common.

If you're unsure, go with 5%. It's the industry sweet spot: good UV blocking, clear view, comfortable rooms. If you're west-facing and cop 4pm sun directly, drop to 3%. If you're mainly after the view and the sun isn't a problem, go 10%.

UV & Glare Performance in Australian Conditions

Australia has the highest UV index of any continent. Melbourne regularly hits UV index 10–12 in summer — the "extreme" category from the Bureau of Meteorology. Ordinary glass blocks most UVB (the sunburn wavelength) but transmits a significant portion of UVA, which drives furniture fade, flooring bleaching, and long-term skin damage.

Quality sunscreen fabrics are rated against AS/NZS 4399 for UV transmission. A standard 5% screen fabric typically achieves UPF 50+, meaning it blocks over 98% of UV that would otherwise pass through the glass. The practical results:

  • Timber floors and rugs retain colour significantly longer
  • Artwork, furniture, and flooring covered by AS 2399 warranty claims from UV fade are reduced
  • Skin UV exposure is dramatically cut for people who work near windows all day
  • Interior temperature reduces by 3–7°C on summer afternoons with 5% fabric (west-facing room)

Sunscreen fabric diffuses and absorbs incoming UV and glare while transmitting ambient light

For glare reduction, sunscreen blinds work differently to blockout. They don't cut the light source — they scatter and soften it. This is ideal for rooms where you want brightness without the discomfort of direct beam sun on screens, surfaces, or eyes. For home offices, it's often the difference between a usable workspace and an unusable one.

Privacy: Day vs Night

This is the most common point of confusion about sunscreen blinds, so it's worth being direct about it.

During the day

Good privacy. From outside, the contrast between bright exterior light and a darker interior makes the screen fabric appear opaque. People outside see the blind, not into your home. Lower openness (1–3%) gives more daytime privacy; higher openness (10%+) gives less.

At night — with lights on inside

No privacy. When interior lighting is brighter than outside, visibility reverses completely. Looking in from outside is straightforward. The open-weave mesh provides no nighttime privacy when interior lights are on.

The solution for full privacy

Pair your sunscreen with a blockout roller blind on a dual roller system. Use the sunscreen all day, drop the blockout at night. This is iSeekBlinds' most-requested combination — it handles both privacy situations without compromise.

If budget is a concern and you only want one blind, a 3% sunscreen gives adequate daytime privacy in most situations and the nighttime view-in is only visible from very close range in well-lit conditions. But for living rooms and bedrooms with street-facing windows, a dual system is strongly recommended.

Dual Roller Systems — The Best of Both

A dual roller blind mounts a sunscreen and a blockout blind on the same fascia, operating independently. During the day, run the sunscreen. At night, drop the blockout for full privacy and darkness.

This combination is iSeekBlinds' most popular window treatment for master bedrooms and open-plan living areas. The two blinds share a single headrail cassette, so the installation is clean — no double brackets or exposed hardware.

  • Motorised dual rollers let you automate both — schedule the blockout to open at dawn and close at sunset automatically via your phone or voice assistant
  • Custom fabric pairing: pick a 5% screen in a light linen tone, pair with a white blockout — the two complement each other without clashing
  • Inside mount recommended for dual rollers — ensures both blinds hang flush within the frame
Motorisation note

If you're ordering a dual roller, consider motorising at the same time. The retrofit cost to add a motor later is higher than installing during the initial fit. Motorised blind systems in Melbourne now integrate with Google Home, Apple HomeKit, and Amazon Alexa.

Colour & Fabric Selection

Sunscreen fabrics come in two main colour types that affect both aesthetics and performance:

Light/White/Cream tones

Reflect more solar heat. Make the room feel airier. Better if your room is already dark. Provide less daytime privacy (light fabric appears more transparent from outside). Popular for north-facing rooms where you want brightness maintained.

Dark/Charcoal/Black tones

Absorb more solar heat (bad) but provide better glare control and significantly better outward view clarity. Because dark fabrics don't create reflective haze, the view through a 5% charcoal screen is dramatically clearer than through a 5% white screen. Preferred for home offices and rooms where view quality matters. Popular for living rooms in contemporary Melbourne homes.

The dark-fabric counter-intuition

It seems wrong, but dark screens give better view and better glare control than light screens at the same openness factor. The darker weave reduces the "milky" light scatter from the fabric itself. If your primary goal is glare reduction or view clarity, choose dark. For heat reflection, choose light.

Fabric weight

Heavier fabrics (300–500 g/m²) hang better on wide spans and resist wind movement if the window is open. Lighter fabrics are fine for smaller or interior windows. For windows over 2400mm wide, discuss fabric weight with your consultant — cheaper light fabrics can "bow" when wide.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a sunscreen roller blind?
A roller blind made from open-weave mesh fabric that filters sunlight rather than blocking it. The openness factor (1–14%) determines how much light, UV, and view passes through.
What openness factor should I choose?
5% is the most popular — best balance of view, glare control, and daytime privacy. 3% for stronger shade and privacy (bedrooms, intense west sun). 10% for maximum view where sun is mild.
Do sunscreen blinds provide privacy at night?
No — when interior lights are on at night, sunscreen blinds are transparent from outside. Pair with a blockout blind (dual roller) for nighttime privacy.
How much UV do they block?
Quality fabrics block 85–99% UV depending on openness. A 5% screen typically achieves UPF 50+ — over 98% UV blocked. Effective protection for furniture, flooring, and occupants.
What's the difference between sunscreen and sheer roller blinds?
In Australia the terms are interchangeable — same product. Some suppliers use "sheer" for softer-look fabrics but the function is identical.
Are dark or light fabrics better?
Depends on priority. Dark fabrics give better view clarity and glare control. Light fabrics reflect more solar heat and feel airier. For home offices or rooms where view matters: dark. For heat reduction: light.
How much do sunscreen roller blinds cost in Melbourne?
Single supply and install: $160–$340 per window. Dual roller (with blockout): $320–$640. Motorised: add $250–$400 per blind. iSeekBlinds offers free in-home measure with no obligation.
