A conversation about light

Every studio has different needs. Here's what we hear — and what we know works.

🏺
The Potter
My north-facing windows are brilliant for throwing, but by 2pm the glare off the wheel is blinding. And my glazes look completely different at noon versus 4pm.
🪟
iSeekBlinds
That colour-shift is the number one complaint from ceramicists. You need light that's consistent, not just controlled. We'd recommend sheer rollers in a warm-neutral tone — they diffuse without creating that blue cast that makes red glazes look muddy.
🏺
The Potter
What about near the kiln? It gets seriously hot on that side, and honestly clay dust gets on everything. I don't want something delicate.
🪟
iSeekBlinds
Kiln-side we'd go with aluminium venetians — they handle the heat, wipe clean with a damp cloth, and the tilt lets you redirect afternoon sun without closing off all natural light. For the glazing area, honeycomb thermals give UV protection that keeps your colour evaluations honest.
🏺
The Potter
I run workshops too — sometimes 8 people on wheels. Students need to see what they're doing but we get complaints about the sun being right in their eyes on winter afternoons.
🪟
iSeekBlinds
That's where day-night blinds are perfect. Alternate sheer and opaque bands — you can fine-tune in seconds between throws. No one has to move seats, no one gets blinded. We install motorised options too, so you're not wiping clay on chains mid-class.
🏺
The Potter
Honestly, I never thought about blinds this much. It always felt like a "just pick something" decision.
🪟
iSeekBlinds
That's because most people sell blinds as decoration. In a studio, they're tools. We treat them that way. Free measure, we'll bring samples so you can test how they change your light in situ — not from a catalogue swatch.
