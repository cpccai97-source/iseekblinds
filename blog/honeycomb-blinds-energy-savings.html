Honeycomb Blinds & Energy Savings: How Much Can You Really Save?

11 min read

TL;DR

Honeycomb blinds trap air in cellular pockets, adding insulation your windows desperately lack. Single cell = R-value ~2.0–2.5. Double cell = R-value ~3.8–4.8. Most Melbourne homes save 15–25% on heating/cooling bills. They pay for themselves in 2–4 years — faster on single-glazed windows.

Every Melbourne winter, your windows bleed heat. A standard single-pane window has an R-value of about 0.13 — essentially a hole in your wall with glass over it. Even double glazing only gets you to around 0.78. Meanwhile, your ceiling insulation sits at R-4.0 or higher.

Windows are the weak link, and honeycomb blinds are the most practical fix that doesn't involve ripping out frames and re-glazing.

But how much do they actually save? Not the marketing fluff — the real numbers. Let's break it down.

How Honeycomb Blinds Actually Insulate

The principle is dead simple: trapped air is an excellent insulator. Honeycomb blinds (also called cellular blinds) are made of pleated fabric that forms hexagonal cells — like a cross-section of a beehive. Each cell traps a pocket of still air between your window glass and your room.

Cross-section: single-cell vs double-cell honeycomb blinds. More air pockets = more insulation.

Still air doesn't conduct heat well — that's the same principle behind double glazing, puffer jackets, and your ceiling insulation batts. Honeycomb blinds add one or two layers of trapped air to your window system without any structural changes.

The R-Value Breakdown

R-value measures thermal resistance — higher is better. Here's how honeycomb blinds compare to what you're probably working with right now:

The gap is dramatic. A single-cell honeycomb blind improves your window's thermal resistance by roughly 15–19 times compared to bare single glazing. Double cell takes it further — approaching the performance of a well-insulated wall section.

Worth noting: R-values for window treatments are measured differently across manufacturers. The figures above are industry-standard ranges from laboratory testing. Real-world performance depends on fit — a blind with gaps around the edges loses much of its insulating benefit through convection.

Our recommendation: Single cell covers 80% of Melbourne homes well. Double cell earns its premium on large south-facing windows, floor-to-ceiling glass, and homes with single glazing where every bit of insulation counts. If you already have double-glazed windows, single cell is almost always sufficient.

The Stack Height Trade-Off

Double cell blinds stack higher when raised — the extra layer of cells makes the folded fabric thicker. On a standard 1500mm window, the stack is barely noticeable. On a small 600mm window, it can eat into your glass area. Something to consider for kitchens and bathrooms where you want maximum light when the blind is up.

Summer vs Winter: How Honeycomb Blinds Work Both Ways

Most people think of honeycomb blinds as winter insulation. They're equally valuable in Melbourne's increasingly harsh summers.

Winter: Trapping Heat In

Melbourne loses most home heat through windows. Honeycomb blinds create a thermal barrier that slows heat escaping to the cold glass. Lower them at dusk, raise them on sunny winter mornings to capture free solar warmth. This push-pull approach maximises savings — you're using the sun as a heater during the day and the blinds as insulation at night.

Summer: Blocking Heat Out

On a 40°C day, west-facing windows can push surface temperatures above 60°C. Honeycomb blinds — especially those with reflective aluminium backing — bounce radiant heat back through the glass. Pre-close blinds on west and north windows before the afternoon hits. Thermal-backed honeycomb blinds can reduce solar heat gain by 60–80%.

Smart strategy: Pair honeycomb blinds with motorisation and a timer. Set west-facing blinds to close at 1pm in summer and open at 8am in winter. You'll capture every saving without lifting a finger. See our smart home blinds guide.

Melbourne-Specific Advice

Melbourne's climate is a special case — genuinely cold winters (5–10°C mornings are common from June to August) and increasingly brutal summers (multiple 40°C+ days per season). Most Australian "energy blind" advice is written for Sydney or Brisbane. Here's what actually matters in Melbourne:

  • South-facing windows — Never get direct sun. Always losing heat. These are your highest-priority windows for honeycomb blinds. Consider double cell here.
  • West-facing windows — The afternoon heat problem. Thermal-backed honeycomb blinds with reflective aluminium are the best defence. These windows alone can account for 30% of your summer cooling costs.
  • North-facing windows — Melbourne's sweet spot. Winter sun pours in (good). Summer sun is high and mostly blocked by eaves (usually). Use honeycomb blinds strategically: up during winter days to capture warmth, down in summer afternoons if your eaves don't fully shade them.
  • East-facing windows — Morning sun, then nothing. Important for bedrooms. Blockout honeycomb gives you both darkness for sleeping and insulation year-round.

The Victorian Energy Upgrades (VEU) program sometimes offers rebates on energy-efficient window treatments. Check Victorian Energy Saver for current rebates — the criteria change regularly, but honeycomb blinds have qualified in previous rounds.

The Payback Calculation

For a typical 3-bedroom Melbourne home with 10 windows:

  • Single cell throughout: ~$2,000–$2,800 installed. At $400–$600/year savings = payback in 3.5–5 years
  • Double cell on priority windows, single cell elsewhere: ~$2,800–$3,800. At $500–$700/year savings = payback in 4–5.5 years
  • Homes with single glazing: Savings are higher (25%+), payback drops to 2–3.5 years

After the payback period, the savings are pure profit for the 10–15 year lifespan of quality honeycomb blinds. That's $4,000–$8,000 in lifetime energy savings for a typical home — before factoring in rising electricity prices.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much can honeycomb blinds reduce energy bills?

Most Melbourne homes see a 15–25% reduction in heating and cooling costs after installing honeycomb blinds on all windows. Homes with single-glazed windows or large west-facing glass areas often report even greater savings because the starting heat loss is higher.

Are double cell honeycomb blinds worth the extra cost?

Double cell honeycombs deliver roughly 30–40% better insulation than single cell (R-value 3.8–4.8 vs 2.0–2.5). For most rooms, single cell provides excellent value. Double cell makes the biggest difference on large, exposed windows — particularly south-facing or oversized glass in open-plan areas.

Do honeycomb blinds work in summer as well as winter?

Yes. The trapped air pockets work both ways — they slow heat entering your home in summer just as they slow heat escaping in winter. For maximum summer performance, choose a fabric with a reflective backing which bounces radiant heat back through the glass.

Can I get honeycomb blinds with motorisation?

Absolutely. Motorised honeycomb blinds can be programmed to open and close on a schedule — lowering automatically at dusk to trap heat, and rising at dawn to capture free solar warmth. Most systems integrate with Google Home and Alexa. See our motorised solutions.

How do honeycomb blinds compare to curtains for insulation?

Honeycomb blinds outperform most curtains. A double-cell honeycomb achieves R-value 3.8–4.8, while even heavy lined curtains with pelmets typically reach R-1.5–2.5. Honeycomb blinds also fit more tightly to the window frame, reducing convection currents at the edges.

What colour honeycomb blind is best for energy efficiency?

The colour of the room-facing fabric has minimal impact on insulation — the R-value comes from the air pockets, not the fabric colour. Choose the colour you love; the energy performance stays the same.
