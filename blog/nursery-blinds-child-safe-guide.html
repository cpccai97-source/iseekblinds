The Short Answer

For nurseries, motorised blockout roller blinds are the best all-round choice — completely child-safe, total light control, silent operation via app. If budget is tighter, cordless spring-operated blockout rollers are excellent. For maximum insulation + darkness, cordless honeycomb blinds with blockout fabric. Whatever you choose: no cords, no chains, no exceptions.

Why Nursery Blinds Need Special Attention

A nursery blind has two jobs that no other room demands at the same time: keep the room pitch-dark at 2pm and be impossible for a child to hurt themselves on. That combination eliminates most standard blinds before you even start comparing features.

  • Daytime darkness is critical. Babies nap during the day — often 2–4 times. Melatonin production requires genuine darkness, not "dim." Melbourne's summer daylight runs from 5:30am to 8:45pm. Without proper blockout, you're fighting biology.
  • Safety isn't a feature — it's the baseline. Between 2001 and 2022, at least 21 young children died in Australia from blind cord strangulation. Cords, chains, and looped strings are a strangulation hazard. In a room where a child is unsupervised (sleeping, playing), zero accessible cords is the only acceptable standard.
  • Silent operation matters. Any parent who's tiptoed out of a nursery only to have a rattling chain blind wake the baby knows this viscerally. The blind needs to be operated without entering the room — or at least without making noise.
  • The blind outlives the nursery. A good nursery blind serves through toddlerhood, becomes a kids' room blind, and eventually a guest room blind. Choose for durability and adaptability.

Australian Safety Standards — What You Need to Know

The Numbers That Matter

At least 21 Australian children have died from blind cord strangulation since 2001. The ACCC reports 1–2 deaths per year, making blind cords one of the top hidden hazards in Australian homes. Since 2014, all new corded blinds sold in Australia must comply with AS/NZS 13120.

What AS/NZS 13120 Requires

The standard mandates that all new corded internal window coverings include safety devices that prevent child access to cords. In practice, this means:

  • Chain tensioners must be supplied with any chain-operated blind to keep chains taut against the wall
  • Cord cleats must be included for cord-operated products
  • Breakaway connectors are required on looped cords, designed to separate under a child's weight
  • Warning labels must be permanently affixed to the product
⚠️ Important: While AS/NZS 13120 makes corded blinds safer, it doesn't make them safe enough for a nursery. The standard is a minimum for the general market. For a room where a child sleeps unsupervised, the ACCC, Kidsafe, and every Australian blinds industry body recommend cordless or motorised blinds only. Retrofit safety devices are a compromise, not a solution.

What About Older Blinds?

Blinds installed before 2014 may not comply with current standards. If you're moving into a home with existing nursery blinds, check for:

  • Any accessible cord, chain, or looped string — at any height
  • Missing or damaged chain tensioners
  • Cords that a child could reach by climbing furniture

If any of these exist in the nursery, replace the blinds before the baby arrives. This isn't a "when you get around to it" item. For a full room-by-room audit approach, see our childproofing guide.

Getting the Nursery Dark Enough for Daytime Naps

A blockout blind doesn't mean a blockout room. Light sneaks in around the edges — and babies are far more light-sensitive than adults. Here's the layered approach, from basic to bulletproof:

Cross-section: where light leaks around a standard blind. Side channels, face-fit mounting, and a pelmet close the gaps.
  1. Level 1 — Blockout fabric (standard): A blockout roller blocks 95–99% of light through the material. Some light still enters at the sides, top, and bottom. Fine for nighttime. Often not dark enough for midday naps.
  2. Level 2 — Add side channels: Aluminium channels fixed to the frame guide the fabric edges, eliminating side light gaps. Adds $30–$60 per window. The single biggest improvement you can make.
  3. Level 3 — Face-fit with overlap: Mount the blind on the wall face, 50–75mm wider than the frame on each side. This covers the light gaps that inside-mount misses. See our measuring guide for face-fit instructions.
  4. Level 4 — Pelmet or fascia: A cover at the top seals light from leaking over the roller mechanism. Can be a simple matching fascia or an architectural pelmet that becomes a design feature.
  5. Level 5 — Curtain overlay: Lined blockout curtains over the roller. The curtain seals remaining gaps and adds thermal insulation. The gold standard for nurseries. Make sure curtains don't have tieback cords.
For Melbourne nurseries: At minimum, aim for Level 2 (blockout roller + side channels). This gets the room dark enough for reliable daytime naps without spending a fortune. If the nursery faces east or west, consider Level 4 — those orientations get direct sun that can overwhelm a standard setup. See our bedroom blinds guide for more darkness-maximising strategies.

Choosing Colours & Fabrics

Nursery design matters to parents — and the blind is often the largest single colour block in the room. Here's what to consider beyond aesthetics:

Fabric Backing Matters More Than Face Colour

All blockout roller blinds have a multi-layer construction. The white or silver backing is what reflects light and heat — the face colour is purely aesthetic. Choose whatever colour suits the nursery design; it won't affect light blocking performance. Common nursery-friendly palettes:

  • Warm neutrals (linen, stone, oat) — versatile, grows with the child, matches everything
  • Soft whites (ivory, cloud, chalk) — clean, bright, works with bold furniture or wall colours
  • Muted colours (sage, dusty blue, blush) — adds personality without overpowering the room
  • Dark tones (charcoal, navy) — absorb rather than reflect light, making the room feel psychologically darker, but will absorb heat at the window in summer

Fabric Certifications

For a nursery environment, look for:

  • OEKO-TEX Standard 100 — certified free of harmful substances (relevant since babies breathe close to the fabric)
  • Low-VOC — no off-gassing from the fabric or adhesive layers
  • Fire retardancy — most commercial blind fabrics meet AS 1530.2; confirm with your supplier
  • Wipeable surface — nursery blinds will encounter sticky fingers sooner than you think. Roller blind fabrics are generally easy to wipe; honeycomb cells less so. Our cleaning guide covers maintenance by product type.

Frequently Asked Questions

What blinds are safest for a nursery?

Cordless and motorised blinds are the safest options. Cordless spring-operated roller blinds and cordless honeycomb blinds eliminate all accessible cords and chains. Motorised blinds remove manual operation entirely. Under AS/NZS 13120, all new blinds in children's rooms must be cordless or have inaccessible cords.

Do I need blockout blinds in a nursery?

Yes — blockout blinds are strongly recommended. Babies need daytime naps in genuine darkness to produce melatonin. Melbourne's summer daylight runs from 5:30am to nearly 9pm, making blockout essential. Standard blockout rollers block 95–99% of light; side channels push that to 99%+.

Are motorised blinds worth it for a nursery?

Absolutely. They're 100% child-safe, can be operated silently from outside the room, and scheduled to close before nap time. They add $150–$250 per window. For a nursery, it's arguably the single best upgrade. How to motorise existing blinds.

How much do nursery blinds cost in Melbourne?

For a standard nursery window (900mm × 1500mm), installed: cordless spring roller $200–$380, cordless honeycomb $280–$520, motorised roller $350–$550, roller with side channels $280–$450, plantation shutters $450–$850. A typical nursery with 1–2 windows costs $350–$900.

What Australian standard applies to nursery blinds?

AS/NZS 13120:2017 is the key standard. It mandates all new corded blinds include safety devices to prevent child access to cords. The ACCC recommends going further — cordless or motorised blinds only — in rooms where children sleep. Full childproofing guide.

Can I make my existing blinds child-safe for the nursery?

Partially. Cord tensioners, cleats, and breakaway connectors help. But for the nursery specifically, replacing corded blinds entirely is the recommended approach. The safety margin is too important to rely on retrofits in a room where a child sleeps unsupervised.
