How to Install Plantation Shutters Yourself

A genuine step-by-step walkthrough — not the watered-down version. Frame types, pilot holes, shimming, hinge pins, and the five mistakes that turn a weekend project into a warranty claim.

18 March 2026 · 10 min read
Time Per Window
30–60 min
Difficulty
Medium
People Needed
1–2

Plantation shutters are one of the most satisfying DIY window treatments to install — provided you know what you're doing. Unlike roller blinds (two brackets and done), shutters involve mounting a frame, hanging hinged panels, and making fine adjustments so everything lines up and operates smoothly.

The good news: if your windows are standard rectangular openings in a timber-framed house, you can absolutely do this yourself with a cordless drill and an hour of patience per window. This guide covers the whole process — from understanding your frame type to dealing with out-of-square reveals.

Before You Start

Already measured? Good. If not, read our plantation shutter measuring guide first. Bad measurements make good installation impossible.

What You'll Need

Tools

  • Cordless drill/driver (charged!)
  • 2mm drill bit (pilot holes — timber)
  • 5mm masonry bit + wall plugs (brick)
  • Phillips head screwdriver (manual)
  • Spirit level — 600mm minimum
  • Steel tape measure
  • Pencil
  • Step ladder

Materials (Usually Included)

  • Shutter frame pieces (top, bottom, sides)
  • Shutter panels (pre-assembled)
  • Hinge pins
  • Frame joining screws
  • Mounting screws
  • Screw caps / cover buttons
  • Plastic shims or packers
  • Magnetic catches (multi-panel kits)

L-Frame vs Z-Frame: Which One Do You Have?

Your frame type determines how the shutter mounts and what recess depth you need. Most Australian suppliers ship one of two profiles. Open your kit and check — it matters for the next steps.

Most Common

L-Frame (Inside Mount)

The L-shaped profile sits inside the recess. The front lip covers the edge of the reveal, hiding gaps of up to 5mm between frame and wall.

  • Minimum recess depth: 65mm
  • Clean, flush finish from inside room
  • May need silicone to fill small gaps
  • Best for: new builds, plasterboard reveals
Shallow Recess

Z-Frame (Outside Mount)

The Z-profile wraps over the face of the wall — one return sits on the wall surface, the other inside the recess. Works when there's not enough depth for an L-frame.

  • Minimum recess depth: only 30mm
  • Protrudes ~25mm from wall face
  • Hides uneven or damaged reveals
  • Best for: old homes, brick, shallow windows

Before You Start: Four Quick Checks

  1. Confirm your reveal is square. Measure diagonals corner-to-corner. If they differ by more than 5mm, you'll need shims to compensate. More than 10mm difference? Consider a Z-frame or outside mount instead.
  2. Identify your wall type. Timber stud + plasterboard? Just drill pilot holes. Brick or stone reveal? You need masonry bits and wall plugs — budget an extra 15 minutes per window.
  3. Check for obstructions. Window handles, security sensors, trickle vents, curtain rod brackets — anything inside the recess that might clash with the frame or stop louvres from opening fully.
  4. Clear the workspace. Move furniture away from the window. Lay a drop sheet if you're drilling into masonry — the dust gets everywhere.
⚠️ Safety First

Shutters are heavy, especially timber and basswood. A 1200mm-wide, 2-panel set can weigh 12–15kg. Have a second person hold panels while you work on the hinge pins if the window is above chest height.

Step-by-Step Installation

1

Unpack and Inspect Everything

Lay all components on a clean surface. You should have: frame pieces (typically 4 — top, bottom, left, right), shutter panels with hinges pre-attached, hinge pins, mounting screws, corner joining screws, screw caps, and shims.

Check panels against your order confirmation. Compare the width and drop to what you measured. Look for shipping damage — chips, cracks, misaligned louvres. It's much easier to resolve issues now than after installation.

2

Assemble the Frame

Most frames arrive in 4 pieces. Lay them on a flat floor and join the corners using the supplied corner screws or brackets. The top and bottom pieces usually sit between the sides (check your kit instructions — some are reversed).

Use a set square or measure diagonals to confirm the assembled frame is square. If the diagonals match within 2mm, you're fine. Tighten corner screws firmly but don't over-torque — PVC frames can crack, and timber frames can strip.

3

Dry-Fit the Frame

Lift the assembled frame into the window recess (inside mount) or hold it against the wall face (outside mount). The fit should be snug but not forced — there should be 2–4mm clearance on each side for an inside mount.

Hold the spirit level against the top and one side. Mark with pencil where gaps exist between the frame and the reveal. These are your shimming points. Also mark the pre-drilled screw holes on the frame onto the reveal or wall behind.

4

Pre-Drill Pilot Holes

This step is not optional. Remove the frame. Drill pilot holes at each marked position — 2mm bit for timber, 5mm masonry bit for brick (then tap in wall plugs).

Skipping pilot holes is the single most common DIY mistake. Without them, screws split timber reveals, wander off-line in plasterboard, and fail to grip in masonry. Two minutes of drilling saves hours of frustration.

5

Secure the Frame with Shims

Place the frame back in the opening. Insert plastic shims behind the frame at any gap points you identified. Start with the top — drive one screw loosely, check level, adjust shims, then tighten. Repeat for the sides and bottom.

Critical: check level and plumb after every screw. Tightening one side can pull the opposite side out of alignment. Work in a sequence: top centre → left top → right top → left bottom → right bottom → remaining points.

Once all screws are in and the frame is level, snap off any protruding shims with a utility knife or pliers. They should break cleanly at the frame edge.

6

Hang the Shutter Panels

This is the satisfying part. Hold the first panel next to the frame with the hinges aligned. The hinges on the panel and the hinges on the frame have matching barrels — line them up and drop a hinge pin in from above. It should slide in smoothly.

Start with the panel closest to the hinge side (usually left). Once the pin is in, the panel should swing open and closed freely. If it binds or drags, the frame isn't quite level — go back to Step 5 and adjust.

Repeat for each panel. For a standard 2-panel window, both panels hinge from opposite sides and meet in the middle.

7

Install T-Posts and Magnetic Catches

Windows with 3 or more panels need a centre T-post (a vertical divider that panels close against). The T-post screws into the top and bottom frame pieces at the marked position. Use your level to ensure it's perfectly vertical.

Attach the magnetic catches or bullet catches to the T-post and corresponding panel edges. When panels close, they should snap gently to the post with an even 2–3mm gap on each side. If one panel sits forward of the other, adjust the hinge packer behind the problem panel.

8

Final Adjustments and Cleanup

Open and close every panel several times. Tilt the louvres through their full range using the tilt rod. If louvres are stiff, locate the tension screw (usually a small Phillips screw on the side of the panel frame, near the top) and loosen it a quarter-turn at a time until the louvres move smoothly but hold their position.

Press in screw caps over every visible screw head. For L-frames with a visible gap between the frame and the reveal, run a thin bead of colour-matched silicone along the edge for a clean finish. Wipe excess immediately with a damp cloth.

Stand back. Admire. You earned it.

Five Mistakes That Ruin a Good Install

⚠️ Mistake #1 — Skipping Pilot Holes

We said it in Step 4, we'll say it again. Driving screws directly into timber reveals splits the wood. Into plasterboard without anchors, the screws pull out within weeks under the shutter's weight. Into brick without plugs, they just spin uselessly. Always pre-drill.

⚠️ Mistake #2 — Over-Tightening Frame Screws

Cranking screws tight feels productive, but it bows the frame inward. Bowed frames mean panels won't close properly — they'll either gap at the top and bottom or bind in the middle. Snug is enough. If in doubt, stop half a turn before you think you should.

⚠️ Mistake #3 — Not Checking Level After Each Screw

The frame looked level before you started screwing. Then you drove three screws on the left side and now the right side is 4mm higher. Tightening one point shifts others. Check level after every single screw. It takes five seconds and saves re-doing the whole frame.

⚠️ Mistake #4 — Wrong Frame Type for Your Recess

An L-frame in a 45mm-deep recess doesn't fit. The shutter panel will protrude past the wall face and look wrong. Conversely, a Z-frame in a 100mm-deep recess wastes depth and looks unnecessarily bulky. Measure your recess depth and match the frame type.

⚠️ Mistake #5 — Forcing Hinge Pins

If a hinge pin won't drop in smoothly, the frame-to-panel hinges aren't aligned. Forcing it bends the hinge barrel and causes permanent binding. Step back, check the frame is level, adjust if needed, then try again. The pin should fall in under its own weight with a gentle push.

Troubleshooting

Problem
Solution
Panels drag on the sill
Frame is tilting forward or bottom isn't level. Loosen bottom screws, add shims behind the bottom of the frame, re-level and re-tighten.
Visible gap between panels when closed
Panel hinges need a packer (thin shim behind the hinge). Loosen the panel, slide a 1–2mm packer behind the hinge mounting point, re-tighten. The panel shifts outward to close the gap.
Louvres won't stay in position
Tighten the tension screw on the panel frame by a quarter-turn. If still loose, the tension spring may need replacement — contact the manufacturer.
Frame won't sit square in an out-of-square reveal
Don't try to match the reveal's angle. Mount the frame level and plumb using shims to fill gaps. Fill the remaining wedge-shaped gap with silicone or use a decorative trim piece.
Screws spinning in plasterboard
You missed the timber stud. Use a stud finder to locate studs, or switch to plasterboard anchors rated for the shutter weight (12–15kg per window). Toggle bolts work well.

When to Call a Professional

DIY shutter installation works well for standard rectangular windows in homes with timber or plasterboard reveals. But some situations genuinely benefit from professional fitting:

  • Bay windows — the angled joins between frame sections require precision cuts and custom corner pieces
  • Arched or shaped windows — curved frames need to be templated and fitted on-site
  • Heritage homes — stone, lime render, or old plaster reveals crumble unpredictably when drilled
  • Very large spans — windows over 2400mm wide with 4+ panels need a T-post and precise alignment across the full width
  • Multi-storey or hard-to-reach windows — safety risk with heavy panels on tall ladders
  • When you're unsure about measurements — a bad install damages expensive shutters and the window reveal. Professional measure-and-install guarantees the fit

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to install plantation shutters yourself?
A single standard window (up to 1200mm wide, 2 panels) takes 30–45 minutes once you have the technique. Your first window will take 60–90 minutes as you figure out the process. A full house of 8–10 windows is a solid weekend project — plan for Saturday.
What's the difference between L-frame and Z-frame?
L-frame has an L-shaped profile that sits inside the recess and covers the front edge. Requires at least 65mm recess depth. Z-frame has a Z-profile that wraps over the wall face — used when the recess is shallower than 65mm or for an outside-mount look. Z-frame needs only 30mm depth but protrudes more into the room.
Can I install plantation shutters into brick?
Yes. Use a 5mm or 6mm masonry drill bit, drill into the brick (not the mortar line), insert plastic wall plugs, then screw the frame through. This adds about 15–20 minutes per window compared to timber reveals. A hammer drill makes it much easier.
Do I need to remove existing blinds or curtains first?
Yes — remove any existing window covering and its brackets before measuring the clear recess. Old bracket holes in timber can be filled with wood putty. If the shutter frame covers the old holes, just leave them. For outside-mount shutters, remove curtain rod brackets if they'd interfere with the Z-frame.
What minimum recess depth do I need?
65mm for a standard L-frame inside mount. 90mm or more gives a fully flush fit where shutters sit entirely within the recess. If your recess is 30–60mm, use a Z-frame. Under 30mm, you need a full outside-mount frame. Always measure depth at all four corners — older homes often taper.
Should I install plantation shutters myself or hire a professional?
DIY works for standard rectangular windows in timber-framed homes if you're comfortable with a drill and level. Hire a pro for bay windows, arched openings, heritage homes with stone or plaster reveals, spans over 2400mm, or if measurements feel uncertain.
