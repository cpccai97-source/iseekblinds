How to Fix a Broken Blind Chain

Your roller blind chain has snapped, slipped off, or jammed? Don't call a tradesman yet. Most chain problems take under 30 minutes to fix with a screwdriver and a bit of patience.

17 March 2026 · 11 min read

It's one of the most common blind annoyances in Australia — you give the chain a tug and it goes slack, the blind freezes halfway, or the chain pops clean off the mechanism. Before you start shopping for a replacement blind, the fix is usually simpler (and cheaper) than you'd expect.

This guide covers the four most common roller blind chain problems, how to diagnose which one you have, and the step-by-step repair for each. If your blind uses a different mechanism — like a venetian cord ladder or a curtain draw cord — some principles apply, but the chain-specific steps here focus on roller blinds with bead-chain sidewinders.

Diagnose the Problem First

Not all chain issues are the same. The fix depends on what actually went wrong. Pull the chain gently and observe:

⛓️

Chain Off the Track

Chain has separated from the internal sprocket. The chain moves freely but the blind doesn't respond. Most common cause: pulling the chain at an angle or too hard.

Easy Fix — 10 min
🔗

Chain Snapped or Broken

The bead chain has physically broken — usually at a connector join or where it's worn thin. You'll need a replacement chain section and a joiner.

Medium Fix — 20 min
⚙️

Chain Jammed in Mechanism

Chain is stuck and won't move in either direction. Usually caused by dust buildup, a bent bead catching on the chain guide, or a foreign object inside the sidewinder.

Easy Fix — 15 min
🔄

Chain Slips / Won't Hold

You pull the chain and the blind goes up, but it slowly creeps back down. The clutch mechanism inside isn't gripping. May need lubrication or clutch replacement.

Harder Fix — 30 min

What You'll Need

For most chain repairs, you'll need these tools and parts:

  • Flat-head screwdriver — to open the sidewinder cover
  • Needle-nose pliers — for stubborn pins and tight spaces
  • Silicone lubricant spray (not WD-40) — for the clutch and sprocket
  • Replacement bead chain — if the chain is broken (match the bead size: 3.2mm or 4.5mm)
  • Chain connector/joiner — to close the loop on a new chain
  • Step ladder — if your blinds are mounted high
  • Clean cloth — to wipe dust from the mechanism
Where to Buy Parts

Bunnings stocks generic sidewinder kits (around $15–$25) that include a new sidewinder housing, 1800mm bead chain, and pin end. Fix 1: Chain Off the Track

This is the most common problem and the easiest to fix. The chain has jumped off the sprocket inside the sidewinder.

  1. Remove the blind from the wall Roll the blind up fully to protect the fabric. Lift the pin-end bracket first (usually a spring clip — push the blind towards the chain end and the pin pops free), then slide the chain-mechanism end out of its bracket.
  2. Lay the blind on a flat surface A kitchen bench or table works well. Position the sidewinder (chain wheel) facing you.
  3. Open the sidewinder cover Look for a small plastic retention tab on the side of the sidewinder housing. Use a flat-head screwdriver to gently pry it — the cover will swing open or pop off, exposing the sprocket wheel.
  4. Clean and inspect Blow out any dust and debris. Check the chain guide tabs (small plastic pieces that keep the chain seated) — if they're cracked, that's why the chain keeps jumping off.
  5. Reseat the chain Thread the bead chain back around the sprocket, making sure each bead sits in its groove. The chain should wrap about halfway around the sprocket.
  6. Close the cover and remount Snap the sidewinder cover back into place. Give the pin-end a few turns to re-tension the spring. Reattach to brackets (chain end first), and test.
⚠️ Don't Force It

If the chain won't sit in the sprocket grooves, don't force it — the bead size might not match. Australian roller blinds use either 3.2mm or 4.5mm bead chains. Using the wrong size will damage the sprocket and cause recurring slippage.

Fix 2: Broken or Snapped Chain

If the chain has physically broken, you'll need a replacement section. Here's the process:

  1. Measure the old chain If you still have both pieces, lay them out and measure the total loop length. Standard chains for most roller blinds are 1500–1800mm. Also measure the bead diameter (3.2mm or 4.5mm) and spacing (6mm or 8mm pitch).
  2. Buy matching chain and a joiner Get replacement chain from Bunnings (sold by the metre), a blind parts supplier, or online. You'll also need a chain connector joiner — a small plastic piece that clicks into a bead slot to close the loop.
  3. Remove the blind and open the sidewinder Same process as Fix 1 above — remove from brackets, open the sidewinder housing.
  4. Thread the new chain Feed the new chain around the sprocket, out through both chain guides (top and bottom). Trim to the correct loop length if needed — the loop should hang about 2/3 of the blind's drop length.
  5. Join the chain Press the chain connector into the last bead on each end. You'll hear a small click. Tug firmly to make sure it holds.
  6. Reassemble and test Close the sidewinder, remount, and test the blind's operation.
Chain Length Tip

Too long and the chain will tangle, catch on furniture, or pose a child safety hazard. Too short and you can't reach it comfortably. The ideal chain loop length is about two-thirds of the blind's drop — so for a 1500mm drop, aim for roughly 1000mm of chain in the loop.

Fix 3: Chain Jammed in the Mechanism

If the chain won't budge at all, something is physically blocking it inside the sidewinder.

  1. Don't pull harder Forcing a jammed chain will strip the sprocket teeth or break the chain guide. If it's stuck, stop pulling.
  2. Remove and open Remove the blind from brackets. Open the sidewinder cover (see Fix 1, Step 3).
  3. Identify the jam Common culprits: a bead wedged between the sprocket and housing wall, dust compacted into a hard lump, or a bent bead catching on the chain guide.
  4. Clear the obstruction Use needle-nose pliers to gently extract stuck beads. Blow out dust. If a bead is bent, straighten it with pliers or trim that section and splice in new chain.
  5. Lubricate Apply a small amount of silicone spray to the sprocket teeth and chain guide channel. Don't use WD-40 — it attracts dust and will cause the problem to recur faster.
  6. Reassemble and test Close up, remount, and test. The chain should now glide smoothly.

Fix 4: Chain Slips — Clutch Won't Hold

This is the trickiest repair. You pull the chain, the blind goes up — then slowly drifts back down. The problem is inside the clutch, not the chain itself.

  1. Clean the clutch barrel Remove the blind, open the sidewinder, and locate the clutch — a cylindrical piece that sits behind the sprocket. Wipe it clean with a dry cloth. Sometimes dust or grease on the clutch pads prevents proper grip.
  2. Apply silicone lubricant sparingly A light spray on the clutch barrel can restore grip. Too much lubricant will have the opposite effect — less is more here.
  3. Check the spring tension If the blind still slips, the internal spring may have lost tension. Remove the fabric tube and manually wind it 6–8 full turns, then test. If it holds position, the spring just needed resetting.
  4. Replace the clutch mechanism If cleaning and re-tensioning don't work, the clutch pads are worn. You'll need a new sidewinder unit — available from Bunnings for $15–$25, or from specialist blind parts suppliers for exact-match models.

Repair vs Replace: When to Call It

Not every blind is worth repairing. Here's a rough guide:

Scenario DIY Cost Time Verdict
Chain off track (no damage) $0 10 min Always repair
Chain broken, fabric fine $5–$10 20 min Repair
Jammed mechanism, cleanable $0–$8 (lubricant) 15 min Repair
Worn clutch, blind under 5 years $15–$25 30 min Repair (replace sidewinder)
Multiple issues, blind 10+ years old $25–$40 45+ min Consider replacing
UV-damaged fabric, bent tube N/A N/A Replace the blind

If your blind is relatively new and the fabric is in good condition, repairs almost always make sense. But if the fabric is yellowed, peeling, or the tube itself is warped, investing repair effort into a dying blind isn't worth it.

Consider Going Cordless

If you're replacing a chain mechanism anyway, this is a good time to upgrade to a cordless or motorised system. Spring-loaded cordless mechanisms eliminate the chain entirely — no more tangles, no child safety risk, and a cleaner look. Motorised roller blinds are another option, especially for hard-to-reach windows.

Preventing Future Chain Problems

A few habits that'll keep your roller blind chain working for years:

  • Pull straight down — angled pulling is the number-one cause of chain derailment. Stand directly in front of the blind, not off to the side.
  • Don't yank — a firm, steady pull is all it takes. Jerking the chain puts stress on the sprocket teeth and chain guides.
  • Keep it clean — dust is the slow killer. A quick wipe of the sidewinder area during your regular blind cleaning routine prevents buildup.
  • Lubricate annually — a tiny spray of silicone lubricant once a year keeps the sprocket and clutch operating smoothly.
  • Check the chain tension — the chain should hang in a relaxed loop, not tight and not so loose it tangles. Adjust with a chain connector if needed.
  • Child safety compliance — keep chains out of reach of children. Use chain tensioners (those wall-mounted clips) to hold the chain loop taut against the wall. Read our childproofing guide for the current Australian standards.

When to Call a Professional

Some situations genuinely need a pro:

  • Motorised blinds with chain-override issues — electrical components shouldn't be DIY'd
  • Blinds mounted very high (3m+) where safe ladder access isn't possible
  • Multiple blinds failing at once — could indicate a batch issue from original installation
  • Warranty claims — DIY repair may void remaining warranty
