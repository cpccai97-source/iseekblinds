Living Room: Curtains vs Blinds — Which Is Actually Best for Your Space?

iSeekBlinds Team 17 March 2026 13 min read

Contents

The Short Answer

There's no universal winner. Curtains bring warmth, acoustic softness, and a sense of scale — perfect for open-plan living areas, south-facing rooms, and anyone who wants their lounge to feel inviting. Blinds (roller, honeycomb, venetian) deliver precise light control, a streamlined profile, and easier maintenance — better for compact lounges, north-facing glare, and modern/minimal aesthetics. The increasingly popular option? Both together — a blind for function, curtains for atmosphere.

Your living room is where window treatments matter most. It's the room visitors see first, where you binge Netflix on Sunday evenings, and where Melbourne's mercurial light swings from blazing summer glare to grey winter afternoons. The right window treatment transforms the experience of that room. The wrong one just hangs there.

This isn't a "10 reasons curtains are better" fluff piece. We'll go through the genuine trade-offs — aesthetics, light control, insulation, cost, maintenance — so you can make the call based on your actual living room, not a styled photo in a magazine.

The Case for Curtains in the Living Room

Curtains do something blinds can't: they fill a wall. Floor-to-ceiling curtains make a 2.4m ceiling look taller, make a narrow room feel wider (hang the rod beyond the window frame), and add a textile layer that softens hard surfaces — timber floors, glass, plaster walls.

Where curtains genuinely win

  • Acoustic dampening. Fabric absorbs sound. In open-plan living/dining/kitchen spaces — increasingly common in Melbourne builds — curtains reduce echo and create a softer acoustic environment. Heavy lined curtains absorb 20–30% more mid-frequency sound than bare windows.
  • Thermal insulation. Lined curtains with a pelmet or covered track create a trapped air layer against the glass. In Melbourne's winter (and those June mornings when the heater's working hard), this makes a genuine difference to heat retention. More on this in the insulation section.
  • Visual warmth and scale. Curtains bring texture, movement, and colour mass. A living room with curtains feels dressed. This matters more in larger rooms and rooms with high ceilings where bare windows can feel stark.
  • Flexibility of style. Sheer, translucent, blockout, lined, unlined; S-fold, pencil pleat, eyelet, pinch pleat; linen, cotton, velvet, polyester blends. The range of fabric, colour, and heading style gives you more creative control than any blind type.
  • Light diffusion. Sheer curtains scatter and diffuse natural light in a way that's distinct from any blind fabric. The effect is gentle, even, and flattering — particularly valued in living rooms where harsh shadows on screens and faces aren't welcome.
Melbourne tip: If your living room faces south or south-east, curtains are usually the better primary treatment. You're managing cold and grey light, not glare — and fabric insulation outweighs precision light control in that scenario.

Where curtains fall short

  • Dust and allergens. Fabric collects dust, pet hair, and pollen. If someone in the household has allergies, curtains need regular washing (or at minimum, a vacuum with an upholstery attachment every few weeks).
  • Space consumption. Curtains stack to the sides when open. Heavy curtains on a small window can eat 150–200mm of glass on each side, reducing your visible window area.
  • No precision light control. Curtains are essentially open or closed. You can't tilt them to redirect light like venetian slats. Sheer curtains soften light beautifully but offer minimal privacy at night.
  • Fading. Direct sun (north-facing Melbourne rooms, especially) degrades fabric over time. Lining extends lifespan, but eventually UV wins. Budget for replacement every 7–12 years.

The Case for Blinds in the Living Room

Blinds cover a broader family: roller blinds, venetian blinds (timber, aluminium, faux wood), honeycomb/cellular blinds, and roman blinds. Each behaves differently, so we'll note which type suits what.

Where blinds genuinely win

  • Precise light control. Venetian blinds let you angle slats to redirect sunlight — bouncing it off the ceiling for diffused ambient light, or angling down for privacy while maintaining airflow. Roller blinds with sunscreen fabric filter glare while preserving your view. No curtain replicates either effect.
  • Compact profile. Roller blinds virtually disappear when rolled up. Venetians raise into a slim headrail. This matters in compact living rooms, studios, and apartments where every centimetre of visual space counts.
  • Lower maintenance. A quick wipe with a damp cloth (roller) or a pass with a microfibre duster (venetian) is all most blinds need. No washing, no dry cleaning, no re-hanging.
  • UV and glare management. Sunscreen roller blinds are specifically engineered to cut UV and glare while maintaining outward visibility — a genuine game-changer for TV-watching and screen use in the living room.
  • Smart home integration. Motorised blinds integrate with Apple HomeKit, Google Home, and Alexa. Automated schedules (open at sunrise, close at sunset) and voice control make daily use seamless. Motorised curtain tracks exist but are more expensive and mechanically complex.
North-facing Melbourne living rooms: A sunscreen roller blind (1–5% openness factor) is arguably the single best treatment for managing summer glare without losing your view. Add a blockout roller behind it (double bracket system) for movie nights and you've covered both scenarios.

Where blinds fall short

  • No acoustic benefit. Hard-surface blinds (aluminium venetian, roller) do almost nothing for room acoustics. In reverberant open-plan spaces, the difference from curtains is noticeable.
  • Less visual warmth. Blinds are functional. Even beautiful timber venetians lack the textile softness and movement that curtains bring. In a sparsely furnished living room, blinds alone can feel clinical.
  • Limited colour/texture range. Roller blind fabrics come in hundreds of colours but the texture is flat. Venetians have slat options but they're uniform. You don't get the drape, gather, and layered dimension that fabric curtains offer.
  • Thermal performance varies. Standard roller blinds provide minimal insulation. Honeycomb blinds are the exception — their cellular structure creates trapped air layers that rival lined curtains. But venetians and aluminium blinds actually conduct cold.

The honest answer for most Melbourne living rooms: Unless you have a very clear single-aesthetic commitment, a layered combination gives you the most flexibility. Read on for how to do that well.

The Layered Approach: Blinds + Curtains Together

This is increasingly the standard recommendation from interior designers — and for good reason. Combining blinds and curtains in the living room gives you functional light control (the blind) and aesthetic warmth (the curtain). Neither has to compromise.

  1. Start with a functional blind — sunscreen roller for north/west-facing (preserves view, cuts glare), blockout roller or honeycomb for south/east-facing (insulation priority).
  2. Add curtains for style and warmth — S-fold in linen or cotton-linen blend is the most popular choice in Melbourne living rooms right now. Mount on a ceiling track for a clean, modern look.
  3. Get the stack-back right — curtains should clear the glass when fully open. Allow 200–300mm of stack space per side so you don't lose window area.
  4. Consider motorisation — motorise the blind for daily light control (the one you'll operate constantly). Leave curtains manual unless the budget allows both.
Cost expectation: A layered setup (roller blind + custom S-fold curtains) for a standard 2m-wide living room window runs $500–$1,500 installed depending on fabric and motorisation. That's not double the cost of either alone — the blind is relatively inexpensive and the curtains don't need to be blockout quality.

Insulation & Energy Efficiency

Melbourne's climate means you're heating more months than you're cooling. Window treatments that retain heat make a real difference to energy bills — especially with single-glazed windows, which are still common in established suburbs.

Thermal performance ranked

Treatment Insulation Rating How It Works
Honeycomb blinds (double cell) ★★★★★ Trapped air in cellular pockets creates an insulating barrier — comparable to adding a second pane
Lined curtains + pelmet ★★★★☆ Fabric + lining + covered track reduces convective heat loss at the glass
Honeycomb blinds (single cell) ★★★★☆ Similar principle, slightly less effective than double-cell
Roman blinds (lined) ★★★☆☆ Fabric layers with lining, but gaps at sides reduce effectiveness
Roller blinds (blockout) ★★☆☆☆ Minimal air gap, no cellular structure — reduces radiant heat only
Unlined curtains / sheers ★★☆☆☆ Light fabric alone does little for insulation
Venetian blinds (aluminium) ★☆☆☆☆ Metal conducts temperature — can actually make cold worse near the window

The best thermal setup for a Melbourne living room is honeycomb blinds (for insulation) + lined curtains (for additional barrier and aesthetics). If budget allows only one: honeycomb blinds for maximum thermal performance per dollar, or lined curtains if aesthetics matter more.

Cleaning & Upkeep

Living rooms collect less moisture than kitchens or bathrooms, but they accumulate dust, and if you have pets — hair. Here's the real maintenance picture:

Curtains

  • Weekly: Shake or lightly vacuum with an upholstery attachment
  • Every 6–12 months: Machine wash (if fabric allows) or dry clean
  • Spot clean: Immediately address marks — fabric absorbs stains quickly
  • Total annual time: ~3–4 hours (including one full wash cycle)

Blinds

  • Roller blinds: Wipe with a damp cloth monthly. That's it.
  • Venetian blinds: Dust slats weekly with a microfibre cloth or blind duster. Deep clean every 3–6 months (close slats, wipe each one).
  • Honeycomb blinds: Light vacuum with brush attachment monthly. Spot clean with damp cloth as needed.
  • Total annual time: ~1–2 hours (roller), ~4–6 hours (venetian)

For detailed cleaning instructions, see our care and maintenance guide.

Pet owners: If you have cats or dogs in the living room, roller blinds or venetians are significantly easier to keep hair-free. Curtain fabric traps pet hair — lint rollers become a way of life.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I put curtains or blinds in my living room?

It depends on your priorities. Curtains add warmth, texture and sound absorption — ideal for larger living rooms, south-facing rooms, or spaces where cosiness matters. Blinds (roller, venetian, honeycomb) provide cleaner light control, lower maintenance, and a more streamlined look — better for compact spaces, north-facing rooms with glare, and modern interiors. Many Melbourne living rooms use both together: blinds for function, curtains for style.

Can you have both curtains and blinds in a living room?

Absolutely — it's one of the most popular combinations in Australian living rooms. A typical setup is a sunscreen or sheer roller blind for daytime light control, paired with curtains for evening warmth and visual softness. This layered approach gives you maximum flexibility: the blind handles glare and UV during the day, while curtains add insulation and ambiance at night.

Are curtains or blinds better for insulation in Melbourne?

For insulation alone, honeycomb (cellular) blinds outperform standard curtains thanks to their trapped-air cell structure. However, heavy lined curtains with a pelmet or curtain track cover provide excellent thermal performance too, especially floor-to-ceiling. The best thermal solution is layering: honeycomb blinds for the air gap insulation, plus lined curtains for the added fabric barrier.

What curtains look best in a living room?

In 2026, the dominant trends for Melbourne living rooms are: S-fold (wave fold) curtains in natural linen or textured cotton for a clean, contemporary drape; pencil pleat curtains in heavier fabrics for traditional homes; and sheer curtains paired with blockout blinds for light-filled modern spaces. Neutral earth tones — warm whites, flax, clay, stone — remain the most versatile choices.

How much do living room curtains cost in Melbourne?

For a standard 2m-wide living room window (supply + install): ready-made curtains $150–$400, custom curtains $400–$1,200+, sheer + blockout combination $600–$1,500. For blinds: roller blinds $120–$280, honeycomb blinds $200–$450, venetian blinds $150–$350. Layered combinations (blinds + curtains) typically run $500–$1,800 per window depending on fabric and motorisation.

Are blinds more modern than curtains?

Not necessarily. Roller and honeycomb blinds suit minimalist and contemporary interiors due to their clean profile. But S-fold curtains, linen sheers, and floor-to-ceiling drapes are equally popular in modern Melbourne homes. The 2026 trend is actually toward softer, more textured window treatments — so curtains are having a strong moment. What matters more than the treatment type is the fabric, colour, and how it's installed.
