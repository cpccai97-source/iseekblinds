Skylight Blinds in Australia — What Actually Works (and What Doesn't)

Skylights are brilliant until February hits. That overhead glass that floods your hallway with winter light becomes a heat cannon by mid-summer — a single unshaded skylight can push room temperatures up 8–12°C on a 38°C Melbourne day. The solution isn't complicated, but choosing the wrong skylight blind is surprisingly easy.

This guide covers what we've learned fitting skylight blinds across hundreds of Melbourne homes: which types actually reduce heat (with numbers), what fits your existing skylight brand, and where the money is well spent versus wasted.

Why Skylights Need Blinds More Than Vertical Windows

A vertical window catches sunlight at an angle. A skylight catches it almost straight on — especially during the Australian summer when the sun is high. Per square metre, a skylight admits 2–3× more solar heat gain than a same-sized vertical window. That's physics, not marketing.

Solar heat gain comparison — skylights cop the sun almost head-on during Australian summers

The second issue: heat rises. Even a small skylight sits at the highest point of the room, where hot air naturally accumulates. Without a blind, you're trapping that heat right where it enters.

🔥 The Numbers

A standard 780 × 1400mm skylight in Melbourne can admit roughly 850W of solar heat at peak summer. That's equivalent to running a bar heater in your ceiling. A good honeycomb blind cuts that to around 350–420W.

Honeycomb Blinds — The Best All-Round Choice for Skylights

There's a reason honeycomb blinds dominate the skylight market. The cellular structure traps air — one layer in a single cell, two in a double — creating genuine insulation rather than just blocking light.

For skylights specifically, honeycomb blinds have a structural advantage: the tensioned cord system holds the fabric taut against the slope. No sagging, no flapping. They also fold into a compact stack at the top of the skylight, preserving your view when open.

Single Cell vs Double Cell

  • Single cell — sufficient for south-facing skylights or rooms where heat isn't extreme. Lighter, cheaper, lower profile when stacked.
  • Double cell — the clear winner for west or north-facing skylights (where Melbourne heat is worst). The extra air pocket makes a real thermal difference. Worth the $40–$80 premium per blind.

💡 Pro Tip

Ask for a reflective-backed honeycomb if heat is your primary concern. The aluminium-coated inner layer bounces radiant heat back out through the glass, rather than absorbing it. This lifts heat rejection from ~50% to ~60% on double-cell blinds.

Blockout Roller Blinds — When Darkness Matters Most

Honeycomb "blockout" is never truly 100% — light leaks through the cord holes and cell edges. If you need absolute darkness (home cinema, shift worker's bedroom, photography darkroom), a blockout roller blind with side channels is the way to go.

The trade-off: less thermal performance than honeycomb (no air pockets), and the fabric needs a tensioning system to prevent sag on sloped skylights. VELUX's own blockout roller blinds handle this well with integrated side rails. Third-party options vary — ask about the tensioning method before buying.

Motorised Skylight Blinds — Nearly Always Worth It

Most skylights are 3–5 metres off the floor. Operating a manual blind up there means a telescopic pole, a step ladder, or (realistically) just leaving it in one position permanently. We see this constantly on re-visits: manual skylight blinds frozen in place because adjusting them is a hassle.

Motorised blinds solve this completely. Three options:

  • Solar-powered — a small panel on the skylight frame charges an internal battery. No electrician needed. Best retrofit option. VELUX's solar blinds are excellent here.
  • Battery-powered — rechargeable lithium battery, typically lasts 6–12 months between charges. Good for skylights without direct sun on the panel area.
  • Hardwired — wired to mains power. Most reliable, but needs an electrician and access to ceiling wiring. Best for new builds.

⚠️ Honest Opinion

If your skylight is above 2.4m, spend the extra $200–$350 on motorisation. The cost of a manual blind you never adjust is the cost of a blind that does nothing. Motorised skylight blinds can also integrate with smart home systems for automatic schedules.

VELUX Skylights — Your Easiest Path

If you have VELUX skylights (most common in Australian homes built after 2000), you're in luck. VELUX manufactures blinds specifically for their skylight dimensions — they clip directly into the frame without drilling.

To order, you need your VELUX model code (stamped on the top of the skylight frame or on the data plate when you open it). Common Melbourne sizes:

  • CK04 (550 × 980mm) — smaller bathrooms, hallways
  • MK04 (780 × 980mm) — standard rooms
  • MK08 (780 × 1400mm) — the most popular size in Melbourne homes
  • SK06 (1140 × 1180mm) — large living areas

VELUX's own range includes honeycomb, blockout roller, light filtering, and solar-powered motorised options. Third-party honeycomb blinds from Australian manufacturers (Veneta, Watson) also make VELUX-compatible sizes — often at lower prices with more fabric choices.

Non-Standard & Custom Skylights

Builder-installed skylights, Fakro, Roto, or custom timber-framed roof windows don't have the plug-and-play convenience of VELUX. These need made-to-measure blinds with:

  • Tensioned wire guides — steel cables on each side keep the blind fabric against the slope
  • Custom side channels — aluminium tracks screwed to the skylight reveal
  • Precise measurements — tolerances are tight (±2mm) because the blind must sit flat against the glass at any angle

A site measure is non-negotiable for custom skylights. The angle, recess depth, frame material, and any obstructions (handles, ventilation latches) all affect the blind specification. We include this in our free measure service.

Common Mistakes We See

1. Buying Manual When the Skylight Is 4 Metres Up

See the motorised section above. A manual blind you never adjust is money wasted.

2. Using Standard Roller Blinds Without Tensioning

A regular roller blind hung at an angle sags away from the glass within weeks. The fabric isn't designed to resist gravity sideways. Skylight-specific blinds use tensioned cables or side channels to hold the fabric flat. Don't repurpose a standard blind — it won't work.

3. Ignoring Recess Depth

Inside-mount skylight blinds need at least 50mm recess depth (75mm+ for honeycomb). If your skylight frame is shallow, you may need a face-mount solution or an external awning instead. Measure the recess before ordering.

4. Choosing Light Filtering When You Need Heat Control

Light filtering sounds pleasant, but thin single-layer fabrics do almost nothing for heat. If summer heat is the problem, you need honeycomb (double cell) or reflective blockout — not a sheer panel.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I fit blinds to any skylight?

Most skylights can be fitted with blinds. VELUX and Fakro have clip-in systems — no drilling. Custom skylights need made-to-measure blinds with tensioned guides or side channels. A site measure is essential for non-standard skylights to get the angles and tolerances right.

How much do skylight blinds cost in Melbourne?

VELUX-compatible: manual blockout from ~$180, honeycomb from ~$220, solar motorised from ~$450. Custom non-VELUX skylights: from $300 manual, $600+ motorised. Installation included in most quotes. A home with 3–4 skylights typically runs $1,500–$2,800 total for motorised honeycomb.

Do skylight blinds actually reduce heat?

Substantially. Blockout blinds cut solar heat gain by ~40%. Double-cell honeycomb: 50–60%. External awning blinds: up to 75%. The difference is immediately noticeable — a room that was uncomfortably hot with bare skylights becomes manageable with properly fitted blinds.

Should I get motorised or manual?

If the skylight is above 2.4m — motorised, always. Solar-powered motors need no electrician and charge from the skylight itself. The extra $200–$350 per blind pays for itself in actual daily use. Manual blinds at height tend to stay in one position permanently.

What's the best fabric for skylight blinds?

Bedrooms: blockout honeycomb. Living areas: light-filtering honeycomb. Maximum heat control: double-cell with reflective backing. Avoid standard roller fabrics — they sag on sloped skylights without proper tensioning.

Can I install skylight blinds myself?

VELUX blinds for VELUX skylights: yes, 15–20 minutes per blind with clip-in brackets. Custom skylights: professional installation recommended — awkward angles, tight tolerances, and ladder work at ceiling height. We include installation in our quotes.
