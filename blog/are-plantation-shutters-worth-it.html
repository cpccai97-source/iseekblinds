Are Plantation Shutters Worth It? An Honest Cost vs Value Guide

iSeekBlinds Team 17 March 2026 13 min read

Contents

The Short Answer

Yes, if you plan to stay 5+ years. Plantation shutters cost 3–5× more than roller blinds upfront ($350–$700/m² installed), but they last 20–25 years, add genuine kerb appeal, and are one of the few window treatments that real estate agents specifically mention in listings. They're not worth it for renters, investment properties with budget tenants, or windows where you need total darkness or motorisation. For those, roller blinds are the smarter spend.

Every plantation shutter website will tell you they're "an investment that pays for itself." Most of them are trying to sell you shutters. We sell shutters too — but we also sell roller blinds, curtains, and venetians, so we have no reason to steer you toward the expensive option if it's not right for your situation.

This guide is the honest breakdown we'd give a friend asking the question. Real numbers, real trade-offs, no fluff.

What Plantation Shutters Actually Cost in Melbourne

Let's start with the number everyone wants. Prices vary wildly depending on material, window size, and configuration — but here's what you'll actually pay in 2026 for professional supply and installation in Melbourne:

*Standard window = approx. 900mm × 1500mm (1.35m²). Prices include measure, manufacture, and installation.

Whole-house pricing

For a typical 3-bedroom Melbourne home with 10–14 standard windows:

  • PVC shutters: $6,000 – $10,000
  • Basswood shutters: $9,000 – $16,000
  • Mixed approach (shutters on street-facing, blinds elsewhere): $5,000 – $9,000

Hidden cost to watch for: Special shapes add 30–60% to the per-window price. Bay windows, arched tops, and triangular windows require custom frames and additional hinges. If your home has several of these, factor that in before committing to a whole-house quote. Ask specifically about surcharges for non-rectangular windows.

By comparison, a full house of quality roller blinds for the same home runs $1,800 – $3,500 installed. That's the price gap you're evaluating.

Which material should you pick?

PVC is the smart pick if budget matters and you want the shutter look without the shutter price. Quality has improved dramatically — modern PVC shutters don't yellow or warp like the cheap imports from 10 years ago. The downside: they're heavier, so panels wider than about 600mm can start to sag over time. Keep individual panel widths under 500mm if possible.

Basswood is the gold standard for interior shutters. It's lightweight (critical for large panels), takes paint and stain beautifully, and has a warmth that PVC can't quite replicate. The grain is subtle even through paint, giving it a depth that synthetic materials lack. Don't use it in wet areas — bathrooms, laundries, or poolside.

Aluminium is purpose-built for outdoor applications — alfresco areas, pergolas, balconies. It handles Melbourne's rain, heat, and UV without degrading. Indoors, it looks too industrial for most homes unless you're going for a very specific warehouse-conversion aesthetic.

Do Plantation Shutters Actually Add Property Value?

This is the claim everyone makes and nobody quantifies. Here's what we know from talking to Melbourne real estate agents and reviewing property data:

The honest answer: yes, but it's indirect. Shutters don't add a dollar-for-dollar return the way a bathroom renovation might. What they do is make your home present better — in listing photos, at open inspections, and in the unconscious "this feels premium" assessment buyers make in the first 30 seconds.

  • Faster sale times — Homes with shutters consistently photograph better. Uniform white shutters create clean, bright listing images that perform well on Domain and realestate.com.au.
  • Perceived value — Agents report that a full house of quality shutters can add $5,000–$15,000 to perceived value, though isolating this from other factors is difficult.
  • Move-in ready signal — Shutters signal to buyers that the home has been cared for. Unlike old curtains or damaged blinds, shutters rarely look dated or neglected.
  • Listing language — Real estate agents specifically mention plantation shutters in property descriptions. They almost never mention roller blinds.

Reality check: If you're spending $12,000 on shutters purely for resale value, and they add $10,000 to the sale price, that's a net loss. The ROI argument only works if you're also enjoying them while you live there. Shutters are an investment in how you live first, and resale value second.

Lifespan & True Cost Per Year

This is where the maths gets interesting — and where shutters start to look less expensive than they initially appear.

Cost Per Year of Use (standard window)

Plantation Shutters (basswood) $32 – $42/yr
Roller Blinds (quality) $22 – $37/yr
Venetian Blinds (timber-look) $20 – $30/yr
Quality Curtains $35 – $55/yr

When you divide the upfront cost by expected lifespan, shutters cost roughly $32–$42 per window per year. Roller blinds cost less per year ($22–$37), but you'll need to replace them 2–3 times over the same period. Factor in the hassle and cost of re-measuring, re-ordering, and re-installing, and the gap narrows considerably.

The real savings come from zero maintenance cost. Shutters need a damp cloth wipe occasionally. No re-stringing (venetians), no chain replacement (rollers), no dry cleaning (curtains). Over 20 years, that maintenance saving is meaningful — especially if you're paying someone else to do it.

Honest Pros & Cons

We'll skip the marketing spin. Here's what matters when you're actually living with shutters day to day:

What's genuinely good

  • Light control precision — tilt louvres to any angle, directing light exactly where you want it. Nothing else does this as well.
  • Longevity — 20–25 years with zero fabric degradation. They look the same at year 15 as year 1.
  • Insulation — the air gap between shutter and glass creates genuine thermal insulation, reducing energy costs by 10–15%.
  • Child safety — no cords, no chains. Inherently compliant with Australian safety standards.
  • Street appeal — they look good from outside. Consistent white shutters across a facade create a clean, cohesive look that roller blinds can't match.
  • Low maintenance — wipe with a damp cloth. No re-stringing, no dry cleaning, no fabric replacement.

What nobody tells you

  • Not true blockout — light leaks through louvre gaps, hinge points, and frame edges. If you need genuine darkness for sleep, blockout roller blinds do a better job.
  • No motorisation — you can't automate shutters. Every open and close is manual. For smart home setups, this is a dealbreaker.
  • Fixed aesthetic — shutters define the room's style. You can't swap them out like curtains when you redecorate. If you repaint your walls every few years, shutters might clash.
  • Window access blocked — opening a window for airflow means folding back shutter panels, which protrude into the room. Casement windows and shutters are a clunky combination.
  • Slow lead times — custom manufacture takes 4–8 weeks. Roller blinds can be made in 5–10 business days.
  • Expensive mistakes — if you get the colour wrong or a panel gets damaged, replacement panels cost $200+ each and require matching to the original batch.

When Shutters Aren't the Right Answer

Sometimes the honest advice is "don't buy shutters." Here's when alternatives genuinely make more sense:

Choose roller blinds instead when…

  • You need total darkness — blockout rollers with side channels beat shutters for sleep quality every time
  • You want motorisation or smart home integration — motorised rollers connect to Alexa, Google Home, and HomeKit
  • Budget is tight — quality rollers cost $120–$240 per window vs $470–$945 for shutters
  • You're renting — portable rollers come with you when you move
  • Very large windows — shutters on floor-to-ceiling glass (2.4m+) become unwieldy and expensive. Panel glide blinds work better.

Choose curtains instead when…

  • You want acoustic dampening — heavy curtains absorb sound in a way hard surfaces can't
  • You want the option to change the look — curtain fabrics can be swapped out for a fraction of the shutter replacement cost
  • Heritage homes — some period properties look better with flowing drapes than with crisp shutters

Choose honeycomb blinds instead when…

  • Energy efficiency is the top priority — honeycomb cells trap air and outperform shutters on insulation by 2–3×
  • You want light diffusion without the structured look of shutters
  • Budget sits between blinds and shutters — honeycombs typically cost $200–$400 per window

The smart hybrid approach: Many Melbourne homeowners put shutters on street-facing windows (where they boost kerb appeal) and roller blinds on rear-facing and bedroom windows (where functionality matters more). This can cut a whole-house budget by 40% while capturing most of the visual and resale benefits.

The Verdict

Our Honest Take

Plantation shutters are worth it if: you own the home, plan to stay 5+ years, value light control and low maintenance over absolute darkness and automation, and have the budget to do it without financial stress.

They're not worth it if: you're renting, renovating purely for resale on a tight margin, need blockout for sleep-critical rooms, want smart home integration, or your windows are awkwardly shaped and the surcharges are steep.

The hybrid approach — shutters where they're seen, blinds where they're used — is often the smartest investment for Melbourne homes. It gives you the kerb appeal and property value uplift of shutters with the functionality and savings of blinds where it counts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do plantation shutters increase home value in Australia?

Yes, but modestly. Real estate agents consistently report that plantation shutters make a property photograph better and feel more premium at inspections. A full house of quality shutters can add $5,000–$15,000 to perceived value, though this is difficult to isolate from other renovation factors. The real value is in faster sale times — homes with shutters tend to present as "move-in ready" which reduces buyer hesitation.

How long do plantation shutters last?

Quality basswood or PVC plantation shutters typically last 20–25+ years with minimal maintenance. This is 3–5× longer than roller blinds (5–8 years) and 2–3× longer than venetian blinds (8–12 years). The hinges and tilt mechanisms occasionally need adjustment, but the panels themselves rarely need replacement unless physically damaged.

What is the cheapest plantation shutter material?

PVC (polyresin) shutters are the most affordable option, typically $350–$550 per square metre installed. They're waterproof and low-maintenance but heavier and less refined-looking than timber. For a budget-conscious full house, PVC shutters can save 30–40% compared to basswood while still delivering the plantation shutter look.

Are plantation shutters better than blinds?

It depends on priorities. Shutters win on longevity (20+ years vs 5–10), light control precision, property value, and visual impact. Blinds win on upfront cost (3–5× cheaper), variety of fabrics and opacity levels, motorisation options, and suitability for very large or unusually shaped windows. Neither is universally "better" — the right choice depends on your room, budget, and how long you plan to stay.

Can I install plantation shutters myself to save money?

Technically yes, but we don't recommend it for most homeowners. Shutters require precise measurement (to the millimetre) and professional fitting to ensure panels align, close flush, and operate smoothly. A badly measured or installed shutter won't close properly, will bind on the frame, or will have visible gaps. Most manufacturers void warranties on DIY installations. The labour cost is typically only 15–20% of the total price, so the savings don't justify the risk.

How much do plantation shutters cost for a 3-bedroom house in Melbourne?

For a typical 3-bedroom Melbourne house with 10–14 windows, expect $6,000–$10,000 for PVC shutters or $9,000–$16,000 for basswood shutters, fully installed. The range depends on window sizes, special shapes (arches, bay windows), and whether you're doing every window or just street-facing rooms. Most installers offer bulk discounts of 10–15% for whole-house orders.
