How to Measure Windows for Blinds

The gap between a blind that fits perfectly and one that embarrasses you every morning? Usually five minutes with a steel tape and knowing exactly which numbers matter.

17 March 2026 · 11 min read

Wrong measurements are the number one reason custom blinds fail. Too narrow and light bleeds around the edges — the whole point of the blind is undermined. Too wide and they jam in the recess. Too short and you've got a gap at the bottom that looks unfinished.

Here's what most people don't realise: measuring is straightforward. You need a steel tape measure, five minutes per window, and an understanding of two concepts — inside mount versus outside mount. Everything else follows from those.

This guide covers measuring for all blind types: roller blinds, venetian blinds, honeycomb blinds, plantation shutters, and curtains. Product-specific guides with exact diagrams are linked throughout.

Step 1: Steel Tape Measure Only

Before anything else: get a steel tape measure. Not a fabric sewing tape. Steel tapes hold their length across a window; fabric tapes sag and stretch by several millimetres — enough to cause a fit problem on an inside-mount blind.

A 3m Stanley or similar from Bunnings costs $12 and will save you from ordering blinds twice. If you're measuring large windows or multi-room installs, a 5m tape is easier to handle solo.

⚠ Measure in Millimetres

Always record in millimetres. The Australian blind industry uses mm exclusively. Rounding to the nearest centimetre introduces errors — 895mm and 900mm are meaningfully different for inside-mount blinds where clearance is 2–5mm per side.

Step 2: Inside Mount or Outside Mount?

This is the decision that changes every measurement that follows. Make it before you pick up the tape.

Most Popular

Inside Mount

Blind sits within the window recess. Flush with the wall. Clean, built-in appearance that doesn't cover the architrave or surrounding wall.

  • Minimal, architectural look
  • Works with curtains alongside
  • Tight tolerances (2–3mm matters)
  • Needs adequate recess depth
  • Small side light gaps are normal
Most Versatile

Outside Mount

Blind mounts on the wall or ceiling above the window. Covers the entire recess and frame. Better for light blocking and shallow reveals.

  • Eliminates most light gaps
  • No minimum recess depth
  • Hides uneven or damaged reveals
  • Makes windows look larger
  • Forgiving measurements (±5mm fine)
Which One?

If your recess is at least 45mm deep and reasonably square (less than 5mm variation), inside mount almost always looks better. Choose outside mount when: the recess is too shallow for the blind type, you want maximum blackout performance, or the window reveal is uneven from wall settling or old plaster.

Step 3: Measure Width (3 Points)

Windows are not perfectly square — especially in Melbourne's older homes. The width at the top may differ from the middle and bottom by 5–10mm. This variation determines which measurement you use.

W1

Top — measure the inside width

For inside mount: place the tape against the left wall of the recess, run to the right wall. This is where brackets usually sit. Record in mm.

W2

Middle — measure again

Same motion, halfway down the recess. On many windows, this differs from the top by a few mm due to frame swelling, paint buildup, or construction variation.

W3

Bottom — one final measure

At the sill level. Compare all three: record the narrowest measurement. A blind cut to the narrowest width will slide in without forcing at any point.

For outside mount, measure the window frame edge to edge, then add your desired overlap on each side — typically 50–100mm per side. Mark where you want the brackets to sit on the wall; this is your ordering width.

Inside Mount — Width Measuring Points

Three widths measured, narrowest recorded (W1, W2, W3 from top to bottom)

Step 4: Measure Drop (3 Points)

D1

Left side — top of recess to sill

Place the tape at the very top of the recess (where the headrail will sit) and measure straight down to the sill. Record in mm.

D2

Centre — repeat

Same measurement from top to sill at the centre of the window. The sill may be slightly sloped — common in older Melbourne homes with timber frames that have shifted over decades.

D3

Right side — record longest

Measure again on the right. Compare all three and record the longest measurement. A blind cut to the longest drop will reach the lowest point of the sill — no gap at the bottom.

Why Longest Drop?

Width uses the narrowest so the blind fits within the recess. Drop uses the longest so it reaches the lowest point of the sill. If the sill slopes 3mm from left to right, a blind cut to the shorter measurement would have a visible gap on one side.

Step 5: Check Recess Depth

This step is only relevant for inside mount. Measure from the front face of the window frame to the glass. If this depth is less than the minimum for your blind type, you need to switch to outside mount or use projection brackets.

Minimum Recess Depth — Inside Mount

Venetian Blinds
50mm
Roller Blinds (single)
45mm
Honeycomb Blinds
45–65mm
Dual Roller
55mm
Plantation Shutters
60–90mm

Don't Deduct — Submit Exact Measurements

This is the mistake that trips up first-timers: they measure the recess, then try to calculate the clearance themselves and deduct before submitting. Don't.

The manufacturer applies factory deductions based on the product's specific bracket and headrail profile — typically 2–5mm per side for roller blinds, different amounts for venetians and shutters. When you deduct as well, the blind ends up 10–20mm too narrow and light gaps appear around both edges.

Submit exact recess dimensions. The supplier handles the math.

⚠ Costly Mistake

Custom blinds are made to your measurements — there's no return or exchange if you've provided wrong numbers. Getting it right the first time saves you the full cost of replacement. For plantation shutters in particular, the tolerances are tighter and errors are more expensive. A free professional measure is worth considering for shutters.

7 Measurement Mistakes That Cause Fit Problems

  1. Measuring once. Windows are not square. Measure each dimension at three points. Every time.
  2. Using a fabric tape measure. They stretch. Use steel only.
  3. Rounding to the nearest centimetre. Always record to the nearest millimetre.
  4. Deducting clearance yourself. Submit exact recess sizes. Manufacturer applies factory deductions.
  5. Not checking recess depth. You might be inside-mounting a blind type that needs more depth than your window has.
  6. Ignoring window handles and locks. A handle protruding 20mm into the recess will prevent the blind from sitting flat. Note their position and projection depth.
  7. Assuming all windows are the same size. Even in new builds, windows that look identical can vary by 5–15mm. Measure each one separately.

Inside vs Outside Mount — Quick Reference

Factor Inside Mount Outside Mount
Look Minimal — fits within frame Statement — covers frame, can make window look taller
Light blockage 1–3mm side gaps (normal) Excellent — overlap eliminates most light ingress
Measurement tolerance Tight — 2–3mm matters Forgiving — 5–10mm either way is fine
Recess depth needed 45–90mm depending on blind type None — mounts on wall or ceiling
Uneven reveals Only works if <5mm variation Yes — covers the reveal entirely
Best for blockout bedrooms Use with side channels or pelmets Yes — with 75–100mm overlap per side

Special Cases: Bay Windows, Arches & Skylights

Bay windows: Each panel is a separate blind. Measure each face independently. The tricky part is the join angle — standard brackets don't work at corners, so each blind gets individual side clearance, or you use angled joining brackets.

Arched windows: Template measuring is required — trace the arch onto cardboard for shaped blinds, or opt for a flat roller/Roman blind that covers the arch as outside mount. For a true arched shutter, plantation shutters with an arch infill panel is the premium solution.

Skylights: Measure the glass area (not the surrounding frame). Note the angle of the window surface. Honeycomb blinds with tension wires are the standard — they hold fabric against gravity without sagging.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I measure in millimetres or centimetres?
Always millimetres. The Australian blind industry uses mm exclusively. Rounding to the nearest centimetre introduces errors — 895mm and 900mm are different blinds for an inside mount where the clearance is 2–4mm per side.
Do I deduct clearance from my inside-mount measurements?
No. Submit the exact recess dimensions. The manufacturer applies factory deductions based on the blind's specific headrail and bracket profile. If you deduct yourself, and they deduct again, the blind comes out 10–20mm too narrow.
What is the minimum recess depth for inside-mount blinds?
It varies: venetians need ~50mm, roller blinds ~45mm (55mm for dual), honeycomb 45–65mm, plantation shutters 60–90mm. If too shallow, use outside mount or projection brackets. Measure from the front face of the window frame to the glass.
How much overlap should I add for outside-mount blinds?
50–100mm per side and 100–150mm above the frame. For blockout roller blinds, use 75–100mm per side to eliminate side light gaps. For decorative venetians or sunscreen rollers where some light gap is acceptable, 50mm per side works.
Can I measure for blinds myself or do I need a professional?
Most homeowners measure successfully with a steel tape. Measure each dimension 3 times, record narrowest width and longest drop, submit exact recess sizes. For arched windows, bay windows, skylights, or plantation shutters, a free professional measure avoids expensive errors.
