Blinds for Victorian Homes in Melbourne — Matching Modern Comfort to Period Architecture

Victorian terraces, Italianate villas, boom-style mansions — Melbourne has more heritage housing stock per capita than almost any city in Australia. Here's how to dress those tall sash windows without wrecking what makes them special.

18 March 2026 · 11 min read

Melbourne's Victorian-era homes — built roughly between 1850 and 1901 — share a set of window characteristics that make standard blinds a poor fit. Tall, narrow double-hung sash windows. Deep timber reveals. Bay windows with awkward angles. Arched fanlights above front doors. If you've tried to buy ready-made blinds for any of these, you already know the problem.

This guide covers the five window treatment types that genuinely work in Victorian homes across Melbourne, ranked by how well they respect the architecture, control light, and hold up to the city's climate extremes.

Why Victorian Windows Are Different

Before picking a product, it helps to understand what you're working with. Victorian-era windows in Melbourne typically share these traits:

  • Height: 1800–2400mm is standard, sometimes taller in formal rooms. Much taller than the 1200–1500mm openings in post-war homes.
  • Width: Narrow — often 600–900mm per sash. Bay windows have three or five narrow panels rather than one wide opening.
  • Reveal depth: 80–120mm of timber frame, deep enough for inside-mount blinds in most cases.
  • Sash mechanism: Double-hung with weights — the window slides up, so any blind needs to clear the sash travel.
  • Heritage overlays: Many suburbs (Fitzroy, Carlton, East Melbourne, South Melbourne) have council Heritage Overlays restricting external changes. Interior treatments are generally exempt, but check with your council if in doubt.
Heritage Overlay Note

Under most Heritage Overlays in the City of Melbourne, City of Yarra and City of Port Phillip, internal window treatments don't require a planning permit. External shutters, awnings or anything visible from the street may need approval. When in doubt, a quick call to your council's planning department saves grief later.

1. Plantation Shutters — The Heritage Gold Standard

Plantation Shutters

The closest modern equivalent to original Victorian interior shutters. Custom-fitted to each window, with independently adjustable louvres for light control. The painted finish (as opposed to stained timber) is the more historically accurate choice for Victorian homes. Ideal for front-facing rooms where street appeal matters.

$350–$700 per window, measured & installed

Victorian homes originally had solid timber panelled shutters on their interior windows — you can still see the hinge mortises in original window frames in many Fitzroy and Carlton terraces. Plantation shutters are the modern evolution: louvred rather than solid, but sharing the same fundamental approach of a timber frame fitted to the window.

For bay windows, plantation shutters are the standout choice. Each angled panel gets its own shutter, and the frame follows the bay's geometry precisely — no gaps, no visible hardware from outside.

2. Timber Venetian Blinds — Versatile and Period-Sympathetic

Timber Venetian Blinds (50mm slat)

The workhorse heritage option. A 50mm basswood or paulownia slat in white, cream or natural timber provides excellent light angle control and sits cleanly inside deep Victorian reveals. Choose cord-free or wand-tilt for child safety compliance under AS 5765.2.

$120–$250 per window, measured & installed

Timber venetians are the practical sweet spot for most Victorian homes. They're less expensive than shutters, fit inside standard reveals without modification, and the horizontal slat lines complement rather than clash with period proportions.

The 50mm slat width is important — 25mm slats look too modern, and 63mm can appear bulky in narrow Victorian windows. White or off-white paint finishes suit most interiors; natural timber works in rooms with exposed floorboards or timber mantelpieces.

3. Roman Blinds — Soft and Elegant

Roman blinds in linen, cotton or a textured weave bring softness to rooms where timber might feel too rigid. They're particularly effective in bedrooms and sitting rooms of Victorian homes, where the fabric folds echo the layered textile aesthetic of the era.

For light control, pair a sheer Roman with a blockout roller behind it — the sheer Roman handles daytime filtering and looks beautiful, while the roller provides total darkness when needed. This layered approach is common in heritage renovations across South Melbourne and Albert Park.

4. Sheer Curtains — Maximum Heritage Feel

If your Victorian home has decorative cornices, ceiling roses or ornate architraves, sheer curtains let those details breathe. Floor-length sheers on a discreet track — ceiling-mounted to avoid drilling into the architrave — provide privacy without blocking the afternoon light that streams into north-facing terrace rooms.

Sheers are also the easiest option if you have arched or unusually shaped windows where rigid blinds would require custom framing.

5. Honeycomb Blinds — The Insulation Winner

Victorian homes are draughty. Single-pane glass, gaps around sash frames, and no wall insulation mean winter heat loss through windows is significant. Honeycomb (cellular) blinds trap air in their double-cell structure, adding an insulating layer with an R-value of approximately 0.6–1.0 — meaningful when your walls might only be R-1.0.

Energy tip: A double-cell honeycomb blind on a single-glazed Victorian sash window can reduce heat loss through that window by 40–50% in winter. Across a typical 10-window terrace, that can translate to $200–$400/year off heating bills. The blinds pay for themselves within 3–5 years.

The trade-off is aesthetics — honeycomb blinds look modern. They work best in rear rooms, bathrooms or bedrooms where heritage appearance is less critical than comfort and efficiency. For front rooms, stick with shutters or venetians.

Melbourne Suburbs: What We See Most

After fitting blinds in Victorian homes across Melbourne's inner ring, patterns emerge. Here's what homeowners in each suburb tend to choose — and why.

Fitzroy & Collingwood

Terraces with Heritage Overlay. Plantation shutters on street-facing windows, roller blinds in rear extensions. Strong preference for white paint finishes.

Carlton & Parkville

Mix of Italianate and Victorian. Roman blinds popular in academic households. Sheer curtains in living areas. Timber venetians in studies and bedrooms.

Richmond & Cremorne

Workers' cottages with smaller windows. Timber venetians dominate — cost-effective for multiple small windows. Honeycomb in bathrooms for moisture resistance and insulation.

South Melbourne & Albert Park

Grand terraces with bay windows and high ceilings. Plantation shutters are the default. Layered sheers + blockout rollers in bedrooms. Budget: $4,000–$8,000 for a full house.

East Melbourne & Jolimont

Large Italianate villas. Premium timber shutters with hidden tilt rods. Custom Roman blinds in imported linens. Often the highest per-window spend in Melbourne.

Williamstown & Newport

Weatherboard Victorians near the bay. Outdoor blinds on verandahs plus timber venetians inside. Salt-air resistance matters — PVC-coated faux wood over real timber near the coast.

What to Avoid in a Victorian Home

Some popular modern blind types clash with Victorian architecture:

  • Vertical blinds: The office-building look has no place in a period home. They also can't handle the narrow widths of Victorian sash windows.
  • Roller blinds on front-facing windows: Functional but visually flat. From the street, a roller blind reads as a blank rectangle — it erases the window's proportions. Save rollers for rear rooms or as a hidden blockout layer behind curtains.
  • Bold printed fabrics: Victorian homes have enough visual detail in their cornices, tiles and ironwork. Window treatments should be neutral — white, cream, linen, charcoal — to let the architecture speak.
  • External aluminium awnings: Apart from looking wrong, these often violate Heritage Overlay rules in inner-Melbourne councils.

Installation Considerations for Heritage Windows

Inside Mount vs Outside Mount

Inside mount (within the window reveal) is almost always preferred in Victorian homes. It shows off the timber architrave, keeps the profile clean, and avoids drilling into decorative surrounds. Most Victorian reveals at 80–120mm depth accommodate venetians, roller blinds and some shutter systems.

Outside mount makes sense when the reveal is too shallow, the window is arched, or you want to make a window appear taller (mount the bracket 100mm above the frame).

Protecting Original Timber

If your window frames are original — and in many Melbourne Victorians, they are — minimise penetration. Use the fewest brackets possible, drill into the reveal rather than the face frame, and keep the screws to a size that can be easily filled if you ever remove the blinds. For Heritage-listed properties, discuss reversible mounting with your installer.

Child Safety (AS 5765.2)

All blinds with accessible cords are now required to comply with AS 5765.2 child safety standards. In a Victorian home with young children, choose cordless options: wand-tilt venetians, motorised blinds, or shutters (inherently cordless). Cord-free isn't just a safety requirement — it also looks cleaner in a heritage setting.

Common Questions

What blinds suit a Victorian terrace?
Timber venetian blinds in a 50mm slat width suit most Victorian terraces. They echo the horizontal lines of original timber shutters, fit inside the deep reveal of sash windows, and provide precise light control. For front rooms visible from the street, plantation shutters offer a more permanent heritage-sympathetic solution. Roman blinds in linen or cotton work well in bedrooms and sitting rooms where softer light is preferred.
Can I install blinds without drilling into heritage window frames?
Yes. Tension-fit brackets, top-fix options that attach to the reveal (not the frame), and freestanding shutter frames can all avoid damage to original timber. If your property has a Heritage Overlay, check with your local council — most interior window treatments don't require a permit, but external changes may. For sash windows, inside-mount blinds that sit within the reveal are the least invasive option.
What's the best blind for Victorian bay windows?
Plantation shutters custom-made for each angled panel give the cleanest result. Each panel gets independently operable louvres, and the frames follow the bay's geometry precisely. Timber venetians also work panel-by-panel if the headrail is cut to the exact angle. Avoid a single wide roller — it won't follow the bay's angles.
Are plantation shutters heritage-appropriate?
Plantation shutters are arguably the most heritage-appropriate blind you can install. Victorian homes in Melbourne originally featured solid timber interior shutters — the louvred plantation shutter is a direct evolution. In suburbs like Fitzroy, Carlton and South Melbourne, plantation shutters are the most commonly specified window treatment in heritage renovations.
How much do blinds for a Victorian home cost in Melbourne?
Expect $120–$250 per window for timber venetians, $350–$700 per window for plantation shutters, and $150–$350 per window for quality Roman blinds — all custom-measured and installed. Victorian windows are often taller and narrower than standard, so off-the-shelf sizes rarely fit. A free in-home measure ensures accurate pricing for your specific windows.
