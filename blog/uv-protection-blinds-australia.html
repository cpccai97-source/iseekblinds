UV Protection Blinds:
What Actually Blocks the Australian Sun

Australia sits under the harshest UV index on earth. The right blinds can block up to 99% of UV rays — the wrong ones barely make a dent.

Australia has the highest rate of melanoma in the world — and a lot of that sun comes through windows. Glass blocks UVB reasonably well, but ordinary glass transmits up to 74% of UVA, the deeper-penetrating radiation linked to aging and melanoma. The blinds you choose make a significant difference to how much UV reaches your family, your floors, and your furniture.

The key number to know: UVA can penetrate standard window glass. A north-facing Melbourne living room can receive dangerous UV doses year-round — not just in summer.

How UV Actually Gets Into Your Home

Window glass is not UV-proof. Standard float glass blocks most UVB (the sunburn wavelength) but transmits a large portion of UVA. Double-glazing helps, but even with double-glazed windows, meaningful UV levels reach interior surfaces. Add skylights, sliding doors, and west-facing windows — common in Melbourne's townhouses and new builds — and you have a real sun exposure problem indoors.

The solution isn't necessarily to block all light. It's about choosing window furnishings with enough UV protection to matter, without turning your home into a cave.

Understanding Openness Factor

For solar/sunscreen fabrics, the openness factor (sometimes called openness percentage) is the critical spec. It describes what percentage of the fabric surface is open to light and air.

For rooms where people spend significant time — especially with north- or west-facing exposure — a 1–3% openness solar blind is the sweet spot between UV protection and usable natural light.

UV and Furniture Fading: The Real Cost

Furniture fading is caused by several factors: UV radiation (~40%), visible light (~25%), heat (~25%), and humidity (~10%). Most Australians focus on air conditioning to protect interiors, but window treatments do the heavy lifting on UV-driven fading.

Timber floors, leather sofas, wool rugs, and artwork are all susceptible. In Melbourne homes — particularly those with large north-facing windows or extensive glazing — unprotected interiors can show significant fading within 2–3 years.

⚠️ West-facing rooms are highest risk. Afternoon sun hits a low angle with high UV intensity. If you have west-facing living areas, solar blinds with 1–3% openness are non-negotiable for furniture protection.

Can Indoor Blinds Protect Skin?

Yes — but with nuance. People who work near windows, in conservatories, or in car-adjacent positions (sunrooms, home offices with large glazing) receive meaningful UV doses. Dermatologists increasingly recommend UV-protective window film or UV-blocking blinds for people who spend hours near glass.

Honeycomb blinds and solar blinds don't carry formal UPF ratings like swimwear, but fabrics tested at tight weave densities (1% openness) consistently exceed UPF 50 performance when closed. If you or someone in your household has photosensitive conditions, blockout or low-openness solar blinds on high-exposure windows are a practical layer of protection.

Which Blind to Choose by Room

North-Facing Living Room

Full sun most of the day. A solar roller blind (1–3% openness) reduces glare and UV while keeping the room bright. Pair with sheer curtains if you want softness in the evening. See our blockout vs sunscreen comparison for the trade-offs.

West-Facing Bedroom or Study

Brutal afternoon sun. Blockout roller blinds give maximum protection. For a home office, consider a solar blind on the lower sash only, keeping upper light without direct glare on your screen.

Kitchen (North or West)

Aluminium venetian blinds are easy to clean and block significant UV when angled correctly. For a cleaner look, waterproof roller blinds in a 3% solar fabric work well here too.

Conservatory or Sunroom

This is where UV protection matters most. Measure-and-install guides for skylight and conservatory blinds cover the specifics — but the fabric choice should be solar or blockout, nothing lighter.

Children's Bedrooms and Nurseries

Children's skin is more UV-sensitive. Blockout blinds serve double duty: they protect from UV and create better sleep conditions. Our nursery blinds guide covers safety compliance alongside UV performance.

Motorised UV Blinds: Set and Forget Sun Protection

The biggest weakness of manual blinds for UV protection is human behaviour — people leave them open. Motorised blinds solve this with scheduled automation: set your solar blinds to lower at 10am on north-facing windows and raise at 4pm, automatically maintaining protection without daily effort.

Integrated with smart home systems (Google Home, HomeKit, SmartThings), motorised blinds respond to UV index alerts or sunrise/sunset triggers. If UV protection is a genuine health priority, automation removes the human variable. Read more in our smart home blinds integration guide.

Common Questions

Do window blinds really block UV rays?
Yes. Depending on fabric type and weave density, window blinds can block between 70% and 99% of UV rays. Solar/sunscreen fabrics with a 1% openness factor block up to 99% UV; blockout fabrics are essentially 100% when fully closed.
What is the best blind for UV protection in Australia?
Solar roller blinds with a tight weave (1–3% openness factor) offer the best balance of UV protection and natural light. For full UV blockage, blockout roller blinds or honeycomb blinds are the top choice when fully closed.
Can UV rays come through closed blinds?
Through blockout blinds: virtually no UV penetrates. Through sunscreen blinds: UV is significantly reduced but some passes through the open weave. The tighter the weave (lower openness %), the better the UV protection.
Do UV protection blinds fade themselves?
Quality solar fabrics are treated with UV-stabilised coatings that resist fading. Cheap fabrics can yellow or degrade over 3–5 years. Ask for fabric samples and check the warranty — reputable suppliers offer 5-year fabric warranties.
What about UV-protective window film instead of blinds?
Window film is an option — it blocks UV while remaining permanently transparent. However, it can't be adjusted for light levels and doesn't provide privacy. Many Melbourne homeowners use both: film on fixed glazing and solar blinds on operable windows for flexible control.
