How to Motorise Existing Blinds

The Short Answer

Roller blinds are the easiest to motorise — a battery retrofit motor slides into the existing tube, replacing the chain. Cost: $120–$250 DIY, 20–30 minutes per blind. Curtains need a motorised track replacement ($400–$800). Venetian blinds are harder and usually not worth retrofitting — buy new motorised ones instead. All can connect to Google Home, Alexa, or Apple HomeKit via a hub.

Can Your Blinds Be Motorised?

Not all blinds are equal when it comes to motorisation. Here's the honest compatibility breakdown:

Easy

Roller Blinds

Battery motor slides into existing tube. Remove chain, insert motor, done. Works with tubes 32–45mm diameter. Most roller blinds manufactured after 2010 are compatible.

Easy

Honeycomb Blinds

Similar to rollers — motor replaces the headrail mechanism. Most honeycomb blinds with standard headrails can be retrofitted. Top-down-bottom-up models are more complex.

Possible

Curtains

Requires replacing the track/rod with a motorised track (Somfy Glydea, MotionBlinds). Your existing curtains rehang on the new track. More expensive but very effective.

Possible

Outdoor Blinds

Zip-track and channel-guided outdoor blinds can be motorised with tubular motors. Requires weather-rated motor. Professional installation recommended due to weight.

Not Recommended

Venetian Blinds

Needs dual-action motor (tilt + raise). Retrofit options exist but are expensive and limited. Usually better to buy new motorised venetians.

Not Recommended

Plantation Shutters

Shutters are manual by design. Motorised louvre tilt systems exist but are niche and expensive ($800+). Not a practical retrofit.

What You'll Need

🔧Phillips screwdriver
🪜Step ladder
📏Tape measure
✏️Pencil
📱Smartphone (for motor app)
🔋USB-C charger (for motor)

You'll also need: A retrofit motor kit ($120–$250) — which includes the motor, bracket adapter, and remote or hub. Popular brands in Australia: MotionBlinds, Automate, Somfy, and Acmeda.

💡 Before you buy: Measure your roller tube's inner diameter. Most retrofit motors need 32mm minimum — some need 36mm or 38mm. Also weigh your blind (fabric + tube) — battery motors typically handle up to 3kg, wired motors up to 6kg.

Step-by-Step: Motorising Roller Blinds

This is the most common retrofit and the most satisfying DIY win. You're essentially replacing the chain mechanism with a motor inside the same tube.

The motor slides into your existing roller tube from the chain end. The drive shaft engages with the tube interior to rotate it.
1

Remove the Blind from Brackets

Lift the blind up slightly and release from the brackets. Most roller blinds have a spring clip on one side and a pin on the other. Lay the blind on a clean flat surface.

2

Remove the Chain Mechanism

On the chain/clutch end, remove the end cap (usually a friction fit or small screw). Pull out the chain clutch mechanism. You won't need it anymore — satisfying moment.

3

Measure the Tube

With the clutch removed, measure the inner diameter of the tube. Most Australian roller blinds use 32mm, 36mm, or 38mm tubes. Your motor kit may include adapter rings for different sizes.

4

Charge the Motor

Battery motors ship partially charged. Fully charge via USB-C before installation — this takes 4–6 hours. A full charge lasts 3–6 months with daily use (one up/down cycle per day).

5

Insert the Motor

Slide the motor into the tube from the chain end. The motor head (with the crown adaptor) engages with the inner ribs of the tube. Push until the motor bracket plate sits flush with the tube end.

6

Install the New Bracket

Replace the old chain-side bracket with the motor bracket (supplied with the kit). The motor bracket has a slot that the motor head sits into, holding it in place while allowing the tube to rotate around it.

If you're switching from a chain bracket to a motor bracket, the screw holes may not align. Fill old holes with filler and drill new ones.

7

Rehang and Set Limits

Clip the blind back into both brackets — motor end first, then pin end. Use the motor's remote control or smartphone app to set the upper limit (fully rolled up) and lower limit (fully extended). Most motors use a "jog" method — tap up/down until the position is right, then hold to save.

Testing tip: Run the blind through 5 full up/down cycles after installation to check the motor is seated properly and the limits are correct. Adjust if the fabric isn't rolling evenly — this usually means the motor isn't fully inserted or the tube has a slight bend.

Motorising Curtains with a Smart Track

You can't just add a motor to an existing curtain rod or track — the mechanism is fundamentally different. Instead, you replace the track entirely with a motorised one. The good news: your existing curtains usually rehang straight onto the new track.

How motorised curtain tracks work

A motorised curtain track has a small motor housed at one end (or in the centre for bi-parting curtains). A belt or cord inside the track connects to a master glider — when the motor runs, the glider moves along the track, pulling the curtain open or closed. The other gliders follow naturally.

Popular motorised track systems in Australia:

  • Somfy Glydea — market leader, excellent smart home integration, handles curtains up to 50kg. $500–$800 per track.
  • MotionBlinds — more affordable, good app, Wi-Fi direct. $400–$600 per track.
  • Automate — Australian brand, reliable, works with most heading styles. $450–$700 per track.
⚠️ Important: Motorised tracks are typically ceiling-mounted. If your current curtains are on a wall-mounted rod (especially eyelet curtains), you'll need to factor in the track mounting change. Eyelet curtains can't be used with tracks — you'd need to convert to a hook or glider heading. Learn more about smart home solutions.

Smart Home Setup

A motorised blind is handy. A smart motorised blind — one that opens with the sunrise, closes when you say "Hey Google, close the blinds," or integrates with your home security — is transformative.

Motor connects via Zigbee/Bluetooth to a hub, then Wi-Fi to your router, enabling voice control and app scheduling.

Hub options

  • MotionBlinds Wi-Fi Bridge ($80–$120) — supports up to 30 blinds, works with Google/Alexa/HomeKit
  • Somfy TaHoma ($150–$200) — premium hub, supports Somfy ecosystem + third-party sensors
  • IKEA DIRIGERA ($80) — budget option if you use IKEA smart products already
  • Matter-compatible hubs — the future standard. Look for motors with Matter support for maximum compatibility.
💡 Scheduling tip: Set your blinds to open 15 minutes before your alarm. Waking to natural light is genuinely better than any alarm tone. Melbourne's variable sunrise times (5:55am in summer, 7:35am in winter) mean an automated schedule based on sunrise/sunset is more useful than a fixed time.

DIY vs Professional Installation

DIY makes sense when:

  • You're doing 1–3 roller blinds with battery motors
  • Your blinds are in easy-to-reach locations (not above stairwells)
  • The existing brackets are in good condition
  • You're comfortable with basic tools and a smartphone app

Call a professional when:

  • You're motorising 5+ blinds (bulk professional rates make the cost difference smaller)
  • You need hardwired motors (requires electrical work — licensed electrician in Victoria)
  • Curtain track replacement is involved (cutting, levelling, heavy lifting)
  • Windows are above stairwells, high ceilings, or hard-to-access locations
  • You want everything on one smart home system, properly configured

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Buying the wrong tube size. The most common mistake. Measure the inner diameter, not the outer. A 38mm motor won't fit in a 36mm tube, no matter how hard you push.
❌ Forgetting to charge the motor first. Battery motors ship partially charged. If you install and it barely moves, it's not broken — it's flat. Fully charge before mounting.
❌ Not checking blind weight. Battery motors handle about 3kg (fabric + tube). A double-layer blockout blind on a 2400mm window can exceed this. Check the motor's weight rating against your actual blind weight.
❌ Skipping the hub. Without a hub, you get a remote-controlled blind — not a smart blind. The hub is what connects to Wi-Fi and enables scheduling, voice control, and scenes. Budget for it.
❌ Mixing incompatible brands. Somfy motors don't talk to MotionBlinds hubs. Pick one ecosystem and stick with it across all your blinds. Check smart home platform compatibility before committing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I motorise my existing roller blinds without replacing them?

Yes — if your roller blind has a standard 32mm, 36mm, or 38mm tube, you can insert a battery or wired motor into the existing tube. You'll remove the chain mechanism, slide the motor in, and reattach the blind. Most retrofit motors fit tubes from 32–45mm diameter.

How much does it cost to motorise existing blinds?

DIY battery retrofit motors cost $120–$250 per blind. Professional motorisation runs $250–$500 per blind (motor + installation). A smart hub adds $80–$200 one-off. For a full home of 8–12 blinds, expect $1,500–$3,500 DIY or $3,000–$6,000 professionally installed.

Do motorised blinds need to be plugged in?

Not necessarily. Battery-powered motors (rechargeable lithium) are the most popular retrofit option — no wiring needed. A single charge lasts 3–6 months with typical daily use. Hardwired motors are available for new builds or renovations where you can run cables inside the wall.

Can I motorise curtains?

Yes, but you'll need to replace the curtain track with a motorised track (e.g. Somfy Glydea, MotionBlinds). You can keep your existing curtains — they rehang on the new track. Motorised curtain tracks cost $400–$800 per window depending on width and brand.

Will motorised blinds work with Google Home or Alexa?

Most modern smart blind motors connect via a hub (Zigbee or Wi-Fi bridge) to Google Home, Amazon Alexa, or Apple HomeKit. Some newer motors have built-in Wi-Fi and connect directly. Check compatibility before buying — not all motors support all platforms.

Can I motorise venetian blinds?

It's possible but more complex. Venetian blinds need a motor that handles both tilting and raising/lowering. Retrofit options exist but are limited and more expensive ($300–$500). For most people, replacing with a new motorised venetian is more practical than retrofitting.
