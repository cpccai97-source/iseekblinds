The Short Answer

Most motorised blinds connect to your smart home through a hub (bridge) that translates Zigbee or Bluetooth signals to Wi-Fi. From there, Google Home, Alexa, and HomeKit can all control them. Setup takes 15–30 minutes per platform. You'll need a hub ($80–$200), a compatible motor, and a stable Wi-Fi network. The new Matter protocol is simplifying everything — but you don't need to wait for it.

How Smart Blinds Actually Connect

There's a common misconception that smart blinds connect directly to your voice assistant. They don't — there's a chain of communication, and understanding it saves you hours of troubleshooting.

The signal chain: motor → hub → router → cloud → voice assistant. The app can control locally (via hub) or remotely (via cloud).

Here's the key thing: every link in that chain matters. If your hub loses power, the motor still works via its remote — but voice control and scheduling stop. If your internet drops, local app control often still works (depends on the hub), but "Hey Google" won't respond.

Already have a motorised blind or thinking about getting one? If you haven't motorised yet, our guide to motorising existing blinds covers the hardware side. This guide picks up from there — getting your motors talking to the rest of your home.

Protocols Explained: Zigbee vs Wi-Fi vs Bluetooth vs Matter

The protocol is how your motor talks to the hub (or directly to your network). Each has trade-offs, and picking the wrong one is the most common source of frustration.

Recommended

Zigbee

Low-power mesh network. Motors last 6–12 months on battery. Needs a hub. Very reliable for blinds — handles 30+ devices without congesting your Wi-Fi. Used by MotionBlinds, IKEA, Somfy.

Convenient

Wi-Fi

Connects directly to your router — no hub needed. But each blind is another device on your network. Battery life is shorter (3–4 months). Fine for 1–4 blinds, congests networks with more.

Limited

Bluetooth

Short range, phone-only control. No scheduling when you're away. Some budget motors are Bluetooth-only — they work but aren't truly "smart." Avoid if you want automation.

Emerging

Matter / Thread

The new universal standard. Works with all platforms natively. Thread mesh network (like Zigbee but open standard). Still early for blinds — few motors support it yet, but it's the future.

💡 Our recommendation: For a Melbourne home with 5+ windows, go Zigbee. It's the most mature protocol for blind motors, has the best battery life, and a single hub handles your whole house. Wi-Fi is fine if you're only doing 2–3 blinds and don't want to buy a hub.

Platform Setup Guides

Once your motor and hub are installed and connected to your Wi-Fi, here's how to link them to each voice platform. These steps assume you're using a Zigbee motor with a MotionBlinds-compatible hub — the process is similar for Somfy and other brands.

Google Home Setup

Google Home has the widest blind motor compatibility and the most intuitive voice commands. It's our go-to recommendation for most households.

1

Open Google Home App

On your phone, open the Google Home app. Tap the + icon in the top left, then select Set up device → Works with Google.

2

Find Your Brand

Search for your motor brand (e.g. "MotionBlinds", "Somfy", "Automate"). Tap it and sign in with the account you created during hub setup.

3

Authorise & Sync

Grant Google Home permission to control your blinds. It will automatically discover all blinds connected to your hub. This usually takes 10–30 seconds.

4

Assign Rooms

Google will list your blinds with default names (often just "Blind 1"). Rename each one clearly — "Master Bedroom Blind," "Living Room Left" — and assign to the correct room. Good naming makes voice control intuitive.

5

Test Voice Commands

Try: "Hey Google, close the bedroom blinds" or "Hey Google, set the living room blind to 50%." Google supports percentage positions, open/close, and room-based group commands.

💡 Google tip: Create a "Good Morning" routine that opens all blinds, tells you the weather, and starts your music. Go to Routines → Starter → "Good morning" and add your blinds under Smart Home actions.

Amazon Alexa Setup

Alexa's Routines feature is the strongest of any platform — especially powerful for blind automation. Setup is straightforward.

1

Enable the Skill

In the Alexa app, go to More → Skills & Games. Search for your motor brand's skill (e.g. "MotionBlinds"). Tap Enable to Use and sign in with your hub account.

2

Discover Devices

After linking, Alexa will scan for devices. You can also say "Alexa, discover devices" or go to Devices → + → Add Device. All hub-connected blinds should appear.

3

Rename and Group

Tap each blind to rename it and assign it to a room group. Alexa uses room groups for commands like "Alexa, close the blinds in the kitchen."

4

Create Routines

Alexa Routines are where the real power is. Go to More → Routines → +. Set triggers (time, sunrise/sunset, voice command, sensor) and actions (open/close blinds, set percentage). You can chain multiple blind actions in one routine.

💡 Alexa tip: Use the "Sunrise/Sunset" trigger in Routines — it automatically adjusts for Melbourne's daylight changes throughout the year. No need to manually update times each season.

Apple HomeKit Setup

HomeKit is the most restrictive platform — not all motors support it natively. But if yours does, the integration is rock-solid and privacy-focused (no cloud dependency for local control).

1

Check Compatibility

Your motor or hub must explicitly support HomeKit. MotionBlinds (with Eve MotionBlinds bridge), Somfy TaHoma, and some IKEA motors work. Check for the "Works with Apple HomeKit" badge before buying.

2

Scan the HomeKit Code

Open the Home app on your iPhone. Tap + → Add Accessory. Scan the HomeKit code on the hub (usually a sticker on the bottom or in the app's settings screen).

3

Assign Rooms & Names

HomeKit will import your blinds. Assign each to a room and give it a clear name. HomeKit uses Siri-friendly naming — "Kitchen Blind" works better than "Blind_Kitchen_01".

4

Create Scenes & Automations

Use Scenes for presets (e.g. "Movie Night" closes all living room blinds to 90%). Use Automations for triggers — time of day, sunrise/sunset, someone arrives home, or when a sensor detects conditions.

⚠️ HomeKit requirement: For automations to run when you're not home, you need a home hub — an Apple TV, HomePod, or iPad set as a home hub and always powered on. Without it, automations only trigger when your iPhone is on the local Wi-Fi.

Best Automation Recipes

This is where smart blinds stop being a novelty and start being genuinely useful. These are the automations we set up most often for our Melbourne customers — based on what actually gets used daily, not what sounds impressive in a brochure.

🌅 Sunrise Wake-Up
Trigger: 15 minutes before sunrise (adjusts automatically year-round)
Action: Open bedroom blinds to 40%, then fully open 15 minutes later
Why it works: Gradual light exposure syncs your circadian rhythm. In Melbourne, sunrise varies from 5:55am (December) to 7:35am (June) — a fixed 6:30am schedule only works half the year. Sunrise-based automation handles this perfectly.
🌡️ Temperature Protection
Trigger: Outdoor temperature exceeds 32°C (via weather integration or smart sensor)
Action: Close north and west-facing blinds to 80%
Why it works: Melbourne's north and west-facing windows cop serious heat in summer. Closing honeycomb blinds before the heat hits can reduce cooling costs by up to 40%. Automate it and you never forget.
🎬 Movie Scene
Trigger: Voice command — "Hey Google, movie time"
Action: Close all living room blinds to 95%, dim lights to 10%
Why it works: One command replaces walking around closing 3–4 blinds manually. Leave them at 95% (not 100%) to allow a sliver of ambient light — complete darkness makes people fall asleep.
🏠 Away Mode
Trigger: Everyone leaves the house (location-based, or set manually)
Action: Close all blinds. At sunset, open living room blinds to 30% (with lights on timer)
Why it works: Security. A house with all blinds shut at 2pm on a Tuesday screams "nobody home." The sunset routine mimics someone being in, making the house look occupied. Simple but effective.
☀️ Smart Sun Tracking
Trigger: Schedule based on sun position (available in advanced automations / Home Assistant)
Action: Adjust blinds on each face of the house as the sun moves — east in the morning, north at midday, west in the afternoon
Why it works: Maximum natural light with minimum glare and heat. More complex to set up (usually requires Home Assistant or advanced Somfy TaHoma scenes), but transforms how a house feels. We've set this up in a few Southbank apartments — the tenants love it.

The Future: Matter Protocol

If you've been researching smart home gear recently, you've seen "Matter" everywhere. Here's what it actually means for your blinds — and whether you should care right now.

Matter is a connectivity standard developed by Google, Apple, Amazon, and Samsung (through the Connectivity Standards Alliance). The promise: buy any Matter device and it works with any Matter platform, out of the box. No more checking compatibility charts. No more brand-specific hubs.

Matter eliminates brand lock-in: one protocol works across all platforms. Still maturing for blind motors in 2026.

Should you wait for Matter? No. Here's why:

  • Current Zigbee and Wi-Fi motors work well right now. You're not missing out on functionality.
  • Several brands (MotionBlinds, IKEA, Somfy) have committed to Matter firmware updates — buy now and get Matter when it's ready.
  • Matter-over-Thread blind motors are trickling in, but the selection is still limited in Australia as of early 2026.
  • The real benefit of Matter (mix-and-match brands without compatibility headaches) matters most when you're building a large system. For 5–10 blinds from one brand, the current hub approach works fine.
Practical advice: Buy from a brand that has announced Matter support. That way you get a working system today and future compatibility tomorrow. MotionBlinds and IKEA are the furthest along in the Australian market.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

❌ "Blind not responding" in the app
Check the signal chain: Is the hub powered? Is it connected to your router (solid LED, not blinking)? Is the blind within range of the hub? Zigbee range is about 10m through walls. If the hub is in the living room and the blind is two floors up, you may need a Zigbee repeater (any mains-powered Zigbee device acts as one).
❌ Voice assistant says "device not found"
The link between your hub account and Google/Alexa/HomeKit has broken. Go to the smart home app → linked services → unlink your brand → re-link it. This forces a re-sync. Happens occasionally after hub firmware updates.
❌ Blind moves but doesn't reach the correct position
The travel limits need recalibrating. Open the manufacturer's app and re-set the upper and lower limits. This commonly happens after a battery replacement or firmware update that resets the motor's memory.
❌ Automations aren't triggering
For Google Home: check that your phone and Nest/Home speaker are on the same Wi-Fi as the hub. For Alexa: ensure the routine is enabled (not paused). For HomeKit: you need a home hub (Apple TV, HomePod) that's online — without it, automations don't run when you're away.
❌ Hub won't connect to Wi-Fi
Most hubs only support 2.4GHz Wi-Fi, not 5GHz. If your router broadcasts a combined network, temporarily create a separate 2.4GHz SSID for setup. Also check that your router isn't blocking new devices (MAC filtering) or has reached its DHCP limit.
❌ Significant delay between voice command and blind movement
Some lag (1–3 seconds) is normal — the command goes phone → cloud → hub → motor. If it's 5+ seconds, your internet may be slow or the cloud service is experiencing issues. Check your internet speed and try controlling the blind directly from the manufacturer's app (which uses local control). If the app is fast but voice is slow, it's a cloud issue — nothing you can fix on your end.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a hub for smart blinds?

Most motorised blinds use Zigbee or Bluetooth to communicate, which means they need a hub (bridge) to connect to your Wi-Fi network and smart home platform. Some newer Wi-Fi motors connect directly to your router without a hub, but they can congest your network if you have many blinds. A hub typically costs $80–$200 and supports 20–30 blinds.

Which smart home platform works best with motorised blinds?

Google Home has the broadest compatibility with blind motors and the most natural voice commands. Amazon Alexa is a close second with excellent routine support. Apple HomeKit is more selective — fewer motors are natively compatible, but those that are work very reliably. For most Australian households, Google Home or Alexa is the practical choice.

Can I control smart blinds when I'm not home?

Yes, as long as your hub and router are powered and connected to the internet. You can open, close, or adjust blinds from anywhere via the manufacturer's app or your smart home app. This is useful for security (making the house look occupied) or letting in light for pets while you're at work.

Do smart blinds work without internet?

The motor itself doesn't need internet — local control via the included remote or physical button always works. Hub-based scheduling may continue locally depending on the hub. However, voice assistants (Google, Alexa, Siri) and remote app control require an active internet connection. Zigbee and Matter devices generally have better local control than Wi-Fi-only motors.

What is Matter and should I wait for it?

Matter is a universal smart home protocol backed by Google, Apple, Amazon, and Samsung. It promises that any Matter device works with any Matter platform — no more compatibility headaches. As of 2026, some blind motors support Matter (mostly via Thread), but the ecosystem is still maturing. Buy from a brand that has committed to Matter firmware updates and you'll get current functionality plus future compatibility.

How many smart blinds can one hub control?

Most hubs support 20–30 devices. The MotionBlinds Wi-Fi Bridge handles up to 30 blinds, Somfy TaHoma supports 200+ devices across its ecosystem, and the IKEA DIRIGERA manages around 30. For a typical Melbourne home with 8–15 windows, a single hub is more than enough. Larger homes or commercial spaces may need a second hub.
