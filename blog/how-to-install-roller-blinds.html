How-To Guide

How to Install Roller Blinds

One drill, two brackets, thirty minutes. Installing roller blinds is one of the most satisfying DIY jobs you can do — visible results before lunch, and you'll wonder why you ever considered paying someone else to do it.

17 March 2026 · 12 min read

Roller blinds are the most DIY-friendly window covering you can install. Two brackets, a handful of screws, and a spirit level — that is genuinely all it takes. The process is the same whether you have ordered a single blockout roller blind for your bedroom or you are fitting out every window in a new home.

This guide covers the complete installation: inside mount (within the recess) and face mount (on the wall above the frame), bracket positioning, child safety compliance under Australian standards, and how to troubleshoot the three most common problems. If you have not measured yet, read the complete measuring guide first — correct measurements are the prerequisite for everything that follows.

Total time: 20–30 minutes per blind for first-timers. Most of that is measuring and levelling — the actual bracket fixing takes under five minutes once you know where you are going.

📌 Plasterboard? Get Wall Anchors

If you are face-mounting into plasterboard (most Australian internal walls), grab nylon wall anchors or spring toggles from Bunnings before you start. Standard screws pull straight out of plasterboard under the weight and daily stress of a roller blind. For inside mount into a timber window frame, the supplied screws are all you need.

Inside Mount vs Face Mount

Confirm your mounting method before drilling a single hole. If you already ordered your blind for a specific mount type, the width was cut accordingly — stick with it. Switching from inside to face mount after the blind arrives can work but may leave visible light gaps on the sides.

Most Common

Inside Mount

Brackets fix to the top of the window recess. The blind sits flush within the frame — clean, architectural look that suits modern and heritage homes alike. Requires minimum 45mm recess depth.

  • Flush, built-in appearance
  • Doesn't cover architrave or wall
  • Works perfectly alongside curtains
  • Brackets screw into solid timber frame
  • Small side gaps (1–3mm) are normal
Best for Blockout

Face Mount

Brackets fix to the wall or ceiling above the window. The blind covers the entire recess and frame. No minimum recess depth. Better light-blocking performance with generous overlap.

  • Eliminates most light gaps
  • No recess depth requirement
  • Makes windows appear taller
  • Conceals uneven reveals or damaged frames
  • Needs wall anchors for plasterboard

Step-by-Step Installation

The steps below cover all standard roller blinds — chain-operated, spring-loaded, and motorised. The only differences between brands are which bracket side the chain sits on and how the tube clips in.

1

Unpack and Check Components

Lay everything out: the roller blind itself, two brackets (one chain-side, one idle-side — they are often different shapes), screws, child-safety chain tensioner, and pelmet fascia if included. Compare against the packing list. Check the fabric for transit damage and confirm the width matches your order. Sorting a discrepancy before drilling is trivial; sorting it after is painful.

2

Mark Bracket Positions

Inside mount: Hold a bracket against the top of the window recess, within 10mm of each side wall. Mark the screw holes with pencil. Repeat for the other side. Place your spirit level across both marks and adjust until dead level. Even 2mm of unlevel across a 900mm span is enough to make the blind track sideways.

Face mount: Mark bracket positions 75–100mm above the window frame — this gap lets the rolled blind clear the frame when fully raised. Measure equal distance from each side of the frame, then add your overlap (50–75mm per side for solid light blocking). Level across both marks.

3

Drill Pilot Holes

Using a 3mm drill bit, drill pilot holes at your pencil marks. For inside mount into timber window frames: drill 15–20mm deep — the timber is usually 30–40mm thick and holds screws very securely. For face mount into plasterboard: drill through and insert wall anchors or spring toggles before continuing. If your drill hits something solid behind the plasterboard — a timber stud — skip the anchors. Studs are significantly stronger than any anchor in plasterboard.

4

Secure Brackets

Align the bracket over its pilot holes and drive the supplied screws. Firm, but do not over-tighten — stripped threads in plasterboard anchors are a common mistake. The bracket must sit completely flat against the mounting surface with zero wobble. Grip it and try to wiggle it; if it moves, tighten further or use a larger anchor. Check level across both brackets one final time after tightening. It is much faster to correct now than after the blind is up.

5

Clip Blind Into Brackets

Insert the chain-side end of the roller tube into its bracket first — this end carries the mechanism and is heavier, so it needs to be seated before you try to flex the other bracket. Press firmly until it sits in the bracket channel. Then flex the idle-end bracket (most have a spring-loaded clip) and press the other end of the tube in until you hear or feel it click into place. Give the blind a firm but gentle downward tug to confirm both ends are fully seated.

6

Test Operation

Pull the chain to roll the blind fully down — watch the fabric as it descends. It should drop straight with even tension across the full width. No pulling to one side, no bunching. Roll it back up and check again. If your blind has a locking mechanism (a small plastic or metal clip that holds the chain at a fixed position), test that it engages and releases smoothly. If the blind tracks sideways, see the troubleshooting section below.

7

Install Child-Safety Chain Tensioner

This step is not optional. Under AS/NZS 13120:2017 — the Australian and New Zealand standard for corded internal window coverings — all chain-operated blinds must have a safety device that prevents the chain from forming a loose loop. A loose hanging chain loop is a strangulation hazard for children under 36 months. Screw the supplied tensioner to the wall or window frame within 50mm of the chain. Thread the chain through the tensioner so it stays taut against the wall at all times. For households with young children, also read our complete child safety guide for window blinds.

8

Install Pelmet or Fascia Cover

If your blind came with a pelmet (a flat decorative panel that clips onto the front of the headrail), now is the time to fit it. It snaps onto the headrail from one end to the other, concealing the rolled fabric and brackets for a finished, intentional look. If your blind did not include a pelmet and you want that cleaner appearance, pelmet strips can be ordered separately in matching or contrasting fabric colours from most Australian blind suppliers, including iSeekBlinds.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the four mistakes that account for the majority of DIY blind install problems. Each one is easy to avoid with a little forewarning.

⚠ Not Using a Spirit Level

If your brackets are even 2mm out of level across a 900mm span, the blind will roll unevenly — the fabric slowly migrates toward the lower side and eventually jams. Do not eyeball it. Do not use a phone app. Use a proper 600mm spirit level resting across both bracket positions. This one step prevents the most common post-installation problem.

⚠ Skipping the Child-Safety Device

Every corded and chain-operated blind in Australia is required to have a safety device under AS/NZS 13120:2017. A loose hanging chain loop is a documented strangulation hazard for children under 36 months. The chain tensioner takes under two minutes to install and it comes in the box. There is no acceptable reason to leave it off. If you have lost it, a replacement costs a couple of dollars at any blind supplier. See the full childproofing guide for additional measures.

⚠ Screwing Into Plasterboard Without Anchors

Standard screws have almost no holding power in plasterboard when the load is a downward pull from a weighted blind pulled daily. The blind may stay up for a week before the screw works loose, then falls suddenly. For plasterboard face-mount installations, always use wall anchors (for blinds under 3kg) or spring toggles (for heavier blinds). Better still: locate the timber stud behind the plaster with a $20 stud finder and screw directly into the wood. Nothing holds better.

⚠ Incorrect Bracket Orientation

Most roller blind brackets have a correct "up" direction and a correct "forward" direction. The clip or channel that holds the tube faces toward the room; the flat mounting plate sits against the wall or ceiling. Some brackets have a small arrow or "TOP" marking stamped into the metal. If you mount them upside-down or reversed, the tube will not sit securely and may fall under load. Spend 10 seconds confirming orientation before drilling.

Troubleshooting

Installed the blind and something is not quite right? These are the three most common issues and their specific fixes.

🔍 Blind Not Rolling Straight

The fabric tracks sideways when rolling up or down — the most common post-install complaint. In almost every case this is a levelling issue. Remove the blind from the brackets, place your spirit level across both bracket faces, and check. If one is low, loosen its screws, pack behind it with a thin washer or a couple of layers of folded cardboard, re-tighten, and re-hang. If the level reads correctly but the blind still tracks, the tube itself may be slightly bent from transit — roll it on a hard flat floor to check. A bent tube needs to be replaced by the supplier.

🔍 Blind Too Tight in the Recess

For inside-mount blinds: the manufacturer applies a factory deduction (typically 3–5mm per side off the ordered width), but if your recess narrows in the middle or at the bottom, the blind may rub. First confirm whether the fabric or the headrail is catching. Fabric rubbing the side wall is usually tolerable and eases with use. If the headrail itself will not fit, carefully file 1–2mm off the idle-end pin. If you measured at three points and submitted the narrowest width as instructed, this should not happen.

🔍 Chain Hanging on the Wrong Side

You have fitted the blind and the chain is on the side you did not want. The fix depends on your blind type. Most chain-operated roller blinds can simply be unclipped, rotated 180 degrees (swapping left and right in the brackets), and re-clipped with the chain now on the other side. Before flipping, check whether your fabric is wound as a standard roll or reverse roll — a standard roll faces the room; a reverse roll faces the window. If flipping changes this, you may need to re-wrap the fabric on the tube. On motorised blinds, the motor side is fixed and cannot be swapped.

Motorised Roller Blinds

The mounting process for motorised roller blinds is identical to chain-operated models. Same brackets, same positioning, same levelling process. The additional considerations are minimal:

  • Battery-powered motors (Somfy Sonesse, Rollease Acmeda, and similar): fully DIY. No wiring required. The motor sits inside the tube and adds minimal bulk. Charge via USB every 6–12 months depending on usage frequency.
  • Hardwired motors: Need a 240V power point within the ceiling or wall space near the window. The blind mounting is DIY; the electrical connection requires a licensed electrician under Australian wiring regulations.
  • Weight: Motorised blinds are heavier than chain-operated equivalents. Ensure bracket fixing is particularly solid — prefer timber stud or timber frame over plasterboard anchors for any motorised blind wider than 1200mm.

For a complete walkthrough on retrofitting motors to existing chain blinds, see how to motorise existing roller blinds.

Quick-Reference Checklists

Inside Mount Checklist

  1. Recess depth measured: minimum 45mm from frame face to glass ✓
  2. Brackets marked at top of recess, within 10mm of each side wall ✓
  3. Spirit level checked across both marks — dead level ✓
  4. 3mm pilot holes drilled into timber window frame (15–20mm deep) ✓
  5. Brackets screwed in firmly, no wobble ✓
  6. Blind clipped in — chain side first, idle side clicks ✓
  7. Operation tested — rolls straight and smooth ✓
  8. Chain tensioner installed and chain threaded through ✓
  9. Pelmet clipped on (if supplied) ✓

Face Mount Checklist

  1. Brackets marked 75–100mm above window frame ✓
  2. Overlap position marked: 50–75mm wider than frame on each side ✓
  3. Spirit level checked — dead level ✓
  4. Wall type confirmed: timber stud, plasterboard, or masonry ✓
  5. Wall anchors installed if plasterboard (or screwed directly into stud) ✓
  6. Brackets firmly fixed, no wobble ✓
  7. Blind clipped in — chain side first ✓
  8. Operation tested — rolls straight ✓
  9. Chain tensioner installed ✓
Installing Multiple Blinds? Work in Batches

If you are fitting out an entire home, mark and level all windows first, then drill all holes, then fix all brackets, then hang all blinds. Batching by task is faster than completing each window individually. Label each blind with a piece of painter's tape noting the window (e.g. "Master Bedroom — left") before you start — mix-ups between similar-sized blinds waste 30 minutes and the tapes take five seconds.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install roller blinds without drilling?
For lightweight blinds under 2kg, adhesive brackets or tension rods can work — especially useful in rental properties where drilling is restricted. However adhesive strength degrades in Australian summer heat (adhesive pads can soften above 35°C), and tension rods are only reliable up to spans of about 1200mm. For anything larger, heavier, or in a west-facing room that gets hot, drilled brackets are the only reliable long-term fix.
Do I need wall plugs for plasterboard?
Yes, always. Standard screws have almost no holding power in plasterboard under a sustained hanging load. Use nylon wall anchors for blinds under 3kg, or spring toggles for heavier blinds or wide installations. The best option where possible: locate the timber stud behind the plasterboard with a stud finder and screw directly into it. A stud holds more securely than any anchor and will last the lifetime of the blind without re-fixing.
How far apart should roller blind brackets be?
Within 10mm of each end of the headrail. Roller blind brackets are designed to support the tube at its endpoints. Positioning them further inward causes the aluminium tube to bow under the weight of the fabric — you will see this as a slight curve to the headrail, and over time it affects how the blind rolls. On blinds wider than 2400mm, most manufacturers supply a centre-support bracket as standard.
Can I install motorised roller blinds myself?
Yes. The bracket mounting is exactly the same as chain-operated blinds. Battery-powered motors (Somfy, Rollease Acmeda) are fully DIY — no electrician needed, just mount the blind and pair the remote or app. Hardwired motors require a licensed electrician for the 240V connection only. The blind mounting itself remains DIY. See our motorised blinds cost guide for a full breakdown of motor types and running costs.
What if my blind is too wide for the recess?
If it is 1–3mm too wide, you can carefully trim the aluminium tube from the idle end (not the chain/motor end) using a hacksaw, deburr the cut edge with fine sandpaper, and re-insert the end cap. For more than 5mm excess, switching to face mount is safer than risking a poor cut. Contact iSeekBlinds first — if you provided correct measurements and the blind arrived oversized, we offer free replacement cutting.
How long does roller blind installation take?
Allow 20–30 minutes for your first blind, including setup and reading the instructions. Once you have done two or three, each subsequent blind takes 10–15 minutes. A full house of 8–10 blinds can realistically be completed in a morning by one person with the right tools. The levelling step takes the most time and is the most important — do not rush it.
