Child Safety

How to Childproof Your Window Blinds

Blind cords are one of the top hidden hazards in Australian homes. Here's how to eliminate the risk — whether you retrofit what you've got or replace with something inherently safe.

17 March 2026 · 12 min read

Every parent eventually does the "crawl around the house on all fours" check — looking for sharp corners, open sockets, and small objects. Most remember the obvious stuff. But one of the most dangerous hazards in Australian homes hangs in plain sight: blind cords.

This isn't a scare piece. It's a practical, step-by-step guide to making your windows safe. Some fixes take five minutes and a screwdriver. Others mean replacing the blinds entirely. We'll cover both, including which products are inherently child-safe and which Australian standards apply.

⚠️

The Risk Is Real

The ACCC and Kidsafe Australia report that blind cords and chains are among the top five strangulation hazards for children under 5. A young child can become entangled in a looped cord in seconds — silently, often while a parent is in the next room. The risk exists with any cord, chain, or looped string that a child can reach.

1.6m Minimum height cords must be secured — above a child's reach, even when standing on nearby furniture

Understanding the Hazard Types

Not all blind cords carry the same level of risk. The key factor is whether the cord forms a loop — a loop can tighten around a child's neck, while a single free-hanging cord is less dangerous (though still a risk if long enough to wrap).

Highest Risk

Continuous Loop Chains & Cords

Found on roller blinds, Roman blinds, and some venetians. The chain/cord forms a closed loop that can entangle a child's neck. This is the #1 cause of blind-cord incidents.

Medium Risk

Long Pull Cords

Common on venetian blinds and vertical blinds. Not looped, but if long enough (below 1.6m from the floor), they can wrap around a child's neck or become a plaything.

Lowest Risk

Internal / Concealed Cords

Found on some honeycomb blinds and modern roller blinds. Cords run inside the fabric or mechanism with no external access. Safe when the mechanism is intact.

Australian Safety Standards — What the Law Says

Australia has mandatory safety standards for internal window coverings under the Competition and Consumer Act 2010. Here's what you need to know:

  • All corded blinds sold in Australia must come with cord safety devices — cleats, tensioners, or breakaway connectors
  • Continuous loop chains must include a chain restraint device (tensioner) that anchors the chain to the wall or window frame
  • Loose cords must be supplied with cord cleats and instructions to install them at minimum 1.6m height
  • Retailers are required to provide these safety devices and installation instructions with every corded product sold
Important

These standards apply to blinds sold since 2010. If your home has older blinds, they may not have come with safety devices. You can buy retrofit kits from any hardware store — or contact us and we'll fit them during a free consultation.

5-Step Childproofing for Existing Blinds

You don't necessarily need to rip everything out. For many homes, these retrofit steps will eliminate the immediate danger:

1

Audit every single window

Walk through your entire home — including spare rooms, home offices, and spaces you think your child "never goes". Check behind furniture too. A cord hidden behind a couch is still reachable when a toddler discovers the gap. Make a list of every window with an accessible cord or chain.

2

Install cord cleats at 1.6m height

For any blind with a pull cord, screw a cord cleat into the wall or window frame at a minimum 1.6 metres from the floor. Wrap the excess cord tightly around the cleat after every use. This is a 5-minute fix per window — one screw, one cleat, done. Hardware stores sell them for under $5.

3

Add chain tensioners for roller blinds

If your roller blinds have a continuous chain loop, install a chain tensioner (also called a chain restraint or anchor). This device screws into the wall and holds the chain taut so it can't form a free-hanging loop. Most roller blinds sold since 2010 include one — if yours didn't, buy one and install it. It takes 10 minutes.

4

Move furniture away from windows

This is the step people forget. Cots, beds, chairs, toyboxes — anything a child can climb on — should be at least 1.5m from any window with a blind cord. Even with cleats installed, a child standing on a chair can reach higher than you'd expect.

5

Replace high-risk blinds in key rooms

For nurseries, children's bedrooms, and playrooms, retrofit fixes aren't enough — replace corded blinds with inherently cordless options. Motorised roller blinds, cordless honeycomb blinds, or plantation shutters eliminate the hazard entirely.

Room-by-Room Recommendations

Not every room needs the same treatment. Here's a practical breakdown:

🍼

Nursery / Baby's Room

Non-negotiable: fully cordless. This is where your child sleeps — often unsupervised for hours. Motorised blockout rollers are ideal: no cords, excellent darkness for sleep, and you can open them from outside the room without waking the baby.

Motorised Blockout Roller
🧸

Children's Bedroom / Playroom

Cordless is strongly recommended. Kids climb, explore, and pull on things. Spring-assist rollers or cordless honeycomb blinds work well here — affordable and completely safe. If budget allows, go motorised for the convenience factor.

Cordless Honeycomb or Spring Roller
🛋️

Living Room

If the living room is part of your child's daily play space, cordless is the safer choice. For rooms where children are always supervised, corded blinds with properly installed cleats and tensioners are acceptable — but check monthly that the devices are still secure.

Cordless Preferred — Retrofit OK if Supervised
🍳

Kitchen

Plantation shutters are excellent here — no cords, moisture-resistant (PVC options), and easy to clean around cooking grease. If you have roller blinds with chains, install a tensioner and ensure no chairs or stools are within reach of the window.

Plantation Shutters or Retrofit Chain Tensioner
🛁

Bathroom

Often overlooked — but children use step stools in bathrooms, bringing them closer to window height. Cordless honeycomb blinds handle moisture well and are completely safe. Avoid fabric Romans with exposed cords in bathrooms.

Cordless Honeycomb or PVC Shutters
🏠

Spare Room / Home Office

Even rooms your child "never enters" need attention. Toddlers are explorers. At minimum, install cord cleats. If there's any chance the room becomes a nursery or guest room for visiting families, go cordless now.

Print this out or bookmark it. Work through each item — it's comprehensive enough to cover every common scenario:

Window Safety Audit Checklist

Audit every window in your home — including behind furniture, in spare rooms, and in the garage/laundry
Install cord cleats at minimum 1.6m height on every window with a pull cord
Install chain tensioners on all roller blinds with continuous chain loops

Move all climbable furniture at least 1.5m away from windows with cords
Replace all blinds in nursery/children's bedrooms with cordless alternatives
Check that no inner cords are exposed or dangling from the bottom of blinds
Brief all caregivers, grandparents, and babysitters about cord safety
Set a calendar reminder to re-check all safety devices every 3 months
If visiting or renting a new property, audit window cords before your child explores

What About Renters?

If you're renting in Melbourne, you can still childproof without modifying the property:

  • Adhesive cord cleats — 3M-backed cleats that stick to the window frame without drilling. Less secure than screwed-in versions, but better than nothing. Check them weekly.
  • Cord shorteners — plastic wind-up devices that shorten the cord by wrapping it inside a housing. Clip them on in seconds.
  • Request the landlord to upgrade — under Victorian rental laws, landlords have safety obligations. A written request to install child-safe blinds or safety devices is reasonable, especially in properties marketed as family homes.
  • Bring your own — some families buy their own cordless roller blinds for key rooms and store the original blinds safely. Not ideal, but it works.
Melbourne Rental Tip

Keep the original blinds and all hardware. When you move out, reinstall the originals and take your cordless blinds to the next place. We can custom-cut roller blinds to fit your new windows — most of the hardware is reusable if you're staying within a similar window size.

Common Mistakes Parents Make

  1. "They never go in that room." Until they do. Toddlers are faster and more curious than you think. Childproof every room with a window — not just the rooms they "use".
  2. Installing cleats too low. 1.2m feels high when you're kneeling. It's not. A child standing on a bed or chair can reach 1.4m easily. Go to 1.6m minimum, measured from the floor.
  3. Forgetting the chain tensioner. Cord cleats are great for pull cords, but roller blind chains need a tensioner — a cleat won't work because the chain is a continuous loop. Different problem, different device.
  4. Wrapping the cord once and forgetting. Cords slip off cleats. Build the habit: every time you adjust the blinds, wrap the cord back around the cleat. It takes 3 seconds.
  5. Not checking at grandparents' houses. Many child-cord incidents happen at grandparents' or relatives' homes, where blinds may be decades old with no safety devices. A kind conversation and a $10 pack of cleats can prevent a tragedy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are corded blinds illegal in Australia?
Corded blinds are not illegal, but they must comply with mandatory safety standards under the Competition and Consumer Act 2010. All corded blinds sold must include safety devices (cleats, tensioners, breakaway connectors), and retailers must provide installation instructions. Non-compliant products can be recalled by the ACCC.
What is the safest type of blind for a child's room?
The safest options have no accessible cords: motorised roller blinds (the gold standard), plantation shutters, spring-assist rollers, or cordless honeycomb blinds. Motorised is best for nurseries — you can operate them silently from outside the room without disturbing a sleeping baby.
Can I retrofit my existing blinds to make them child-safe?
Yes, for many blinds. Install cord cleats at 1.6m height, add chain tensioners for roller blinds, and use cord shorteners to keep excess cord out of reach. However, some older blinds with internal cords or severely degraded mechanisms are difficult to fully secure — replacement is the safer option for nurseries and children's bedrooms.
How high should blind cords be kept from the floor?
The ACCC recommends cords be secured at a minimum height of 1.6 metres from the floor — well above a child's reach even when standing on furniture. Any excess cord should be wrapped around a cleat screwed into the wall or window frame at this height. Check monthly that the cleat is still firmly attached.
Do motorised blinds need child safety devices?
No. Motorised blinds have no accessible cords or chains, so they are inherently child-safe and don't require additional safety devices. This is one of the key reasons families with young children choose motorised options — zero ongoing safety maintenance.
