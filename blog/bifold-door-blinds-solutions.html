Why Bifold Doors Are Different

A bifold door opening can span 2.4m to 6m wide. The panels fold and stack — which means any blind covering the whole opening must work above the door track, not attached to individual panels. Or, the blind mounts on each individual panel and folds with the door.

Two approaches, completely different products. Getting it wrong means a blind that blocks your door from opening, or worse — one that tangles in the mechanism every time you use it.

⚠ The most common mistake

Installing a single wide roller blind over the entire bifold opening. The blind can't travel to the side when the doors fold, so you're stuck choosing between light control or using your doors. Avoid this entirely.

There are two mounting strategies for bifold doors:

  1. Above-opening mount: One blind (panel glide, vertical, or curtain) mounted to the wall/ceiling above the door frame. The blind stacks completely clear of the door path when open.
  2. Per-panel mount: A small roller blind attached to each individual door panel. The blind folds with the door, and each panel controls its own light independently.

Solution 1: Panel Glide Blinds

1

Panel Glide Blinds

Best Overall Above-Opening Mount Motorisable
Installed from $450

Panel glide blinds consist of wide fabric panels (typically 450–900mm each) that slide along a ceiling-mounted or wall-mounted track. They were designed specifically for large openings — bifold and sliding doors are their natural habitat.

When closed, the panels line up end-to-end to cover the entire opening. When open, they stack neatly on one or both sides, completely clearing the door path. The look is clean, modern, and minimal — one continuous fabric face with no slats or seams visible.

Pros

  • Built for wide openings
  • Doesn't interact with door mechanism
  • Full blockout, sunscreen, or sheer fabric options
  • Motorises beautifully
  • Clean, contemporary look
  • Easy to operate (one gesture opens all panels)

Cons

  • Needs 150–250mm stack space each side
  • Can't partially open (open or closed)
  • Track must be level for smooth operation
  • Higher cost than vertical blinds

💡 Pro tip

Mount the track 100–150mm above the door frame with a matching fabric fascia to conceal the track. This gives a built-in look and prevents light gaps at the top.

Solution 2: Vertical Blinds

2

Vertical Blinds

Best Value Above-Opening Mount
Installed from $350

Vertical blinds have been the go-to for large openings for decades — and for good reason. Individual fabric or PVC louvres (typically 89mm wide) hang from a ceiling-mounted headrail and can rotate open to let in light, or traverse to stack on one or both sides.

They're the most affordable option for wide bifold openings and handle wind and air movement better than fabric panels. The ability to angle the louvres gives you partial light control without fully closing the blind — something panel glides can't do.

Pros

  • Lowest installed price for wide openings
  • Angled louvres for partial light control
  • Handles breeze without tangling
  • Wide fabric choice (PVC, fabric, sunscreen)
  • Easy to replace individual louvres

Cons

  • Louvres can clip together in strong drafts
  • Less contemporary aesthetic than panel glide
  • Chains and wands can feel dated
  • Not as motorisation-friendly

Solution 3: Individual Panel Rollers

3

Individual Panel Roller Blinds

Per-Panel Mount Most Flexible
Installed from $300

One small roller blind per door panel, mounted directly onto each panel's frame. The blind rolls up to the top of the panel when not needed, and rolls down for privacy or light control. Because it's attached to the panel, it folds with the door — no interference with the mechanism.

This approach gives you granular control: close just the middle panels for privacy, leave the end panels open. Great for bifolds that open partially most of the time.

Pros

  • Each panel controlled independently
  • No stack-back space needed
  • Works with partial door openings
  • Affordable — pay per panel
  • Door doesn't need to be fully open to use blinds

Cons

  • Small panel width = narrow blinds (can look busy)
  • Must lower all blinds before folding doors
  • Alignment gaps between panels
  • Spring mechanism can loosen over time

⚠ Important

Always roll the blinds up before folding the bifold doors. Folding with the blinds down risks snapping the spring mechanism or tearing the fabric at the panel edges.

Solution 4: Plantation Shutters (Fixed Window)

4

Plantation Shutters

Premium Look Fixed Frame Mount
Installed from $800

Plantation shutters work on bifold doors when mounted as a fixed window treatment — installed in the wall opening above or beside the door track, not on the door panels themselves. The shutters remain stationary and the bifold doors open underneath or beside them.

This approach is common in heritage homes or high-end renovations where shutters suit the aesthetic better than fabric. They add genuine architectural value to the space.

Pros

  • Premium, architectural look
  • Adjustable louvres for light control
  • Extremely durable — 20+ year lifespan
  • Adds resale value

Cons

  • Highest cost option
  • Requires careful frame planning
  • Doesn't fully blackout
  • Not all bifold openings suit shutters

Solution 5: Curtains & Drapes

5

Curtains & Drapes

Above-Opening Mount Softest Look
Installed from $600

Floor-to-ceiling curtains on a ceiling-mounted rod or track can work beautifully with bifold doors — if you have adequate wall space on both sides for the fabric to stack when the doors are open. This approach softens the large opening and adds warmth that hard window furnishings can't match.

Sheer curtains with a blockout blind layer is a particularly popular combination for bifold doors that face living areas — sheers for daytime privacy and ambience, blockout for evening.

Pros

  • Softest, most residential feel
  • Works with any decor style
  • Blockout, sheer, thermal options
  • Can be paired with other treatments

Cons

  • Needs large stack-back space (400–600mm)
  • Fabric can catch in door mechanism
  • Less precise light control
  • Higher fabric cost for wide openings

Solution 6: Motorised Solutions

6

Motorised Panel Glide or Roller

Smart Home Ready Best for 3m+ Openings
Installed from $750

Any of the above-opening solutions (panel glide, vertical, curtains) can be motorised. For bifold openings wider than 3m, motorisation shifts from a luxury to a practical necessity — manually operating a 4m wide panel glide by hand every day becomes tedious quickly.

Motorised panel glides connect to apps like Google Home, Amazon Alexa, or Apple HomeKit. You can set sunrise/sunset schedules so the blinds close as the sun drops and open automatically in the morning.

Pros

  • One-touch operation of wide openings
  • Scene integration with smart home
  • Scheduled opening/closing
  • Child-safe — no cords or chains
  • Voice control compatible

Cons

  • Higher upfront cost ($300–$600 extra)
  • Requires power or battery management
  • Professional installation needed

How to Measure Bifold Doors for Blinds

Measuring for Panel Glide or Vertical Blinds (Above-Opening)

  1. Width: Measure the full door opening width. Add 150–200mm on each side for stack-back clearance. Example: 2,400mm opening → order 2,700–2,800mm track.
  2. Drop: Measure from the underside of your planned fascia mount to the floor. Subtract 10–15mm for clearance. Ceiling-mounted tracks: measure floor-to-ceiling, subtract 30mm for the headrail + fascia.
  3. Stack space: Mark on the wall where the blind panels will sit when open. Ensure no light switches, door handles, or artwork fall in that zone.

Measuring for Individual Panel Rollers (Per-Panel)

  1. Width per panel: Measure each door panel's inner frame width. Subtract 10–15mm from each side so the blind clears the frame seams when folding.
  2. Drop per panel: Measure from the top of the panel's inner frame to the bottom inner frame. Subtract 10mm clearance at the bottom.
  3. Note: each panel may vary slightly — measure each one individually.

Frequently Asked Questions

What blinds work best for bifold doors?

Panel glide blinds and vertical blinds are the top-recommended solutions — they're designed for wide openings, stack neatly to one side, and don't interfere with the door mechanism. Individual roller blinds per panel are the best option when you want to operate each panel independently or when stack space is limited.

Can you put roller blinds on bifold doors?

Yes — as individual panel rollers only. One blind per door panel, mounted to each panel's frame. A single roller spanning the entire bifold opening doesn't work because it blocks the door from opening. Panel-mounted rollers fold with the door, but must be fully raised before the doors are folded.

How much do bifold door blinds cost in Melbourne?

For a standard 3-panel bifold opening (~2.4m wide), installed prices are: panel glide blinds $450–$900, vertical blinds $350–$700, individual panel rollers $300–$600, plantation shutters $800–$1,800. Motorisation adds $300–$600 to any solution. Prices vary with fabric choice, panel height, and site conditions.

How do you prevent light gaps with bifold door blinds?

For above-opening panel glide or vertical blinds: mount the track on a ceiling bracket (not the wall above the door) and add a fabric fascia below it to seal the top gap. Extend the track 100mm wider than the opening on each side so the blind fully covers the frame reveals. For panel rollers, ensure each blind slightly overlaps the panel frame edges.

Are motorised blinds good for bifold doors?

Excellent for openings wider than 3m where manual operation becomes cumbersome. Motorised panel glide blinds can be programmed to open/close with your daily routine and are fully controllable via app or voice assistant. Budget an additional $300–$600 per motor channel.

Can I install bifold door blinds myself?

Individual panel rollers are the most DIY-friendly — measure each panel, clip the bracket, done. Panel glide tracks require precise levelling and wall anchoring into studs; a professional installation is strongly recommended. Ceiling-mount installations also need a straight, level surface — an out-of-level track causes panels to drift open on their own.
