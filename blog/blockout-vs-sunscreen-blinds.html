Blockout Blinds vs Sunscreen Blinds: Which Should You Choose?

iSeekBlinds Team 17 March 2026 10 min read

Contents

The Short Answer

Blockout blinds block 100% of light — use them in bedrooms, media rooms, and nurseries where darkness matters. Sunscreen blinds filter light while preserving your view outside — use them in living rooms, kitchens, and home offices. For the best of both worlds, a double roller blind gives you both fabrics on one window. Most Melbourne homes benefit from a mix of both.

What Are Blockout Blinds?

Blockout blinds use an opaque, multi-layered fabric that prevents all light from passing through. The fabric typically has a foam or acrylic backing bonded to the face material, creating a complete light barrier. When fully closed and properly fitted, they make a room genuinely dark — not dim, not dusky, but properly dark.

This makes them the default choice for any room where sleep quality matters. Shift workers, young children, and anyone in a bedroom facing east will tell you they're non-negotiable. They're also the standard in media rooms and home cinemas, where even a sliver of light ruins screen contrast.

Key properties of blockout fabric

  • Light transmission: 0% — completely opaque
  • UV protection: 100% blockage
  • Privacy: Total, day and night
  • Thermal performance: Moderate insulation (R-value 0.3–0.5 depending on colour and backing)
  • View through: None — you can't see out when the blind is down

Darker-coloured blockout fabrics absorb more heat at the window, while white or light-coloured backings reflect solar energy back through the glass. If heat management is a priority alongside darkness, choose a blockout with a white or silver backing — or consider honeycomb blinds for superior insulation.

What Are Sunscreen Blinds?

Sunscreen blinds (sometimes called solar screen or mesh blinds) are made from an open-weave fabric that filters sunlight rather than blocking it entirely. The fabric has thousands of tiny perforations — visible up close, invisible from a distance — that let diffused light and partial views pass through while cutting glare and UV radiation.

The key specification is the openness factor, which describes the percentage of the fabric that's open weave. This directly controls how much light, view, and UV passes through:

1% openness — Maximum protection. Blocks ~99% of UV. Faint view of the outside, excellent glare reduction. Best for west-facing windows and home offices with screens.

3% openness — The most popular choice. Blocks ~97% of UV. Good balance of view preservation and sun protection. Works well in most living areas.

5% openness — Maximum view-through. Blocks ~95% of UV. More natural light enters, but less glare control. Suits rooms where the view is the priority.

Key properties of sunscreen fabric

  • Light transmission: 5–15% depending on openness and colour
  • UV protection: 95–99% depending on openness factor
  • Daytime privacy: Good — outside is brighter, so people can't see in
  • Night-time privacy: Poor — interior lights make you visible
  • Thermal performance: Reduces solar heat gain by 60–80%
  • View through: Maintained, especially with darker fabrics

An important detail: darker sunscreen fabrics provide better outward visibility. A charcoal sunscreen acts like sunglasses — it reduces glare while keeping the view sharp. A white or cream sunscreen reflects more heat but obscures the view. Choose based on which matters more to you.

Notice something counterintuitive: sunscreen blinds are actually better at reducing heat gain. Because blockout fabrics absorb solar energy (particularly dark colours), they can radiate that heat into the room. Sunscreen fabrics, especially those with reflective coatings, redirect heat back through the glass before it enters your space. For serious energy efficiency, consider combining sunscreen blinds with double glazing.

Rather than picking one fabric type for the whole house, most Melbourne homes benefit from mixing blockout and sunscreen depending on the room's purpose.

Can You Use Both? The Double Roller Solution

Yes — and this is the most popular option for bedrooms and living rooms across Melbourne. A double roller blind (also called a dual roller or day-night blind) fits two separate blinds on one bracket system. The sunscreen sits closer to the glass, and the blockout sits behind it, closer to the room.

During the day, you lower just the sunscreen for glare control and UV protection while keeping your view. At night, you drop the blockout for complete privacy and darkness. The two fabrics operate independently.

Pro tip: For a double roller, choose a sunscreen and blockout in the same colour family. A charcoal sunscreen paired with a charcoal blockout looks cohesive. Mismatched colours — say, a white sunscreen with a navy blockout — can look disjointed. Your installer can show you fabric combinations that work. Learn more about layered window treatments.

Double rollers add roughly 60–80% to the cost of a single blind, but they eliminate the need for separate curtains or secondary window coverings. For rooms that serve multiple purposes — a bedroom that doubles as a study, or an open-plan living area — they're usually the most practical investment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you see through sunscreen blinds at night?

Yes. During the day, sunscreen blinds offer reasonable privacy because the outside is brighter than inside. At night this reverses — interior lights make your room visible from outside. Pair sunscreen blinds with a blockout layer or curtain for night-time privacy.

What openness factor should I choose for sunscreen blinds?

1% openness provides maximum glare reduction and UV protection while still allowing a faint view out — ideal for home offices and west-facing windows. 3% is the most popular choice, balancing view and protection. 5% lets in more light and view but offers less glare control. For Melbourne's strong afternoon sun, 1–3% is usually best.

Are blockout blinds good for insulation?

Blockout blinds provide moderate insulation by creating a barrier between the window glass and your room. They can reduce heat gain in summer and heat loss in winter by roughly 15–25%. For superior insulation, consider honeycomb (cellular) blinds, which trap air in honeycomb-shaped pockets and achieve up to 40% better thermal performance.

Can I have both blockout and sunscreen on the same window?

Absolutely. A double roller blind (also called a dual roller) fits two fabrics on one bracket — a sunscreen for daytime and a blockout for night. This is the most popular option for bedrooms and living rooms in Melbourne. The sunscreen sits closer to the glass and the blockout sits behind it.

How much do blockout and sunscreen blinds cost in Melbourne?

For a standard 900mm × 1500mm window, expect to pay $120–$220 for a single blockout roller blind and $130–$240 for a sunscreen roller blind (installed). A double roller combining both fabrics typically costs $250–$400 per window. Prices vary with fabric quality, motorisation, and the number of windows.

Do sunscreen blinds block UV rays?

Yes. Quality sunscreen fabrics block 90–99% of UV radiation depending on the openness factor. A 1% openness sunscreen blocks approximately 99% of UV, while a 5% openness blocks around 95%. This protects your furniture, flooring and artwork from fading — a genuine concern in Melbourne's high-UV climate.
