How to Clean Venetian Blinds the Right Way

Dusty slats driving you mad? Here's how to clean aluminium, timber, and faux wood venetians — without warping, scratching, or spending your entire Saturday on it.

17 March 2026 · 10 min read

Venetian blinds are one of the most popular window treatments in Australian homes — and one of the most complained-about to clean. Those horizontal slats are dust magnets, and once grime builds up, every beam of sunlight turns into a guilt trip.

Good news: cleaning venetians doesn't need to be an ordeal. With the right technique for your specific material, you can keep them looking sharp in minutes a week, and deep-clean them once or twice a year without damage.

First: Know Your Material

The single biggest cleaning mistake is treating all venetian blinds the same. Aluminium, timber, and faux wood each respond differently to water, chemicals, and pressure. Here's the quick breakdown:

Easy Clean
🔩

Aluminium

Water-proof and durable. Can be fully submerged for deep cleaning. Watch for bending — aluminium dents easily under pressure.

Handle with Care
🪵

Timber

Never soak. Water causes swelling, warping, and finish damage. Damp-cloth only, dry immediately. Polish to maintain sheen.

Moderate
🛡️

Faux Wood (PVC)

More water-resistant than real timber but not immune. Wipe with a damp cloth freely. Avoid prolonged soaking — PVC can yellow.

Pro Tip

Not sure what material your blinds are? Tap a slat with your fingernail. Aluminium rings like a tin can. Timber gives a solid, dull thud. Faux wood sounds hollow and plastic-like. You can also check the weight — genuine timber blinds are noticeably heavier.

What You'll Need

Your Cleaning Kit

Microfibre cloth (2–3)
Vacuum with brush attachment
Old cotton socks (the secret weapon)
Warm water + mild dish soap
White vinegar (optional degreaser)
Old towels (protect floor/bathtub)
Soft-bristle brush or old toothbrush
Furniture polish (timber blinds only)

Quick Weekly Clean (5 Minutes)

This is the routine that stops dust building into grime. Do this every week or two and you'll rarely need a deep clean.

The Sock Method

Both sides of every slat in one motion — no tools needed

1

Close the slats fully

Rotate the tilt wand so slats overlap. This gives you a flat surface to wipe and stops dust falling between them onto your floor.

2

Dust from top to bottom

Using a microfibre cloth or the sock method, wipe each slat left to right. Start at the top — gravity means anything you dislodge falls onto slats you haven't cleaned yet.

3

Flip and repeat

Rotate slats fully the other direction. Dust the reverse side the same way. This is the step most people skip — and it's why their blinds still look grey after "cleaning".

4

Vacuum the stragglers

Run your vacuum's soft brush attachment along the slats on low suction. This catches fine particles embedded in textured surfaces. High suction on aluminium blinds can bend slats — keep it gentle.

Monthly Wipe-Down (15 Minutes)

Once a month — or whenever you notice visible grime — it's time for a wet wipe. The approach differs by material:

Aluminium & Faux Wood

Mix a few drops of dish soap into a bowl of warm water. Dip your cloth, wring it until barely damp, and wipe each slat individually. For kitchen venetians with grease buildup, add a splash of white vinegar — it cuts through cooking oil without leaving residue.

Timber

Use a cloth dampened with plain water only — wrung almost dry. Wipe one slat, immediately dry it with a second cloth, then move to the next. Never let moisture sit on timber. Once all slats are clean and dry, apply a thin layer of furniture polish with a soft cloth. This restores the sheen and creates a dust-resistant barrier.

⚠ Don't Do This

Never spray cleaning product directly onto blinds — liquid pools in the cord holes and bracket slots, causing rust on aluminium and water stains on timber. Always apply product to your cloth first.

Deep Clean: The Bathtub Method

Once or twice a year, your aluminium venetians deserve a proper bath. This removes ingrained dust, kitchen grease, and that grey film that regular wiping can't shift.

1

Remove blinds from brackets

Open the bracket clips, slide the headrail out, and carefully carry the blinds to the bathroom. Lay old towels in the bathtub to protect both surfaces.

2

Fill, soak, scrub

Fill the tub with warm (not hot) water and a squirt of dish soap. Lay the blinds flat and soak for 15–20 minutes. Then wipe each slat with a cloth or soft brush. Stubborn spots? A paste of baking soda and water works without scratching.

3

Rinse twice

Drain the soapy water and refill with cool, clean water. Agitate gently. Drain again and do one final rinse — soap residue attracts dust faster than a dirty blind does.

4

Drip-dry before reinstalling

Hang the blinds over the shower rod or a clothesline. Rotate slats every 20–30 minutes to let both sides dry evenly. Only reinstall when completely dry — trapped moisture inside the headrail breeds mould.

⚠ Timber & Bamboo Warning

This bathtub method is for aluminium blinds only. Soaking timber or bamboo venetians will cause irreversible warping, swelling, and finish cracking. For timber deep cleans, hang them outside on a mild day and wipe each slat individually with a barely-damp cloth.

5 Common Mistakes That Ruin Venetian Blinds

  1. Soaking timber blinds. Water is timber's enemy. The slats swell unevenly, the finish cracks, and once warped they don't flatten back. Damp cloth only, dry immediately.
  2. Spraying cleaner directly onto slats. Liquid pools in cord holes and the headrail mechanism, causing rust and staining. Always spray your cloth, never the blinds.
  3. High vacuum suction on aluminium. Thin aluminium slats bend easily. Use the brush attachment on the lowest setting, or just wipe by hand.
  4. Reinstalling while still damp. Moisture trapped inside brackets and the headrail creates mould — especially in Melbourne bathrooms where humidity is already high.
  5. Ignoring the cords and ladder tapes. Dust settles on the strings and tapes too. Run a damp cloth along each cord during your monthly clean. Fraying cords are also a safety signal — if they're deteriorating, it may be time for new blinds.

Melbourne-Specific Tips

Living in Melbourne adds a few cleaning curveballs:

  • Road dust in inner suburbs. If you're on a busy road in Richmond, Brunswick, or Footscray, you'll notice grey buildup faster. Bump your dusting up to twice a week.
  • Coastal salt near the bay. Salt residue from Port Phillip Bay air corrodes aluminium over time. A monthly wipe with a vinegar solution prevents pitting.
  • Mould after Melbourne winters. Condensation on windows drips onto the bottom slats. Keep an eye on the lowest 2–3 slats through winter, and wipe down any moisture before mould gets a foothold.
  • Pollen season (September–November). Yellow pollen film is harmless but annoying. A quick vacuum with the brush attachment every few days during spring does the job.
Melbourne Hack

Time your big annual deep clean for late November — after pollen season ends but before summer entertaining starts. Your blinds will look their best for the holidays, and you're starting summer with clean slats rather than six months of accumulated dust.

When to Stop Cleaning and Start Replacing

Cleaning can only do so much. If you're experiencing any of these, it might be time for new venetians:

  • Permanent yellowing that won't shift (UV damage on older PVC or aluminium)
  • Slats that are bent, cracked, or no longer close flush
  • Cords fraying or breaking — a child safety concern
  • The headrail mechanism sticks, jams, or won't tilt smoothly
  • Visible mould inside the headrail that keeps returning

Quality venetian blinds should last 8–15 years with regular care. If yours are approaching that age and showing wear, upgrading is more cost-effective than fighting a losing battle with a cloth and a bucket of vinegar.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I clean venetian blinds?
Light dusting every 1–2 weeks prevents buildup. A deeper wipe-down monthly, and a full deep clean (soaking or detailed wash) once or twice a year. If you're near a busy road, construction, or have pets, increase frequency — Melbourne's inner-city dust is no joke.
Can I put venetian blinds in the dishwasher?
No. The heat and harsh detergents in dishwashers will warp aluminium slats and strip protective coatings. Some people suggest pressure washers too — also a bad idea. Stick to a bathtub soak with mild soap for aluminium, and damp-cloth wiping for timber and faux wood.
How do I clean venetian blinds without removing them?
The sock method is the fastest in-situ technique: put an old cotton sock on your hand, dip two fingers in a water-vinegar mix, and run your fingers along each slat — you clean both sides at once. For general maintenance, close the slats and wipe top-to-bottom with a microfibre cloth, then flip and repeat. A vacuum brush attachment on low suction handles the rest.
What should I never use on wooden venetian blinds?
Never soak wooden or bamboo venetians in water — they'll swell, warp, and the finish will crack. Avoid bleach, ammonia-based cleaners, and abrasive pads. Use only a lightly damp cloth (wrung almost dry) and dry each slat immediately. Furniture polish or timber oil once or twice a year keeps them looking good.
My venetian blinds have yellowed — can I fix that?
Yellowing on white aluminium or PVC blinds is usually UV damage or nicotine buildup. For mild yellowing, soak the slats in a bathtub with warm water, a cup of baking soda, and a splash of white vinegar for 30 minutes. For heavy discolouration, add a few tablespoons of hydrogen peroxide. Timber blinds that have yellowed usually need a fresh coat of lacquer or paint — it's often more practical to replace them.
