How to Install Curtain Tracks

Ceiling mount, wall mount, bay windows — every curtain track installation method broken down into the steps that actually matter. The track is the invisible infrastructure behind every curtain that draws smoothly and hangs properly.

17 March 2026 · 14 min read

A curtain track is the part nobody sees and everybody blames when something goes wrong. Install it properly and your curtains draw in a single smooth motion, hang with consistent folds, and look like they were fitted by a professional. Install it badly and you get curtains that bunch, snag, or sag away from the ceiling — problems that no amount of steaming or adjusting can fix because the fault is structural.

This guide covers all three installation methods — ceiling mount, wall mount, and bay window — with the specific bracket spacing, fixing types, and material considerations for Australian homes. If you have not measured yet, start with our curtain measuring guide to get the track length and drop right.

Total time: 30–60 minutes for a single straight run. Bay windows take 60–90 minutes for the first installation due to the angle work.

Track Types: Which Do You Have?

Before you touch a drill, identify what you are working with. The track type determines your bracket style, spacing, and mounting options.

━━

Standard Glide Track

Flat aluminium or PVC channel with loose gliders. Suits pencil pleat and pinch pleat headings. Available in white, black, or raw aluminium. The most common residential track in Australia — cheap, functional, invisible when mounted behind a pelmet or close to the ceiling.

〰️

S-Fold / Wave Track

Precision-engineered track with evenly-spaced carrier hooks (60mm, 80mm, or 120mm pitch). Creates the uniform wave pattern that defines S-fold and ripple-fold curtains. Usually ceiling-mounted. More expensive — and worth it if you want that contemporary, hotel-lobby look.

⌒

Flexible / Bendable Track

Aluminium or reinforced PVC track that bends by hand to follow bay windows, curved walls, and L-shaped corners. Available in both standard glide and S-fold variants. The go-to solution for any window that is not a straight line.

📌 Before You Start: Ceiling vs Wall?

Ceiling mount is the default for S-fold and wave curtains, and the best choice if you want curtains to run floor-to-ceiling. Wall mount works when ceiling access is difficult (concrete slab, bulkhead, air conditioning ducting in the way) or when the curtain header is decorative and meant to be seen. Choose before buying the track — ceiling and wall brackets are different parts.

Ceiling Mount vs Wall Mount

Most Popular

Ceiling Mount

Brackets fix directly to the ceiling. The track sits flush overhead, completely hidden once curtains are hung. This is the standard method for S-fold, wave, and any modern installation where the hardware should disappear.

  • Track invisible from room level
  • Creates floor-to-ceiling curtain drop
  • Makes rooms feel taller
  • Required for S-fold and wave heading
  • Must fix into ceiling joists or use rated toggles
  • Brackets every 300–400mm
Simpler Install

Wall Mount

Top-fix brackets mount on the wall above the window frame — similar to where a curtain rod would go. Easier to access, easier to find studs, and the default when ceiling mounting is not practical.

  • Easier access for drilling and levelling
  • Works under bulkheads and pelmets
  • Suits pencil pleat and pinch pleat
  • Can be combined with a pelmet board
  • Brackets every 500–600mm
  • Must clear window frame by 75–100mm

Bracket Spacing Reference

Bracket spacing is the single most common error in DIY curtain track installation. Too few brackets and the track sags under the curtain weight — especially noticeable on ceiling-mounted tracks where gravity works directly on the bracket screws.

Track Type Mount Bracket Spacing End Bracket
Standard glide Ceiling 300–400mm Within 50mm of track end
Standard glide Wall 500–600mm Within 50mm of track end
S-fold / wave Ceiling 300mm max Within 30mm of track end
Flexible / bay Ceiling 200–300mm Within 30mm + extra at bends
Heavy lined curtains Any Reduce by 25% Double-fix at ends if possible

Step-by-Step: Ceiling Mount Installation

Ceiling mount is the most common modern method, and the one that requires the most care with fixings. Follow these steps in order — shortcuts here cause problems that take longer to fix than doing it properly the first time.

1

Calculate Track Length and Position

Measure the window width and add 150–250mm to each side for stackback — the space the curtain fabric occupies when drawn fully open. Without adequate stackback, open curtains block part of the glass, reducing natural light. For a 1500mm window, your track should be approximately 1800–2000mm total. Mark the desired track centreline on the ceiling, parallel to the wall and set back 100–150mm from the wall face so the curtain clears the window frame and any sill projection below.

2

Locate Ceiling Joists

Run your stud finder along the proposed track line. Mark every joist position with a pencil cross. In Australian homes, ceiling joists typically run at 450mm or 600mm centres. Align as many brackets as possible with joists — screwing directly into timber is the strongest possible fix. For brackets that fall between joists, you will use spring toggles or metal butterfly anchors rated for the weight.

3

Mark All Bracket Positions

Start with a bracket within 50mm of each end of the track. Then space intermediate brackets every 300–400mm (tighter for S-fold tracks — 300mm maximum). Snap a chalk line or use a straightedge across all marks to confirm they sit on a single line. For ceiling mount, also measure the wall setback at every bracket position — if the ceiling-to-wall distance varies (common in older Melbourne homes), adjust each bracket so the track runs parallel to the wall, not parallel to the joists.

4

Drill and Fix Brackets

Wear safety glasses — ceiling drilling drops material directly into your eyes. For joist positions: 3mm pilot hole, 20mm deep, then drive the supplied screw. For plasterboard between joists: 5mm hole, insert a spring toggle, thread the bracket onto the toggle bolt, and tighten until the toggle grips the backside of the plasterboard. Each bracket must sit completely flush against the ceiling with zero play. Wobbling brackets will telegraph through the track as a visible sag within weeks.

5

Cut Track to Length (If Needed)

If your track is longer than the span, mark the cut point and use a fine-tooth hacksaw. Cut slowly to avoid burring the aluminium — burrs inside the channel will snag gliders. After cutting, deburr the cut end with fine sandpaper or a small file. Fit the end cap or end stop onto the cut end. Never cut from the motor-end of a motorised track — always cut from the draw-end.

6

Attach Track to Brackets

Starting from one end, click the track into each bracket in sequence. Most ceiling brackets have a spring clip that snaps over the track profile. You should hear a click at each bracket. After all brackets are engaged, slide the track laterally to centre it, then check that it moves smoothly — push the track with your palm and it should glide freely within the brackets (this is important for thermal expansion in Australian conditions, particularly on west-facing windows).

7

Load Gliders and Hang Curtains

Before fitting the final end stop, thread gliders or S-fold carriers into the open end of the track. For S-fold, ensure the carriers face the correct direction (the hook should point toward the room). Space is predetermined by the carrier pitch — 60mm, 80mm, or 120mm depending on the track system. For standard pencil pleat, add one glider per hook point plus 2–3 extras for adjustment. Fit the end stop. Hang the curtain and draw it fully closed, then fully open, checking for smooth operation the entire way.

Wall Mount Installation

Wall mounting uses top-fix brackets that screw into the wall above the window frame. It is mechanically simpler than ceiling mount because you are screwing horizontally into a vertical surface rather than vertically into an overhead one — gravity works with your fixings, not against them.

Position brackets 75–100mm above the window frame top. If you are fitting a pelmet board, the track brackets fix to the underside of the board, and the board itself mounts to the wall with L-brackets. Otherwise, the process mirrors ceiling mount: mark positions, find studs, drill, fix, attach track, load gliders.

📌 Wall Mount Bracket Spacing

Wall-mounted tracks can handle wider bracket spacing — 500–600mm — because gravity pulls the curtain weight straight down rather than pulling the track away from the fixing surface. But add a bracket either side of any join in a multi-piece track, and always within 50mm of each track end.

Bay Window Installation

Bay windows are the most challenging curtain track installation, but entirely achievable with careful marking. The key is maintaining a consistent distance from the glass all the way around the bay.

  1. Map the bay geometry. Measure the angle at each corner. Standard Victorian bays in Melbourne are 135° (three-panel bay) or 120° (angled bay). Measure the width of each section.
  2. Choose your track type. Flexible tracks bend to follow the bay angles and produce a continuous, smooth curve. Rigid tracks use angled connector pieces at each corner — these give a slightly more defined corner line but require precise angle matching.
  3. Pre-bend or connect on the ground. For flexible track, bend it to roughly match the bay shape before taking it up the ladder. For rigid sections, join them with angle connectors and confirm the assembled shape matches the bay.
  4. Fix brackets starting from the centre. Fix the centre-most bracket first, then work outward toward each end, checking the wall-to-track distance at every bracket. Add an extra bracket within 50mm of each corner bend — corners carry more stress.
  5. Test the draw. A curtain should draw smoothly around the bay bends without bunching at the corners. If it catches, the track bend is too tight — widen the curve radius or add a bracket immediately before the bend to keep the track profile consistent.

S-Fold and Wave Curtain Tracks

S-fold (also known as ripple fold or wave) curtains are the dominant heading style in contemporary Australian interiors — and they require a specific track system. The evenly-spaced carriers in an S-fold track maintain the uniform wave pattern across the full width. You cannot achieve this heading on a standard glide track with loose hooks.

📌 S-Fold Track Requirements

S-fold tracks must be ceiling-mounted (wall mount does not give the correct header angle). Bracket spacing should not exceed 300mm — the wave pattern magnifies any sag between brackets. If ordering S-fold curtains from iSeekBlinds, the track is supplied with the correct carrier pitch matched to your fabric weight and wave size. Do not mix carrier pitches — 60mm carriers in a track designed for 80mm spacing will not produce the correct fold depth.

For installation specifics on hanging the curtains themselves — hook placement, draw direction, and overlap settings — see our curtain installation guide.

Fixing Into Different Materials

Australian homes use a range of ceiling and wall materials. The correct fixing for each material is non-negotiable — a bracket is only as strong as what it is screwed into.

⚠️ Timber Joists / Studs — Best Case

Screw directly with a 3mm pilot hole and the supplied wood screw. Timber holds the strongest. If your stud finder shows a joist at a bracket position, always use it. A single screw into a timber joist holds more weight than two screws into plasterboard anchors.

⚠️ Plasterboard — Most Common

Standard plasterboard is 10mm or 13mm thick and has minimal pull-out strength by itself. For curtain tracks, use spring toggles (rated 15–25kg each) or metal butterfly anchors (rated 10–15kg each). Nylon wall plugs are not rated for overhead ceiling loads and should only be used for light wall-mount tracks with lightweight sheers. For heavy lined curtains: ceiling-mount into joists only, or add a timber batten between two joists and screw through the plasterboard into the batten.

⚠️ Concrete Slab — Apartments

Melbourne apartments often have exposed concrete slab ceilings. Use a masonry bit (5mm for most concrete anchors), a hammer drill, and concrete sleeve anchors. Concrete is extremely strong — once the anchor is in, the bracket is not going anywhere. The challenge is the drilling: concrete dust, noise, and the need for a hammer drill make this the most physically demanding installation. Wear hearing protection and a dust mask.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️ Not Enough Brackets

The most common DIY mistake. Every unsupported span of track is a potential sag point. Curtain fabric is heavy — a pair of lined, floor-length curtains on a 2400mm track can weigh 8–12kg. That weight is distributed across the track, but concentrated at the points where gliders cluster when the curtain is drawn. Space brackets at the intervals in the table above, and add extras near draws and bends.

⚠️ Inadequate Stackback

If you install the track the exact width of the window, open curtains will sit in front of the glass, blocking light and making the window look smaller. Extend the track 150–250mm beyond each side of the window frame. This is the single biggest contributor to whether curtains look professional or afterthought.

⚠️ Track Not Parallel to Wall

A ceiling-mounted track must run at a consistent distance from the wall. If the setback varies — 120mm at one end, 90mm at the other — the curtain will hang further from the glass on one side, creating visible unevenness. Measure the wall-to-bracket distance at every single bracket position and adjust before tightening.

⚠️ Using the Wrong Fixings for the Material

Standard wood screws in plasterboard. Nylon wall plugs in a ceiling. Masonry anchors in plasterboard. All of these fail, and when a curtain track fails it typically does so suddenly under the accumulated daily stress of being drawn — the screws pull free, the track drops, and you are re-doing the entire job plus patching the ceiling. Identify the material first, buy the correct fixings, and do not improvise.

Troubleshooting

🔍 Track Sagging Between Brackets

Add intermediate brackets. This is almost always the fix. If the sag is visible to the eye, the bracket spacing is too wide for the curtain weight. For ceiling mount: aim for 300mm spacing. For heavy curtains, reduce to 250mm. You can add brackets without removing the track — most bracket types clip around the track from below.

🔍 Curtain Not Drawing Smoothly

Three possible causes: (1) Gliders are catching on a burr in the track — usually at a cut end or join. Slide a glider slowly along the full track length by hand and feel for resistance. Sand any rough spots with fine sandpaper. (2) The track is bowed or kinked — straighten the section or replace it. (3) Too many gliders are bunching at one point — redistribute the curtain hooks so the fabric weight is even across the track. A drop of silicone spray lubricant on the gliders helps in all three cases.

🔍 Bracket Pulling Away from Ceiling

The fixing has failed — most likely a plasterboard anchor that was undersized for the load. Remove the bracket, pull out the failed anchor, and re-fix with a spring toggle in a new position (you cannot re-use the same hole for a toggle because the plasterboard is damaged). If possible, shift the bracket 20–30mm to hit a joist. Fill the old hole with plasterboard filler.

🔍 S-Fold Curtain Not Holding Wave Pattern

The carriers may be in the wrong orientation (hook facing the window instead of the room), the carrier pitch may not match the curtain spacing, or the track is sagging between brackets — even 2mm of sag distorts the wave. Check carrier direction first, then bracket spacing. S-fold tracks are the most bracket-hungry of all track types.

Frequently Asked Questions

How far apart should curtain track brackets be?
For ceiling-mounted tracks: every 300–400mm. For wall-mounted tracks: every 500–600mm. Always place a bracket within 50mm of each track end, and add extra brackets near joins and bends in bay window installations. S-fold and wave tracks should not exceed 300mm between brackets due to the precision required to maintain the fold pattern.
Can you ceiling-mount curtain tracks on plasterboard?
Yes, but only with appropriate fixings. Spring toggles or metal butterfly anchors rated for the combined weight of track plus curtains. For heavy curtains (lined, floor-length), fixing into ceiling joists is strongly recommended — a stud finder locates joists through plasterboard. Never use nylon wall plugs for ceiling-mount applications; they are not rated for sustained overhead loads.
What is the difference between a curtain track and a curtain rod?
Tracks are flat or curved channels with internal gliders — the curtain slides smoothly along the track and the hardware is almost invisible. Rods are visible decorative poles with rings. Tracks suit modern interiors, S-fold and wave heading styles, bay windows, and ceiling mounting. Rods suit traditional rooms where the hardware is part of the design. If you want floor-to-ceiling, hardware-free look, track is the right choice.
How wide should a curtain track be compared to the window?
Extend the track 150–250mm beyond each side of the window frame. This stackback space allows curtains to clear the glass completely when drawn open, maximising natural light and making the window appear wider. For S-fold curtains, allow slightly more stackback — the wave folds occupy more lateral space when stacked open compared to pencil pleat.
Can I install a curtain track in a bay window myself?
Yes. Flexible tracks bend to follow the bay angle. Rigid tracks use angled connectors at each corner. Ceiling mount is most common for bay windows. The main challenge is keeping the track equidistant from the glass around each bend — use a consistent spacer measurement at every bracket point. Pre-bend the track on the floor to match the bay shape before mounting.
Do S-fold curtains need a special track?
Yes. S-fold (also called ripple fold or wave) curtains require a track with evenly-spaced carrier hooks — typically at 60mm, 80mm, or 120mm intervals depending on the fold size. The carriers maintain the uniform wave pattern. Standard tracks with loose gliders will not hold the S-fold shape.
